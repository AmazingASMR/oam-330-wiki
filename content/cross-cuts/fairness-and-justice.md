# Cross-cut — Fairness and justice

## Why this matters
Fairness is one of the few constructs that genuinely runs cross-module: equity theory frames it as input/output ratios in Motivation; distributive vs. procedural justice splits the construct; in Negotiation, fairness perceptions can override BATNA and tank rationally-good deals; in Ethics, fairness/reciprocity is one of the five Moral Foundations and Thomas & Ely's paradigm 1 makes it the moral case for diversity. Williams returns to "fair process" as a near-universal expectation even when "fair allocation" varies.

## The two-axis frame
- **Distributive justice** — was the *outcome* fair? (did I get the share I deserved?)
- **Procedural justice** — was the *process* fair? (transparent, voice, dignity, consistent across identities)
- Williams's headline counterintuitive finding: **procedural justice is the strongest predictor of job performance** (deck 08, slide 16) — managers over-focus on outcomes; the data say *how* you decide matters more than *what* you decide. MCQ-likely.

## How it surfaces by module

### Module 5 — Motivation
The heart of "fair workplaces motivate."
- [[concepts/equity-theory]] — Adams; people compare *input/output ratios* to a referent. Under-reward → low effort/quitting; over-reward → guilt/rationalization (weaker). Rewards evaluated **relatively, not absolutely** (Solnick & Hemenway: $50K when others earn $25K beats $100K when others earn $200K).
- [[concepts/distributive-vs-procedural-justice]] — outcome vs. process fairness; **procedural is the stronger performance predictor**.
- [[concepts/cultural-differences-fairness]] — three allocation principles: **merit / equality / need**. Equity is the individualist default; collectivist contexts may favor equality or need, and credit ingroup-solidarity work as legitimate input.
- Distractor pair: [[concepts/expectancy-theory]] (forward effort → performance → reward → goal chain, *multiplicative*) vs. [[concepts/equity-theory]] (sideways comparison to referent). Totally different mechanism.

### Module 8 — Social Identity & Diversity
- [[concepts/discrimination-and-fairness]] — Thomas & Ely paradigm 1: the *moral/legal* case for diversity (avoid harm, treat fairly, comply with disparate-impact standard). Distinguish from [[concepts/access-and-legitimacy]] paradigm 2 (strategic/market case). Paradigm 1 = right; paradigm 2 = profitable.

### Module 9 — Negotiation
Fairness can constructively or destructively override BATNA.
- [[concepts/batna]] — your *outside option* sets the rational floor. **Always accept above BATNA, even if it "feels unfair"** — Malhotra/Shapiro: rejected $8.5M because it felt unfair next to $10M, but litigation EV was only $7.4M. He should have taken the deal.
- [[concepts/reservation-price]] — quantification of BATNA; the in-deal walk-away number. Olivia's car: reservation $31K, accepted $32K → BATNA-violating accept driven by concession illusion.
- [[concepts/positions-vs-interests]] — orange / cake-and-juice: fighting over a "fair split" of positions hides the fact that interests (peel vs. juice) are aligned. Fairness-on-positions can suppress integrative gains.
- [[concepts/cultural-differences-negotiation]] — relationship-oriented (often collectivist) cultures may treat walking to BATNA as more costly because the long-term tie *is* the asset; fairness anchors and contract-vs-relationship framings differ.

### Module 11 — Ethics
- [[concepts/moral-foundations-five]] — **Fairness/Reciprocity** is one of the five categories ("rules should be fair for everyone"). Slide-canonical violation: secretly committing to hire only people of your own race/religion (Fairness, not Care — common trap).
- [[concepts/ethical-frameworks-sources]] — faith, moral foundations, professional codes; each frames "fairness" differently. Professional codes list fairness explicitly.

## High-yield MCQ traps
- **Distributive vs. procedural** — highest-frequency distractor pair in this cross-cut; memorize the one-liners.
- **"Procedural is the stronger performance predictor"** — counterintuitive, slide-flagged, almost certain to be tested.
- **Equity ≠ expectancy** — comparison to referent (sideways) vs. multiplicative effort→reward chain (forward). Different mechanisms.
- **Above-BATNA deals that "feel unfair" are still rationally accept-worthy** — Malhotra/Shapiro is the entire reading's punchline.
- **Cultural fairness preferences are not universal** — equity is the *individualist* default; merit/equality/need is the trio. The *desire for fair process* is universal; the *allocation principle* isn't.
- **Discrimination need not be intentional** to violate the law — disparate-impact standard. Paradigm 1 is morally driven; don't swap with access-and-legitimacy.
- **Fairness/Reciprocity vs. Care/Harm** — discriminatory hiring is a Fairness violation, not Care.

## Synthesis question patterns
- *"Two employees receive identical denied-promotion outcomes; one feels far more fairly treated because her manager explained criteria, listened, and treated her with dignity. This illustrates ___."* → procedural justice (and its outsized link to performance).
- *"Shapiro rejects an $8.5M settlement when his litigation BATNA's expected value is $7.4M, citing the offer 'feels unfair' relative to $10M. Per Malhotra, this is ___."* → a fairness perception overriding BATNA — irrational; the deal should have been accepted.
- *"In a collectivist context, an employee who mentors juniors and maintains team harmony expects this to count as ___ when bonuses are allocated."* → legitimate input under equity theory; allocation may also lean toward equality/need rather than pure merit.
