# Cross-cut — Cultural differences

## Why this matters
Cultural differences are Williams's recurring through-line: practices that work in NYC backfire in Beijing. The theme appears in 5+ modules (M4, M5, M6, M7, M8, M9), so MCQ synthesis stems can pull a scenario from one module and demand a cultural-dimension answer from another. The exam will reward students who can name *which* dimension is at play, not just "culture matters."

## Master dimensions
**Hofstede's 5 dimensions** (slide-listed in M4): uncertainty avoidance · long- vs. short-term orientation · masculinity vs. femininity · **power distance** · **individualism–collectivism**.
- M4 emphasizes **individualism–collectivism** as the master cultural lens for the course.
- M7 emphasizes **power distance** as the cockpit/leadership lens.
- The other three dimensions are listed but not deeply developed in the concept pages.

## How it surfaces by module

### Module 4 — Personality & Culture
- **Rice-vs-wheat (Talhelm 2014)** is the origin-story mechanism: rice paddies require coordinated labor → collectivist/holistic; wheat is solo → individualist/analytic. Holds country, language, religion, government constant.
- **Individualism vs. collectivism** is the master cultural lens; "best in my class" vs. "us best class," "squeaky wheel" vs. "nail that sticks up."
- Cultural values originate in **geography, economics, disease exposure, climate, religion, crop type** — not just "history."
- Moral dilemmas split on **universalism (individualist) vs. particularism (collectivist)** — Ben in LA, friend-loyalty vignettes, Crunchers Inc. Beijing.
- Links: [[concepts/individualism-collectivism]], [[concepts/rice-vs-wheat-farming]], [[concepts/personal-cultural-values-origins]], [[concepts/culture-and-moral-dilemmas]]

### Module 5 — Motivation (fairness)
- Allocation principle varies: **merit (equity) / equality / need**. Equity dominates in individualist cultures; collectivist contexts may favor equality or need.
- What counts as **input** varies: collectivist contexts credit ingroup-solidarity efforts (mentoring, harmony), not just individual task output.
- **Universal across cultures**: desire for interesting work + autonomy and fair, transparent procedures. The *content* of fair allocation varies; the *desire for fair process* doesn't.
- Links: [[concepts/cultural-differences-fairness]]

### Module 6 — Influence & Persuasion
- **Reciprocity** (Cialdini) can backfire in collectivistic cultures — gifts read as brown-nosing/bribery rather than sincerity. Slide flags reciprocity is "not always nonconscious — especially in collectivistic cultural contexts."
- Links: [[concepts/cialdini-reciprocity]]

### Module 7 — Power & Leadership
- **Power distance** = Hofstede dimension; describes cultures, not individuals. High PD ≠ "bad" (brings respect for elders, role clarity).
- **KAL 801 (Guam, 1997)** is the canonical PD scenario: high-PD cockpit, mitigated speech, captain misses hints, 228 die. Pilot PDI rank: Brazil #1, S. Korea #2; PD-crash correlation r = .47.
- **Receiver- vs. transmitter-oriented communication** (Gladwell): high-PD listener decodes; low-PD speaker is blunt. Korean Air's fix: English in cockpit + mitigated-speech training.
- Links: [[concepts/power-distance]], [[concepts/cultural-power-leadership]]

### Module 8 — Social Identity & Diversity
- **Culture shock** = realizing others operate under different rules — and that there *was* a rule. Conflicts come from *small unrecognized* differences, not big obvious ones.
- Predictable pattern when groups diversify: home-team blames newcomers as ignorant or cheating.
- Reducers: having a **partner** with shared rules; having survived a **prior shock**.
- Links: [[concepts/culture-shock]]

### Module 9 — Negotiation
- **Contract vs. relationship** as the goal: individualistic = paper agreement is the deal; collectivistic = relationship primary, expect renegotiation when climate changes (Australia → Japan sugar).
- **Direct vs. indirect** communication: directness can read as disrespect; "read between the lines" is normative in some cultures.
- Slide bottom line: **integrative > distributive globally** — travels better, preserves relationships, accommodates indirectness.
- Links: [[concepts/cultural-differences-negotiation]]

## High-yield MCQ traps
- Distractors swap **individualism** with **low power distance** — they correlate but are *separate* Hofstede dimensions. PD is about hierarchy; I-C is about group orientation.
- "Cultural differences" answers usually require naming *which* dimension (PD vs. I-C vs. universalism/particularism).
- **Rice-wheat is China-specific** — don't generalize; northern (wheat) China looks more like the West than southern (rice) China does. Defeats "Asia = collectivist."
- **KAL 801** is the canonical high-PD scenario — recognize on sight; Korean Air's fix (English + mitigated-speech training) is a PD-reduction intervention.
- High PD is **not** universally bad (slide 13); individualism is **not** "selfish" (it's authenticity/initiative).
- Across cultures, employees universally want **fair process** and **interesting/autonomous work** — only the *allocation principle* (merit/equality/need) varies.

## Synthesis question patterns
- *"Manager X exports practice Y from country A to country B and it backfires."* → Identify the dimension mismatch. Crunchers Inc. (NY → Beijing individual sales contest) = I-C. KAL 801 / cockpit procedure = PD.
- *"Which is universal across cultures vs. varies?"* → Universal: desire for fair *procedures*, autonomy, interesting work. Varies: allocation principle, contract-vs-relationship, directness, deference to authority.
- *"What reduces conflict when groups with different rules first meet?"* → Culture shock predictors: partner with shared rules, prior shock experience — not "more obvious differences" (those are easier; small unrecognized ones cause the trouble).
