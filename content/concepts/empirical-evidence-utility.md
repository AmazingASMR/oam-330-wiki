## Definition
**Empirical evidence** = evidence arising from the senses, from experience, or from observation (Kant's philosophical origin) — and in modern science, evidence arising from **systematic testing or experimentation**.

Contrast with: hearsay, logical reasoning alone, divine instruction, authority, or intuition.

## Why it matters
Empirical evidence is **the** standard for the course (Theme 3). It outperforms other sources because:
- It avoids the [[concepts/n-of-1-problem]] (one anecdote ≠ generalizable truth).
- It surfaces the [[concepts/correlation-vs-causation]] problem and forces tests that resolve it.
- It moves you up the [[concepts/evidence-hierarchy]] toward predictions of the next event.

"The higher up you go on the pyramid, the more accurately you can predict the NEXT event."

## Key distinctions
- **Empirical vs. anecdotal** — anecdotes are richly detailed, negative, or first-encountered → **memorable** but not **useful**. Slide 20: "More memorable" ≠ "More useful."
- **Empirical vs. logical reasoning** — logic alone (e.g., "all bachelors are unmarried") yields tautologies, not predictions. "*Some bachelors are very happy*" requires data.
- **One review vs. all reviews** — one review = worse source; all reviews aggregated = better source (slide 18).

## Example(s)
- Yelp: trusting one 5-star review (worse) vs. the full distribution of all reviews (better).

## Notes on application
- Definition match: "evidence from systematic observation/testing."
- Why dramatic anecdotes mislead: richly detailed = memorable, negative = memorable, first-encountered = memorable, "worked for our ancestors."
- Pyramid logic: the higher you go, the better you predict the **next** event.
