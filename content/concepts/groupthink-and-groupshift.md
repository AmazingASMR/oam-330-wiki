## Definition
**Groupthink**: a phenomenon in which the group's norm for consensus overrides the realistic appraisal of alternative courses of action; group pressures for conformity deter critical appraisal of unusual, minority, or unpopular views.

**Groupshift (group polarization)**: a change between a group's decision and what an individual member would have decided alone — the group's discussion shifts members toward a more extreme version of the position they already held (toward either greater caution OR greater risk).

## Why it matters
Both are by-products of group decision making that degrade quality. Groupthink kills dissent; groupshift exaggerates prior leanings. Together they explain disasters like Samsung's Galaxy Note 7 recall and many policy fiascos.

## Key distinctions
- **Groupthink = consensus pressure suppresses dissent.** Triggered by clear group identity, positive group image to protect, perceived collective threat. The mode here is ARTIFICIAL AGREEMENT.
- **Groupshift = polarization toward extremes.** Triggered by persuasive discussion, diffused responsibility, desire to differentiate from outgroup. The mode here is AMPLIFIED ORIGINAL POSITION.
- **Groupshift can be a special case of groupthink** (R&J), but on the exam treat them as DISTINCT: groupthink suppresses minority views; groupshift exaggerates the majority view.
- **Social loafing** — neither; that's effort reduction, not appraisal failure.

## Mitigations for groupthink
- Monitor group size (smaller).
- Have leaders play an impartial role.
- Appoint a devil's advocate.
- Use exercises that surface diverse alternatives without threatening identity.
- Discuss risks BEFORE possible gains.

## Example(s)
- Samsung Galaxy Note 7 (2016) recall — "cannot fail" attitude inside Samsung blamed on groupthink.
- Asch's lone dissenter findings underpin the groupthink concept.
