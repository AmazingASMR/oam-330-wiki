## Definition
Tactics for persuading via System 2: stronger arguments, two-sided presentation, and protecting your message from inoculation effects.

## Why it matters
Central-route persuasion is the *only* route that produces long-lasting attitude change, so it's the foundation any peripheral tactic must support. The strategies are counterintuitive — more arguments can hurt you, and disclosing your intent too early can backfire.

## Key distinctions
- **Central route** — works through argument quality (this concept). Peripheral route works through cues (Cialdini's 6).
- **Argument dilution effect** — adding *weak* arguments to a strong case makes the overall pitch *less* persuasive, not more. Make your strongest arguments, not all your arguments.
- **Inoculation effect** — "forewarned is forearmed." If the audience knows your persuasion intent in advance, they pre-build counterarguments. Keep quiet about your intent until you can lay out the full case.

## Example(s)
- Two-sided framing: present both sides, then argue against the side you disagree with — more persuasive than one-sided.
- Don't pad a strong recommendation with a weak ancillary reason; the weak reason dilutes the strong one.

## Notes on application
Argument dilution and inoculation are high-yield. Expect a question on "why might more arguments be less persuasive?" (dilution) or "why disclose intent late?" (inoculation). Distractor: claiming central route is faster than peripheral — it's slower but more durable.
