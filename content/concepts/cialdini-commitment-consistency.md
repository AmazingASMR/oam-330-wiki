## Definition
Once people take a position publicly, they feel internal and external pressure to behave consistently with it — and changing behavior can in turn change attitude.

## Why it matters
Peripheral-route lever ([[concepts/central-vs-peripheral-route]]). Two-way street: attitude → behavior is obvious, but the slide emphasizes the reverse — *behavior change drives attitude change*. Public commitments are far stickier than private ones; active commitments stickier than passive defaults.

## Key distinctions
- **Route** — peripheral; consistency is an internalized System 1 norm, not a reasoned argument.
- **Active + public + voluntary** > active + public > active > passive default. Coerced commitments produce **backlash**, not alignment.
- **vs. Reciprocity** — consistency binds you to your *own* prior commitment; reciprocity binds you to repay *another's* gift.

## Example(s)
- When **patients** (vs. office staff) filled out the appointment card themselves, missed appointments dropped **18%**.
- Cioffi & Garner (1996): students who actively signed a written volunteer commitment showed up at far higher rates than those who failed to opt out of a default.
- *12 Angry Men*: **ask for a private vote when in the minority** (to encourage dissent); **public vote when in the majority** (to discourage backsliding) — leverages public-commitment stickiness.

## Likely MCQ angles
The private-vs-public-vote rule is highly testable. Distractor: claiming coerced commitments produce stronger compliance — they backfire. Memorize the active/public/voluntary ladder.
