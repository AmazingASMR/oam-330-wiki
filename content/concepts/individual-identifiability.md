## Definition
People are dramatically more persuaded — and more generous — when an appeal centers on **a single identifiable individual** rather than on aggregate statistics about a large group.

## Why it matters
Peripheral-route lever ([[concepts/central-vs-peripheral-route]]): a System 1 emotional response to a named, faceable person bypasses the numbing effect of large numbers. The slide's headline: in a Save the Children study, **donations more than doubled** when the appeal was for "Rokia, a 7-year-old girl from Mali" rather than for the millions of statistics-described hungry people across Africa.

## Key distinctions
- **Route** — peripheral (System 1 emotional engagement), not central.
- **vs. Social proof** — social proof leans on a *group's* behavior to persuade; individual identifiability leans on a *single person's* face/story.
- **vs. Negative advertising** — both peripheral, but identifiability is positive (sympathy for one) while negative advertising is contrast-driven (attack the other).

## Example(s)
- **Save the Children**: donations more than doubled when the appeal switched from statistics about food shortages affecting millions in Malawi/Zambia/Angola/Ethiopia to a personal appeal for **Rokia**, a named 7-year-old girl from Mali.

## Notes on application
The Rokia/Save the Children example with the "more than doubled" lift is highly testable. Distractor: claiming aggregate statistics persuade better because they convey scope — they don't, they numb. Distractor: confusing this with social proof — identifiability is one named person, not a peer group.
