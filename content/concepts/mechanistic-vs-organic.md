## Definition
Two contrasting structural models. **Mechanistic** = bureaucratic, with strict departmentalization, top-down decisions, formalized job requirements. **Organic** = fluid, with few clear departments, distributed decision authority, and flexible job requirements.

## Why it matters
Determines fit between structure and strategy/environment. The wrong model in the wrong environment depresses performance — Wal-Mart needs mechanistic; Apple needs organic.

## Side-by-side (slides)
| Feature | Mechanistic | Organic |
|---|---|---|
| Departments | Strict departmentalization | Few clear departments |
| Decisions | Made from above | Made at many levels |
| Job requirements | Formalized | Fluid |
| Skills required | Fewer; salaries lower | More varied |
| Employee outcomes | — | More satisfied, more creative |
| Downside | Less satisfaction/creativity | More jockeying for status |

## When each fits best
- **Mechanistic**: cost-minimization strategies (Wal-Mart); stable / routine technologies (railroads).
- **Organic**: innovation strategies (Apple); smaller companies and startups; uncertain environments (young industries with many competitors).

## Key distinctions
- **Mechanistic ≠ inherently bad.** It's optimal for stable, cost-driven contexts.
- **Organic ≠ unstructured.** Organic still has structure; it's just fluid, distributed, and less formal.
- **Span of control** is a related but distinct lever — mechanistic tends narrow/tall, organic tends wider/flatter, but the two concepts shouldn't be conflated.
- **Hierarchy** as a general concept is broader; mechanistic models depend heavily on hierarchy, organic models lean on it less.

## Example(s)
- Wal-Mart, railroads → mechanistic.
- Apple, startups, young-industry firms → organic.
