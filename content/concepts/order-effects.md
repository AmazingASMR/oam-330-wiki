## Definition
In serial competition (figure skating, *American Idol*, Kidney Case-style advocacy rounds), **the last shall be first in the rankings** — the most recent advocate is best remembered and tends to win. This is a **recency** effect.

## Why it matters
A peripheral-route consideration ([[concepts/central-vs-peripheral-route]]) about *positioning* rather than content. In the Kidney Case, going last was a strategic advantage — a testable applied takeaway.

## Key distinctions
- **Recency effect** (the slide's emphasis) — *last* speaker advantaged in serial-judgment competitions.
- **Primacy effect** — *first* speaker advantaged; happens when judgments are made *after a delay* or when the first impression anchors all subsequent processing. The slide privileges recency for the live-competition format described.
- **vs. Argument dilution** — argument dilution is about *what* you say (don't pad strong with weak); order effects are about *when* you go.

## Example(s)
- **Figure skating**: skaters who go last tend to score highest, even controlling for skill.
- ***American Idol***: contestants performing later in the show tend to advance.
- **Kidney Case** classroom exercise: groups advocating later in the round tend to do better.

## Notes on application
"In serial competition the last shall be first" is the slide's exact phrasing — likely lifted into a question. Distractor: claiming primacy always dominates; or claiming order is irrelevant. Distractor: confusing with anchoring (a different bias from the Decision Making module).
