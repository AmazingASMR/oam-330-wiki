## Definition
Pre-negotiation work that answers three questions: **What do I want? What do they want? What's the larger situation?** Slide framework structures preparation around target points, BATNAs, multiple issues, and contextual research.

## Why it matters
"Not preparing" is the #1 common mistake on the slides. Preparation determines whether you can recognize a good deal, avoid the concession illusion, and shift a seemingly distributive negotiation into an integrative one.

## Key distinctions
- **Target vs. reservation/BATNA** — Target = highest aspiration. Reservation point = quantified BATNA = walk-away. Failing to distinguish them is a flagged mistake.
- **Self-prep vs. counterpart prep** — Most people only think about themselves. Equally important: **what do they want?** What's their BATNA? You can ask. Korkki: ask connections "what do you think a good salary for this job would be?"
- **One-shot vs. long-term framing** — Prep for integrative if relationship is long-term; otherwise classic distributive defaults take over and damage future interactions.

## Example(s)
**What do I want?** Set target, set BATNA (backup offer? moving home?), watch for biases — false anchors, sunk costs, endowment effect, IKEA effect. Think beyond core price (Job A: $91K + 10 days vs. Job B: $84K + 3 weeks + Tokyo conference).
**What do they want?** Research their interests; try to assess their BATNA (other offers on the house? wholesale cost?).
**Larger situation:** short-vs-long-term relationship, negotiation norms, reputational/precedent linkages.
**Korkki additions:** gather salary intel (Glassdoor, PayScale), defer salary talk early, don't accept on the spot, ask 10% above target, know your "walk-away."

## Likely MCQ angles
- Common mistake: conflating target with reservation point.
- Common mistake: assuming distributive when integrative prep would help.
- Endowment / IKEA effects show up as anti-prep biases.
- Counterpart-side prep (assessing their BATNA, interests) is as important as your own.
