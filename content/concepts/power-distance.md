## Definition
A Hofstede cultural dimension: the degree to which a culture accepts and expects unequal distribution of power between "regular people" and "important people," and how comfortable members are with that distance. **Describes cultures, not individuals.**

## Why it matters
Power distance shapes how subordinates speak to superiors, how bosses are expected to act, and whether grievances or dissent are voiced. Procedures designed in low-PD cultures (e.g., commercial-aviation Crew Resource Management) often fail when imported into high-PD cultures because subordinates won't directly contradict a captain. The cockpit case is canonical: time-pressured, intercultural, hard to decode indirect speech.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. individualism-collectivism / uncertainty avoidance** — different Hofstede dimensions; PD is specifically about *hierarchy*.
- **vs. [[concepts/power-changes-behavior]]** — PD is cultural/structural; that concept is individual-psychological.
- **High PD ≠ "bad."** Slide 13: high PD also brings respect for elders, fewer conflicts, sensitivity to roles, clarity of belonging. No "right way."
- **Low PD vs. high PD at work:** same rules for all vs. rank brings privileges; consult employees vs. boss should know; flat vs. tall structure; small vs. large CEO/floor-pay gap; grievance structures vs. no way out; boss-as-expert/friend vs. boss-as-benevolent-parent; open door vs. gatekeepers.

## Example(s)
**KAL 801 (Guam, 1997)** — high-PD Korean cockpit: first officer hints "Don't you think it rains more here?" and engineer hints "the weather radar has helped us a lot" instead of saying "abort." Captain misses it. 228 die. Korean Air's loss rate was 17× United's. Pilot PDI ranking: 1. Brazil, 2. South Korea (top); US/Ireland/SA/Aus/NZ at the bottom. Power-distance correlation with crashes r = .47.

## Notes on application
Definition; "describes cultures, not individuals"; identifying the KAL crash as a high-PD scenario; distinguishing PD from other Hofstede dimensions; recognizing that high PD is *not* universally bad.
