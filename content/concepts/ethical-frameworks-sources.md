## Definition
Three sources from which people derive their ethical principles, presented as three back-to-back slides titled **"Ethical frameworks: Faith traditions / Moral Foundations / Professional guidelines"** (slides 15-17, deck 26). Williams uses the umbrella label **"Ethical frameworks"** for the trio.

## Why it matters
The slide opener for ethics says: *"All of us have ethical principles – but not necessarily the same principles."* People disagree because they draw on different framework sources. Knowing the three lets you identify *which* framework a stakeholder is invoking when judging behavior.

## Key distinctions (slide 15-17 verbatim)
- **Faith traditions** — *"Religious traditions outline the rules people should live by"* (slide 15 — that single bullet is the entire slide body). Top-down, doctrinal.
- **Moral Foundations** — *"Across cultures, people's intuitive beliefs about what is 'right' fall into ~5 categories"* (slide 16). Bottom-up, evolved/cross-cultural intuitions. The 5 categories are Care/Harm, Fairness/Reciprocity, Ingroup/Loyalty, Authority/Respect, Purity/Sanctity. See [[concepts/moral-foundations-five]] for the full table with violation examples.
- **Professional guidelines** — *"Many professions have formal guidelines (or informal norms) for ethical behavior"* (slide 17). Slide-listed examples: Honesty, Confidentiality, Integrity, Fairness, Accountability, Transparency, Social responsibility.

## Example(s)
- **Faith**: Religious commandments / rules from religious traditions.
- **Moral Foundations**: YourMorals.org self-test (slide 16 reference). Violation prompts like "kick a dog in the head" (Care/Harm) or "use holy water to wash your clothes" (Purity).
- **Professional**: medical/legal/accounting codes of conduct; the 7-item slide list above.

## Notes on application
"Which of the following is NOT a source of ethical frameworks discussed in class?" — the three to recognize are **Faith traditions, Moral Foundations, Professional guidelines**. Distractors likely include "personal taste," "the law," or "outcome focus" (outcome focus is actually one of the 4 *enabling mechanisms* of unethical behavior in deck 27 — see [[concepts/employee-misconduct-types]] — not a framework source). The 5 Moral Foundations categories are a separate, more frequently tested item.

## Per Bazerman & Tenbrunsel
The reading does not enumerate framework *sources*; it focuses on cognitive biases (System 1 vs. System 2) that block ethical action regardless of which framework one nominally holds. This concept is slide-only, but the three-source taxonomy is explicit and structured across slides 15-17 of deck 26 — confidence is high on the framework names and slide-listed contents.
