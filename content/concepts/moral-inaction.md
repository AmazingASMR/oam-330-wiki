## Definition
Moral inaction is the **failure to act even when we recognize unethical behavior** — the bystander problem inside organizations.

## Why it matters
~**2/3 of people who witness ethical violations in their companies do not report them** (slide 19). It's the fourth enabling mechanism (slide 8) and the reason misconduct persists once it begins.

## Drivers (slide 19)
- **Powerlessness → inhibition** — feeling unable to influence the situation.
- **Apathy and withdrawal (learned helplessness)** — repeated exposure to ineffective protest produces disengagement.
- **Moral disengagement** — psychologically distancing oneself from responsibility.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Moral justification** — Justification rationalizes one's *own* unethical act; moral inaction is failing to confront *someone else's* unethical act.
- **vs. Social norms / obedience** — Obedience is *actively complying* with an unethical directive; inaction is *passively failing to intervene*. Note: Williams groups Milgram-style obedience under social norms (slide 15), not moral inaction — keep them separate.
- **vs. Ethical fading** — Fading = ethics not perceived; inaction = ethics *is* perceived but no action follows.

## Example(s)
- ~2/3 non-reporting rate of witnessed misconduct.
- Wells Fargo bankers who saw colleagues opening fake accounts but didn't escalate; bystanders to harassment.

## Likely MCQ angles
"An employee sees a colleague falsifying expense reports but says nothing — which mechanism?" → moral inaction. Distractor: moral *justification* (which would require the bystander herself to commit and rationalize the act). Powerlessness / learned helplessness / moral disengagement are the three drivers — expect a "which is NOT a driver of moral inaction" item.
