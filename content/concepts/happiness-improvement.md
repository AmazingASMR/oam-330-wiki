## Definition
Although ~50% of life satisfaction is genetic and ~10% is circumstantial, ~40% comes from intentional actions — most reliably from investing in social connections and from spending money on experiences and on others.

## Why it matters
The actionable lever. Set point and circumstances are largely fixed; behavior is not. Slides explicitly emphasize social connections and money use as the two main intentional levers covered.

## Key distinctions
- **Strong vs. weak social ties** — Strong: people with 5+ confidants are much more likely to be very happy (applies to both introverts and extroverts). Weak: even small talk with strangers/acquaintances raises well-being — though most people avoid it.
- **Experiential vs. material purchases** — Experiences yield more happiness because (1) no easy comparison, (2) hard to adapt to, (3) often social. Material goods invite comparison and adaptation.
- **Spending on self vs. on others** — Across countries, spending on *self* does not predict happiness; spending on others (gifts, charity) does. The $20 campus experiment: prosocial spending produced more happiness — opposite to what observers predicted.

## Example(s)
- Epicurus quote: friendship is the greatest source of happiness.
- "What are you doing right now?" study: people happier when others are around, even strangers.
- $20 prosocial-spending experiment.
- Brooks (Atlantic): happy people socialize more — 11 more times/year with relatives, 7 more with neighbors, 5 more with friends; "cohesive" families (59%) vs. "enmeshed" (22%) and "disengaged" (19%).

## Likely MCQ angles
Experiential > material and why (3 reasons). Prosocial spending counter-intuitively beats self-spending. Weak ties also help.
