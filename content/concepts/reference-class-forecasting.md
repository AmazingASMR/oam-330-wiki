## Definition
Bent Flyvbjerg's debiasing technique that operationalizes Kahneman & Tversky's "outside view": forecast a project's completion time and cost by (1) identifying a reference class of similar past projects, (2) building a distribution of their actual overruns, (3) locating the current project in that distribution, and (4) applying a corresponding uplift to the inside-view estimate.

## Why it matters
The most concrete documented antidote to the planning fallacy. UK Department for Transport adopted reference-class forecasting in 2004 and average project overruns fell to ~7%. It is the canonical example of *replacing intuition with statistics about the right reference class* — a specific tool inside the broader [[concepts/reducing-decision-biases]] kit.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. inside view** — the inside view builds a bottom-up plan from this project's specifics; reference-class forecasting deliberately ignores those and asks "how did similar projects actually go?" Inside view feels rigorous but rides on best-case System-1 simulation.
- **vs. [[concepts/reducing-decision-biases]]** — reducing biases is the umbrella ("focus on broad samples of data," consider opposites, use algorithms); reference-class forecasting is one specific implementation aimed at planning-fallacy/overconfidence.
- **vs. [[concepts/overconfidence]]** — overconfidence is the bias; reference-class forecasting is the corrective technique.
- **vs. [[concepts/availability-heuristic]]** — availability says we recall vivid past projects selectively; reference-class forecasting forces a representative *distribution* over the class instead of a remembered exemplar.
- **vs. strategic misrepresentation** — that's deliberate lowballing; reference-class forecasting addresses honest forecasting errors, not deception.

## Example(s)
- **Second Avenue Subway:** 1968 plan = 8.5 miles, 15 stations; 2016 reality = 2 miles, 3 stations, ~48 years late. A reference class of US urban transit megaprojects would have predicted huge overruns.
- **UK DfT 2004 adoption:** mandated reference-class forecasting for major infrastructure → average overrun dropped to ~7%.

## Likely MCQ angles
Attribute to Flyvbjerg. Identify it as the operationalized outside view. Recognize the four-step procedure. Distinguish from "more detailed planning" (which makes forecasts *worse*, per the Christmas-shopping study).
