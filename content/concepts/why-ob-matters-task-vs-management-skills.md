## Definition
Williams' framing for *why* OB is required in every BBA/MBA program: **career success = task skills + management skills**. As long as organizations are made up of people, technical/task expertise alone is insufficient — managing humans is the other half.

## Why it matters
Slide 01 grounds the whole course in this premise. Two pieces of evidence support it:
- **Economist Foresight 20/20 Study** (N = 1,656 senior executives) — surveyed what executives say drives success.
- **Cambridge alumni survey** — alums rated *Management & Strategy* courses **4.0/5** for usefulness vs. **3.5/5** for other core courses.
- Every top program (Stanford GSB, Haas, Kellogg, Wharton, HBS, Booth) requires an OB-style course.

## Key distinctions
- **Task skills vs. management skills** — task = the technical work; management = working through other people. Both required; neither alone is sufficient.
- **Common sense vs. evidence** — even though we all have intuitions about people (see [[concepts/three-course-themes]] Theme 1), running an organization on common sense alone underperforms an evidence-based approach.

## Example(s)
- A brilliant engineer who can't motivate their team underperforms a competent engineer who can. The science of organizations ([[concepts/ob-definition-and-goals]]) is what closes that gap.

## Notes on application
- Recognize the Cambridge survey ratings (Management & Strategy = 4.0; other core = 3.5) or that the Economist study had **1,656 senior executives**.
- "As long as organizations are made up of people …" → success requires **task skills + management skills**.
- Trap: claiming OB is optional or only for HR-bound students — it's required in every BBA/MBA.
