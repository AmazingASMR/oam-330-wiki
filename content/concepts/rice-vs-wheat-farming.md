## Definition
**Talhelm et al. (2014)** finding: within China, regions that historically grew **rice** are more **interdependent / collectivist / holistic-thinking**; regions that grew **wheat** are more **independent / individualistic / analytic-thinking** — even today, even among urban office workers who never farmed.

## Why it matters
This is a high-yield slide moment because it identifies a *causal mechanism* for cultural value differences. Rice paddies require irrigation infrastructure and labor coordination across families (~2× the labor of wheat) — generations of forced cooperation cultivate collectivist norms. Wheat is a solo crop. The finding holds the country, language, religion, and government **constant**, isolating crop type as the driver.

## Key distinctions
- **Within-country variation, not just East–West** — defeats the lazy "Asia = collectivist" stereotype; northern (wheat) China looks more like the West than southern (rice) China does.
- **Mechanism, not just correlation** — labor demands of the crop, not climate or wealth, do the work.
- **Cultural persistence** — modern urban workers retain ancestral patterns; values outlast their original economic cause ([[concepts/personal-cultural-values-origins]]).
- **vs. Hofstede's national-level individualism scores** — rice-wheat shows that "national" values mask large within-nation variance ([[concepts/individualism-collectivism]]).

## Example(s)
- Triad task (sort dog/rabbit/carrot): wheat-region Chinese sort taxonomically (dog+rabbit, both animals); rice-region Chinese sort relationally (rabbit+carrot, rabbit eats carrot).
- Implicit-self drawings: rice-region participants draw themselves smaller relative to others.

## Notes on application
Definition match (rice → interdependent, wheat → independent within China). Scenario: "Why might a manager in Guangzhou (rice) face different team dynamics than one in Beijing (wheat)?" → Talhelm. Distractor: claiming China is uniformly collectivist. Distractor: attributing the effect to climate or wealth rather than crop labor demands.
