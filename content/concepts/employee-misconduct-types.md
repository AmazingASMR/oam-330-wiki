## Definition
Per slide 7 of deck 27 ("Unethical behavior in organizations adds up"): **"Employee misconduct — Annual cost to companies: $50 billion. Many varieties, big and small."** Williams introduces it under the header *"Unethical Behavior at Work"* as the entry point to deck 27.

## The 7 slide-listed categories (verbatim, slide 7)
1. **Embezzlement** — diverting company funds for personal use.
2. **Stealing supplies** — taking physical materials/equipment.
3. **Time theft** — using paid hours for non-work activities.
4. **Harassment & bullying** — interpersonal mistreatment.
5. **Cutting safety corners** — bypassing protocols to save time/cost.
6. **Conflicts of interest** — personal interests biasing professional decisions.
7. **Leaking information** — disclosing confidential data.

Slide 7 ends with the prompt *"Your observations?"* — i.e., the list is meant to be exemplary/non-exhaustive, but the 7 items are the canonical ones.

## Why it matters
Misconduct isn't rare or extreme — most cases are mundane and committed by ordinary employees. The dollar figure (**$50 billion/year**) anchors why organizations need a culture of ethics. The slide title ("...adds up") emphasizes accumulation of small acts.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Psychological enabling mechanisms** — Misconduct is the *behavior*. Slide 8 of deck 27 then introduces the **4 enabling mechanisms**: (1) **Moral justification** (cognitive rationalizations — anonymity of victim/actor; sense of inequity), (2) **Social norms** (everyone's doing it; obedience to authority — Milgram 2/3 went to "XXX"), (3) **Outcome focus** (ends justify means — Ford Pinto, Wells Fargo), (4) **Moral inaction** (failure to act; ~2/3 of witnesses don't report). Don't confuse the *behavior list* with the *mechanism list*.
- **vs. Outcome-focused culture** — Outcome-focused culture is one *organizational aggravator* (Wells Fargo "Eight is great" cross-sell quotas; Ford Pinto fuel-tank lawsuits-cheaper-than-fix decision) that produces misconduct; misconduct is the resulting behavior.
- **Antidote** (slide 19): Culture of ethics = transparency, checks and balances, value process not just outcomes (J&J CEO 1982: *"First, how do we protect the people?"*). Enron is the counterexample — "black box" with "synergistic corruption" (lawyers, bankers, accountants all involved).

## Example(s)
- **Wells Fargo** — bankers opening fraudulent accounts under quota pressure ("Eight is great"); CEO John Stumpf blamed entry-level employees, fired shortly after. Categories: deception, conflicts of interest, conflicts driven by outcome focus.
- **TSA officer Pythias Brown** — stole $800,000 of items from luggage; quote: *"They ain't paying me. They're treating me wrong."* Theft + sense of inequity (moral justification facilitator).
- **Ford Pinto** (late 1960s) — CEO mandated <$2k small car by 1970; rushed to production with fuel-tank-behind-rear-axle flaw; even after discovery, decided lawsuits would be cheaper than the fix; 27 people died; criminal homicide charges. Cutting safety corners + outcome focus.
- **Enron** — TX traders manipulated CA power prices causing blackouts (anonymity of victim — moral justification facilitator).

## Likely MCQ angles
- "Annual cost of employee misconduct to U.S. companies?" → **$50 billion**.
- "Which of the following is NOT a type of misconduct discussed?" — distractors are likely items from the *enabling mechanisms* list (e.g., "moral justification") or non-listed items like "stock price volatility."
- "How many witnesses fail to report ethical violations?" → **~2/3** (slide 18).
- "Milgram's obedience studies — what fraction went to 'XXX'?" → **2/3** (slide 14).
- The category list is the testable item; pair it with the 4-mechanism list and don't conflate the two.
