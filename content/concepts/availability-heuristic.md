## Definition
Drawing conclusions based on **what information comes easily to mind**. Easily-available information may not be representative of all information — but its mental ease feels like evidence of frequency or importance.

## Why it matters
A [[concepts/system-1-system-2|System 1]] shortcut at the **evidence-gathering** stage. We feel like we're scanning the world; we're really just sampling the most vivid, recent, or emotionally charged memories.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/confirmation-bias]]** — availability is about **ease of recall** (what pops up); confirmation bias is about **selective seeking** (what we go look for). Availability is passive sampling; confirmation is active filtering.
- **vs. [[concepts/anchoring-bias]]** — availability biases what evidence you consider; anchoring biases the *numeric starting point* for an estimate.
- **vs. [[concepts/halo-effects]]** — halo generalizes one trait across attributes for the same target; availability generalizes from a vivid example to a frequency estimate.

## Example(s)
- **Slides: 9/11 driving fatalities.** After the 9/11 attacks, more people chose to drive (vs. fly), causing **2,170 additional fatal car accidents** — nearly matching the **2,606** Twin Towers deaths. Vivid plane-crash coverage made flying feel more dangerous than it was.
- **Academy Award winners list (slides):** judging frequency by what you can recall.
- **"Top salespeople" boss-question scenario (slides):** the names that come to mind first ≠ the actual top performers; recency, recent interactions, and visibility distort the sample.

## Notes on application
The 2,170 / 2,606 9/11 numbers are worth memorizing precisely. Distinguish from confirmation bias (passive recall vs. active seeking). The fix per slides: "make sure you are making a decision based on **all** the evidence, not vivid examples that come to mind."
