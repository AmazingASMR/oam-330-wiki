## Definition
The correlation between employee job satisfaction and job performance is **"medium" (~.30)** — real and replicable, but far from perfect. Employee satisfaction also predicts **organizational citizenship behaviors** and **less turnover, absenteeism, and deviance**.

## Why it matters
Managers often assume happy = productive (or vice versa). Slides emphasize the relationship is *moderate, not deterministic*. The link survives controls for past stock returns, firm characteristics, and industry performance — i.e., it's not just confounded.

## Key distinctions
- **Reverse causality** — performance might *cause* satisfaction (success feels good) rather than the other way around.
- **Third variables** — personality, supervision quality, etc. could drive both.
- **Why not perfect (~1.0)?** — many other factors drive performance: ability, resources, role clarity, luck. Satisfaction is one input among many.
- **Vs. job satisfaction factors** — that concept asks *what causes satisfaction*; this asks *what satisfaction causes*. See [[concepts/job-satisfaction-factors]].

## Example(s)
- Amabile & Kramer (NYT): inner work life (positive emotions, motivation, perceptions) predicts creative and productive behavior on a daily basis.
- An employee can hate their job but still perform out of fear, professionalism, or external rewards.

## Notes on application
Know the magnitude: **~.30, "medium"** — not "no relationship" and not "very strong." Distractor: claiming satisfaction is unrelated to performance, or claiming the relationship is causally one-directional. Recognize reverse causality as a candidate explanation.
