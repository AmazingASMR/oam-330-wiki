## Definition
Ethics are the guidelines and principles we use to evaluate whether our (or others') actions are "right" or "wrong."

## Why it matters
Ethical principles are reflected in rules and laws, but only imperfectly. Williams emphasizes that *all* of us hold ethical principles, but we do not necessarily hold the *same* ones — making ethics inherently contestable in organizations.

## Key distinctions
- **Laws/rules** — Laws codify *some* ethical principles, but ethics is broader: an action can be legal yet unethical (e.g., Ford Pinto cost-benefit) or illegal yet defensible.
- **Morals** — Often used interchangeably, but in this course "ethics" emphasizes the evaluative *guidelines* (societal, cultural, professional) we apply to actions, not just personal moral intuitions.
- **Etiquette/norms** — Social norms describe what is *common*; ethics prescribes what is *right*. Conflating the two is a key cause of unethical behavior (Lecture 27).

## Example(s)
- MBA Hackers case — applicants who hacked admissions sites were judged by Harvard, MIT, Stanford deans as failing tests of "integrity," "moral compass," "character."
- Sources of ethical principles per slides: societal, cultural, professional (varies across people and groups).

## Notes on application
Expect a definition match item: "Ethics are best defined as ___" — correct answer = guidelines/principles for evaluating right vs. wrong, NOT laws, NOT personal preferences. Distractor: "rules and laws perfectly capture ethics" (false — slide 7 explicitly).
