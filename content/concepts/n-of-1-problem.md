## Definition
The **"N of 1" problem** is **Reason #1** that empirical evidence outperforms other types: you cannot generalize from a single case (one person, one company, one event).

Three sub-problems on slide 21:
1. **Can't know all the reasons** for one person's (or one company's) experience.
2. **Low likelihood** that your experience will be the same.
3. **Cannot generalize** from an N of 1.

## Why it matters
"N of 1" is the empirical case against benchmarking, war stories, and CEO biographies. Pfeffer & Sutton's evidence-based-management example: GE's "rank-and-yank" being copied because it worked at GE (one company), or "Southwest is the most successful airline ... Herb Kelleher drinks Wild Turkey." Don't overgeneralize from a single benchmark.

## Key distinctions
- **N of 1 vs. small N** — both are weak, but N of 1 is the floor of the [[concepts/evidence-hierarchy]].
- **N of 1 vs. correlation problem** — N-of-1 says you don't have *enough cases*; the [[concepts/correlation-vs-causation]] problem says even with many cases, you can't infer causation without manipulation. They are the **two distinct reasons** empirical evidence wins.
- **Anecdote ≠ data** — an N-of-1 story can be richly detailed and memorable, but memorability is not validity.

## Example(s)
- "Steve Jobs wore turtlenecks and ran a great company → I should wear turtlenecks." Classic N-of-1.
- GE's rank-and-yank copied by other firms based on one company's success.

## Notes on application
- Identify N-of-1 reasoning in a vignette.
- Distinguish N-of-1 (Reason #1) from correlation ≠ causation (Reason #2). common confusion: swapping which is which.
