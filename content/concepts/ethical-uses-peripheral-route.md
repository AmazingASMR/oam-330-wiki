## Definition
Peripheral-route tactics ([[concepts/central-vs-peripheral-route]]) are **ethically defensible** when they *supplement* a strong argument the central route would also support — and **ethically dubious** when they're used to *distract* the central route from a weak argument it wouldn't support.

## Why it matters
The slide gives one operational test: **"Would your audience be upset if they knew about your use of peripheral-route persuasion techniques?"** If yes, you're manipulating. Cialdini adds the strategic argument: manipulation works once, then destroys the trust on which all future persuasion depends.

## Key distinctions
- **Defendable** — System 1 cues amplifying a case System 2 would already accept (e.g., genuine credentials, real similarities, true scarcity).
- **Dubious** — System 1 cues distracting from a case System 2 would reject (fabricated authority, fake commonalities, manufactured scarcity).
- **vs. Central-route ethics** — central-route persuasion has fewer ethics traps because it asks the audience to *think*; peripheral works around thinking, so the ethics burden is on the persuader.

## Example(s)
- **Defendable**: surfacing real expertise via a third-party introduction (authority) when your case is strong.
- **Dubious**: inventing fake deadlines (manufactured scarcity), planting false testimonials, or fabricating common ground.
- Cialdini: principles are "amplifiers of legitimate cases, not substitutes for them."

## Likely MCQ angles
The transparency test ("would the audience be upset if they knew?") is highly testable. Distractor: claiming peripheral tactics are inherently unethical — they're not. Distractor: claiming manipulation is effective long-run — it isn't, because reputational cost destroys future persuasion.
