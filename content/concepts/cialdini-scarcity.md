## Definition
People want something more when there is less of it to be had — limited availability, exclusive information, and one-time opportunities all increase perceived value.

## Why it matters
Peripheral-route lever ([[concepts/central-vs-peripheral-route]]) driven by **loss aversion**: the prospect of losing access looms larger psychologically than the prospect of gaining. Frame in terms of what will be **lost**, not just what will be gained.

## Key distinctions
- **Route** — peripheral (System 1 emotional cue), not central.
- **vs. Social proof** — both can drive urgency, but for different reasons: scarcity says "few people *can* have this"; social proof says "many people *are* choosing this." Opposite mechanisms — exclusivity vs. consensus.
- **vs. Authority** — scarcity is about the *thing's* availability; authority is about the *speaker's* expertise.

## Example(s)
- "One-time opportunity" framings.
- "I wanted you to know this before it goes out broadly" — exclusive information.
- Loss-framed pitch: describe what the org will forgo if it doesn't act in time, not just what it gains by acting.

## Likely MCQ angles
The loss-aversion mechanism is the testable hook. Distractor: framing scarcity as a gain (it's loss-framed). Cialdini's ethical caveat is testable: **manufactured/fake scarcity** works once but destroys trust — must be true scarcity. Don't confuse with social proof: scarcity = rare, social proof = popular.
