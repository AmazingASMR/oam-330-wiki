## Definition
A **grandiose sense of self-importance** — belief in one's superiority, craving for attention/admiration, low responsiveness to others' input. Sample items: "I am more capable than other people," "I really like to be the center of attention." Extreme values are clinical (NPD); milder forms are common in business contexts.

## Why it matters
Narcissists tend to **emerge as leaders disproportionately** because the same traits that look like dysfunction internally — overconfidence, charisma, vision, decisiveness — read as leadership in interviews and short interactions. Long-term, the costs (poor advice-taking, lawsuits, follower discontent) catch up.

## Drivers
Biological (NPD risk), being younger, wealth, coming of age in a good economy, being an only child, **individualistic culture**, urban (vs. rural).

## Key distinctions
- **Strengths vs. risks** — strong vision, charisma, swift decisions, energizes followers vs. low empathy, can't take criticism, low impulse control, more lawsuits, follower resentment.
- **Emergence vs. effectiveness** — narcissists *emerge* as leaders more often, but don't necessarily *perform* better long-term ([[concepts/leader-emergence-factors]]).
- **vs. high Extraversion** — extraverts are sociable and energetic; narcissists are self-aggrandizing and lack empathy. Extraversion alone is healthy; narcissism layers in entitlement.
- **Cultural component** — individualistic cultures cultivate it; collectivist cultures suppress it ([[concepts/individualism-collectivism]]).

## Example(s)
- Armand Hammer (Occidental Petroleum): "The brilliance of my mind can only be described as dazzling. Even I am impressed by it."
- Frequent in business "visionary" archetypes.

## Likely MCQ angles
"What predicts narcissism?" → individualistic culture, wealth, only-child, urban. Distractor: confusing it with confident leadership in general. Scenario: charismatic interviewee with strong vision but dismissive of feedback — narcissism risk, not just high E.
