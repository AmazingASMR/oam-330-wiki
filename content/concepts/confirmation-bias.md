## Definition
We tend to **seek and gather information that confirms** our existing theories. A better test would be to deliberately look for information that might **disconfirm** the theory.

## Why it matters
A [[concepts/system-1-system-2|System 1]] failure at the **evidence-gathering / interpretation** stage. Even smart, well-intentioned people unconsciously design their inquiries to ratify what they already believe — which masquerades as objective investigation.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/availability-heuristic]]** — availability = what *pops* to mind; confirmation = what we *go looking for*. Passive vs. active.
- **vs. [[concepts/motivated-perception]]** — motivated perception bends what we **see**; confirmation bias bends what we **seek and weigh**.
- **vs. [[concepts/halo-effects]]** — halo generalizes across traits of one person; confirmation bias generalizes evidence search toward an existing belief about anything.

## Example(s)
- **Slides — Job interview preparation study:** Interviewers told an applicant was either *introverted* or *extraverted* and instructed to "get to know" them. Interviewers chose questions that would **confirm** the label given:
  - Told extraverted → "In what situations are you most talkative?"
  - Told introverted → "In what situations do you wish you could be more outgoing?"
  - The applicant predictably "confirmed" the assigned label because the questions only allowed confirming answers.

## Notes on application
Identify the introvert/extravert interviewer study. Distractor: framing it as availability heuristic (it isn't — the interviewer is *actively choosing* questions). Fix per slides: actively seek **disconfirming** evidence, not just more confirming evidence.
