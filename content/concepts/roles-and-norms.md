## Definition
A **role** is a function assumed by someone occupying a given position in a group; **norms** are acceptable standards of behavior shared by group members that express what they ought (and ought not) to do.

## Why it matters
Roles tell members what to do; norms regulate how they do it. Together they explain conformity (Asch line study), deviant workplace behavior, and why two teams with identical members can perform very differently (Duhigg/Project Aristotle).

## Key distinctions
- **Role perception vs. role expectations** — perception = how YOU think you should act; expectations = how OTHERS believe you should act. The psychological contract is the unwritten employer–employee version of role expectations.
- **Role conflict vs. interrole conflict** — role conflict = divergent expectations within one role; interrole conflict = expectations across separate group memberships clash (e.g., parent vs. manager).
- **Norms vs. policies** — norms must be accepted by all to stick; leader-imposed policies aren't norms unless adopted.
- **Reference groups** — important groups whose norms we are most likely to conform to (vs. groups we belong to but don't identify with).

## Example(s)
- Asch line-judgment study: 75% of subjects conformed at least once; avg conformer was wrong 37% of the time.
- Hawthorne studies: bank wiring room workers enforced "don't be a rate buster, don't be a chiseler, don't squeal" with ridicule and arm-punches.
- Gusto's "no shoes" office norm.
- Duhigg: at Google, group norms (turn-taking, social sensitivity) override individual proclivities.
