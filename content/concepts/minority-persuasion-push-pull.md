## Definition
When persuading from a **minority** position, use a **PULL** approach — attracting, finding common ground, visioning, bridging, involving, listening, disclosing — rather than a **PUSH** approach (persuading, proposing, reasoning, asserting, stating expectations, evaluating, using incentives/pressures). Switch to push only after you've achieved a majority.

## Why it matters
Asch ([[concepts/asch-conformity]]) shows the deck is stacked against dissenters. The push/pull distinction and the *12 Angry Men* tactics are the slide's prescriptive answer to "how do you win as the lone voice?"

## Key distinctions
- **PUSH** — directive, assertive, evaluative. Effective once you have numbers/legitimacy on your side.
- **PULL** — invitational, relational, exploratory. Effective when you're outnumbered and need allies.
- **vs. Central-route strategy** — push/pull is about *style* of engagement; central-route is about *content* (argument quality). Both matter; minority dissent demands *hyper-rational* central plus *pull* style.

## Critical points (slide's "how to persuade from a minority position")
1. **Look for allies** — similar, lacking conviction, on the periphery, dissatisfied with the majority.
2. **Ask for a private vote** — to encourage dissent (public vote locks people in via [[concepts/cialdini-commitment-consistency]]).
3. **Frame dissent as attacking the issue, not the people** — plant a seed of doubt.
4. **Be hyper-rational** — emotion only when situationally appropriate and professional.
5. **Use PULL until you achieve a majority**, then push.

## Example(s)
- *12 Angry Men*: Juror #8 stands at window (signals openness, not confrontation), frames debate as discussion not verdict, uses soft sell — building & bridging, not attacking.

## Likely MCQ angles
Match push vs. pull terms to columns; identify which is appropriate for minority position (pull). The private-vs-public-vote rule is a near-certain MCQ. Distractor: pushing harder when in the minority — wrong; pull until majority.
