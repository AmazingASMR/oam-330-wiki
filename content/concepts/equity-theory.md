## Definition
**Equity theory** (Adams): motivation depends on the **comparison of one's own input/output ratio to a relevant referent's ratio**. Bottom line: *employees evaluate rewards based on their **relative** value, not their absolute value.*

- **Equity** — my ratio ≈ comparable colleague's ratio → I feel fairly rewarded.
- **Under-reward** — I work hard for OK pay; colleague works less for the same pay → I feel under-rewarded.
- **Over-reward** — possible but generally weaker reaction.

## Why it matters
Maps to pillar 2 of motivating environments (fair outcomes). Explains why **absolute raises can demotivate** when they violate relative comparisons. The WSJ "Push for $15" article is the textbook case: minimum-wage hikes compress veteran/newcomer pay → veterans feel under-rewarded even though no one's pay was cut.

## Key distinctions
- **Vs. expectancy theory** — expectancy is forward-looking effort→reward probability; equity is backward/sideways comparison to others. Different mechanism. See [[concepts/expectancy-theory]].
- **Vs. distributive justice** — distributive justice is the *outcome-fairness* feeling; equity theory is the *comparison-based mechanism* generating that feeling. Closely linked. See [[concepts/distributive-vs-procedural-justice]].
- **Relative, not absolute** — Solnick & Hemenway (cited in WSJ): half of subjects preferred earning $50K when others earn $25K to earning $100K when others earn $200K.

## Example(s)
- Slide example: I work REALLY hard and get OK salary; comparable colleague works pretty hard and gets OK salary → under-rewarded.
- Baseball: team performance is **negatively related to pay inequity** among players.
- WSJ: Gap, Walmart, Mud Bay employees demanded raises for veterans after entry pay rose.
- Capuchin monkeys reject cucumber when peers get grapes (equity sensitivity in non-humans).

## Notes on application
"Equity theory says employees compare ___" → input/output ratios to referents. Distractor: claiming equity is about absolute pay levels. Distractor: confusing with expectancy. Know the WSJ "wage compression" example.
