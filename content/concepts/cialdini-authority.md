## Definition
People defer to credible experts — experts are more persuasive than non-experts because audiences use authority signals as a System 1 shortcut.

## Why it matters
A peripheral-route lever ([[concepts/central-vs-peripheral-route]]) — newcomers and junior people systematically *under*-invoke it because they assume their credentials are already known. Surfacing expertise (ideally via a third party so you don't sound arrogant) materially shifts compliance.

## Key distinctions
- **Route** — peripheral (System 1 cue), not central; the *signal* of expertise persuades, not the argument itself.
- **vs. Liking** — authority works through perceived competence/credentials; liking works through similarity, praise, and rapport. They can stack.
- **vs. Social proof** — authority is *vertical* (defer to expert above you); social proof is *horizontal* (follow similar peers).

## Example(s)
- Physiotherapists who hang diplomas in view get higher patient compliance with prescribed exercises.
- Strangers give parking-meter change more readily to a uniformed requester than to one in casual clothes.
- Real estate firm trained reception staff to mention each agent's years of experience — measurable lift in appointments and contracts.

## Notes on application
The "have a third party introduce your credentials" tactic is testable. Distractor: claiming authority is a central-route strategy — it's peripheral. Another distractor: confusing it with social proof (peers) — authority is about expertise/position, not similarity.
