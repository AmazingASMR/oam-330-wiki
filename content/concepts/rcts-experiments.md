## Definition
A **randomized controlled trial (RCT)**, a.k.a. **true experiment**, is the only design from which **causation can be concluded**. Two criteria (slide 8):

1. **Experimenter has control over the input (independent) variable** — the experimenter decides who gets the treatment and how much.
2. **Random assignment** of individuals to conditions, including a **control or comparison condition**.

## Why it matters
The top of the [[concepts/evidence-hierarchy]]. RCTs are the only way to escape the [[concepts/correlation-vs-causation]] problem because [[concepts/random-assignment]] equalizes all other variables across conditions, leaving the IV as the *only* possible cause.

"You don't have to be a scientist to run an experiment."

## Key distinctions
- **RCT vs. quasi-experiment** — RCT has true random assignment by the experimenter; a [[concepts/quasi-experiments-correlational|quasi-experiment]] uses a *natural* or *existing* division (not randomized).
- **RCT vs. correlational study** — correlational measures variables as they exist; RCT manipulates the IV.
- **Control over IV** — YES = "you tell salespeople what to wear and track their sales." NO = "you write down what salespeople chose to wear and track sales" (that's correlational).

## Example(s)
- **Harrah's** (slide 11): control group got a $125 promo package (room + 2 steak dinners + $30 chips); experimental group got $60 in chips. Random assignment of casino-goers ensured the mix of regulars vs. first-timers was equal across groups. Finding: the $60 offer generated **more revenue at lower cost**. Because of random assignment, the package size **caused** the revenue change — nothing else could have.
- "Do salespeople who wear suits get more sales?" — only an RCT can answer causally.

## Likely MCQ angles

- Why only RCT proves causation (random assignment averages out confounds).
