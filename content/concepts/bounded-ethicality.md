## Definition
Bazerman & Tenbrunsel's umbrella term for the systematic, mostly-unconscious cognitive limits that prevent people from behaving as ethically as they believe they do — an analog of bounded rationality, but for ethics.

## Why it matters
Most unethical behavior in organizations comes from smart, honest people, not bad apples. Bounded ethicality is the master frame for the entire Ethics module: it explains why predictions, decisions, and recollections about ethical dilemmas systematically diverge from each other, producing a self-flattering blind spot at every stage.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. bounded rationality** — bounded rationality limits cognitive accuracy in general; bounded ethicality specifically limits accurate self-perception as an *ethical actor*. Same logic, different domain.
- **vs. [[concepts/ethical-fading-visceral]]** — ethical fading is one *mechanism* of bounded ethicality (the ethics drops out of awareness at decision time). Bounded ethicality is the umbrella; fading sits inside it.
- **vs. [[concepts/moral-justification]]** — justification is post-hoc reasoning to defend a chosen action; bounded ethicality is upstream and largely unconscious — you didn't see the ethical dimension at all.
- **vs. hypocrisy** — hypocrisy implies awareness; bounded ethicality is the absence of awareness.

## Example(s)
- Sexual-harassment interview prediction: 62%/68% predict they would confront the interviewer; 0% actually refused.
- Daffodil Days: ~80% predicted buying; ~50% bought.
- Ford Pinto: cost-benefit framing made the ethical dimension fade — engineers dropped the $11/car fix.

## Likely MCQ angles
Definition recall — Bazerman & Tenbrunsel as source, "bounded rationality but for ethics" as the giveaway. Recognize ethical fading, want/should gap, and memory revisionism as components, not synonyms. Distinguish from deliberate hypocrisy.
