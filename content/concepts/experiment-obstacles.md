## Definition
Sometimes a true experiment **isn't possible**. Slide 14 frames it: "It may be **impractical, impossible, or unethical** to assign people to different conditions."

The three obstacles (verbatim from slide 14):
1. **Impractical** — too costly, too disruptive, or logistically unrealistic to randomize.
2. **Impossible** — you cannot manipulate the variable at all.
3. **Unethical** — assigning a harmful or unfair condition to people would violate ethics.

## Why it matters
Explains *why* OB relies heavily on [[concepts/quasi-experiments-correlational|quasi-experiments and correlational studies]]. When you can't run an RCT, you fall back on weaker designs and lean on [[concepts/control-variables]] to rule out alternatives.

## When can't you experiment? (slide 14 examples)
- **Agreeableness** — you can't randomly assign someone a personality trait. (Impossible.)
- **East Coast vs. West Coast branches** — you can't randomly relocate offices. (Impractical.)
- **Salary** — you can't randomly pay employees different amounts. (Unethical / impractical.)

## Key distinctions
- **Impractical vs. impossible** — impractical = could be done in principle but not in this setting; impossible = the variable itself can't be manipulated (e.g., personality, age, gender).
- **Unethical vs. impossible** — you *could* randomly underpay people; you just *shouldn't*. That's unethical, not impossible.
- **Obstacles vs. external validity** — these obstacles are about *whether you can run the study at all*, not whether the results generalize.

## Example(s)
- Personality traits (impossible), gender (impossible), regional office assignments (impractical), pay (unethical).
