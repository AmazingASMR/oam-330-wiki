## Definition
The course is organized around **three themes** introduced in M1 and M2 (verbatim from slides):

1. **Common sense is necessary — but not sufficient — for predicting human behavior in organizations.** (M1)
2. **Behavior is predictable.** (M2, today + last time)
3. **Empirical evidence outperforms other types.** (M2, today)

## Why it matters
These themes recur throughout every later module. They explain *why* the course teaches OB the way it does: not as folk wisdom but as a body of empirically tested findings about predictable human behavior.

## Theme expansions
- **Theme 1 (common sense)** — intuition matters but fails alone. Empirical study fills the gap.
- **Theme 2 (predictable)** — "Humans aren't irrational … just insufficiently modeled." "Behavior is complex … but not random." A sufficiently complex model can predict — and therefore influence — behavior.
- **Theme 3 (empirical wins)** — empirical evidence beats hearsay, intuition, anecdote, or logical reasoning. This drives the [[concepts/evidence-hierarchy]].

## Key distinctions
- **Theme 1 vs. Theme 3** — Theme 1 says common sense is *insufficient*; Theme 3 says the *better alternative* is empirical evidence (not, e.g., authority or tradition).
- **Theme 2 vs. determinism** — predictable ≠ fully determined. It means trends are modelable in aggregate.

## Example(s)
- "Salespeople in suits sell more" — common sense (Theme 1) prompts the question; empirical evidence (Theme 3) tests it; and behavior is predictable enough (Theme 2) for the test to yield a stable answer.

## Notes on application
- Identify which theme a quote/example expresses.
- Trap: a 4th fake theme like "leaders matter most" — only three themes exist.
- Verbatim phrase recognition: "necessary but not sufficient," "insufficiently modeled," "outperforms other types."
