## Definition
**Social identity** = how you define who you are in the world. The sentence-test: "Being ______ is a big part of who I am." Categories include race/ethnicity, gender, age, sexual orientation, profession, hobbies/interests, political beliefs, country of birth, country of family origin, religion, social class, region, disability, health status, education, sports fanship, parental status, club membership, etc.

**Diversity** = the heterogeneity of identities in social life.

## Why it matters
Two framing claims set up the whole module: (1) every new person is simultaneously an in-group member and an out-group member; (2) each of us is a minority in some situation. So diversity is universal, not a problem confined to a few "diverse" employees.

## Key distinctions
- **Demographic diversity vs. social identity** — demographics are observable categories; social identity is the subjective importance the person attaches to them.
- **Visible vs. invisible identities** — visible ones (race, gender, age) are like "a hat worn all day"; invisible ones (religion, sexual orientation, class background) raise the question of whether to disclose.
- **In-group / out-group framing** — in this module, in-group/out-group is relative and shifting, not fixed.

## Example(s)
The slide's identity list is intentionally broad to include profession and hobby, signaling that even "majority" employees have meaningful identities that affect work.

## Likely MCQ angles
"Which is the textbook definition of social identity?" — pick the one tied to self-concept ("how you define who you are"), not the one about demographic counts. Watch for diversity defined as "heterogeneity of identities," not "presence of minorities."
