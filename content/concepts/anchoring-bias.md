## Definition
**Over-weighting initial information** — even when that information is **irrelevant or random** — when making numeric judgments. The first number you see contaminates every subsequent estimate.

## Why it matters
A [[concepts/system-1-system-2|System 1]] failure in **numeric** decision making. Slides: anchoring bias plays a role in **all price and salary negotiations**, and is **very difficult to avoid even when deliberately trying**. Best fix per slides: **avoid seeing the anchor in the first place**.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/framing-and-risk|framing]]** — anchoring is about a **starting number**; framing is about how the **same outcome** is verbally cast (gain vs. loss). Both are "biases in numeric decision making" per slides, but framing flips risk preference, anchoring just shifts magnitude.
- **vs. [[concepts/availability-heuristic]]** — availability biases evidence selection; anchoring biases a numeric starting point even with full evidence.
- **vs. [[concepts/sunk-cost-fallacy]]** — both involve over-weighting prior information, but sunk costs are *past investments*; anchors are *initial reference numbers*.

## Example(s)
- **Slides — Gandhi study:** When Gandhi died, was he more or less than **114** years old? Or more or less than **35** years old? (Correct answer: 78.)
  - "More/less than 114" anchor → mean estimate **79.4 yrs**.
  - "More/less than 35" anchor → mean estimate **70.7 yrs**.
  - F(1,63) = 6.90, p = .01. Same person, anchor shifted answers by **~9 years**.
- **iPad pricing (slides):** Pundits anchored expectations under $1,000. Apple announced **"just $499"** — making $499 feel cheap by contrast.

## Notes on application
The Gandhi 114 / 35 / 78 numbers. The fix: avoid the anchor (don't try to "ignore" it — it doesn't work). Distractor: confusing with framing (different mechanism on the same kind of "numeric bias" slide).
