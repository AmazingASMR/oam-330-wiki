## Definition
**Expectancy theory** (Vroom): motivation is maximized when **input-to-output relationships are clear** at three sequential links:
1. **Effort → Performance** ("If I work hard, will I perform well?") — *expectancy*
2. **Performance → Rewards** ("If I perform well, will I be rewarded?") — *instrumentality*
3. **Rewards → Personal goals** ("If I'm rewarded, will the rewards help me reach my goals?") — *valence*

Motivation = **Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence**. Any link near zero kills motivation.

## Why it matters
Maps to the first pillar of motivating environments: **clear paths from inputs to outputs**. Diagnoses *why* an employee isn't motivated by zooming in on which link is broken.

## Key distinctions
- **Vs. equity theory** — expectancy is about whether effort *will produce* rewards (a forward-looking probability chain). Equity is about whether the rewards are *fair relative to others* (a comparison). Different mechanisms — easy distractor pair. See [[concepts/equity-theory]].
- **Multiplicative, not additive** — three links multiplied. A weak link can't be compensated by strong other links.
- **Subjective expectancies** — what matters is the *employee's perception* of each link, not the objective probability.

## Example(s)
- "I work hard, but my boss promotes by favoritism" — performance→reward link broken (instrumentality = 0).
- "I'm offered a bigger bonus, but I don't care about money right now" — valence = low.
- "My role is so unclear I don't know if effort produces good performance" — expectancy = low.

## Notes on application
The three components and the **multiplicative** structure are high-yield. "If valence is zero but expectancy and instrumentality are high, motivation is..." → **zero** (because multiplied). Distractor: confusing with equity theory.
