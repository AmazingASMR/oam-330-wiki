## Definition
The **same outcome**, framed as a **gain** vs. a **loss**, flips risk preference:
- **Gain frame → risk-AVERSE** (take the sure thing).
- **Loss frame → risk-SEEKING** (gamble to avoid the loss).

Even when the **net change is identical.** Kahneman & Tversky's foundational finding.

## Why it matters
[[concepts/system-1-system-2|System 1]] does not evaluate numeric outcomes objectively — it reacts to the *verbal cast*. We want to evaluate numeric information for what it is, not based on how it is presented.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/anchoring-bias]]** — anchoring biases the **starting number**; framing biases the **risk preference** depending on gain vs. loss language. Same slide bucket ("biases in numeric decision making"), different mechanism.
- **vs. [[concepts/sunk-cost-fallacy]]** — sunk cost is over-weighting past investment; framing is over-weighting verbal presentation of present options.

## Example(s)
**Gain vs. loss gamble (slide-confirmed):**
- "Get $900 sure" vs. "90% chance to get $1,000" → **79% sure thing** (risk-averse for gains).
- "Lose $900 sure" vs. "90% chance to lose $1,000" → **81% gamble** (risk-seeking for losses).
- χ²(N=64) = 23.40, p < .001.

**Asian disease problem (Kahneman & Tversky) — confirmed in slides:** Country preparing for a rare disease, expected death toll 600.
- *Lives-saved (gain) frame:* Plan A saves 200 for sure / Plan B 1/3 chance to save 600, 2/3 chance no one saved → **81% pick A** (sure thing).
- *Deaths (loss) frame:* Plan A 400 will die for sure / Plan B 1/3 chance no one dies, 2/3 chance 600 die → **76% pick B** (gamble).
- χ²(N=57) = 14.92, p < .001. **Plans A and B are mathematically identical across frames.**

**Other slide examples:** "90% employed" vs. "10% unemployed"; "95% fat-free" vs. "5% fat" — same fact, different feel.

## Likely MCQ angles
Asian disease problem is the classic — memorize the direction (gain → A/sure; loss → B/gamble). The 81%/76% percentages. The rule: **risk-averse for gains, risk-seeking for losses.**
