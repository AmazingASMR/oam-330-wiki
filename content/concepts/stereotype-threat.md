## Definition
**Stereotype threat**: *awareness* of a stereotype about your group hurts your performance in that specific domain. The performance decrement is triggered when the relevant identity is made salient before/during the task.

## Why it matters
Identifies a within-individual mechanism by which stigmatization translates into measurable performance loss — independent of any actual ability gap and independent of any biased evaluator. The threat is the cognitive load of *worrying about confirming the stereotype*.

The canonical study on the slide: African-American students taking GRE problems. Some just took the test; some were first asked to "tell us what it's like to be African-American on this campus." The identity-salience prompt produced **worse** scores. Replicated with women on math tests, White men taking jump shots, etc. — i.e., it is not a "minorities only" phenomenon, it is whoever is the stereotyped group on a given task.

A related cost shown on slides: *concealing* identity also taxes performance — gay and straight participants asked to hide their sexual orientation from a work partner did worse on cognitive/physical tasks.

## Key distinctions
- **Stereotype threat vs. stereotype tax** — threat is a *performance decrement* in the stereotyped person under identity salience; the **stereotype tax** is the *career cost paid by acting against an in-group stereotype* (e.g., assertive woman penalized). Different actor, different mechanism.
- **Stereotype threat vs. workplace stigmatization** — stigmatization is interpersonal devaluing by others; threat is the internal performance hit from identity awareness, even with no overt mistreatment.
- **Stereotype threat vs. identity concealment cost** — both hurt performance, but threat comes from salience of identity; concealment comes from suppressing it.

## Example(s)
GRE / African-American identity prompt; women on math; White men on jump shots.

## Likely MCQ angles
"What causes the performance drop in stereotype threat?" → awareness/salience of the stereotype, not the stereotype's accuracy. Distractor: "stereotype tax." Distractor: discrimination by an evaluator.
