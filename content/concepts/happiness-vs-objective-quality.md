## Definition
Objective conditions (wealth, health, technology) have improved dramatically, yet subjective happiness has not tracked them — past a baseline of meeting needs, more wealth does not reliably yield more happiness.

## Why it matters
Explains a core puzzle for managers and policy: improving measurable conditions (pay, perks, GDP) does not mechanically produce happier employees or citizens.

## Key distinctions
- **Objective quality of life** — Measurable indicators: GDP, indoor plumbing (55% in 1940 vs. 99% today), infant mortality (4.7% to 0.6%), life expectancy, education. These have soared.
- **Subjective happiness** — Self-reported well-being; has stayed roughly flat despite the gains above.
- **Absence-of-misery threshold** — Per Kahneman & Deaton (2010): "Wealth is like health: its utter absence breeds misery, yet having it is no guarantee of happiness." Money matters at the bottom, decouples at the top.

## Example(s)
- 1940 → today: indoor plumbing 55%→99%, life expectancy women 68→81, HS by age 25 5%→90%; happiness essentially unchanged.
- Frank (NYT): Easterlin's finding — once basic obligations are met, more income does not straightforwardly buy more happiness; *relative* position and job conditions matter more.

## Likely MCQ angles
Easterlin paradox / Kahneman-Deaton quote. Distractors: "money never matters" (false — utter absence breeds misery) or "GDP linearly predicts happiness" (false).
