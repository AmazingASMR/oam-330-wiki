## Definition
**Reason #2** that empirical evidence outperforms other types: even when two variables are reliably correlated, you **cannot conclude** one causes the other. A correlation only tells you they move together.

## Why it matters
Most published findings (and most NYT-style headlines) report correlations and tempt readers into causal conclusions. The slide example: "Children ages 6–11 being **read aloud to** regularly and having **restricted online time** were **correlated with frequent reading for fun**." The temptation to read "→ causes" is strong but unjustified.

Causation can only be concluded from a true experiment with [[concepts/random-assignment]].

## Three alternative explanations (besides causality)
See [[concepts/alternative-explanations-correlation]]. From slide 25:
1. **Spurious / coincidence** — the correlation is merely chance.
2. **Reverse causal direction** — Y causes X, not X causes Y.
3. **Third variable** — some Z causes both X and Y.

## Key distinctions
- **Correlation** — measured association between variables (no manipulation).
- **Causation** — established only via experiment + random assignment.
- **Correlational study vs. quasi-experiment vs. RCT** — see [[concepts/quasi-experiments-correlational]] and [[concepts/rcts-experiments]].

## Example(s)
- Slide 25: "Fewer highway deaths → more lemons?" (spurious / reverse direction). "Road quality → ability to import goods" + "Road quality → auto safety" (third-variable: road quality drives both).
- Reading-to-kids ↔ reading-for-fun: parent income, books in home, free time could all be the real cause.

## Notes on application
- Given a correlation, name the three alternative explanations.
- Identify the only design that supports causation: **RCT with random assignment**.
- Trap: assuming a strong correlation = causation.
