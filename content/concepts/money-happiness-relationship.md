## Definition
Money buys happiness — **with caveats**. Slide 13 (deck 09):
- Positive relationship, **but not linear** (diminishing returns at higher income).
- **Correlation ≠ causation**.
- People **predict money will increase happiness more than it actually does** (affective forecasting error).
- **Believing money is important for happiness predicts *less* happiness.**

Life lesson on slide: enjoy your money, but don't pursue it too hard.

## Why it matters
Counters the lay assumption that more pay always means more motivation/happiness. Sets up the rest of deck 09: financial incentives have downsides; non-monetary rewards are often more cost-effective.

## Key distinctions
- **Diminishing returns, not zero returns** — money does matter, especially below comfort thresholds (Pink calls this "baseline rewards"). Above that, the slope flattens.
- **Predicted vs. actual gain** — we systematically overestimate how much a raise will improve happiness.
- **Caring about money is itself a predictor** — *valuing* money correlates negatively with happiness.
- **Vs. financial-incentive tradeoffs** — this concept is about money & subjective happiness; the other is about money & motivation/performance. See [[concepts/financial-incentives-tradeoffs]].

## Example(s)
- Two students offered identical jobs at $80K vs. $120K: forecast a big happiness gap, observe a small one a year in.
- "I'll be happy when I make $X" — usually false; the goalpost moves.

## Likely MCQ angles
"Money and happiness are ___" → positively but **non-linearly** related. Distractor: "no relationship" (wrong) or "linear" (wrong). Distractor: claiming caring about money helps happiness — the opposite is true.
