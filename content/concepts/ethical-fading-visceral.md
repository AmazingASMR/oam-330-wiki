## Definition
At the **decision** stage, two distinct "want self" mechanisms drag actual behavior away from principles: **ethical fading** (the ethical dimension of the situation drops out of awareness) and **visceral responses** (hot, in-the-body drives — hunger, anxiety, fear, sleepiness — that hijack attention toward short-term self-preservation).

## Why it matters
Real-life situations don't come with an "Ethical Dilemma!" label, and our bodies are wired to satisfy immediate needs. Together these are the proximate cause of "smart, honest people doing unethical things."

## Key distinctions
- **Ethical fading vs. visceral responses** — Fading is **cognitive/perceptual**: we reframe a moral choice as a "business decision" so principles become non-salient (Ford Pinto: cost-benefit lens). Visceral responses are **bodily/affective**: hot states (hunger, fear of losing a job, lust, anger) override deliberation. Bazerman & Tenbrunsel argue visceral responses are a *cause* of ethical fading, but on the slide they're listed as two separate decision-stage mechanisms — keep them distinct on questions.
- **Forecasting errors (prediction)** — Both fading and visceral responses happen at decision time; forecasting errors happen *before*.
- **Moral justification (Lecture 27)** — Justification = active rationalizing story we tell. Fading = the ethics simply isn't perceived in the first place. Justification post-hoc fills in for fading.

## Example(s)
- **Ethical fading**: Ford Pinto — engineers viewed the fuel tank as a cost-benefit calculation, not a life-safety issue; ethics "faded."
- **Visceral**: Snooze-button decision (Bazerman example) — at 5:30 a.m. the want-to-sleep drive overrides the prior commitment. Hunger making people grab a daffodil-budget for lunch instead.
- Ford executives: competitive pressure produced survival-instinct visceral state ("avoid market loss, get bonus") which drove out ethical considerations.

## Notes on application
HIGH-distractor pair: an item describes a manager who "didn't even notice the ethical angle while crunching the numbers" → ethical **fading** (NOT visceral). An item where the manager "was hungry/sleep-deprived/panicked" → **visceral**. Memorize: fading = invisible ethics; visceral = hot bodily drive.
