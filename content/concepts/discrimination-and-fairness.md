## Definition
The first organizational reason to care about diversity: **avoiding workplace discrimination**. Per U.S. federal law, organizations must avoid discrimination based on race, gender, religion, age, pregnancy, disability, and national origin (states/cities may protect more identities locally).

Crucially, **discrimination does not have to be intentional** — the legal standard is whether a practice "operates to exclude." Courts may look for evidence of **disparate impact** (e.g., a company that hires primarily via referrals will tend to reproduce the demographics of current employees).

Stereotyping/discrimination is usually a **System 1 process** — most people *want* to be fair (System 2), but behavior diverges from intent.

## Why it matters
Frames diversity as a compliance + fairness imperative, not a business value-add. Sets up the contrast with paradigm 2 (access and legitimacy).

What can be done (per slide): **collect data** (audit hiring/promotion/exit rates; find the leaky pipeline) and **shape the environment** (broader recruitment, blind(er) hiring with criteria set in advance, exposure-based bias reduction). The Gilbert quote: *"Because the brain cannot see itself fooling itself, the only reliable method for avoiding bias is to avoid the situations that produce it."*

Canonical evidence: 5,000 Boston/Chicago résumés — fewer callbacks for African-American-sounding names. 6,400 Boston AirBnB requests — first name only differed. U.S. symphony **blind auditions** raised female-hire likelihood by ~25%.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Access & legitimacy (paradigm 2)** — paradigm 1 = *avoid harm / be fair*; paradigm 2 = *use diversity as a market/business asset*. THE Thomas-and-Ely distractor pair: paradigm 1 is morally/legally driven; paradigm 2 is strategically driven. Paradigm 1 assumes diversity is right; paradigm 2 assumes diversity is profitable.
- **vs. Better ideas (paradigm 3-ish)** — better-ideas is about *innovation* from heterogeneity, not fairness or markets.
- **Intentional vs. unintentional discrimination** — both count legally; the disparate-impact standard captures unintentional.

## Example(s)
Résumé-name callback gap; AirBnB host bias by first name; symphony blind auditions → +25% female hires.

## Likely MCQ angles
"Discrimination must be intentional to violate law" → FALSE. "Best fix per Gilbert" → avoid the bias-producing situation. Distractor: confusing this paradigm with access-and-legitimacy.
