## Definition
Keltner's term for the self-defeating loop in leadership: you *gain* power because the group perceives you as committed to its welfare and attentive to others, but *having* power makes you less aware of others' needs — eroding the very capacities that got you there. Slide formulation: perceived commitment to group  power  less awareness of others' needs  failures of perspective taking and moral hypocrisy.

## Why it matters
Explains why leaders predictably fail. It's not that bad people get power; it's that power changes good people. The same trait set (attentiveness, empathy, mirroring) is both the on-ramp and what gets shut off. Useem's article gives the neurological backing: Obhi's TMS work shows power literally dampens mirror-neuron response, and effort to compensate doesn't help.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/power-changes-behavior]]** — that concept lists the *three behavioral changes* (action , pressure of others , perspective taking ). The power paradox is the *circular structure*: the changes undo what got you the power. EASY DISTRACTOR PAIR.
- **vs. hubris syndrome** (Owen) — hubris syndrome is a clinical disorder requiring sustained power and minimal constraint; the power paradox is a general psychological pattern that shows up even in college students primed for 10 minutes.
- **Moral hypocrisy** is one specific failure mode of the power paradox (judging others harshly while excusing self).

## Example(s)
- John Stumpf at the Wells Fargo hearing: rose by reading people, then "couldn't read the room."
- Berkeley lab studies: priming students to feel powerful immediately produces less mirroring, worse perspective-taking.

**Preventing it** (slide 14): transparency, accountability (procedural justice, employee voice, anonymous input), and selecting leaders high on agreeableness, low on narcissism, with responsibility experiences (kids, younger siblings).

## Notes on application
Definition recall — the *circular* nature is the giveaway. Distinguish from "power changes behavior" (mechanism vs. paradox). Identify Keltner as the source. Spot prevention strategies.
