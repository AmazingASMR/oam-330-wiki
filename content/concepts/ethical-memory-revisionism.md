## Definition
At the **recollection** stage, we distort our memory of past unethical behavior so that we *still* see ourselves as ethical people — by not coding the prior decision as ethics-relevant, by minimizing the act, or by assuming "anyone would have done the same."

## Why it matters
This is why the prediction → decision → recollection cycle *repeats*: because we never update our self-view downward, we make the same biased prediction next time. It's the engine of chronic blind spots.

## Key distinctions
- **Forecasting errors (prediction)** — Forward-looking optimism about *future* behavior; memory revisionism is backward-looking distortion of *past* behavior.
- **Ethical fading (decision)** — At the moment of acting, ethics is invisible; in revisionism, ethics is later *re-read out* of the memory ("that wasn't really an ethical issue").
- **Moral justification** — Justification can be in-the-moment or after-the-fact; revisionism is specifically a *memory* distortion ("I was still ethical, I assume others would have done the same"), often paired with justification.

## Example(s)
- Kenneth Lay (Enron), Bill Clinton, Rod Blagojevich — all insisted they had done nothing wrong; Bazerman argues they *believed* it.
- 93% of teens self-rate as ethically satisfied while 2/3 cheated, 1/3 plagiarized, 1/3 stole — the gap is sustained by revisionist recollection.
- Williams' three revisionism mechanisms (slide 13): (1) we don't recall the decision as ethics-relevant; (2) we still see ourselves as ethical; (3) we assume others would have behaved similarly, normalizing it.
