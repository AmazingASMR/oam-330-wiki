## Definition
We are more persuaded by those we like — liking is driven primarily by similarity, praise, and physical attractiveness.

## Why it matters
Peripheral-route lever ([[concepts/central-vs-peripheral-route]]). Slide advice: build genuine relationships *before* you need them; foster interdependence; in tense moments find commonalities and aspects to praise. Liking built reactively under pressure reads as instrumental.

## Key distinctions
- **Route** — peripheral (System 1 affective cue), not central.
- **vs. Social proof** — liking persuades via *one-to-one* affective bond (you like me, so you say yes); social proof persuades via *many-to-one* peer behavior (lots of people like us did X).
- **vs. Authority** — liking is "you like me"; authority is "you respect my expertise." Different shortcuts; can stack.

## Example(s)
- **Tupperware parties** (Cialdini): purchase predicted not by product quality but by social bond with the host.
- F. B. Evans (1963): insurance prospects more likely to buy from agents matching them on age, religion, politics, or smoking habits.
- New manager taking over a struggling branch: build relationships first, *then* introduce operational changes.

## Notes on application
Tupperware is the canonical example. Praise — even partly unearned praise — still shifts attitudes. Distractor: confusing liking with social proof (similarity to *you* vs. similarity among a *group*). Distractor: claiming liking is central-route.
