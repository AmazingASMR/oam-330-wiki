## Definition
**We see what we expect or want to see.** Perception is not neutral; it is shaped by our motives, incentives, and expectations. Originally demonstrated by **Bruner & Goodman (1947)**.

## Why it matters
This is the [[concepts/system-1-system-2|System 1]] failure at the *input* stage of decision making — before any reasoning happens, perception itself has already been bent toward what we want. Important for managers: two people can witness the same event and "see" different things.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/confirmation-bias]]** — motivated perception bends what we *see in the first place*; confirmation bias is selectively *seeking and weighing* information after the fact. Perception step vs. evidence-gathering step.
- **vs. [[concepts/perception-vs-reality]]** — motivated perception is the *motivational* subtype of the broader claim that perception ≠ reality. (Other subtypes: expectations, attention, priming.)
- **vs. [[concepts/self-fulfilling-prophecies]]** — motivated perception is purely internal (you see what you want); self-fulfilling prophecies are interpersonal (your expectations change *the other person's* behavior, which then matches your expectation).

## Example(s)
- **Horse-or-seal study (slides):** ambiguous image. When perception is **not** tied to outcomes, ~50/50 split. When seeing one animal led to **orange juice** and the other to a **nasty smoothie**, **70% "saw"** the animal that led to the better outcome.
- Bruner & Goodman (1947) — the foundational study cited in slides.

## Notes on application
The 50% vs. 70% horse/seal numbers are worth memorizing precisely. Distractor: confusing motivated perception (input) with confirmation bias (evidence selection). Distractor: Bruner & Goodman as the cited researchers.
