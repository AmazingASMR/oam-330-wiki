## Definition
**Overconfidence that we could have predicted an event in advance.** "I knew it all along." Once we know the outcome, we restructure our memory of prior beliefs to match it.

## Why it matters
Slides flag three organizational consequences:
1. **Learning from mistakes** is undermined — if we "knew" the outcome, there's nothing to learn.
2. **Improving prediction skills** stalls — we never face our miscalibration.
3. **Placing blame for failure** becomes punitive and unfair — decisions look obviously wrong only because the outcome is now known.

Closely tied to the **curse of knowledge** — once you know something, you can't put yourself back in the perspective of not knowing it.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/overconfidence]]** — hindsight is *backward* ("I would have predicted that"); overconfidence is *forward* ("I will predict this correctly"). They reinforce each other: hindsight inflates perceived past accuracy, fueling forward overconfidence.
- **vs. [[concepts/halo-effects]]** — both depend on outcome knowledge, but halo recolors *traits of the actor* (CEO "decisive" if win, "rigid" if lose); hindsight recolors *predictability of the outcome*.

## Example(s)
- **Slides — magazine circulation study:** "Which magazine had the highest circulation in 1970?"
  - **No answer given:** 32% chose Playboy, 68% Time.
  - **Answer ("Playboy") shown in boldface:** 66% then "knew" Playboy was the answer (vs. 32% who would have picked it without the cue). χ²(N=57) = 6.35, p = .017. Knowing the answer reshaped recalled prior belief.
- **Dobelli:** 2007 economists rosy on the economy → 2008 crash → same experts then list the "obvious" causes (Greenspan, lax mortgages, rating agencies). Causes look inevitable only after the fact.

## Likely MCQ angles
"Curse of knowledge" pairing. The Playboy/Time numbers (~32% → 66%). The three organizational consequences. Distractor: confusing with overconfidence (direction matters: forward vs. backward).
