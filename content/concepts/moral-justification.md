## Definition
Moral justification is the set of **cognitive strategies and rationalizations** people use to justify unethical behavior so that they can still see themselves (or be seen) as ethical.

## Why it matters
It is the first of the four enabling mechanisms of organizational misconduct (slide 8, Lecture 27). Two key facilitators are **anonymity** and **sense of inequity**.

## Two facilitators (slides)
- **Anonymity** — of the *victim* (no clear identifiable target → less moral concern; Enron TX traders manipulating CA power prices remotely) and of the *actor* (masks, dim lighting, road rage all increase cheating/aggression).
- **Sense of inequity** — Employees who feel unfairly treated are more likely to eat food without paying, take supplies, take money. TSA officer Pythias Brown: *"They ain't paying me. They're treating me wrong."*

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Social norms** — Justification = "I have a *reason* this is OK"; social norms = "*everyone* does it." Both legitimize misconduct, but through different cognitive routes.
- **vs. Ethical fading** — Fading = ethics never enters awareness; justification = ethics is acknowledged but explained away.
- **vs. Outcome focus** — Outcome focus is a specific kind of justification ("ends justify means"), but Williams treats it as its own enabling mechanism in the four-part list.

## Example(s)
- Enron CA blackouts (anonymity of victim).
- Halloween candy-stealing experiment (masks → more theft).
- Pythias Brown TSA case (inequity → escalating theft).

## Notes on application
"Which mechanism is illustrated by employees taking supplies because they feel underpaid?" → moral justification via **inequity**. "Which is illustrated by traders harming distant unidentified customers?" → justification via **anonymity** (NOT social norms — no peer-behavior cue).
