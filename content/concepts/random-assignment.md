## Definition
**Random assignment** = each person (or team, company, patient, lab rat, tree — whatever the unit of study) has an **equal chance** of ending up in the experimental group or the control group.

Methods (slide 10): pulling names from a hat, every other person, random number generator.

## Why it matters
Random assignment is **the engine that makes RCTs work**. The result: "Irrelevant variables will average out to be equal across conditions … *except the one you are manipulating*." Therefore the IV is the **only** thing that differs between groups, so any outcome difference **must be caused** by the IV.

It's how the [[concepts/rcts-experiments|RCT]] solves the [[concepts/correlation-vs-causation]] problem without needing to measure every possible [[concepts/control-variables|control variable]].

## Key distinctions
- **Random assignment ≠ random sampling.** This is a classic confusion.
  - **Random sampling** = how you *select participants* from a population (concerns external validity / generalizability).
  - **Random assignment** = how you *allocate participants to conditions* once they're in the study (concerns internal validity / causal inference).
  - You can have one without the other. Most lab experiments have random assignment but **not** random sampling.
- **Random assignment vs. quasi-experiment** — quasi-experiments use a natural or existing division, *not* randomization. So they cannot establish causation cleanly.

## Example(s)
- **Harrah's** (slide 11–13): Casino-goers (mix of regulars and first-timers) randomly split. Experimental group = $60 chips; control group = $125 package. The number of regulars is the **same** in both groups because of randomization. Promotional package is the only thing that differs → it MUST be the cause of the revenue difference.

## Likely MCQ angles
- Definition: equal chance of any condition.
- The mechanism: averages out irrelevant variables across conditions.
- **Trap: random sampling vs. random assignment** — distractors will swap them.
- Without random assignment, you cannot conclude causation.
