## Definition
Things — and **especially people** — that are "good" in one area tend to be seen as "good" in other areas. Kahneman's term: **"exaggerated emotional coherence."** One positive trait colors all subsequent judgments of unrelated traits.

## Why it matters
A [[concepts/system-1-system-2|System 1]] shortcut at the **interpretation** stage. Hugely consequential in hiring, performance review, and leadership perception — we mistake one observable cue for evidence about traits we haven't actually observed.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/confirmation-bias]]** — confirmation bias is selective evidence-seeking about a belief; halo is auto-generalizing across *traits of the same target*.
- **vs. [[concepts/self-fulfilling-prophecies]]** — halo is a perceiver-side bias (you misjudge); self-fulfilling prophecies actually change the target's behavior.
- **vs. [[concepts/hindsight-bias]]** — halo says "good at X → good at Y"; hindsight says "I knew the outcome was coming."

## Example(s)
- **Attractiveness halo (slides):** more-attractive people are inappropriately judged as more **intelligent and competent**, and they earn more. **Taller men earn more than shorter men.**
- **Outcome halo on CEOs (slides):**
  - Successful company → CEO described as *"flexible, methodical, and decisive."*
  - Failing company → same kind of CEO described as *"confused, rigid, and authoritarian."*
  - The trait labels follow the outcome, not the actual behavior.

## Notes on application
"Exaggerated emotional coherence" wording. The CEO success/failure trait-flip is a clean scenario stems. Distractor: confusing halo with [[concepts/hindsight-bias]] (knew-it-all-along) — both depend on outcome knowledge but operate differently.
