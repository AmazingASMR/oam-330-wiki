## Definition
Slide-flagged dimensions on which negotiation styles vary cross-culturally: **what counts as the goal of the negotiation**, and **how communication is conducted**. Slide takeaway: **"Around the world, integrative negotiation works better."**

## Why it matters
Importing a one-size-fits-all (often Western, distributive, contract-focused) approach into a different cultural context can blow up a deal even when terms look favorable. Cultural assumptions about contracts, directness, and relationships shape what behaviors signal cooperation vs. threat.

## Key distinctions
- **Binding contract vs. relationship as the goal:**
  - In some (often more individualistic) contexts, the **paper agreement** is the deal; signing = closing.
  - In **collectivistic** contexts, the **relationship** is primary. The paper agreement may be secondary, and parties may **expect to renegotiate when the climate changes** (slide example: Australia → Japan sugar exports).
- **Direct vs. indirect communication:**
  - In some cultures, **direct confrontation** is seen as **threatening or disrespectful**.
  - The listener is expected to **"read between the lines."**
  - A negotiator who insists on blunt, position-based exchange may be read as hostile.
- **Mode tag:** Cultural variation cuts across both modes, but slides emphasize that **integrative** approaches travel better globally — they preserve relationships, accommodate indirectness, and don't depend on adversarial signaling.
- **Connection to BATNA:** Implicit — relationship-oriented cultures may treat walking away (using your BATNA) as more costly because the long-term tie is the actual asset.

## Example(s)
- **Australia → Japan sugar exports:** Japanese counterparts expected to renegotiate as conditions changed; Australian side treated the contract as fixed. Cultural mismatch on what the deal *was*.

## Likely MCQ angles
- Recognize: paper agreement may be **secondary** in collectivistic contexts.
- Recognize: directness can read as disrespect; "read between the lines" is normative in some cultures.
- Slide's bottom-line claim: **integrative > distributive globally.**
