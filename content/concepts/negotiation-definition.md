## Definition
Negotiation is **deciding on the terms of an exchange** — when people exchange goods or services and attempt to agree on the exchange rate for them. Slide framing covers three flavors: (1) price for goods and services (including employment), (2) dividing a shared resource, and (3) resolution of a conflict.

## Why it matters
Negotiation is one of the most ubiquitous workplace activities — salary, vendor contracts, project scope, conflict resolution. Failing to negotiate (or treating every negotiation as a fight) leaves value on the table and damages relationships. The Korkki reading underscores that even one declined salary negotiation reverberates for years through percentage-based raises and retirement contributions.

## Key distinctions
- **Persuasion / influence** — Persuasion is changing attitudes or behavior (Cialdini principles); negotiation is agreeing on **terms of an exchange**. They overlap (you persuade in a negotiation), but negotiation always involves a deal.
- **Conflict** — A negotiation can resolve a conflict, but not all negotiations involve conflict (e.g., routine procurement). Conflict is the disagreement; negotiation is one mechanism for resolving it.
- **Bargaining** — Often used interchangeably; in this course "bargaining" splits into distributive vs. integrative as sub-modes of negotiation.

## Example(s)
- Salary negotiation (employment).
- Buying a service station (Texoil exercise).
- Paper Clip exercise — trading up from a paper clip through a chain of integrative trades.

## Likely MCQ angles
- Define negotiation as exchange-rate agreement, not just "winning."
- Recognize the three contexts (price, resource division, conflict).
- Distinguish negotiation from raw persuasion or conflict.
