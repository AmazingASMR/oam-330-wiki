## Definition
The slide's explicit takeaway from Five Tricks: organizations that want to maximize the benefits of diversity should expect, **in the short term**:
- **Conflict**, and
- **Employee dissatisfaction**.

And **in the long term**:
- **Better ideas** and a more successful organization.

This is the J-curve / "things get worse before they get better" pattern of diversifying.

## Why it matters
Sets correct managerial expectations. Leaders who interpret the early conflict as evidence that "diversity isn't working" will reverse course before the long-term creativity payoff arrives. The pattern is *predicted*, not pathological.

## Key distinctions
- **Short-term cost vs. long-term benefit** — both are real; the temptation is to weigh only one. Slides explicitly call out both.
- **vs. Diversity → innovation (mechanism)** — this concept is about the *time profile* (J-curve); the diversity-innovation page covers *why* heterogeneous teams generate better ideas (different perspectives → broader information set).
- **vs. Culture shock** — culture shock is the *micro mechanism* causing the short-term conflict on the J-curve; the J-curve is the *org-level* trajectory the shocks aggregate into.
- **vs. Group decision-making (M10)** — homogeneous groups are *easier* and *faster* but produce less innovation. Same trade-off, narrower setting.

## Example(s)
Five Tricks game: conflict and dissatisfaction during early rounds when winners moved tables; over time, players who had been through prior shocks adjusted faster, and the pooled experience produced better play.

## Likely MCQ angles
"What should an organization expect in the short term as it diversifies?" → conflict and dissatisfaction (NOT immediate productivity gains). "Long term?" → better ideas. Distractor that fails: "more harmony than a homogeneous team in the short term" — the slide says the opposite.
