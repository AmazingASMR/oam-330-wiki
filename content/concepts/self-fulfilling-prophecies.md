## Definition
A self-fulfilling prophecy occurs when **expectancies influence others' actions**, which then *confirm the original expectation*. The expecter doesn't just see what they want — they unwittingly produce it.

## Why it matters
The interpersonal version of perception bias. Manager expectations of an employee → manager treats the employee accordingly → employee performs accordingly → manager's expectation appears "validated." Pernicious because the loop *looks like* objective evidence to the manager. (Pygmalion / Rosenthal effect tradition.)

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/motivated-perception]]** — motivated perception bends *your own perception*; self-fulfilling prophecies bend *the other person's actual behavior*. The behavior change is real, not illusory.
- **vs. [[concepts/confirmation-bias]]** — confirmation bias is one-sided info-gathering; self-fulfilling prophecy creates the very evidence it then confirms.
- **vs. [[concepts/halo-effects]]** — halo is generalizing one trait to others; self-fulfilling prophecy is generating actual behavior change in the target.

## Example(s)
- **Slides — "HOT! NOT." voice study:** Always the same woman speaking on the phone. Listeners told she was attractive judged her differently than listeners told she was unattractive — and her actual conversational behavior shifted in response to how listeners treated her. *Same woman; expectation changed her behavior.*
- Pygmalion-in-the-classroom-style framing implied by slides (teacher expectations → student outcomes).

## Likely MCQ angles
Identify the *interpersonal* loop (expectation → treatment → behavior → confirmation). Distractor: framing it as a purely internal perception bias (it's not — actual behavior changes). Distractor: confusing it with [[concepts/hindsight-bias]] ("knew it all along") — different mechanism.
