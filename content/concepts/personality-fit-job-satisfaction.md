## Definition
**Person–job fit:** the degree of match between an individual's Big 5 profile and the demands of their role. Williams's takeaway: people whose personality matches the job earn **~15% more** and are **more satisfied** than those who don't match.

## Why it matters
Traits are mostly stable across the lifespan — you can't easily *become* more conscientious or less neurotic — but you **can choose an environment where your existing profile thrives**. This reframes career decisions away from "fix yourself" toward "find the right context."

## Key distinctions
- **Fit > raw trait level** — high E doesn't help a long-haul truck driver; high A doesn't help a courtroom litigator. Performance flows from **trait × demand**, not trait alone ([[concepts/ocean-job-performance]]).
- **All five traits matter** — Williams's caution: fit a job to the person's *whole* profile, not just one extreme score.
- **vs. selection-side fit** — most fit research is *self-selection* (people drift toward jobs that suit them, per Wolfram 2023); employers screening for fit is a smaller, harder lift.

## Example(s)
- Williams's class exercise: pick a job for a partner using all five Big 5 traits.
- Wolfram (2023): Extraversion correlates positively with sales/management roles, negatively with solitary technical roles; opposite pattern for Openness in research vs. routine work.

## Likely MCQ angles
"What is the best evidence for the value of personality–job fit?" → ~15% pay premium and higher satisfaction. Distractor: "fit determines performance more than ability" (false; GMA still dominates). Scenario: career advice to a high-N introvert — choose a low-stress, low-interpersonal-demand environment, not "work on your personality."
