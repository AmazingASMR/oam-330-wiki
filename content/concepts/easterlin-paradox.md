## Definition
The empirical puzzle that as a nation's GDP and average income rise over time, average self-reported happiness does *not* keep pace once basic needs are met. Subjective happiness saturates well below the ceiling at which objective indicators keep climbing.

## Why it matters
Provides the macro foundation for the entire Happiness module. If more money straightforwardly bought more happiness, almost every other lever the course discusses (relationships, meaningful work, flow, intrinsic motivation) would be second-order. Easterlin's finding says they aren't — past a threshold, *relative* position and qualitative factors dominate.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/happiness-vs-objective-quality]]** — that concept covers the broader lecture treatment of subjective vs. objective indicators; Easterlin paradox is the specific named finding (national GDP up, happiness flat) that anchors it.
- **vs. [[concepts/money-happiness-relationship]]** — the money–happiness concept covers individual-level patterns (e.g., diminishing returns, spending on experiences/others). Easterlin paradox is the macro-level / cross-national observation.
- **vs. [[concepts/happiness-stability]]** — stability covers genetics/adaptation/social-comparison mechanisms that *explain* the paradox; the paradox itself is the descriptive puzzle.
- **vs. compensating differentials** — Frank's related point that better working conditions trade against pay, separate from the Easterlin macro pattern.

## Example(s)
- Frank's two-parallel-worlds thought experiment: relative income shapes satisfaction more than absolute income — a key driver of the Easterlin pattern.
- US/post-WWII data: GDP per capita has multiplied, average happiness essentially flat.
- Brooks's GSS data: 30% of Americans "very happy" in 2018 — comparable to earlier decades, despite massive income growth.

## Notes on application
Identify by description ("GDP rises, happiness doesn't"). Distinguish macro (Easterlin) from individual-level money–happiness pattern. Connect mechanisms — adaptation and social comparison — to the paradox.
