## Definition
Culture systematically shapes **how people resolve moral conflicts** — especially **obligations to ingroup members vs. universal rules** — and **what motivates workplace effort**. Individualistic cultures lean toward **universalism** (same rules for everyone); collectivistic cultures lean toward **particularism** (loyalty to ingroup overrides abstract rules).

## Why it matters
Same dilemma, different "right answer" depending on cultural lens. Same incentive system, different effect on behavior. Managers who export practices without translating them get the Crunchers Inc. outcome — sales drop instead of climbing.

## Key distinctions
- **Universalism vs. particularism** — abstract-rule vs. relational-obligation ethics; maps onto individualism vs. collectivism ([[concepts/individualism-collectivism]]).
- **vs. moral foundations** — moral foundations theory ([[concepts/moral-foundations-five]]) catalogues *which* values vary; this concept is about the universalist/particularist axis specifically.
- **vs. culture-blind incentive design** — what motivates is culturally contingent; team-level recognition often outperforms individual leaderboards in collectivist offices.

## Example(s)
**The "Ben in LA" dilemma (slide):** Ben needs his stolen ticket replaced to deliver wedding rings to his best friend. He sees a stranger's coat with a ticket and cash sticking out.
- Universalist (individualist) reading — stealing is wrong regardless of stakes.
- Particularist (collectivist) reading — obligation to best friend can outweigh harm to a wealthy stranger.

**Friend-loyalty vignettes (slide):** Would you testify falsely for a friend? Leak insider info to an investor friend? Fake a medical exam? Inflate a friend's restaurant review?
- "Yes, my friend is counting on my loyalty" (particularist / collectivist).
- "No, the same rules apply to everyone" (universalist / individualist).

**Workplace motivation — Crunchers Inc. case:** Jim transfers from NY (individualist) to Beijing (collectivist), keeps the individual sales-contest reward plan that worked in NY. In Beijing the top performer (Pang Jiayang) drops below the most senior salesperson (Zhou Jiawei); everyone clusters at the same level. Lesson: **individual-recognition incentives suppress effort in collectivist contexts** because standing out breaks group harmony and seniority norms.

## Likely MCQ angles
Scenario: a NY-style individual-sales contest fails in Beijing — explain why (collectivist norms / particularism / standing out punished). Distractor: attributing failure to "low motivation" rather than cultural mismatch. Distractor: claiming moral universalism is objectively correct — slides frame both as cultural defaults, not one as right.
