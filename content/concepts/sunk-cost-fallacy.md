## Definition
**Over-weighting what you have already invested when choosing a next step.** Rational decision making requires forgetting prior costs — only future costs and benefits should drive the choice.

## Why it matters
Slides identify **two directions** of the fallacy:
1. **Escalation of commitment** ("throwing good money after bad") — sticking with the original plan because of past investment, even when the plan is bad. *Staying in a long line, staying on hold.*
2. **Reluctance to fix** — sunk costs weigh so heavily you refuse new investment to fix the mistake. *Wearing painful expensive shoes instead of replacing them.*

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/anchoring-bias]]** — anchoring is over-weighting an *initial reference number*; sunk cost is over-weighting *past investment* of money/time/effort.
- **vs. [[concepts/framing-and-risk]]** — framing flips risk by gain/loss wording; sunk cost is the irrational pull of *already-spent* resources regardless of frame.
- **vs. [[concepts/hindsight-bias]]** — hindsight distorts memory of *predictability*; sunk cost distorts current *commitment* using past spend.

## Example(s)
- **Slides — Lost-ticket vs. lost-cash thought experiment:**
  - **Sunk cost framing:** You bought a non-refundable $40 ticket; you lost it; new ones are available for $40. Do you buy another? Most people: NO (feels like paying $80).
  - **No sunk cost framing:** You're going to a $40 show; you lose $40 cash from your pocket; you still have enough for a ticket. Do you buy? Most people: YES.
  - The **decision is identical** ($40 lost + $40 ticket); only the *labeling* of the lost money changes the answer.
- **Slides — "Gourmet Food / William Chan" case (cognitive repairs):** Consultant kept doubling down on his original assumption ("product is fine, marketing is the problem") through Instagram/Facebook ads, then premium grocery placement, then a *bigger* social media push — escalation of commitment until the client fired him.
- **Dobelli:** Concorde ("the Concorde effect"), Vietnam War extension, sticking with a losing stock because of acquisition price.

## Likely MCQ angles
The two flavors (escalation **vs.** refusing-to-fix). Lost-ticket / lost-cash mirror pair. "Concorde effect" alias from reading. Distractor: classifying it as anchoring (both involve prior info, but different mechanism).
