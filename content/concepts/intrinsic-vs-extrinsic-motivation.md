## Definition
Two sources of motivation (slide 14, deck 09):
- **Intrinsic** — "I like doing this." The activity itself is rewarding.
- **Extrinsic** — "I like what doing this brings me." External rewards/punishments drive behavior.

**Key claim (Pink):** using incentives to boost extrinsic motivation can **undermine intrinsic motivation** ("crowding out" / "Sawyer Effect"). Pink frames intrinsic motivation around three drivers: **autonomy, mastery, purpose**.

## Why it matters
Standard incentive design assumes work is unpleasant and people need to be paid more to do more. Pink's argument: for interesting/creative work, extrinsic incentives can *reduce* output. Sets up [[concepts/financial-incentives-tradeoffs]].

## Key distinctions
- **Vs. financial-incentive tradeoffs** — this concept is the *taxonomy*; the tradeoffs concept covers *when incentives backfire* (Glucksberg candle problem, blood donation, day-care fines). Overlapping but distinct. See [[concepts/financial-incentives-tradeoffs]].
- **Sawyer Effect** (Pink) — turning play into work via contingent rewards. Tom Sawyer convinces other boys to pay him to whitewash a fence; "if-then" rewards likewise turn intrinsic enjoyment into a chore.
- **Baseline rewards still matter** — Pink: if pay is inadequate or inequitable, you get neither extrinsic nor intrinsic motivation. The intrinsic story applies *above* the baseline.

## Example(s)
- Lepper/Greene preschool drawing study: kids in the **expected-award** group lost interest two weeks later; **unexpected-award** and **no-award** kids drew just as much. Only **contingent** ("if-then") rewards corrupted intrinsic motivation.
- Pink notes intrinsically motivated artists produced art judged superior years later — and "those least motivated to pursue extrinsic rewards eventually receive them."

## Likely MCQ angles
Define both terms; identify Pink's three intrinsic drivers (**autonomy, mastery, purpose**); recognize the **Sawyer Effect** label and the expected-vs-unexpected reward distinction. Distractor: claiming all rewards undermine intrinsic motivation (only **contingent/expected** ones do).
