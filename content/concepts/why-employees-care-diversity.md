## Definition
Employees care about social identity at work because they want to be **authentic but not conspicuous**:
- *Authentic*: "I can be myself here. I don't have to hide that I'm _____."
- *Not conspicuous*: "People here see me as a whole person, not just that I'm _____."

The lecture frames this tension as the employee-side reason diversity matters — a question about your own experience, fit, and belonging, not (yet) about firm performance.

## Why it matters
Job satisfaction and turnover hinge on this dual standard. An environment that punishes authenticity drives stigmatization-related dissatisfaction (next concept). An environment that makes identity hyper-salient triggers tokenism and stereotype threat. Both ends of the spectrum cost the employee.

## Key distinctions
- **Authentic vs. conspicuous** — these are not opposites; they are *both* desired states, and a good workplace satisfies both at once.
- **Employee reasons vs. organizational reasons** — this page covers the *employee* side ("why you care"). The org-side reasons are discrimination/fairness, access/legitimacy, and better ideas.
- **Belonging vs. fit** — fit asks whether you match the org; belonging asks whether the org makes room for you. The lecture leans on belonging.

## Example(s)
- Hiding your sexual orientation at work (taxing — performance suffers).
- Being the only Asian-American on a team and getting only the "Asian" questions (conspicuous — tokenizing).

## Likely MCQ angles
"Why do employees care about diversity?" → authentic but not conspicuous. Distractor: "to comply with anti-discrimination law" (that's the *organization's* reason, paradigm 1). Distractor: "to gain access to minority customer markets" (paradigm 2, also organizational).
