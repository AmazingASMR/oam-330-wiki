## Definition
The systematic differences across high- and low-power-distance cultures in how leaders are expected to behave, how subordinates communicate with them, how organizations are structured, and which leadership styles are seen as appropriate. Procedures developed in one PD context don't easily translate to another.

## Why it matters
There is no universal "right" leadership style — exporting a low-PD playbook (open door, flat structure, blunt feedback) into a high-PD office (or vice versa) produces breakdowns ranging from disengagement to fatal cockpit miscommunication, as in the Korean Air case.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/power-distance]]** — power distance is the underlying cultural *dimension*; this concept is the *downstream consequences* for leadership practice.
- **vs. universal leadership-trait theories** — there is no "right" leadership style globally; expectations of leaders vary with culture.
- **Receiver-oriented vs. transmitter-oriented communication** (Gladwell): high-PD/Asian cultures place burden on listener to decode; low-PD/Western cultures place burden on speaker to be clear. Affects how subordinates raise concerns.

## Example(s)
**Low PD vs. High PD — at work** (slide 11):
- Same rules for all vs. rank brings privileges (parking spaces, cubicles).
- Employees expect to be consulted vs. expect manager to know the right thing.
- Flat structure with fewer managers vs. tall structure with more.
- Small salary spread vs. large.
- Grievance structures vs. potentially no way out of a bad boss.
- Boss as expert/friend vs. boss as benevolent father/mother.
- Open door vs. top management protected by gatekeepers.

**Korean Air response (slide 17):** recognized the PD problem; introduced training to *reduce mitigated speech*; required pilots to speak in **English** in the cockpit (forces direct, low-PD register). Today: 5-star Skytrax rating.

**High-PD upside:** in the food-poisoning case, high-PD managers expected to take more responsibility/ownership regardless of direct fault.

## Likely MCQ angles
Match a workplace artifact (CEO parking spot, open door, large pay gap) to high or low PD. Recognize Korean Air's *interventions* (English, mitigated-speech training) as PD reduction. Identify receiver-oriented vs. transmitter-oriented communication. Avoid the trap of calling high PD "bad."
