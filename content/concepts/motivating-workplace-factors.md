## Definition
Slide 11 (deck 08) and slide 4 (deck 09) give the canonical three-part list of what makes a workplace motivating:
1. **Clear paths from inputs to outputs** — i.e., expectancy theory.
2. **Fair procedures and outcomes** — i.e., equity theory and procedural/distributive justice.
3. **Meaningful work that affects others** — purpose, end-user impact.

## Why it matters
This is the spine of Module 5. Each pillar maps to a separate testable framework. If you remember this triad, you can reconstruct most of the lecture content.

## Key distinctions
- **Vs. job satisfaction factors** — *satisfaction* is about liking the job; *motivating environments* are about driving effort and engagement. Different dependent variable. See [[concepts/job-satisfaction-factors]].
- **Each pillar has its own theory** — clear paths → [[concepts/expectancy-theory]]; fairness → [[concepts/equity-theory]] + [[concepts/distributive-vs-procedural-justice]]; meaning → [[concepts/meaningful-work]].
- **Cross-cultural baseline** — slide 21 (deck 08) and slide 18 (deck 09): across cultures, *all* employees want interesting/autonomous work and fair, transparent procedures.

## Example(s)
- University fundraising callers who met a student beneficiary for 10 minutes raised significantly more money than control (meaningful work).
- Pay scales explained transparently, with employee voice, satisfy the fairness pillar.

## Notes on application
"Which of the following is *not* one of the three pillars of motivating environments?" — likely distractor is something pay/incentive-focused. The triad is **clear paths, fair procedures/outcomes, meaningful work**. Memorize verbatim.
