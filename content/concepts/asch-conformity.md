## Definition
Solomon Asch's 1950s line-judgment experiments showed that **74% of people conformed to a unanimously wrong group** at least once when asked to make a perceptual judgment (matching a target line to comparison lines).

## Why it matters
Establishes the baseline difficulty of being a public dissenter — the situation Juror #8 in *12 Angry Men* finds himself in. Sets up the [[concepts/minority-persuasion-push-pull]] tactics: if conformity is this strong, a minority needs deliberate strategies to break it.

## Key distinctions
- **Conformity (audience-side)** — the *target's* tendency to align with the group. Distinct from social proof, which is the *persuader's* tactic of leveraging that tendency.
- **vs. Obedience (Milgram)** — Asch is conformity to peers; obedience is compliance with authority. Different mechanisms, same surprising-strength theme.
- **Variations matter** — conformity is not fixed; it shrinks under specific conditions (below).

## Variations that *decrease* conformity
- Having **one** other person agree with the real participant (one ally cracks unanimity).
- **Written** answers (vs. verbal/public).
- **Small** group (1–3 others) vs. large group (>3).

## Example(s)
- Original task: pick which of three comparison lines matches a target line. Answer is obvious. Confederates unanimously give the wrong answer; 74% of real subjects went along at least once.
- *12 Angry Men*: Juror #8 is the lone dissenter (1 of 12) — Asch territory.

## Notes on application
The **74%** number and the three conformity-reducing variations are high-yield. Distractor: claiming conformity stays constant regardless of group size or response privacy — it doesn't. Distractor: confusing with Milgram (authority obedience).
