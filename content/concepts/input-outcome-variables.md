## Definition
- **Input (independent) variable** = the predictor — the thing you measure or manipulate to explain something else.
- **Outcome (dependent) variable** = the result you're trying to predict (e.g., job performance).

A "sufficiently complex model" sums many inputs to predict the outcome.

## Why it matters
Frames every empirical OB study: which side of the equation is each variable on? Slide 11's worked example uses **job performance** as the outcome and lists the inputs feeding it.

## Inputs from the slide example (predicting job performance)
- Agreeableness
- Type of job
- Diversity of your team
- Years of experience
- Math grades in college
- Boss's mentoring skill
- Bonus at stake
- Size of your breakfast
- Temperature in the room
- (and more)

## Key distinctions
- **Independent (input) vs. dependent (outcome)** — IV is manipulated/measured as cause; DV is the effect being predicted. Mnemonic: DV *depends on* the IV.
- **Input variable vs. control variable** — an input is what you care about; a [[concepts/control-variables]] is one you measure to *rule out* as an alternative explanation.
- **Multiple inputs needed** — a single IV almost never predicts behavior alone; "need to know both person's agreeableness level AND type of job to predict performance."

## Example(s)
- Job performance = f(agreeableness, job type, mentoring, bonus, breakfast size, room temperature, …).

## Likely MCQ angles
- Map a variable in a study description to IV vs. DV.
- Recognize that interactions matter (agreeableness × job type) — neither variable alone is sufficient.
