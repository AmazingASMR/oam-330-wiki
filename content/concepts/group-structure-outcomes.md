## Definition
Group and organizational structure — including hierarchy, role assignments, span of control, and overall structural model — systematically shapes outcomes such as decision speed, decision quality, member satisfaction, creativity, and consistency.

## Why it matters
Slides 25 frames structure as a deliberate design lever: managers choose hierarchy, role definition, and span of control because each choice trades off across outcomes (speed vs. creativity, control vs. satisfaction).

## Key takeaways from the slide exercise
- **Hierarchy** — often increases efficiency and consistency, sometimes at the cost of creativity and job satisfaction.
- **Roles** — give everyone something to work with and step up to; can also be limiting and frustrating, especially if no ultimate decision-maker exists.
- **Unstructured teams/orgs** — more variable outcomes ("works wonderfully OR terribly"); won't remain unstructured indefinitely (hierarchies form spontaneously); much more dependent on individuals' personalities and skills.

## Key distinctions
- **Mechanistic vs. organic** — see [[concepts/mechanistic-vs-organic]]. Structure choice is the macro version of this lever.
- **Span of control** — see [[concepts/span-of-control]]. A specific structural variable.
- **Norms / cohesion** — these are emergent properties of a group; structure is the imposed framework that shapes them.

## Example(s)
- Slides exercise: RED/ORANGE groups had imposed hierarchy; BLUE/GREEN and PINK/PURPLE were structure-free at start. Question posed: did hierarchies form anyway in the unstructured groups? (Yes, generally.)
- Decision-making speed and member satisfaction tend to trade off across structural choices.

## Likely MCQ angles
Scenario application: matching a structural feature (hierarchy / fluid roles / no formal leader) to a likely outcome (consistency / creativity / variability). Expect a "tradeoff" framing — the right answer rarely says structure is uniformly good or bad.
