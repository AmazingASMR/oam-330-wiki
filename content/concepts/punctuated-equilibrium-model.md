## Definition
A model of group development in which temporary groups with finite deadlines pass through phases of inertia punctuated by a transition at the midpoint that triggers major changes and a final burst of activity at completion.

## Why it matters
Predicts the actual rhythm of project teams under deadline: directions are set early, the group "stands still" for the first half, the midpoint acts like an alarm clock, and a flurry of activity finishes the work. Helps managers time interventions.

## Key distinctions
- **Forming/Storming/Norming/Performing (Tuckman)** — alternative model emphasizing sequential interpersonal stages; per R&J, those stages may all occur within Phase 1 of the punctuated equilibrium model.
- **Continuous-progress assumption** — naive view that groups make steady linear progress; punctuated equilibrium says progress is bursty, not linear.
- **Permanent/standing groups** — punctuated equilibrium specifically applies to TEMPORARY task groups with deadlines, not ongoing teams.

## Example(s)
- The six steps (R&J): (1) first meeting sets direction, (2) Phase 1 inertia, (3) transition at half-allotted-time, (4) major changes, (5) Phase 2 inertia, (6) accelerated final-meeting activity.
- Whether the project lasts an hour or six months, the transition still hits at the midpoint.

## Notes on application
Definition recall ("when does the transition occur?" → at the midpoint, regardless of total time) and identifying which group type the model applies to (temporary, deadline-driven). Distractor: confusing the midpoint trigger with completion deadline.
