## Definition
**Perception** = organizing, interpreting, and making meaning from what your senses tell you. The slides' bold claim: **Perception ≠ Reality**. The first step in decision making is perceiving the situation and gathering information — and that step is already biased before any "thinking" happens.

## Why it matters
If the inputs to a decision are distorted, no amount of downstream rationality fixes the output. Slides frame this as the entry point to every decision-making bias: we don't see the world directly; we see an interpretation shaped by expectations, attention, prior knowledge, and motivation. "Accurate perception takes lots of concentration" — i.e., it requires [[concepts/system-1-system-2|System 2]] effort.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/motivated-perception]]** — perception/reality is the general claim (we don't perceive reality directly); motivated perception is the specific case where *desires* shape perception.
- **vs. attribution** — perception is about *seeing* the situation; attribution is about *explaining* observed behavior (internal vs. external causes).
- **vs. memory bias** — perception is biased at input; memory biases (e.g., hindsight) distort the record after the fact.

## Example(s)
- Familiar optical illusions used in slides to show the System 1/System 2 conflict — what we *see* is not what is physically there.
- Slides: "What do you see?" prompts where the same image is read different ways depending on context/priming.

## Notes on application
The claim "**Perception equals reality**" is a likely false-statement distractor — it is explicitly denied in slides. Expect items asking what the *first* step of decision making is (perception/gathering info) and why it's already biased.
