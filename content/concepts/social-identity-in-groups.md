## Definition
Social identity theory proposes that people have emotional reactions to the success or failure of their group because their self-esteem becomes tied to whatever happens to the group.

## Why it matters
Explains why employees feel pride, anger, or even schadenfreude on behalf of their employer or workgroup, why CEOs using "we/us" language correlates with higher sales, and why ingroup–outgroup dynamics drive workplace favoritism and discrimination.

## Key distinctions
- **Ingroup vs. outgroup** — ingroup = members of a group we belong to (we play favorites with them); outgroup = an identified other group seen as "all the same" and often derogated. Favoritism, not hostility, drives most discrimination.
- **Relational vs. collective identification** — relational = connecting via roles; collective = connecting via aggregate group characteristics. Workgroup identification is usually stronger than organizational identification.
- **Module 8 social identity** — same theory; here applied specifically to work groups and OCBs.

## Example(s)
- Tom's Marine Sales 50th anniversary — employees identify with the company AND the Crawfordsville community.
- Wendy's Twitter "roasting wars" — fans feel pride when Wendy's burns a competitor.
- Low identification with workgroup AND organization → reduced satisfaction, fewer organizational citizenship behaviors (OCBs).

## Notes on application
Scenario application: identifying ingroup/outgroup behavior, or matching "schadenfreude / pride / 'we' language" to the social identity construct. Distractor risk: confusing this with cohesion (cohesion = bond to stay together; social identity = self-esteem tied to group fate).
