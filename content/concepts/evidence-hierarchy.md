## Definition
The **evidence pyramid** ranks sources of OB knowledge from worst to best. Williams' version (slide 7 of deck 03 + slides 18–20 of deck 02):

From bottom to top (worse → better):
1. **Anecdote / Small Ns** — one story, one company.
2. **Correlations (not causal)** — measured relationships between variables.
3. **Causal conclusions** — only available from a true experiment / RCT.

"The higher up you go on the pyramid, the more accurately you can predict the **NEXT** event."

## Why it matters
Tells you how much weight to give a claim. A juicy CEO anecdote (Herb Kelleher drinks Wild Turkey) sits at the bottom; an [[concepts/rcts-experiments|RCT]] sits at the top.

## Key distinctions
- **More memorable ≠ more useful** (slide 20). Anecdotes are sticky but low on the pyramid.
- **Correlations vs. causal conclusions** — correlations only tell you that variables move together. Only an experiment with [[concepts/random-assignment]] supports a causal claim.
- **N of 1 vs. small N vs. large N** — the [[concepts/n-of-1-problem]] sits at the very bottom; aggregating across many cases moves you up.

## Example(s)
- One Yelp review (worse) → all Yelp reviews (better) → an RCT comparing two restaurant menus (best for causation).
- Pfeffer & Sutton's mockery: "Southwest is the most successful airline ... Herb Kelleher drinks a lot of Wild Turkey bourbon." — N-of-1 benchmark fallacy.

## Likely MCQ angles
- Order the levels of the pyramid correctly.
- Identify which level a given study sits at.
- Trap: claiming that a strong correlational finding establishes causation — it does not.
