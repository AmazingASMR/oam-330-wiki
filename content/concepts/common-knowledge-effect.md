## Definition
The tendency for groups to over-discuss information that is already shared (common knowledge) and under-discuss information held privately by individual members, producing worse decisions than the group's pooled information would warrant.

## Why it matters
Defeats the central advantage of group decision making (pooling private info and unique perspectives). The Grogan Air exercise demonstrated it: with complete info, 100% of MBA students pick Janet; with divided info, ~75% pick Katherine — the WRONG answer.

## Why it happens (slides give two reasons)
**1. Commitment & consistency** — group discussion anchors on initially stated preferences. People defend publicly stated positions, stress info aligned with those positions, and avoid revealing positive info about non-preferred candidates. Shared info is mentioned early and repeated.

**2. Social comparisons** — people use others to gauge what info matters. Shared info gets evaluated as more important, relevant, and accurate than unshared info (though "more agreed-upon ≠ more important"). Sharing common info elicits validation; revealing unique info carries social cost (perceived as less competent).

## How to avoid it (slides — three levers)
1. **Get information on the table BEFORE people vote or commit to a preference** (e.g., the VC firm where partners privately rate founders on pre-set criteria, share ratings, then discuss only disagreements — went from 80% time on agreement to focused-on-disagreement).
2. **Frame the task as problem solving, not judgment/personal preference** — yields greater info sharing.
3. **Cultivate a norm of constructive disagreement** — even one dissenter helps; pre-discussion disagreement increases unique-info sharing. (A dissenter advocating a BAD choice can also help, by breaking conformity.)

## Key distinctions
- **Groupthink** — about suppressing dissent; common-knowledge effect is about which INFO gets discussed (shared vs. unshared) regardless of dissent.
- **Groupshift** — polarization toward extremes; common-knowledge effect is about info sampling, not position extremity.
- **Social loafing** — effort reduction; this is information-sharing failure.

## Example(s)
- Grogan Air exercise (slides): divided info → wrong VP candidate chosen ~75% of the time.
- VC firm: partners privately rate founders before discussion → only DISAGREEMENT gets discussed.

## Likely MCQ angles
Definition match (over-discuss shared / under-discuss unshared). Application: which intervention prevents it (private rating before discussion is the strongest answer). Distractor risk: groupthink-flavored answers that emphasize dissent suppression rather than info sampling.
