## Definition
The tendency to accept **vague, general personality descriptions as uniquely accurate** about oneself. Named after P.T. Barnum ("a little something for everyone"). Fuels the felt validity of horoscopes, cold reads, and cheap personality tests.

## Why it matters
This is the mechanism that makes weak instruments like MBTI feel authoritative. When a description applies to almost anyone ("at times you are extroverted… at other times reserved"), readers fill in the blanks with their own self-image, then mistake that fit for the test's predictive power. This is why **widespread use ≠ validity** ([[concepts/personality-testing-utility]]).

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Validity** — Barnum is a *subjective experience of fit*; validity is an *objective relationship* with behavior. A test can score 5/5 on Barnum and 0/5 on validity.
- **vs. Big 5 feedback** — Big 5 results give percentile placement on continuous traits; Barnum statements are universal generic claims with no comparative anchor ([[concepts/ocean-traits]]).
- **vs. MBTI feel-good fit** — MBTI's resonance with users is largely a Barnum-effect artifact, not evidence the type system predicts behavior ([[concepts/myers-briggs]]).

## Example(s)
- Williams's class demo: 64 students read the *same* generic personality paragraph ("You have a great need for other people to like and admire you… disciplined outside, worrisome inside…") and most rated it as personally accurate.
- Horoscope columns; tarot readings; "fortune cookie" insights.

## Notes on application
Definition match (vague descriptions feel personal). Scenario: "Why does the MBTI feel accurate to its takers?" → Barnum effect, not measurement validity. Distractor: confusing Barnum with confirmation bias — Barnum is about generic descriptions, not about seeking confirming evidence.
