## Definition
Per slide 3 of deck 14 (verbatim, the entire slide entry on this concept): *"Negative advertising: Risky, but effective."* Listed as one of the **"Kidney Case: Effective strategies for influence"** alongside (a) appealing to principles and values ("who am I?") and (b) order effects ("in serial competition — figure skating, American Idol — the last shall be first in the rankings"). Slide 4 then introduces individual identifiability (Save the Children Rokia example) and slide 5 covers the advocacy effect — these are separate strategies on subsequent slides, not part of negative advertising itself.

## Why it matters
Listed as one of the slide's "effective strategies for influence" in the Kidney Case context. The "risky, but effective" framing is the testable phrase — both halves matter.

## Key distinctions
- **Route** — peripheral (System 1); works on emotional contrast, not reasoned argument. Per deck 12 slide 4: peripheral = emotional, indirect, normative, usually not conscious.
- **vs. Advocacy effect** (deck 14 slide 5) — advocacy makes you *more positive about your own side* from arguing for it (you search for positive arguments, motivated consistency). Negative advertising tries to make the audience *more negative about the other side*. Different direction of attitude movement.
- **vs. Central-route strategy** (deck 12 slide 6) — central-route advice is *"Present both sides – not just one. Then argue against the one you disagree with."* That's substantive disagreement on merits. Negative advertising attacks more affectively, often peripherally.

## Example(s)
- Political attack ads on opponents (general framing — slide does not name a specific ad).
- Kidney Case context: arguing why another candidate *shouldn't* get the kidney rather than only why yours should.

## Notes on application
- *"Risky, but effective"* is the slide's exact framing. Distractors: "always effective," "never effective," "always backfires."
- May appear in a list-the-Kidney-Case-strategies question alongside **principles/values appeals**, **order effects** (last-shall-be-first in serial competition), **individual identifiability** (Rokia/Save the Children), and the **advocacy effect**.
