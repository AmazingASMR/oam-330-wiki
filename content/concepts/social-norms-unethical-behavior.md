## Definition
Social norms enable unethical behavior in two ways: (1) people are more likely to behave unethically when they *think* others are doing it, and (2) people frequently **misperceive** what coworkers actually consider ethical — meaning the norm we follow may not be the real norm at all.

## Why it matters
Norms are a powerful enabling mechanism (#2 of 4 in slide 8). The *pluralistic ignorance* angle is what Williams emphasized in class: studies showed coworkers were significantly *more* tolerant of family-friendly scheduling and *less* punitive about minor supply-policy violations than people privately predicted (p < .001, η² = .14–.17).

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Moral justification** — Justification is internal rationalization ("I have a reason"); norms are external/social ("everyone does it"). common confusion: an employee who "doesn't feel bad because everyone does it" = norms; "doesn't feel bad because the company underpays" = justification (inequity).
- **vs. Obedience to authority** — A *sub-form* of norm-following per slide 15: Milgram showed 2/3 went all the way to "XXX" — we are more obedient than we predict.
- **vs. Moral inaction** — Norms drive *active* unethical behavior because "it's normal"; moral inaction is *failure to speak up* about behavior we recognize as wrong.

## Example(s)
- Walking behind a litterer increases your own littering.
- Best predictor of tax cheating is whether you think others cheat.
- Williams' study (slides 13–14): people *think* others judge family-friendly schedule decisions as unethical when in fact they don't (pluralistic ignorance).
- Milgram obedience studies (slide 15).

## Notes on application
Two distinct items expected: (1) "Which mechanism explains why people cheat more when they think peers cheat?" → social norms. (2) Pluralistic ignorance — "Employees over- or under-estimate how strictly coworkers judge a behavior?" → they *misperceive*; norms are often more lenient/different than assumed.
