## Definition
Slide-stated principle: **"Integration is not just compromising."** Compromise (each side gives up something on the same issue, meeting in the middle) can yield **suboptimal terms for both parties**. Integrative bargaining replaces compromise on a single issue with **trades across multiple issues** that each party values differently.

## Why it matters
Common intuition: "win-win" sounds like "we both meet halfway." Slides flag this as wrong. Compromise is a distributive move (split the pie). Integration **expands** the pie by exploiting unequal valuations across issues — both parties can do better than the midpoint of any single dimension.

## Key distinctions
- **Compromise** — both parties concede on the **same issue** until they meet. Symmetric loss; result feels "fair" but may leave both worse off than a creative trade.
- **Integration** — parties trade **across issues**: I give you what I value less and you value more, you give me what you value less and I value more. Result: both above their compromise point.
- **Mode tag:** Compromising belongs more naturally to **distributive** logic. Integrative bargaining transcends compromise.

## Example(s)
- **Orange example (positions vs. interests):** two people fighting over an orange — compromise = cut it in half. Integration = ask why. One wants juice, one wants peel → each gets 100% of what they want. Compromise gave each 50%.
- **Texoil:** compromise on price alone left no ZOPA. Integration (job offer + fuel + ads + health care) shifted the station owner's reservation to $467K and produced a deal.
- **Korkki:** if employer can't budge on base salary, don't just split the difference — package signing bonus, vacation, equity, parking.

## Likely MCQ angles
- TRUE/FALSE distractor: "Integrative bargaining means compromising fairly." → **False.** Compromise can leave both worse off.
- Integration works because issues are valued **unequally** across parties.
- "Meeting in the middle" is a distributive default, not an integrative outcome.
