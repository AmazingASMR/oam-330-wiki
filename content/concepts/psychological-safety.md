## Definition
A shared belief — coined by Amy Edmondson (1999) — that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking: confidence that speaking up, admitting a mistake, or asking a "dumb" question will not lead to embarrassment, rejection, or punishment.

## Why it matters
Project Aristotle's single biggest finding: across 180+ Google teams, *who* was on the team mattered far less than the team's norms, and the dominant productive norm was psychological safety. The two operational behaviors that produce it are equality in conversational turn-taking and high average social sensitivity (Reading the Mind in the Eyes test).

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/group-cohesion]]** — cohesion is a bond that keeps members together; cohesion can be a TRAP if performance norms are low (Hawthorne bank-wiring room) or if it produces conformity pressure. Psychological safety is specifically about willingness to dissent and speak up — closer to a productive norm than to a bond.
- **vs. [[concepts/groupthink-and-groupshift]]** — groupthink suppresses dissent; psychological safety enables it. Direct opposites on the speak-up dimension.
- **vs. [[concepts/roles-and-norms]]** — psychological safety IS a norm (not a role/composition feature); Duhigg is the canonical reading on norms beating composition.

## Example(s)
- Julia Rozovsky's two Yale groups: same caliber of people, opposite dynamics — the toxic study group vs. the high-functioning case-competition team.
- Matt Sakaguchi's Google team: his disclosure of Stage 4 cancer at an off-site triggered openness and adopted new norms.
- Team A (efficient, expert-driven, no chitchat) vs. Team B (free-flowing, interrupting, socializing) — Team B is more collectively intelligent.

## Likely MCQ angles
Definition recall (Edmondson, interpersonal risk-taking). The two behaviors that produce it (equal turn-taking + social sensitivity). Distinguish from cohesion — cohesion alone is NOT what Project Aristotle found.
