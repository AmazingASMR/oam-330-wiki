## Definition
Happiness is anchored around a stable "set point" by three forces: a genetic baseline, hedonic adaptation to new circumstances, and social comparison that re-anchors standards as one's situation changes.

## Why it matters
Explains why circumstance changes (raises, marriage, lottery wins, even tragedies) produce only short-lived shifts in well-being — and why "trying to be happier is as futile as trying to be taller" overstates but captures the puzzle.

## Key distinctions
- **Genetics (the baseline)** — Heritable set point; Minnesota Twin Studies (3,200 twins raised apart): identical twins had similar happiness levels, fraternal twins showed little relationship. ~50% of life-satisfaction variance per slide breakdown.
- **Adaptation (the leveling force)** — Humans adjust to *both* good and bad changes. Marriage boosts happiness ~2 years before returning to set point (Germans, n=1,761); Illinois lottery winners ($50k–$1M) no happier than others after 1 year; loss of all four limbs — most rate life good/excellent within a year; bereavement — positive affect typically returns within a year.
- **Social comparison (the relativity force)** — Happiness depends on relative, not absolute, standing. People are happier earning $50k in a $25k neighborhood than more in a richer one — but standards shift when circumstances change, so we move to richer neighborhoods. Vladimir's Choice and the Olympics silver-vs-bronze case (bronze medalists often happier than silver).

## Example(s)
Lottery, marriage, limb loss, Olympic medalists, Vladimir's Choice ("take out one of my eyes").

## Likely MCQ angles
Identify which driver fits a scenario. Distractor: confusing adaptation (absolute level resets) with social comparison (relative standing matters). Twin study attribution.
