## Definition
At the **prediction** stage of an ethical decision, we systematically *over*-predict that we will act on our principles — because we consider only what we think we *should* do and ignore the in-the-moment "want" pressures we will actually face.

## Why it matters
We see ourselves as good, ethical people, so our forecasts are biased toward the "should self." This blind spot is why ethical scandals shock the perpetrators themselves: they truly didn't anticipate they'd act this way.

## Key distinctions
- **Ethical fading (decision stage)** — Forecasting errors happen *before* the decision; ethical fading happens *during* the decision when ethical considerations drop out of awareness.
- **Memory revisionism (recollection stage)** — Forecasting is forward-looking ("I will…"); memory revisionism is backward-looking ("I did…the right thing"). Together they form the prediction → decision → recollection arc.
- **Planning fallacy** — Generic time-estimation bias; forecasting errors here are specifically *ethical* over-confidence, driven by neglect of "want" motives.

## Example(s)
- Sexual-harassment interview study: 62% of women predicted they would confront the harasser, 68% said they'd refuse to answer; in the actual situation, *zero* refused.
- Daffodil Days: 80% predicted they'd buy a daffodil; only ~50% did.
- Clinical trial vignette: people predict they'd enroll their child for societal benefit; reality flips when their own child is sick.

## Likely MCQ angles
Match the *stage* (prediction) to the error name. Distractor: confusing forecasting errors with ethical fading (which is the decision-stage error). Key cue: forecasting = "should self dominates predictions."
