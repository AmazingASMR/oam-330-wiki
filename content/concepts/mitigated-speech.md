## Definition
Indirect, softened, hint-laden communication used by a subordinate to convey a concern to a higher-status person — typical of high-power-distance cultures. Instead of asserting "we must turn back," a first officer hints "Don't you think it rains more in this area, here?"

## Why it matters
In time-pressured cockpits (and operating rooms, and battlefields), mitigated speech kills people. It is the linguistic mechanism by which [[concepts/power-distance]] becomes lethal: the captain literally doesn't decode that a warning was issued. The Korean Air fix — mandating English in the cockpit — worked precisely because English (a low-PD register) shifts pilots out of mitigated registers.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. [[concepts/receiver-vs-transmitter-oriented]]** — mitigated speech is the *transmitter* behavior of high-PD culture (speaker softens); receiver-orientation is the *listener* expectation (listener decodes). They co-occur but are distinct moves.
- **vs. [[concepts/power-distance]]** — PD is the cultural dimension; mitigated speech is the conversational behavior PD produces in subordinate-to-superior speech.
- **vs. assertive/direct speech** — the low-PD opposite (Suren Ratwatte talking openly with ATC during the Helsinki diversion).
- **vs. [[concepts/cialdini-authority]]** — authority makes listeners over-defer to a high-status speaker; mitigated speech is the inverse direction (low-status speaker softens *up*).

## Example(s)
- **KAL 801 (Guam, 1997):** first officer hints "Don't you think it rains more in this area, here?"; engineer hints "the weather radar has helped us a lot." Captain doesn't decode. 228 die.
- **Avianca 052 (Long Island, 1990):** Klotz says "we're running out of fuel, sir" with mitigating "ah" instead of declaring "emergency." NY ATC dismisses it. Plane crashes from fuel exhaustion.
