## Definition
Your **Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement** — what you will actually do if this negotiation fails. Not a wish, not a fair number — the real outside option you'd pursue. Slide rule: **always accept an offer that is better than your BATNA.**

## Why it matters
BATNA sets the floor for any rational accept/reject decision. Malhotra's central thesis: negotiators routinely violate this rule — rejecting offers that beat their BATNA because they "feel unfair," or accepting offers that fall short because the concession sequence "felt like winning." Knowing your BATNA (and trying to assess theirs) is the single most important prep step.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Reservation price:** BATNA is the **outside option** (e.g., another job offer, secondhand store quote, litigation). Reservation price is the **quantification of that BATNA into a number** — the worst in-deal terms you'd still accept. BATNA = the alternative; reservation price = the number it produces.
- **vs. Target point:** Target = highest aspiration (best plausible outcome). BATNA = walk-away alternative (worst acceptable). Confusing the two is a classic prep mistake.
- **vs. Fairness anchor:** A "fair" number (e.g., 49% pro-rata share) is not a BATNA. Shapiro rejected $8.5M because it felt unfair vs. $10M, but his litigation BATNA's expected value was only $7.4M — he should have accepted.

## Example(s)
- Job search: backup offer, or moving back in with parents.
- Ivy League U selling equipment: BATNA = secondhand store ($35K quote = reservation point).
- Malhotra/Shapiro: litigation BATNA = (0.7 × $10M) + (0.3 × $3M) − $0.5M = **$7.4M**.

## Notes on application
- BATNA = outside option, not a target or fair number.
- Always accept offers above BATNA — even if they "feel unfair."
- Uncertain BATNAs need **expected-value** math, not best-case.
- Mostly relevant to **distributive** prep (the price floor), but also frames every negotiation.
