## Definition
A two-step strategy for closing the gap between actual behavior and ethical principles: **when anticipating** a decision, give voice to your *want* self; **when making** the decision, give voice to your *should* self.

## Why it matters
Because the "should self" dominates predictions and the "want self" dominates the moment of action, you have to deliberately invert which voice you amplify at each stage to neutralize the bias.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Forecasting errors** — Those are the *problem* (prediction is too rosy); these strategies are the *fix* (anticipate the want self so the prediction is realistic).
- **vs. Ethical fading** — Ethical fading is the decision-stage problem; "name your principles out loud / mom-test" counteracts it by re-surfacing ethics during the decision.
- **vs. Outcome-focused culture / antidote (Lecture 27)** — These strategies are *individual*; the antidote (culture of ethics) is *organizational*. Don't confuse them — easy MCQ trap.

## Example(s)
- **Anticipating (give voice to want self)**: "Will I really stand up to my boss — even if it starts a conflict I hate?" "Will I give to homeless people even if I'm starving on the way to lunch?" Bazerman's job-negotiation rehearsal — prep the very question you hope won't be asked.
- **Deciding (give voice to should self)**: imagine telling Mom/Dad later (the "mom litmus test"); name your principles out loud; think abstractly ("what would Mom do?"); precommitment devices (lockbox, public commitment); evaluate options jointly rather than one at a time.

## Likely MCQ angles
"To better align behavior with principles, when *anticipating* a decision you should…" → answer: voice the **want** self (counterintuitive — easy to flip). When *making* a decision → voice the **should** self. The flipped-pairing distractor is the high-likelihood trap.
