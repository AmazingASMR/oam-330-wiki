## Definition
The variables that predict who ends up perceived as the leader of a group, separate from whether they are good leaders. Slides distinguish primary factors (which most strongly drive emergence) from additional factors that boost emergence at the margin.

## Why it matters
Leader emergence is largely about being seen as competent and committed by the group; many "additional" factors (height, attractiveness, extraversion, formal title) operate as superficial cues to those underlying perceptions. Hence the question on the next slide — do the "right" people end up emerging?

## Key distinctions
- **Leader effectiveness** — emergence is about who is *perceived as* leader; effectiveness is about whether they actually produce good outcomes. Emergent leaders are not necessarily effective.
- **Leader emergence helpfulness** — separate question of whether *any* leader emerging is good for the group (see [[concepts/leader-emergence-helpful]]).
- **Power (situational/role-based)** — power is a *consequence* of becoming a leader; emergence factors describe who gets there in the first place.

## Example(s)
**Primary factors** (slide 6):
- Perceived competence
- Perceived commitment to the group

**Additional factors:**
- Formal appointment / title, seniority
- Control over resources (power)
- Personal characteristics: age, attractiveness, height, extraversion

Emergent leaders also speak and act more, have more influence over the group's final decisions, and are more resistant to group norms.

## Likely MCQ angles
Pick the *primary* factor (perceived competence or commitment) vs. the additional/cosmetic factor (height, attractiveness). Recognize talkativeness as a *behavior of* emergent leaders, not its own primary cause. Distinguish emergence from effectiveness.
