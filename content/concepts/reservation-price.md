## Definition
The **quantification of your BATNA** into a single in-deal number — the worst terms inside this negotiation you would still accept before walking away to your alternative. Below it, your BATNA is better than the deal.

## Why it matters
The reservation price is the operational floor (or ceiling, if you're a buyer) you carry into the room. Where two parties' reservation prices overlap = the **ZOPA (zone of possible agreement)**; where they don't overlap, "no overlap → no deal" (Texoil slide). Without a pre-committed reservation number, negotiators are vulnerable to the concession illusion (Olivia's car: $32K accepted, $1K above her $31K reservation — she should have walked).

## Key distinctions
- **vs. BATNA:** BATNA is the **outside alternative** (the thing you'd actually do); reservation price is the **number that alternative translates to** inside this deal. BATNA = secondhand-store-quote; reservation price = $35,000.
- **vs. Target point:** Target = aspiration / best plausible outcome (Ivy League U: ~$75K). Reservation = walk-away ($35K). Slide-flagged classic prep mistake: failing to distinguish them.
- **Mode of negotiation:** A reservation price is most explicit in **distributive** bargaining (one-issue floor); in integrative bargaining you're trading across multiple issues, but each party still has a walk-away threshold.

## Example(s)
- Texoil rep reservation: **$500K max** (above this, build a new station). Station owner reservation: **$553K min** (below this, take BP offer). Initial ZOPA = empty → no deal until integrative moves shifted reservation to $467K.
- Olivia's car: reservation $31K, accepted $32K → BATNA-violating accept.

## Notes on application
- Reservation price = quantified BATNA, NOT the target/aspiration.
- Overlap of reservation prices = ZOPA; no overlap = no deal possible.
- "Walk-away threshold" / "resistance point" are synonyms.
- Primarily a **distributive** concept but lives behind every integrative deal.
