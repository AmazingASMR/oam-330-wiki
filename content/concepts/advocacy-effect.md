## Definition
The tendency to develop **more positive attitudes** toward something **as a result of advocating for it** — the act of arguing for a position pulls your private attitude toward that position.

## Why it matters
A self-persuasion effect that works on the *advocate*, not the audience. Two mechanisms from the slide:
1. While **preparing**, we search for *positive* arguments (not negative) — biased exposure to one's own pro-side reasoning.
2. We are motivated to be **consistent** — "if I said it, I must believe it."

This connects directly back to [[concepts/cialdini-commitment-consistency]] — the same consistency drive operates on the speaker's own attitude.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Commitment & consistency** — commitment/consistency targets *future behavior* after a public stance; advocacy effect targets the advocate's *attitude* about the substance itself, even before any external commitment.
- **vs. Negative advertising** — negative advertising changes the *audience's* attitude toward an opponent; advocacy effect changes the *speaker's* attitude toward their own side.
- **vs. Central-route effects** — central-route is audience-side; advocacy effect is speaker-side. The advocate is being persuaded by their own preparation.

## Example(s)
- Kidney Case classroom exercise: groups assigned to advocate for a particular candidate end up *more genuinely convinced* their candidate deserves the kidney — even compared to their pre-advocacy private rating.
- Debate prep: the side you're assigned to argue feels more correct after you prepare.

## Likely MCQ angles
The two mechanisms (selective positive-argument search + consistency motive) are testable. Distractor: claiming advocacy makes you *more* skeptical of your assigned position — wrong direction. Distractor: confusing with central-route persuasion of the audience — advocacy effect is on the *advocate*.
