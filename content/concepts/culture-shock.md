## Definition
A **culture shock** is the disorienting realization that others are operating under different rules — including the meta-realization that there *was* a rule at all. Working across cultures is "like playing with different rules"; conflicts arise not from major obvious cultural differences but from **unrecognized, minor ones**.

## Why it matters
Names the predictable interpersonal pattern when diverse groups first interact. The slide's Five Tricks game (card game where rules differ silently across tables, no talking allowed when players move tables) is the in-class demonstration of how unspoken rule differences produce conflict, blame, and discomfort even with no malice on any side.

## Key distinctions
- **vs. Stereotype threat** — culture shock is *interpersonal* and rule-based (norm mismatch); stereotype threat is *intrapersonal* and identity-salience-based.
- **vs. Stigmatization** — shock is about norms colliding; stigmatization is about identity being devalued.
- **Slide's own predictors / lessons:**
  - **We treat our rules as the standard** — we perceive our own rules as more correct than alternatives.
  - **Home-team advantage / blame the newcomer** — during chaotic situations we tend to blame newcomers; default assumptions are that newcomers are ignorant of the rules or trying to cheat. Easier to play with people who already "know the rules."
  - **Predictors that *reduce* the impact of a shock**: having a **partner** (someone who shares your rules); having **been through a shock before**.
  - **Language-barrier analogue** — getting along without talking is like working across a language barrier; harder for those who are not assertive or who are easily embarrassed; some give up rather than explain their perspective, but remain unsatisfied with the outcome.

## Example(s)
Five Tricks card game: silent rule variation across tables, players blamed newcomers; veterans of prior rounds adjusted faster.

## Notes on application
"Which reduces the impact of a culture shock?" → having a partner, having prior shock experience. "What kind of cultural difference causes most workplace conflict?" → small/unrecognized ones, not large/obvious ones.
