## Definition
The **stereotype tax** is the career cost paid for behavior that violates an in-group stereotype, even when that behavior is otherwise valued. Slide framing: discrimination is usually a System 1 process, and the résumé-name callback study (5,000 Boston/Chicago résumés where fewer callbacks went to African-American-sounding names) is given as a stereotype-tax example — same résumé content, different perceived identity, lower outcomes.

## Why it matters
Explains why "just be excellent and you'll be rewarded" doesn't work uniformly — the same behavior is read differently depending on the actor's perceived identity. It is the mirror of stereotype threat: where threat is the *internal* cost the stereotyped person pays, the tax is the *external* cost others impose on them through System 1 evaluation.

## Key distinctions
- **Stereotype tax vs. stereotype threat** — high-confusability pair.
  - **Threat**: target's *own* performance drops under identity salience. Internal mechanism.
  - **Tax**: target *pays a cost* (e.g., fewer callbacks, lower ratings) imposed by *others'* System 1 inference. External mechanism.
- **vs. Discrimination & fairness (paradigm 1)** — the stereotype tax is the *micro-mechanism* by which paradigm-1 discrimination shows up in everyday hiring/evaluation, even when no one intends to discriminate.
- **vs. Workplace stigmatization** — stigmatization is interpersonal devaluing in everyday interaction; the tax is specifically the *career-outcome* cost from System 1 stereotyping.

## Example(s)
- Identical résumés, fewer callbacks for African-American-sounding names (5,000-résumé study).
- Identical AirBnB requests, lower acceptance for first names signaling minority race (6,400-request study).

## Likely MCQ angles
Distinguishing tax from threat is the trap: pick "tax" when the question describes an *evaluator* penalizing identity; pick "threat" when the question describes the *target's* performance dropping under identity salience. Distractor: "intentional discrimination" — the tax is usually System 1 and unintentional.
