## Definition
Cohesiveness is the shared bond driving group members to work together and to stay in the group.

## Why it matters
Cohesion alone does not predict productivity — its effect is moderated by performance norms. The classic 2×2 (Exhibit 9-4) is exam gold.

## The 2×2 (cohesion × performance norms)
- High cohesion + High norms → **High productivity**
- High cohesion + Low norms → **Low productivity** (worst case for the org)
- Low cohesion + High norms → Moderate productivity
- Low cohesion + Low norms → Moderate-to-low productivity

## Key distinctions
- **Social identity** — identity = self-esteem tied to group fate; cohesion = the bond that keeps the group together. You can identify without cohesion and vice versa.
- **Groupthink** — high cohesion is a precursor to groupthink, but cohesion itself is not pathological — the danger comes when cohesion combines with conformity pressure and a desire to protect group image.

## How to build cohesion (R&J 7 levers)
1. Make the group smaller.
2. Encourage agreement with group goals.
3. Increase time spent together.
4. Increase group status / difficulty of joining.
5. Stimulate competition with other groups.
6. Reward the group, not individuals.
7. Physically isolate the group.

## Example(s)
- Small workgroups bonded by external threat or shared time become highly cohesive.
- Hawthorne bank wiring room — extreme cohesion + LOW production norms → restricted output.

## Notes on application
The 2×2 is the most testable bit. Expect a scenario asking what predicts productivity given a cohesion/norm combination — the right answer is NOT "cohesion alone."
