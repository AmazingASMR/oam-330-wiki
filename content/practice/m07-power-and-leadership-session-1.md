# Practice — Module 7 Power & Leadership (Session 1)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 8 without referencing the concept pages. Score yourself and use the "why each distractor is wrong" notes to surface fuzzy thinking — especially the power-paradox vs. how-power-changes-behavior pair.

## Questions

### Q1. Per the slides and Useem's Atlantic article, which definition BEST captures Keltner's "power paradox"?

A. The observation that powerful people take more action toward goals while feeling less pressure from others around them.
B. The circular pattern in which the very capacities that earn someone power (attentiveness to others, perceived commitment to the group) are eroded once that person actually has power.
C. A clinical disorder, requiring sustained power and minimal constraint, characterized by 14 features including contempt for others and reckless action.
D. A Hofstede cultural dimension describing the degree to which a society accepts unequal distribution of power between regular people and important people.

### Q2. The slides and Useem describe three documented behavioral changes that occur once a person gains power. Which set correctly lists all three?

A. Increased risk-taking, increased narcissism, increased moral hypocrisy.
B. More action toward goals, reduced felt pressure of others, decreased perspective-taking.
C. Increased empathy, increased mirroring, increased deference to subordinates.
D. Increased uncertainty avoidance, increased individualism, increased status-seeking.

### Q3. A Berkeley undergraduate is randomly assigned to the "high-power" condition in a 10-minute lab priming task. Asked to draw a capital E on her own forehead, she draws it so that *she* can read it (backwards to anyone facing her). Her body also stays calm while listening to a tape of someone describing a parent's terminal illness. Which concept BEST explains this pattern?

A. Hubris syndrome — a clinical disorder produced by sustained power.
B. Power distance — a cultural dimension that shapes deference to superiors.
C. How power changes behavior — power decreases perspective-taking and quiets the Behavioral Inhibition System, even after brief priming.
D. Leader emergence — perceived competence and commitment driving who gets seen as the leader.

### Q4. KAL 801 (Guam, 1997): the first officer says "Don't you think it rains more in this area, here?" and the flight engineer adds "the weather radar has helped us a lot," rather than directly telling the captain to abort the visual approach. The captain misses the hints; 228 of 254 people die. Which concept BEST explains the cockpit dynamic?

A. The power paradox — the captain's empathy capacities had been eroded by his accumulated power.
B. Groupshift — the crew polarized toward a riskier landing than any individual would have chosen alone.
C. High power distance combined with mitigated speech — subordinates in a high-PD culture hint rather than assert, and the burden falls on the listener to decode.
D. Hubris syndrome — the captain met the 14 clinical features outlined by Lord David Owen.

### Q5. A student says: "I scored low on the power-distance dimension on a personality test, so I should expect to be a less hierarchical manager." Per the slides, what is the BEST response?

A. Correct — power distance is a stable individual trait that predicts how hierarchical a person's leadership style will be.
B. Incorrect — power distance is a Hofstede dimension that describes *cultures*, not individuals; it characterizes how a society accepts unequal power, not a personal trait.
C. Correct — power distance is one of the Big Five personality traits and reliably predicts leadership style.
D. Incorrect — power distance describes individual neural mirroring capacity, which is the wrong construct for leadership style.

### Q6. After a string of catastrophic accidents in the 1990s, Korean Air implemented two interventions: (1) it required pilots to communicate in *English* during cockpit operations, and (2) it trained pilots to recognize and reduce *mitigated speech*. Per the slides and Gladwell, what is the BEST description of what these reforms targeted?

A. They targeted hubris syndrome in captains by adding clinical screening before promotion.
B. They targeted the cultural-power-leadership problem by lowering the effective power distance in the cockpit — English forces a more direct, low-PD register and mitigated-speech training shifts the burden of clarity to the speaker.
C. They targeted the power paradox by selecting captains who were high on agreeableness and low on narcissism.
D. They targeted leader emergence by ensuring that the most competent first officer, rather than the most senior captain, made the final call.

### Q7. A consulting firm wants to predict who, among the 12 members of a newly formed project team, will be perceived as the team's leader by week 3. Per the slides, which factor pair is the BEST predictor of leader emergence?

A. Height and physical attractiveness.
B. Extraversion and talkativeness.
C. Perceived competence and perceived commitment to the group.
D. Formal seniority and control over the project budget.

### Q8. A classmate confuses two Module 7 concepts. Concept X is the *circular, self-defeating loop*: you gain power by attending to others, and having power makes you less attentive to others — undoing what got you there. Concept Y is the *list of three behavioral shifts* documented after someone acquires power: more action toward goals, less felt pressure of others, less perspective-taking, all linked to a quieted Behavioral Inhibition System. Which pairing is correct?

A. X = how power changes behavior; Y = the power paradox.
B. X = the power paradox; Y = how power changes behavior.
C. X = hubris syndrome; Y = the power paradox.
D. X = power distance; Y = how power changes behavior.

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — The power paradox is Keltner's term for the *circular* loop: perceived commitment to the group buys you power, then power erodes the very attentiveness/empathy that earned it. The "circular structure" wording is the giveaway.
- A wrong: That's *how power changes behavior* (the three behavioral shifts), not the paradox itself. Common distractor pair — see Q8.
- C wrong: That's *hubris syndrome* (Lord David Owen, 2008/2009 *Brain*) — a proposed clinical disorder requiring sustained power with minimal constraint, distinct from the general psychological pattern Keltner describes.
- D wrong: That's *power distance* — a Hofstede cultural dimension, not Keltner's individual-psychological loop.

### Q2. **B** — The slides list exactly three changes: increased action toward goals, reduced felt pressure of others, and decreased perspective-taking. Underlying mechanism: the Behavioral Inhibition System (BIS) quiets.
- A wrong: Risk-taking and narcissism aren't the canonical three; moral hypocrisy is one *failure mode* of the paradox, not one of the three behavioral changes themselves.
- C wrong: This is the *opposite* of what Keltner/Obhi find — power *decreases* mirroring/empathy (Obhi's TMS rubber-ball study), it doesn't increase it.
- D wrong: Uncertainty avoidance and individualism are Hofstede *cultural* dimensions, not individual-level behavioral changes from gaining power.

### Q3. **C** — Both the Galinsky "E on the forehead" finding (high-power participants 3× more likely to draw it self-readable) and the muted distress response to a tape of suffering are the canonical illustrations of how power changes behavior — specifically reduced perspective-taking and a quieted BIS, induceable by brief priming.
- A wrong: Hubris syndrome requires *sustained* power with minimal constraint; a 10-minute priming task can't produce it. Useem is explicit that the lab effect is functional, not clinical.
- B wrong: Power distance is a *cultural* dimension; the experimental manipulation here is individual-level power priming, not cultural variation.
- D wrong: Leader emergence is about who gets *perceived as* leader in a group; this is a solo lab task with no group-perception measure.

### Q4. **C** — High power distance + mitigated speech is Gladwell's canonical reading: a high-PD first officer hints rather than asserts, and a receiver-oriented communication norm leaves it to the captain to decode under time pressure. Pilot PDI rank: Brazil #1, S. Korea #2; PD-crash correlation r = .47.
- A wrong: The power paradox is an individual-psychological loop; the KAL story Gladwell tells is structural-cultural (Hofstede), and the reforms Korean Air actually implemented were PD-reduction, not empathy-restoration.
- B wrong: Groupshift is polarization toward more extreme positions in group decisions — that's not what's happening; the FO and engineer aren't pushing the captain toward extremes, they're failing to push him at all because of mitigated speech.
- D wrong: Hubris syndrome is a clinical proposal (Owen) requiring sustained unconstrained power; nothing in the case establishes the captain's 14 features, and Gladwell's argument is explicitly cultural.

### Q5. **B** — Per the slides, power distance is a Hofstede dimension that describes *cultures, not individuals*. This is one of the module's flagged traps. High PD ≠ "bad" either; it brings respect for elders, role clarity, and fewer conflicts (slide 13).
- A wrong: PD is not a stable individual trait; it characterizes how a society accepts unequal power. Calling it a personal trait is the canonical misuse.
- C wrong: PD is not a Big Five trait. The Big Five (OCEAN) are individual personality dimensions; PD is a Hofstede cultural dimension — different framework, different unit of analysis.
- D wrong: Mirroring/neural-empathy capacity (Obhi) is an individual-level mechanism, but it's not what "power distance" means; this answer rejects A's claim with a wrong substitute.

### Q6. **B** — Slide 17 frames Korean Air's response exactly this way: identify the PD problem, then introduce mitigated-speech training and an English-in-cockpit mandate. English forces a transmitter-oriented (low-PD) register; the training pushes the FO toward direct assertion. Result today: 5-star Skytrax rating.
- A wrong: Korean Air didn't add clinical screening for hubris syndrome; that's Owen's framework and isn't what the slides credit for the turnaround.
- C wrong: The agreeableness/narcissism/responsibility-experience selection criteria are *power-paradox prevention* strategies (slide 14) for an individual psychological problem, not the cockpit-culture reform.
- D wrong: Korean Air's reforms didn't change the chain of command — captains still command. The fix was about how the FO *speaks*, not who *decides*.

### Q7. **C** — Slide 6 is explicit that perceived competence and perceived commitment to the group are the *primary* leader-emergence factors; everything else (height, attractiveness, extraversion, formal title) is "additional," operating mostly as superficial cues to those underlying perceptions.
- A wrong: Height and attractiveness are listed under *additional* personal characteristics — they boost emergence at the margin, but they're not the primary drivers.
- B wrong: Talkativeness is a *behavior of* emergent leaders (they speak and act more), not a primary cause of emergence. Extraversion is an additional factor, not primary.
- D wrong: Formal appointment/title and control over resources are explicitly listed as *additional* factors; they predict emergence less strongly than perceived competence and commitment.

### Q8. **B** — The power paradox is the *circular loop* (gain by attending to others → power erodes that capacity → undoes what got you there). How power changes behavior is the *list of three* behavioral shifts (action ↑, pressure of others ↓, perspective-taking ↓) with the BIS-quieting mechanism. The slides flag this as an easy distractor pair.
- A wrong: Reverses the two — the loop description matches the *paradox*, and the list of three behavioral shifts matches *how power changes behavior*.
- C wrong: Hubris syndrome is a different construct (Owen's clinical proposal requiring sustained unconstrained power); it's not the circular loop, and Y here clearly describes the three behavioral changes, not the paradox.
- D wrong: Power distance is a *cultural* dimension; it's not the individual-psychological loop described in X. Y is correctly identified as how power changes behavior, but X is wrong.
