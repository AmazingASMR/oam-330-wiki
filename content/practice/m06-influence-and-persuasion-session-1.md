# Module 6 Influence & Persuasion — practice questions
## Instructions
Try to answer all 12 without referencing the concept pages. This module is dense (15 concepts), so expect sibling-distractor swaps among Cialdini's six. Score yourself and use the per-distractor rationales to surface fuzzy thinking — Cialdini scenario IDs are the highest-leverage drill.

## Questions

### Q1. Which statement BEST captures the central vs. peripheral route distinction as taught in the module?

A. The central route engages System 1 cues and produces durable attitude change; the peripheral route engages System 2 reasoning and produces fragile change.
B. The central route engages System 2 (argument quality) and produces durable attitude change; the peripheral route engages System 1 (cues) and produces faster but more fragile change. The two routes co-occur, not sequentially.
C. The two routes are mutually exclusive — a given pitch travels through one channel only, and effective persuaders must pick one.
D. Both routes engage System 2 but differ in speed; the peripheral route is simply the central route under time pressure.

### Q2. Match the definition: "We feel obligated to repay others' good deeds — receiving something first triggers a System 1 pull to give back in kind." Which Cialdini principle is this?

A. Commitment & consistency
B. Liking
C. Reciprocity
D. Social proof

### Q3. Match the definition: "Once people take a position publicly, they feel internal and external pressure to behave consistently with it — and changing behavior can in turn change attitude." Which Cialdini principle is this?

A. Reciprocity
B. Commitment & consistency
C. Authority
D. Scarcity

### Q4. The Disabled American Veterans tested two mailings: one was a plain donation request that returned 18%, the other added a small personalized address-label gift and the response rate jumped to 35% — nearly doubling. Which Cialdini principle BEST explains the lift?

A. Liking — recipients felt warmer toward the DAV because the labels were attractive.
B. Social proof — recipients inferred that other people had already donated.
C. Reciprocity — receiving the unsolicited gift triggered a felt obligation to give back in kind.
D. Authority — the official DAV letterhead signaled institutional credibility.

### Q5. A physiotherapy clinic hangs each therapist's framed diplomas in plain view of the patient during exercise instruction. Patient compliance with prescribed home-exercise programs measurably rises. Which Cialdini principle is at work?

A. Liking — diplomas make the therapist seem more similar to the patient.
B. Authority — credentials signal expertise, a System 1 vertical-deference cue.
C. Commitment & consistency — patients feel locked in once they see the diploma.
D. Scarcity — the credentials suggest a rare, hard-to-find provider.

### Q6. A medical office tested two ways of issuing appointment cards. Previously, office staff filled out the card. In the new condition, **patients themselves** wrote the date and time on the card. Missed appointments dropped by 18%. Which Cialdini principle BEST explains the drop?

A. Reciprocity — patients felt indebted to the staff for the appointment slot.
B. Authority — the doctor's printed name on the card signaled expertise.
C. Commitment & consistency — the act of writing the appointment themselves was an active, voluntary commitment that bound future behavior.
D. Social proof — patients inferred that peer patients reliably show up.

### Q7. A Tupperware host invites neighbors to a home demonstration. The single best predictor of whether a guest buys product is NOT the product's quality or price but the guest's social bond with the host — the affective tie of being invited by a friend. Which Cialdini principle is the canonical fit?

A. Reciprocity — the host gave food and drink, triggering repayment.
B. Liking — purchase tracks the dyadic affective bond between host and guest.
C. Social proof — the guest sees other guests buying and follows the group.
D. Commitment & consistency — RSVPing to the party committed the guest to buy.

### Q8. A boutique luxury watchmaker emails its mailing list: "We're opening a private waitlist for 40 units of our new model — we wanted you to know before this goes out broadly. Once these are gone, we will not produce more." Sales spike. Which Cialdini principle is the dominant lever?

A. Authority — the brand's prestige signals expertise.
B. Social proof — knowing many others are interested drives the urgency.
C. Liking — the personal email tone builds rapport.
D. Scarcity — limited availability plus exclusive information triggers loss aversion.

### Q9. A hotel chain tests two towel-reuse signs in guest rooms. Sign A makes a generic environmental appeal. Sign B says "75% of prior guests in *this room* chose to reuse their towels." Sign B substantially outperforms Sign A. Which Cialdini principle BEST explains Sign B's edge?

A. Authority — the hotel's environmental claim carries institutional weight.
B. Social proof — peer-similar others (guests who stayed in *this room*) modeled the behavior, and similarity strengthens the cue.
C. Commitment & consistency — the sign forces a public commitment.
D. Scarcity — towels are framed as a limited resource.

### Q10. Both **scarcity** and **social proof** can spike urgency to act, but they do so through OPPOSITE mechanisms. Which option correctly states that contrast?

A. Scarcity says "many people are choosing this" (consensus); social proof says "few people can have this" (rarity).
B. Scarcity says "few people *can* have this" (rarity / exclusivity); social proof says "many people *are* choosing this" (consensus / peers like me).
C. Both work through the same loss-aversion mechanism; the labels are interchangeable in practice.
D. Scarcity is a central-route cue (argument-driven); social proof is peripheral.

### Q11. The Asch line-judgment studies and the *12 Angry Men* tactical advice converge on one rule about voting in groups. Which option correctly states the slide's prescription AND the Asch finding?

A. Asch found that ~25% of subjects conformed to a unanimously wrong group; minorities should always ask for a public vote because publicness amplifies dissent.
B. Asch found that ~74% of subjects conformed to a unanimously wrong group at least once; when in the **minority**, ask for a **private** vote (to encourage dissent), and when in the **majority**, ask for a **public** vote (to lock in commitment via consistency).
C. Asch found that ~74% of subjects conformed; the rule reverses — public vote when in the minority, private when in the majority.
D. Asch's findings apply only to perceptual tasks and have no implications for organizational decision making.

### Q12. Three Module 6 strategies appear in the Kidney Case context: **negative advertising**, **order effects (recency)**, and **individual identifiability**. Which option correctly characterizes ALL THREE?

A. Negative advertising is risky but effective; the recency effect means the **last** advocate in serial competition tends to be best remembered ("the last shall be first"); individual identifiability means a single named person (e.g., Save the Children's "Rokia") persuades more than aggregate statistics.
B. Negative advertising always backfires; primacy (the first speaker) dominates serial competition; aggregate statistics persuade more than single-person stories because they convey scope.
C. Negative advertising is a central-route tactic; order effects are a System 2 phenomenon; individual identifiability works because aggregate statistics activate System 1 numbing.
D. All three are versions of social proof.

---

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — The central route is System 2 (argument quality) and produces durable change; the peripheral route is System 1 (cues) and is faster but more fragile. The routes co-occur — effective persuasion typically activates both.
- A wrong: It reverses the System 1 / System 2 mapping. Central = System 2; peripheral = System 1. (Highest-yield distractor on this module.)
- C wrong: The slide explicitly says the routes are NOT mutually exclusive — they operate simultaneously, and the ethics question is whether peripheral cues supplement a strong central case or distract from a weak one.
- D wrong: Peripheral is System 1 (not "System 2 under time pressure"); the difference is the *channel*, not the *speed setting* of one channel.

### Q2. **C** — Reciprocity is the felt obligation to repay another's gift, triggered nonconsciously by receiving first.
- A wrong: Commitment & consistency binds you to your *own* prior public stance, not to another's gift.
- B wrong: Liking accrues through ongoing similarity, praise, and rapport — not from a single discrete favor.
- D wrong: Social proof is peer behavior as a heuristic ("people like me are doing it") — not a felt debt for a gift received.

### Q3. **B** — Commitment & consistency: public stances generate internal/external pressure to act consistently, and behavior change can drag attitude along with it.
- A wrong: Reciprocity is repayment of *another's* gift, not consistency with one's own prior position.
- C wrong: Authority is deference to credible expertise — a vertical signal, not a self-consistency mechanism.
- D wrong: Scarcity is rarity/loss-framed availability — nothing about prior public commitment.

### Q4. **C** — Reciprocity. The unsolicited personalized gift triggered a System 1 obligation to repay; the lift went from 18% to 35%, nearly doubling response. This is Cialdini's canonical reciprocity numeric anchor.
- A wrong: Liking would predict the labels are persuasive because they create rapport or similarity — but the mechanism Cialdini documents here is **felt debt**, not affection. Liking accrues through ongoing similarity/praise, not a single mailed token.
- B wrong: Social proof would require evidence to recipients that other people had donated (e.g., a donor list) — the DAV mailing showed no peer-donation cue, just a personal gift.
- D wrong: Letterhead authority was held constant across both conditions; the difference was the *gift*, not the credibility signal.

### Q5. **B** — Authority. Credentials are a peripheral System 1 cue: visible expertise produces vertical deference and lifts compliance with the expert's instructions.
- A wrong: Liking is dyadic affect (similarity, praise, attractiveness) — diplomas don't make the therapist "more like the patient." Liking and authority can stack, but the diploma mechanism is expertise signaling, not similarity.
- C wrong: Commitment & consistency would require the patient to have made a prior public stance the diploma now binds them to — not the case here.
- D wrong: Scarcity is about the *thing's* availability; authority is about the *speaker's* expertise. The diploma signals expertise, not rare supply.

### Q6. **C** — Commitment & consistency. Patients writing the appointment themselves makes the commitment **active, voluntary, and personally enacted** — the ladder Cialdini identifies as stickiest. The 18% missed-appointment drop is the slide's anchor for active vs. passive commitment.
- A wrong: Reciprocity would require the staff to have given the patient a gift triggering felt debt — not the mechanism here.
- B wrong: Authority signaling was held constant; the difference was *who wrote the card*, not whose name was on it.
- D wrong: Social proof would require peer-show-up evidence the patient sees — the card change is about the patient's own act, not peer information.

### Q7. **B** — Liking. The Tupperware finding (Cialdini) is that purchase tracks the **social bond with the host**, not product quality — a one-to-one affective tie.
- A wrong: Reciprocity would predict that the food/drink offered triggers obligation; but the documented predictor is the *prior friendship with the host*, not the snacks.
- C wrong: Social proof is the *group* mechanism — many similar peers doing X. Tupperware's mechanism is the **dyadic** host-guest bond, not group consensus.
- D wrong: An RSVP isn't an active public commitment to *purchase* — it's a commitment to attend. Consistency would predict the guest must commit to buying, which the study didn't require.

### Q8. **D** — Scarcity. Limited units + "before this goes out broadly" stacks two scarcity cues (limited availability + exclusive information), both loss-framed (you'll lose access if you wait). This is the slide's loss-aversion mechanism.
- A wrong: Authority signals expertise of the *speaker*; here the cue is rarity of the *product*. Different shortcut.
- B wrong: This is the textbook social-proof / scarcity trap — the email gives no peer-uptake data ("X others have already bought"). It signals rarity, not consensus. (Confusing these two is exactly the high-yield trap.)
- C wrong: The personal tone is a minor liking touch; the dominant lever is rarity + exclusivity, which is scarcity.

### Q9. **B** — Social proof, with similarity amplifying the cue. Sign B works because the modeled behavior comes from peer-similar others (prior guests in *this very room*), not just generic "guests" — the slide stresses that similarity makes social proof work.
- A wrong: Authority would require an expert source (e.g., "environmental scientists recommend"); Sign A is closer to a generic appeal and underperforms — the lift comes from peer modeling, not expert endorsement.
- C wrong: The sign doesn't ask for any commitment from the current guest; commitment & consistency requires a prior stance that constrains future behavior.
- D wrong: Towels aren't framed as scarce — there's no rarity / loss-framed access cue. Social proof and scarcity both spike action but through opposite mechanisms (consensus vs. rarity).

### Q10. **B** — Scarcity = "few *can* have this" (rarity, exclusivity, loss aversion); social proof = "many *are* choosing this" (consensus, peer behavior). They produce urgency through OPPOSITE signals.
- A wrong: It reverses the two — this is the canonical sibling-swap distractor.
- C wrong: Loss aversion is the engine of *scarcity* specifically; social proof runs on similarity-based heuristic peer-following, not loss aversion.
- D wrong: Both are peripheral-route cues (System 1), not central-route arguments — every Cialdini principle is peripheral.

### Q11. **B** — Asch's line study: ~74% of subjects conformed to a unanimously wrong group at least once. The slide rule from *12 Angry Men*: **private vote when in the minority** (to encourage dissent — public votes lock people in via commitment & consistency); **public vote when in the majority** (to harden support and discourage backsliding).
- A wrong: 25% is wrong (it's 74%); the directionality of public vs. private is also reversed.
- C wrong: 74% is correct, but the public/private rule is reversed. Public votes amplify commitment-consistency stickiness — useful when you have the majority, harmful when you don't.
- D wrong: Asch was perceptual, but the slide explicitly applies the conformity baseline to organizational dissent (Juror #8 territory) — that's the whole point of the *12 Angry Men* mapping.

### Q12. **A** — All three correct: negative advertising is "risky but effective" (slide's exact phrasing); recency dominates serial competition ("the last shall be first" — figure skating, *American Idol*, Kidney Case); individual identifiability boosts persuasion via a single named person (Save the Children's Rokia, donations more than doubled vs. aggregate statistics).
- B wrong: Negative advertising can work (risky but effective, not "always backfires"); recency — not primacy — wins in live serial competition; aggregate statistics *numb* rather than persuade (the Rokia finding is the inverse of this claim).
- C wrong: All three are peripheral-route, System 1 effects; they're not central-route or System 2 phenomena. The numbing-by-statistics direction is also stated correctly only in option A.
- D wrong: They're three *distinct* peripheral effects — only individual identifiability is even adjacent to social-proof-style sympathy mechanics, and even it is one named person, not a peer group.
