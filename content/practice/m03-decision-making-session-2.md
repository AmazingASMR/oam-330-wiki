# Module 3 Decision Making — practice questions (Part 2)
## Instructions
Session 2 deliberately targets material Session 1 underweighted: Bruner & Goodman horse-or-seal motivated perception, the HOT/NOT voice study self-fulfilling prophecy, the perception-vs-reality umbrella, and Dunning-Kruger directionality. High-yield distractor pairs (availability vs. confirmation, halo vs. SFP, sunk cost vs. overconfidence, hindsight vs. overconfidence) are re-tested with NEW scenarios. Answer all 10 cold; use the per-distractor "why wrong" notes to surface fuzzy thinking.

## Questions

### Q1. The slides claim that the **first step** in any decision is gathering information by *perceiving* the situation, and that this step is already biased before any reasoning happens. Which statement BEST captures the slide's umbrella claim about perception?

A. Perception equals reality, provided the perceiver is paying close attention.
B. Perception is organizing and interpreting sensory input — and **perception ≠ reality**, because expectations, motivation, and prior knowledge bend interpretation at the input stage.
C. Perception is purely a System 2 process, so it is reliably accurate when the perceiver is well-rested.
D. Perception is identical to attribution — both are about explaining the causes of observed behavior.

### Q2. Which definition BEST matches the slide's account of **motivated perception**, as originally demonstrated by **Bruner & Goodman (1947)**?

A. The tendency to actively seek out information that confirms an existing belief, while neglecting disconfirming evidence.
B. The tendency to over-weight initial numeric reference points when making subsequent estimates.
C. We see what we *expect or want* to see — perception itself is bent by motives, incentives, and expectations before any reasoning occurs.
D. The tendency to reconstruct memory of prior beliefs once an outcome has been revealed.

### Q3. In a slide demo, students were shown an ambiguous image that could be read as a **horse or a seal**. With no incentives attached, the split was roughly **50/50**. When seeing one animal led to a glass of orange juice and the other to a "nasty smoothie," **70%** of students "saw" the animal that paid off. Which bias does this 50→70 shift most directly illustrate?

A. Confirmation bias
B. Motivated perception
C. Halo effect
D. Availability heuristic

### Q4. In the slide-cited **HOT/NOT voice study**, a single woman had identical phone conversations with multiple male callers. Callers told she was attractive engaged with her warmly; callers told she was unattractive engaged with her coldly. The woman herself ended up sounding **measurably warmer or cooler** depending on how she was treated — even though she was the same person each time. Which bias BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Halo effect
B. Confirmation bias
C. Self-fulfilling prophecy
D. Motivated perception

### Q5. A regional sales VP is asked, "Who are your top three reps right now?" She rattles off three names instantly — they happen to be the reps who emailed her this week, the one who pitched at last Friday's all-hands, and the one whose deal closed yesterday. The actual top-three by quota attainment are different, but *those* names didn't pop to mind. Which bias BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Confirmation bias
B. Availability heuristic
C. Halo effect
D. Anchoring bias

### Q6. A first-year medical resident has read two textbook chapters on cardiac arrhythmias and now confidently overrides the attending's diagnosis on a difficult case. A senior cardiologist on the same case offers a much more hedged, probabilistic read. Per the slides' framing of **Dunning-Kruger**, which statement is correct?

A. The senior cardiologist's hedged confidence reflects expertise; the resident's certainty reflects Dunning-Kruger — *less* skill in a domain produces *more* overconfidence.
B. The resident's confidence is appropriate because Dunning-Kruger says experts are systematically over-cautious.
C. Confidence rises monotonically with skill, so the resident must actually be more skilled than the cardiologist.
D. Dunning-Kruger predicts that confidence and accuracy are perfectly correlated, so whichever person is more confident is more likely correct.

### Q7. A startup founder has burned $1.2M and 18 months on a payments product that is consistently failing user testing. Investors and her own data team agree the next round of investment is very unlikely to recover. She insists on continuing because *"we've come too far to walk away."* A second founder, in a separate meeting, predicts her *next* product launch will hit revenue targets in 90 days — a forecast based purely on best-case assumptions about that specific launch. Which pairing of biases is correct?

A. Founder 1 = overconfidence (planning fallacy); Founder 2 = sunk cost fallacy.
B. Founder 1 = sunk cost fallacy (escalation of commitment); Founder 2 = overconfidence (planning fallacy).
C. Founder 1 = anchoring; Founder 2 = framing.
D. Founder 1 = hindsight bias; Founder 2 = confirmation bias.

### Q8. After a regional bank collapses, financial-news pundits assemble a tidy list of "obvious" warning signs — the loan book, the rate exposure, the management style — and explain that the failure was inevitable. Six months earlier, with the same data publicly available, none of these pundits flagged the bank as at risk. A second commentator on the same panel separately predicts that *next year's* bank failures are easy to forecast and that her hit rate will be near 100%. Which pairing is correct?

A. Pundits = overconfidence; second commentator = hindsight bias.
B. Pundits = hindsight bias ("I knew it all along"); second commentator = overconfidence (forward-looking calibration failure).
C. Pundits = halo effect; second commentator = self-fulfilling prophecy.
D. Pundits = sunk cost fallacy; second commentator = anchoring bias.

### Q9. A consulting team is reviewing a client's expansion strategy. The lead partner has already concluded the strategy will work. He requests three case studies of "comparable expansions that succeeded," asks the analyst to interview executives at firms that grew under similar conditions, and screens out a memo flagging a comparable that failed. A second partner, working independently, simply notices that successful expansions *come to mind faster* than failed ones because the success stories are widely covered in the financial press, and uses that mental ease as evidence the strategy is sound. Which pairing of biases is correct?

A. Partner 1 = availability heuristic; Partner 2 = confirmation bias.
B. Partner 1 = confirmation bias (active selective seeking); Partner 2 = availability heuristic (passive ease of recall).
C. Both = confirmation bias.
D. Partner 1 = halo effect; Partner 2 = motivated perception.

### Q10. Two biases both depend on a perceiver's prior expectation about another person. **Bias X** is purely *perceiver-side*: the perceiver generalizes one observed cue (e.g., the firm's stock outcome) across unrelated traits of the target, but the target's actual behavior is unchanged. **Bias Y** is *interpersonal*: the perceiver's expectation changes how they treat the target, the target's actual behavior shifts in response, and the perceiver then cites that real change as confirmation. Which pairing of X and Y is correct?

A. X = self-fulfilling prophecy; Y = halo effect.
B. X = halo effect; Y = self-fulfilling prophecy.
C. X = confirmation bias; Y = motivated perception.
D. X = hindsight bias; Y = overconfidence.

---

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — The slides' headline claim is **perception ≠ reality**: perception is the organizing/interpreting step, and it is already bent by expectations, motivation, and prior knowledge before any reasoning happens. This is the umbrella claim under which motivated perception, confirmation bias, etc. all sit.
- A wrong: "Perception equals reality" is the **explicit false-statement distractor** the slides flag — it is precisely the claim the module rejects. Attention does not eliminate the distortion (Gilbert: the brain cannot see itself fooling itself).
- C wrong: Slides say "accurate perception takes lots of concentration" — i.e., it requires effortful System 2 — but perception is *not* a pure System 2 process. The default is fast, automatic System 1 interpretation.
- D wrong: Perception (organizing sensory input) and attribution (explaining causes of observed behavior) are different stages. Conflating them is a classic distractor.

### Q2. **C** — Motivated perception per Bruner & Goodman: we see what we expect or want to see; motives, incentives, and expectations bend perception at the input stage, before reasoning begins.
- A wrong: That is **confirmation bias** — active selective seeking of evidence at the *evidence-gathering* stage, not bending of perception at the *seeing* stage. The cross-cut explicitly flags motivated perception ↔ confirmation as a sister-bias swap.
- B wrong: That is **anchoring bias** (over-weighting initial numeric reference points) — different bucket entirely (numeric decision making, not perception).
- D wrong: That is **hindsight bias** — outcome-driven memory reconstruction, a backward-looking memory bias, not a perceptual input bias.

### Q3. **B** — Motivated perception: the 50/50 baseline shifts to 70% in favor of the *outcome-favorable* animal once incentives are attached. Perception itself is bent by what the perceiver wants to see — Bruner & Goodman's foundational finding.
- A wrong: Confirmation bias is *active* selective seeking of confirming evidence. Subjects here aren't searching evidence — perception of the ambiguous image is *itself* shifted at the seeing stage. (Cross-cut: motivated perception = input stage; confirmation = evidence-gathering stage.)
- C wrong: Halo is generalizing one observed trait of a target across other traits of the same target. There is no trait-generalization here — there's a single ambiguous image and a wanted outcome.
- D wrong: Availability is judging frequency by ease of recall of vivid examples. Subjects are perceiving an image in the moment, not retrieving memories.

### Q4. **C** — Self-fulfilling prophecy: the perceivers' expectations (told she was attractive vs. unattractive) changed how they treated her on the call → the woman's *actual conversational behavior* shifted in response → the perceivers' expectations appeared "validated" by real behavior change. Same woman; expectation produced the very evidence cited.
- A wrong: Halo is purely perceiver-side — the target's actual behavior doesn't change, only the perceiver's labels do. Here the woman's behavior demonstrably shifts, which is the SFP signature.
- B wrong: Confirmation bias is one-sided info-*gathering* about a belief; it doesn't cause the target's behavior to change. SFP creates the evidence; confirmation merely filters it.
- D wrong: Motivated perception is internal — *your* perception is bent. Here the *target's actual behavior* changes, which is the interpersonal hallmark of SFP, not motivated perception.

### Q5. **B** — Availability heuristic: the names that *come easily to mind* (recent emails, recent pitches, yesterday's close) are mistaken for evidence of who the top performers actually are. Vivid/recent examples crowd out the actual base rate. The slides use this exact "top salespeople" boss-question scenario.
- A wrong: Confirmation bias is *active selective seeking* of evidence to confirm a prior belief. The VP isn't filtering inquiries — names are *passively popping* to mind. (Cross-cut: availability = passive recall; confirmation = active seeking.)
- C wrong: Halo is generalizing one trait across other traits of the *same* target. Here the VP is naming three different targets based on recall ease — different mechanism.
- D wrong: Anchoring requires an initial *number* contaminating a numeric estimate. There is no anchor number in this stem.

### Q6. **A** — Dunning-Kruger: people are MORE overconfident in domains where they have LESS experience or skill. Counterintuitive. Novices feel certain because they can't yet see what they don't know; experts have learned to hedge because they've encountered the edge cases.
- B wrong: Reverses Dunning-Kruger. The slides' direction is **less skill → MORE overconfidence**. Experts are not "systematically over-cautious"; they are better calibrated.
- C wrong: Slides explicitly say confidence and accuracy are nearly *uncorrelated*. More-confident does not mean more-skilled.
- D wrong: Confidence and accuracy are not perfectly correlated — that's exactly the calibration failure overconfidence describes.

### Q7. **B** — Founder 1 is the **sunk cost fallacy** (escalation of commitment / "we've come too far"): she's over-weighting non-recoverable past spend instead of evaluating future costs and benefits. Founder 2 is **overconfidence (planning fallacy)**: forecasting based on best-case assumptions about *this specific* launch, not the average of past launches.
- A wrong: Reverses the constructs. Founder 1 explicitly cites *past* investment ("come too far") — that's sunk cost, not forward-looking overconfidence. Founder 2 makes a *forward* best-case prediction — that's planning fallacy, not sunk cost.
- C wrong: Anchoring requires an initial reference number contaminating a numeric estimate; framing requires gain/loss verbal cast. Neither stem has the numeric/framing mechanism.
- D wrong: Hindsight bias is "I knew it all along" *after* an outcome — neither founder is reconstructing memory of prior beliefs. Confirmation bias is selective evidence search, not best-case forecasting.

### Q8. **B** — Pundits show **hindsight bias**: once the outcome is known, the warning signs look "obvious" and the failure looks inevitable, even though the same pundits didn't flag them in advance. The second commentator shows **overconfidence**: forward-looking calibration failure ("near 100% hit rate"). Hindsight feeds overconfidence — having "known" the past inflates predicted future accuracy.
- A wrong: Reverses temporal direction. Pundits are looking *backward* (post-collapse rationalization) — that's hindsight, not overconfidence. The commentator is forecasting *forward* — that's overconfidence, not hindsight.
- C wrong: Halo recolors *traits of an actor* based on an outcome (CEO "decisive" vs. "rigid"). The pundits aren't relabeling a person — they're relabeling the *predictability of the outcome*, which is hindsight's signature, not halo's. SFP is irrelevant — there is no target whose behavior is being changed by expectations.
- D wrong: Sunk cost is over-weighting past investment in a *current* commitment decision — pundits aren't deciding whether to keep investing in something. Anchoring requires a contaminating starting number.

### Q9. **B** — Partner 1 = **confirmation bias**: he is *actively selecting* sources, interviews, and memos that can only confirm his prior conclusion (and screens out a disconfirming memo). Partner 2 = **availability heuristic**: he passively notices that success stories *come to mind faster* (because they're more covered) and treats that mental ease as evidence. Active selection vs. passive recall — the cross-cut's headline distinction.
- A wrong: Reverses the two. Partner 1's *active filtering* of inputs is the confirmation signature; Partner 2's *passive ease of recall* is the availability signature.
- C wrong: Partner 2 isn't filtering inquiries — examples are popping to mind based on press coverage. That's availability, not confirmation. Calling them both confirmation bias collapses the active/passive distinction.
- D wrong: Halo is trait-generalization across one target; motivated perception is bending of perception at the input stage. Neither stem describes those mechanisms.

### Q10. **B** — Halo is *perceiver-side* trait generalization: one observed cue colors unrelated trait judgments of the same target, but the target's actual behavior is unchanged (the CEO success/failure trait-flip). Self-fulfilling prophecy is *interpersonal*: the perceiver's expectation changes treatment of the target, the target's behavior actually shifts, and the perceiver cites that real shift as confirmation. The behavior change distinguishes SFP from halo.
- A wrong: Reverses the two. SFP requires *real behavior change in the target*; halo does not. The stem's Bias X explicitly stipulates "target's actual behavior is unchanged" — that's halo, not SFP.
- C wrong: Confirmation bias and motivated perception are not the perceiver-side / interpersonal pair. Confirmation is selective evidence-seeking; motivated perception is input-stage bending of one's own perception. Neither involves changing a *target's* behavior.
- D wrong: Hindsight (backward) and overconfidence (forward) are the temporal-direction pair tied to outcome knowledge and self-prediction — not the perceiver-side / interpersonal pair tied to expectations about another person.
