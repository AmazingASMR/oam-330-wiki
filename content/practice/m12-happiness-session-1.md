# Module 12 Happiness & Wrap-up — practice questions
## Instructions
Try to answer all 6 without reference. Score yourself, then re-read the linked concept pages on misses. Module 12 is a smaller wrap-up — focus is on the set-point drivers, the two intentional levers (connections + money use), and the three mega-takeaways.

## Questions

### Q1. A manager notes that their employee got a substantial raise and a corner office six months ago and was clearly thrilled at first — but today reports being about as happy at work as they were before the change. Which set-point driver best explains this pattern?

A. Genetic baseline
B. Hedonic adaptation
C. Social comparison
D. Negativity bias

### Q2. Two analysts earn the same $120k. Analyst X works on a team where peers earn $90k and feels great about her pay. Analyst Y switches to a team where peers earn $160k and immediately feels underpaid, even though her absolute salary did not change. Y's drop in satisfaction is best explained by:

A. Hedonic adaptation
B. The Easterlin paradox at the country level
C. Social comparison (relative standing)
D. Genetic set point

### Q3. Which of the following is NOT one of Williams's three explicit "mega-takeaways" closing OAM 330?

A. Human behavior is predictable — humans aren't irrational, just insufficiently modeled.
B. What we think is not always reality — common sense is necessary but not sufficient; ground decisions in evidence.
C. Other people matter — social context, relationships, and groups shape outcomes.
D. Humans are fundamentally irrational and behavior in organizations is largely random.

### Q4. According to the slide-19 breakdown of life-satisfaction variance referenced in the module, roughly what share is attributed to genetics, intentional activity, and circumstances, respectively?

A. 10% genetics / 50% intentional activity / 40% circumstances
B. 50% genetics / 40% intentional activity / 10% circumstances
C. 40% genetics / 10% intentional activity / 50% circumstances
D. 33% genetics / 33% intentional activity / 33% circumstances

### Q5. A new HR director has a $200-per-employee wellness budget and wants to maximize happiness gains. Based on the slide research on how money is spent, which option is best supported?

A. Give each employee a $200 cash bonus to spend on themselves however they want.
B. Buy each employee a $200 high-end branded item (e.g., a logo jacket) so the upgrade is durable.
C. Fund a $200-per-employee shared team experience or a prosocial-giving match (donate / spend on others).
D. Bank the money toward next year's salary increases, since absolute pay is the strongest happiness driver.

### Q6. In the Frank (NYT) reading, the principle that satisfaction depends more on one's *position* in the income distribution than on absolute income — and that, past basic obligations, more income does not straightforwardly buy more happiness — is most directly associated with:

A. The Kahneman–Deaton "absence of misery" finding only
B. The Easterlin paradox / relative income
C. Compensating differentials
D. Deliberate practice (Ericsson)

---

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Hedonic adaptation. The set-point model holds that humans adjust to *both* good and bad changes; circumstance shifts produce only short-lived swings. Slide examples: marriage boosts happiness ~2 years before reverting; Illinois lottery winners ($50k–$1M) no happier than others after 1 year.
- A wrong: Genetics is the stable baseline itself, not the leveling-off mechanism.
- C wrong: Social comparison would require a peer-relativity story (e.g., what coworkers earn), which the prompt does not include.
- D wrong: Negativity bias (Brooks) explains weighting of bad news, not return-to-baseline after a positive change.

### Q2. **C** — Social comparison (relative standing). Slides: people are happier earning $50k in a $25k neighborhood than more in a richer one — happiness depends on relative, not absolute, standing.
- A wrong: Adaptation is about absolute level resetting over time; here Y's drop is immediate and driven by *peers*, not time.
- B wrong: Easterlin paradox is the country-level / cross-time version of the same idea — the *mechanism* labeled in the slides for the individual peer-comparison case is social comparison.
- D wrong: Genetics is a stable baseline, not a response to a peer change.

### Q3. **D** — Humans are fundamentally irrational and behavior in organizations is largely random. Slides explicitly say the *opposite*: "humans aren't irrational, just insufficiently modeled" — the predictability mega-takeaway.
- A wrong: Predictability is one of the three mega-takeaways verbatim from the wrap-up slide.
- B wrong: "What we think is not always reality" is one of the three mega-takeaways verbatim from the wrap-up slide.
- C wrong: "Other people matter" is one of the three mega-takeaways verbatim from the wrap-up slide.

### Q4. **B** — 50% genetics / 40% intentional activity / 10% circumstances. Per the slide-19 breakdown: ~50% genetic set point (Minnesota Twin Studies — 3,200 twins raised apart), ~40% intentional activity (the actionable lever), ~10% circumstances. The order matters: genetics dominates, intentional activity is the actionable middle, circumstances are surprisingly small.
- A wrong: Scrambles the shares — under-weights genetics and over-weights circumstances relative to the slide.
- C wrong: Scrambles the shares — under-weights intentional activity, the actionable lever the module emphasizes.
- D wrong: A common-sense even-split distractor not supported by the slide.

### Q5. **C** — Fund a shared team experience or a prosocial-giving match. Slides distinguish three spending categories: experiences > things (no easy comparison, hard to adapt to, often social), and prosocial spending > self-spending (the $20 campus experiment showed prosocial spending produced more happiness — opposite to what observers predicted; across countries, spending on self does not predict happiness, spending on others does).
- A wrong: Self-spending cash — slides find it does not predict happiness across countries.
- B wrong: A material branded item invites comparison and adaptation — the weakest category.
- D wrong: Once basic obligations are met, more absolute pay does not straightforwardly buy more happiness (Easterlin / Kahneman–Deaton).

### Q6. **B** — The Easterlin paradox / relative income. Frank explicitly invokes Easterlin: position in the income distribution shapes satisfaction more than absolute income, and past basic obligations more income does not straightforwardly buy happiness.
- A wrong: Kahneman–Deaton ("wealth is like health: its utter absence breeds misery, yet having it is no guarantee of happiness") is related and appears in the same concept page, but it is the *absence-of-misery threshold* finding, not the relative-income principle.
- C wrong: Compensating differentials is Frank's term for "jobs with better conditions tend to pay less" — a separate idea.
- D wrong: Deliberate practice (Ericsson) is about expertise hours, not income–happiness.
