# Practice — Module 11 Ethics (Session 2)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 10 without referencing the concept pages. Score yourself and use the "why each distractor is wrong" notes to surface fuzzy thinking. Session 2 emphasizes the four enabling mechanisms (which Williams flags every term), bounded ethicality, want/should-self, the antidote pillars, and the fading vs. justification distractor pair.

## Questions

### Q1. Bazerman & Tenbrunsel coined "bounded ethicality" as a deliberate parallel to which earlier concept, and what is its core claim?

A. A parallel to *bounded rationality*: people are subject to systematic, mostly-unconscious cognitive limits that prevent them from being as ethical as they believe they are.
B. A parallel to *bounded confidence*: people calibrate their ethics to whatever level of social confidence they can sustain in front of peers.
C. A parallel to *moral relativism*: ethics is bounded by the cultural context in which the actor was raised.
D. A parallel to *cognitive dissonance*: people change their ethical beliefs to match behavior they have already committed to.

### Q2. Per the want self / should self model, which mapping correctly identifies which "self" dominates at which stage of an ethical decision?

A. The *want* self dominates the prediction stage and forecasts ethical action; the *should* self dominates at decision time and acts impulsively.
B. The *should* self dominates the prediction stage and forecasts ethical action; the *want* self dominates at decision time and acts on self-interest.
C. Both selves dominate equally at every stage, which is why behavior is unpredictable.
D. The *should* self dominates only after the act, during recollection, while the *want* self dominates everywhere else.

### Q3. A pharmaceutical regional manager is told by HQ that her bonus, her team's bonuses, and her continued employment all depend on hitting an aggressive new prescription-volume target. Within six months her reps are paying physicians for sham "speaker programs" that are really kickbacks for prescriptions. Internal investigators later find leadership never asked *how* the targets were being met — only whether they were met. Which enabling mechanism is the BEST fit?

A. Moral justification — the reps rationalized the kickbacks by telling themselves the patients still benefited.
B. Social norms — the reps copied a behavior they saw widespread among peers.
C. Outcome-focused culture — leadership built a goals-and-incentives structure where ends justified means and process was invisible.
D. Moral inaction — the reps witnessed misconduct by colleagues and failed to report it.

### Q4. A new analyst at a hedge fund notices that on Friday afternoons, every senior trader on the desk inflates the closing marks on illiquid bonds by a few basis points to make the week's P&L look better. No one talks about it; it's just *how the desk closes the week*. After two months the analyst starts doing it too — not because she rationalized any harm, but because it seemed like the standard, unremarkable practice on this desk. Which enabling mechanism is the BEST fit?

A. Outcome-focused culture — leadership pressured her on weekly P&L numbers.
B. Social norms — peer behavior signaled "this is what people here do," and she conformed without independent rationalization.
C. Moral justification via anonymity — the harm was diffuse and the victims unidentifiable.
D. Visceral response — Friday-afternoon fatigue overrode her deliberation.

### Q5. A defense contractor sells weapons systems through layered intermediaries to end users in countries where the sale would be politically explosive if made directly. Executives privately joke about "plausible deniability." When asked, the VP of sales says, "Look, by the time it gets to the end user it's gone through four brokers. We don't know who's actually buying it, and frankly that's the point — it lets us sleep at night." Which enabling mechanism is the BEST fit, and via which slide-listed facilitator?

A. Moral justification, via *anonymity of the victim* — diffusing the identifiable target reduces moral concern (parallel to Enron traders harming distant CA customers).
B. Social norms, via pluralistic ignorance — the VP misperceives industry peers as more permissive than they actually are.
C. Moral inaction, via learned helplessness — the VP feels powerless to change industry practice.
D. Outcome-focused culture, via goals and incentives — leadership has installed quotas that demand the sale.

### Q6. An associate at a law firm watches a senior partner bill a client for hours that were obviously not worked. She recognizes it as fraud, feels disgusted, but says nothing — she has tried raising concerns once before, was brushed off, and now feels she has no real ability to change a partner's behavior. Which mechanism is the BEST fit, and which slide-listed driver of that mechanism is operating?

A. Moral justification — the associate rationalizes the partner's billing as a victimless overcharge.
B. Ethical fading — the associate doesn't really see the over-billing as an ethical issue, just an accounting quirk.
C. Moral inaction, driven by *powerlessness / learned helplessness* — she perceives the wrong but feels unable to influence the situation.
D. Memory revisionism — she retroactively reframes the partner's conduct as ethical.

### Q7. Which pairing correctly contrasts **ethical fading** with **moral justification**, the most-tested distractor pair in Lectures 26–27?

A. Fading is in-the-moment perception (System 1) — the ethical dimension never enters awareness; justification is an active rationalization (System 2) — ethics is acknowledged and explained away, often through anonymity or inequity facilitators.
B. Fading is post-hoc rationalization while justification is in-the-moment perception — fading happens after, justification happens during.
C. Fading and justification are synonyms — both refer to ignoring the ethical content of a decision.
D. Fading is an organizational mechanism (cultural); justification is an individual mechanism (cognitive) — they operate at different levels of analysis.

### Q8. Which Moral Foundation is BEST illustrated by an employee who **publicly speaks at a competitor's recruiting event and badmouths her current company** while still drawing a paycheck from it?

A. Care / Harm — she causes reputational harm to a vulnerable target.
B. Fairness / Reciprocity — she has not given back what she received.
C. Ingroup / Loyalty — she has put outside loyalties above her own team / employer.
D. Authority / Respect — she has disrespected her superiors.

### Q9. Which pairing correctly contrasts **forecasting errors** (prediction stage) with **memory revisionism** (recollection stage)?

A. Forecasting is backward-looking distortion of past behavior; revisionism is forward-looking optimism about future behavior.
B. Forecasting is forward-looking — over-predicting that we'll act on our principles because the *should self* dominates the prediction; revisionism is backward-looking — distorting memories of past unethical acts so we still see ourselves as ethical (e.g., "anyone would have done the same").
C. Both occur at decision time; the only difference is whether the actor is in a hot or cold state.
D. Forecasting is an organizational-level bias; revisionism is an individual-level bias.

### Q10. Williams's slide-21 antidote to widespread organizational misconduct is a *culture of ethics* built on four ingredients. Which option lists all four correctly?

A. Stronger compliance training, harsher punishments, anonymous reporting hotlines, and an ethics officer.
B. Transparency, checks and balances, process focus (not just outcomes), and dissent encouraged rather than suppressed.
C. Outcome-based incentives, individual accountability, market discipline, and regulatory oversight.
D. Faith traditions, moral foundations, professional guidelines, and the law.

## Answer Key

### Q1. **A** — Bounded ethicality is Bazerman & Tenbrunsel's deliberate analog of bounded rationality: same logic, different domain. Core claim is that smart, honest people fail ethically because of systematic, mostly-unconscious cognitive limits — not because they are bad apples.
- B wrong: "bounded confidence" is not a real construct in the reading.
- C wrong: bounded ethicality is a *cognitive-bias* claim about individuals, not a moral-relativist claim about cultures.
- D wrong: cognitive dissonance describes belief-revision *after* committed behavior; bounded ethicality is broader and operates *upstream* (predictions, decisions, and recollections all biased).

### Q2. **B** — The *should* self is deliberate / principled / System-2-like and dominates *before* the decision (predicts ethical action). The *want* self is impulsive / emotional / System-1-like and dominates *at* the decision (acts on self-interest). The gap between the two is where ethics fail (62/68% predict → 0% act).
- A wrong: reverses which self speaks at which stage.
- C wrong: the asymmetry is structural; the model's whole point is that *which* self dominates depends on the stage.
- D wrong: should self dominates prediction, not recollection; recollection is the territory of memory revisionism.

### Q3. **C** — Outcome-focused culture: leadership-built goals + incentives + a "didn't ask how" posture is the canonical pattern (Wells Fargo / Ford Pinto). This is *cultural* and *structural*, not an individual cognitive event.
- A wrong: justification is an *individual* rationalization in one employee's head; the question stem describes an organization-wide structure built by leadership.
- B wrong: social norms requires a peer-behavior cue ("everyone on the desk does it"); the stem describes top-down quota pressure, not horizontal peer modeling.
- D wrong: moral inaction is bystander failure to report someone *else's* misconduct; the reps here are the active perpetrators.

### Q4. **B** — Social norms: peer behavior signals "this is what people here do," and the analyst conforms without independent rationalization. Best predictor of misconduct is whether you think peers do it (cf. tax-cheating literature, slides).
- A wrong: no leadership-driven goal pressure is described; the stem is purely horizontal peer modeling, not vertical incentive structure.
- C wrong: justification via anonymity requires *the actor* to invoke an identifiable-target-is-missing rationale; the stem explicitly says she did *not* rationalize, she just conformed.
- D wrong: visceral response requires a hot bodily state (hunger, fear, fatigue actively driving the behavior); fatigue is not the cause described — habituation to peer norms is.

### Q5. **A** — Moral justification via *anonymity of the victim*: diffusing the identifiable end user reduces moral concern. The slide-listed parallel is Enron traders manipulating CA power prices remotely (anonymous, distant victims). The VP's "we don't know who's actually buying it… that's the point" is the textbook anonymity tell.
- B wrong: no peer-behavior cue or pluralistic-ignorance survey is described; this is internal rationalization, not norm-following.
- C wrong: moral inaction is bystander silence about *someone else's* misconduct; the VP is the active perpetrator.
- D wrong: outcome-focused culture would describe a leadership-installed quota system; the stem describes a rationalization technique (anonymity), not a goals-and-incentives structure.

### Q6. **C** — Moral inaction: she clearly perceives the wrong but takes no action. The slide-listed driver is *powerlessness / learned helplessness* — she has tried before, been brushed off, and now feels unable to influence the situation. (The other two slide drivers are apathy/withdrawal and moral disengagement.)
- A wrong: justification is for the actor's *own* misconduct; the associate is a bystander, not the over-biller.
- B wrong: ethical fading would require her *not* to perceive the issue; the stem says she "recognizes it as fraud" — ethics is fully in awareness.
- D wrong: memory revisionism is a *recollection-stage* distortion of past behavior; the stem is decision-time bystander silence.

### Q7. **A** — Fading = System 1 perceptual failure (ethics never enters awareness; cost-benefit reframing is the canonical trigger, e.g., Pinto). Justification = System 2 active rationalization (ethics IS perceived but explained away through anonymity, inequity, or other facilitators). This is the most-exposed distractor pair on the slides; memorize the System 1 / System 2 mapping.
- B wrong: reverses fading and justification on the System 1/2 axis — fading is during, justification can be during *or* post-hoc, and fading is NOT post-hoc.
- C wrong: they are not synonyms; the whole module hinges on distinguishing them.
- D wrong: both are individual cognitive mechanisms; the org-vs-individual cut is fading/justification (individual) vs. outcome-focused culture (organizational), not within fading vs. justification.

### Q8. **C** — Ingroup / Loyalty: putting outside loyalties (the competitor's recruiting event) above the needs of one's own team / employer. Slide canonical violation parallel: "publicly bet against your hometown team."
- A wrong: Care/Harm is the "kick a dog in the head" example — direct harm to the weak/vulnerable. Reputational damage to a corporation is not the slide's Care frame.
- B wrong: Fairness/Reciprocity is the discriminatory-hiring example; trap because "draws a paycheck while badmouthing" *sounds* like reciprocity, but the slide categorizes employer/team disloyalty as *Loyalty*.
- D wrong: Authority/Respect is the "curse your parents" example; bashing a *peer organization* you work for is not about superior-subordinate authority structure.

### Q9. **B** — Forecasting errors are forward-looking over-confidence driven by *should-self* dominance at the prediction stage (we predict we'll confront the harasser; we don't). Memory revisionism is backward-looking distortion at the recollection stage (we still see ourselves as ethical, assume "anyone would have done the same," don't code the prior decision as ethics-relevant). Together they bookend the prediction → decision → recollection arc.
- A wrong: reverses the temporal direction of each.
- C wrong: neither is decision-stage; that's where ethical fading and visceral responses live.
- D wrong: both are individual cognitive biases, not organizational mechanisms.

### Q10. **B** — Williams' slide 21 antidote: **transparency**, **checks and balances**, **process focus (not just outcomes)**, and **dissent encouraged rather than suppressed**. J&J 1982 ("First, how do we protect the people?") is the canonical positive case; Enron is the counter-example.
- A wrong: training, punishment, and ethics officers are compliance-style answers; Williams explicitly frames *culture*, not policy or training, as the antidote (synergistic corruption at Enron shows formal codes are insufficient).
- C wrong: outcome-based incentives are exactly what *creates* outcome-focused culture, the third enabling mechanism — this option is the disease, not the cure.
- D wrong: those are the three sources of ethical *frameworks* (Q2 of Session 1), plus "the law" as a non-source distractor — not the four antidote ingredients.
