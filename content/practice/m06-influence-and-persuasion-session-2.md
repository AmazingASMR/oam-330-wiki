# Practice — Module 6 Influence & Persuasion (Session 2)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 10 without referencing the concept pages. Session 2 deliberately re-drills the high-leverage levers Session 1 underweighted: argument dilution, advocacy effect, push vs. pull, primacy vs. recency, individual identifiability, and the ethical-uses transparency test. Cialdini scenarios are FRESH — none of Session 1's DAV / appointment-card / Tupperware stems repeat. Sibling-distractor traps are the dominant error mode; read for the *mechanism*, not the surface tactic.

## Questions

### Q1. Match the definition: "Adding weak arguments to a strong case actually makes the overall pitch LESS persuasive — make your strongest arguments, not all of them." Which Module 6 concept is this?

A. The inoculation effect
B. The argument dilution effect
C. The peripheral-route trade-off
D. The recency effect

### Q2. Match the definition: "The tendency for a person who advocates for a position to develop more positive private attitudes toward it — selective search for pro-side reasons plus the consistency motive ('if I said it, I must believe it')." Which Module 6 concept is this?

A. Commitment & consistency (audience-side)
B. Central-route persuasion
C. Advocacy effect
D. Self-fulfilling prophecy

### Q3. A junior consultant is brought into a client meeting and the senior partner introduces her: "Priya led the analytics workstream on three Fortune 50 supply-chain redesigns last year — she'll walk you through the model." Compliance with her recommendations rises noticeably compared with a meeting where she introduces herself. Which Cialdini principle is the dominant lever, and through what System 1 bias does it operate?

A. Liking — operating through similarity-based affect.
B. Authority — operating through a halo-style deference cue, surfaced via a third-party introducer (so it doesn't sound arrogant).
C. Social proof — operating through peer modeling.
D. Commitment & consistency — the partner's introduction commits Priya publicly.

### Q4. A new VP joins a struggling regional office. Before announcing any changes, she spends her first month learning each direct report's name, asking about their kids, sitting in on team lunches, and praising specific past wins. Three months later, when she rolls out a difficult restructuring, employee buy-in is markedly higher than at a peer office where the new VP started by announcing changes on day one. Which Cialdini principle BEST explains the lift, and what is its key tactical lesson?

A. Reciprocity — she gave attention first, so employees felt obligated to comply with restructuring.
B. Authority — her VP title carried halo expertise weight.
C. Liking — she built ongoing rapport through similarity, attention, and praise *before* she needed it; liking built reactively under pressure reads as instrumental.
D. Commitment & consistency — listening to employees committed her to their views.

### Q5. A SaaS sales team sends prospects a one-page case study saying: "Of the 14 mid-market firms in your industry that adopted our platform last quarter, 12 renewed at year one — peers who looked very much like you." Conversion rates rise. Which Cialdini principle is at work, and what specifically makes it work here rather than merely "popular = good"?

A. Authority — the case study reads as expert endorsement.
B. Scarcity — only 14 firms could adopt last quarter.
C. Social proof — and **similarity** (peers in the prospect's own industry) is what powers it; generic popularity claims would underperform.
D. Reciprocity — the free case study triggers an obligation to convert.

### Q6. A nonprofit testing two donor-acquisition emails finds Email A — which describes 2.3 million children facing food insecurity in the Sahel and cites World Bank statistics — raises modest gifts. Email B — which tells the story of Amadou, a named 6-year-old boy in Mali, with his photo — more than doubles average donations. Which Module 6 concept BEST explains Email B's lift, and which sibling concept is the most likely distractor here?

A. Social proof; sibling = liking. Many donors signal others should give.
B. Individual identifiability; sibling = social proof. Identifiability uses a single named person's face/story (System 1 sympathy bypasses statistic-numbing); social proof would need a peer-group cue.
C. Negative advertising; sibling = scarcity. Email B contrasts Amadou's hardship with the donor's safety.
D. Authority; sibling = central-route data. Email A's statistics carry expert weight.

### Q7. A product manager is the lone voice on a 7-person steering committee arguing that the launch date should slip 6 weeks for a security audit. The other 6 want to ship on schedule. Which set of tactics best fits the slide's prescription for persuading from a minority position?

A. PUSH harder — assert the security risk loudly, propose a vote immediately, and use her positional incentives to pressure dissenters.
B. PULL — find allies on the periphery, ask for a **private** vote (not public), frame the dissent as attacking the issue not the people, stay hyper-rational, and build common ground until she has a majority before switching to push.
C. PUSH on the issue but PULL on the relationships — request a public vote to lock in any wavering members early via consistency.
D. Stay quiet until the launch fails, then invoke hindsight to consolidate authority for the next decision.

### Q8. In a **live** Kidney Case-style serial-advocacy round where five teams each present back-to-back to the same panel and the panel votes immediately after the last presentation, which order effect should a strategist expect to dominate, and what is the slide's exact framing?

A. Primacy — the first team anchors all subsequent judgments; "first impressions are decisive."
B. Recency — "the last shall be first" in the rankings; the most recent advocate is best remembered (figure skating, *American Idol*, Kidney Case classroom exercise).
C. Neither — order has no measurable effect once content quality is held constant.
D. Anchoring — the first dollar figure mentioned by any team becomes the reference point for the panel.

### Q9. Cialdini and the slide give a single operational test for whether a peripheral-route tactic is being used **ethically** or is sliding into manipulation. Which option states the test correctly AND gives the right reason it works?

A. "Would a regulator approve?" — because legality is the binding constraint on persuasion.
B. "Would your audience be upset if they knew about your use of peripheral-route persuasion techniques?" — because peripheral cues should *amplify* a case the central route would already accept; if disclosing them would anger the audience, you're distracting System 2 from a weak case rather than supplementing a strong one.
C. "Is the tactic central or peripheral?" — peripheral tactics are inherently unethical; central-route persuasion is the only ethical channel.
D. "Did the tactic work?" — short-run effectiveness is the right measure; long-run reputation is irrelevant to the ethics call.

### Q10. Both **commitment & consistency** (Cialdini, audience-side) and the **advocacy effect** (speaker-side) are powered by a "consistency motive," but they target DIFFERENT people and DIFFERENT outcomes. Which option correctly distinguishes them?

A. Commitment & consistency targets the speaker's attitude; the advocacy effect targets the audience's future behavior.
B. Commitment & consistency targets the **audience's future behavior** after they take a public stance (e.g., patients writing their own appointment cards); the advocacy effect targets the **advocate's own private attitude** about the substance, shifting toward the position they argued (e.g., Kidney Case teams becoming more genuinely convinced of the candidate they were assigned).
C. They are synonyms — both label the same effect at different stages of the persuasion cycle.
D. Commitment & consistency is central-route (System 2 reasoning), while the advocacy effect is peripheral-route (System 1 cue).

---

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Argument dilution effect: weak ancillary arguments water down a strong case. The slide's prescription is "make your strongest arguments, not all your arguments."
- A wrong: Inoculation is the *audience*-side effect — forewarning the audience of your persuasion intent lets them pre-build counterarguments. It's a separate central-route concept. (Easy sibling-distractor swap.)
- C wrong: There's no "peripheral-route trade-off" concept by that label; argument dilution is a **central-route** strategy concern, not peripheral.
- D wrong: Recency is an order/positioning effect (when you go), not a content effect (what you say).

### Q2. **C** — Advocacy effect: arguing for a position pulls your *own* private attitude toward that position via (1) selective positive-argument search during prep and (2) the consistency motive.
- A wrong: Cialdini's commitment & consistency targets the *audience's future behavior* after they take a public stance — not the advocate's private attitude about the substance itself. Same underlying drive (consistency), different target.
- B wrong: Central-route persuasion describes how the *audience* is persuaded by argument quality. The advocacy effect operates on the *speaker*, often before any external commitment.
- D wrong: SFP is a Module 3 perception phenomenon — expectations change the *target's* behavior. Wrong module and wrong target.

### Q3. **B** — Authority, surfaced via a third-party introducer. The slide explicitly recommends having someone *else* mention your credentials so it doesn't sound arrogant; the underlying System 1 mechanism is halo-style deference to expertise cues.
- A wrong: Liking is similarity/praise/attractiveness — the partner isn't building affective similarity, he's surfacing credentials. Different shortcut.
- C wrong: Social proof is *horizontal* (peers like me are doing X). The partner is signaling expertise *vertically* — that's authority, not peer modeling.
- D wrong: Commitment & consistency requires Priya herself to take a public stance that constrains her future behavior; an introduction doesn't commit her, it credentials her.

### Q4. **C** — Liking: ongoing similarity, attention, and praise *built before they were needed*. The slide's tactical lesson is that liking built reactively under pressure reads as instrumental and fails — invest in relationships before you need to draw on them.
- A wrong: Reciprocity binds you to repay a *discrete gift* — attention given over time is rapport-building, not a reciprocity transaction. Liking and reciprocity are sibling distractors here; the mechanism is ongoing affect, not a felt debt.
- B wrong: Authority is the title and credentials channel — the VP didn't lead with title; she led with relationship-building. The lift comes from rapport, not deference.
- D wrong: Listening doesn't constitute a public commitment that binds her future behavior — that's not the consistency mechanism. Consistency would require her to have publicly staked out a position the audience now holds her to.

### Q5. **C** — Social proof, and crucially **similarity** (peers in the prospect's own industry) is what powers it. The slide's repeated point: similarity is what makes social proof work — generic popularity claims (e.g., "thousands of customers love us") are weaker than peer-similar evidence ("firms like yours, in your industry").
- A wrong: There's no expert credential cue here — the persuasive content is *peer behavior*, not authoritative endorsement. Authority is vertical; this is horizontal.
- B wrong: Scarcity would require a rarity / loss-framed access cue. The case study isn't framing the platform as scarce; it's framing it as **chosen by similar peers** — opposite mechanism (consensus vs. exclusivity).
- D wrong: A free case study isn't a personalized gift triggering felt debt — that's reciprocity's mechanism, and it's not what's at work here.

### Q6. **B** — Individual identifiability, with social proof as the most likely sibling distractor. The Save the Children "Rokia" finding is the slide anchor: a single named person's story more than doubles donations vs. aggregate statistics, because System 1 emotional engagement bypasses the numbing effect of large numbers.
- A wrong: Social proof would require a *peer-group* cue (other donors giving). Email B doesn't show that — it shows one named child. Mistaking this for social proof is exactly the sibling trap.
- C wrong: Negative advertising attacks an opposing option; Email B is positive sympathy for one named person, not contrast against an opponent. Different mechanism.
- D wrong: Aggregate statistics in Email A do *not* persuade better — they numb. The slide's whole point is that scope-conveying statistics underperform single-person stories.

### Q7. **B** — PULL strategy: look for allies on the periphery, ask for a **private** vote (public votes lock people in via commitment & consistency, which hurts you when you're the minority), frame dissent as attacking the issue not the people, stay hyper-rational, and only switch to push once you've achieved a majority.
- A wrong: Pushing harder when you're outnumbered is the slide's named anti-pattern — push works when you have the numbers, not when you don't. Public votes amplify majority commitment, harming the minority. Direction reversed on multiple axes.
- C wrong: Public votes hurt the minority (they harden the majority via commitment & consistency, the same mechanism that makes them useful when you *have* the majority). The rule is **private when in the minority, public when in the majority** — this option reverses it.
- D wrong: Hindsight isn't a Module 6 minority-influence tactic; it's a Module 3 bias. Staying silent and waiting for failure abandons the dissenter's role entirely.

### Q8. **B** — Recency dominates in **live** serial competition: the last advocate is best remembered when judgment immediately follows. The slide's exact phrasing — "the last shall be first" — is anchored to figure skating, *American Idol*, and the Kidney Case classroom exercise.
- A wrong: Primacy dominates when judgment is *delayed* (so the first impression has time to anchor) or in written/asynchronous settings. In live serial competition with immediate voting, recency wins. Direction reversed — this is the canonical primacy/recency sibling swap.
- C wrong: The slide explicitly stakes out an order effect; "no effect" is the null distractor.
- D wrong: Anchoring is a Module 3 numeric-estimation bias — wrong module. The first *number* anchors numeric estimates; the first *speaker* doesn't anchor judgment in a live serial round (recency does).

### Q9. **B** — The transparency test: "Would your audience be upset if they knew about your use of peripheral-route persuasion techniques?" The reason: peripheral cues are ethically defendable when they *supplement* a case System 2 would already accept; they are dubious when they *distract* System 2 from a case it would reject. Audience anger on disclosure is the diagnostic that you're in the second mode.
- A wrong: Legality is necessary but not sufficient — many legal tactics (manufactured scarcity, fabricated testimonials) fail the transparency test. The slide's test is audience-upset, not regulator-approval.
- C wrong: Peripheral tactics are NOT inherently unethical — surfacing real credentials, true scarcity, genuine similarity all amplify legitimate cases. The slide explicitly distinguishes defendable from dubious peripheral use.
- D wrong: Cialdini's strategic argument is the opposite — short-run effectiveness is exactly what manipulation buys, but it destroys the trust persuasion depends on long-run. Effectiveness alone is not the ethics test.

### Q10. **B** — Different *targets*: commitment & consistency targets the **audience's future behavior** after a public stance (patients writing the appointment card → 18% drop in no-shows); advocacy effect targets the **advocate's own private attitude** about the substance (Kidney Case teams becoming more genuinely convinced of their assigned candidate). Same consistency drive, different person, different outcome.
- A wrong: It reverses the targets — commitment & consistency is audience-side; advocacy effect is speaker-side. The canonical sibling swap.
- C wrong: They are *not* synonyms. The slide separates them as two distinct effects of the same underlying motive operating on different parties.
- D wrong: Both are peripheral-route / System 1 phenomena (consistency is a peripheral lever; advocacy effect operates via nonconscious selective search and consistency drive). Neither is central-route.
