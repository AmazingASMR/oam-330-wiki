# Practice — Module 11 Ethics (Session 1)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 10 without reference. Score yourself.

## Questions

### Q1. Which of the following BEST captures the course definition of "ethics"?
A. The codified rules and laws that determine which behaviors are punishable.
B. The guidelines and principles we use to evaluate whether actions are right or wrong.
C. The personal preferences and tastes that vary from individual to individual.
D. The set of social norms describing how most people in a culture actually behave.

### Q2. Per the slides, the three sources from which people derive their ethical frameworks are:
A. Faith traditions, moral foundations, and professional guidelines.
B. Faith traditions, the law, and personal taste.
C. Moral foundations, outcome focus, and social norms.
D. Professional guidelines, faith traditions, and the law.

### Q3. According to the slides, U.S. companies lose roughly how much annually to employee misconduct?
A. $5 billion
B. $50 million
C. $50 billion
D. $500 billion

### Q4. A Ford engineer working on the Pinto runs the cost-benefit numbers and concludes lawsuits are cheaper than the $11/car fuel-tank fix. He never frames the choice as a life-or-death moral question — it just looks like a routine business calculation. Which decision-stage mechanism BEST describes this?
A. Visceral response — a hot bodily drive overrode his deliberation.
B. Ethical fading — the ethical dimension dropped out of awareness as the choice was reframed in cost-benefit terms.
C. Moral justification — he rationalized the harm because he felt unfairly treated.
D. Memory revisionism — he later rewrote the decision to preserve his self-image.

### Q5. Before her job interview, Maya predicts she will absolutely refuse to answer inappropriate personal questions. In the actual interview — anxious, eager for the offer, blindsided — she answers them all. Which mechanism BEST explains the gap between her prediction and her behavior?
A. Memory revisionism at the recollection stage.
B. Moral inaction at the decision stage.
C. A behavioral forecasting error: her "should self" dominated the prediction and ignored the in-the-moment "want" pressures.
D. Pluralistic ignorance about interview norms.

### Q6. A TSA officer who feels chronically underpaid begins pocketing items from passengers' luggage, telling himself, "They ain't paying me. They're treating me wrong." A different employee at the same airport steals office supplies because "everyone on my shift does it — it's basically expected." Which two mechanisms are at work, in order?
A. Both are moral justification (via inequity).
B. Moral justification (via inequity) for the first; social norms for the second.
C. Social norms for the first; moral inaction for the second.
D. Moral inaction for the first; outcome-focused culture for the second.

### Q7. Wells Fargo's "Eight is great" cross-sell quotas, daily account-opening targets, and public shaming of bankers who missed numbers — leading to millions of fraudulent accounts — is the canonical example of which enabling mechanism?
A. Pluralistic ignorance.
B. Outcome-focused culture: leadership pushed results so hard that *how* the numbers were achieved became invisible.
C. Moral inaction: bankers failed to report colleagues.
D. Visceral response: bankers were too fatigued to deliberate.

### Q8. Which pairing correctly contrasts ethical fading with moral justification?
A. Fading is post-hoc rationalization (System 2); justification is in-the-moment perception (System 1).
B. Fading is *not seeing* the ethical issue at decision time (System 1); justification is acknowledging the issue and *explaining it away*, often after the fact (System 2).
C. Fading happens at the prediction stage; justification happens at the decision stage.
D. Fading is organizational; justification is individual.

### Q9. Which Moral Foundation is violated by an executive who secretly commits to hiring only people of his own race and religion?
A. Care / Harm.
B. Authority / Respect.
C. Fairness / Reciprocity.
D. Ingroup / Loyalty.

### Q10. An auditor walks past a colleague's desk, sees clearly falsified expense reports, recognizes the behavior as wrong, but says nothing — feeling powerless to influence the situation. Which mechanism BEST describes the auditor's behavior, and which mechanism is the closest *wrong* answer?
A. Moral inaction; closest wrong answer is moral justification (which would require the auditor herself to commit *and* rationalize the act).
B. Moral justification; closest wrong answer is moral inaction.
C. Ethical fading; closest wrong answer is visceral response.
D. Memory revisionism; closest wrong answer is forecasting error.

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Slide 7 / ethics-definition concept page: ethics = guidelines/principles for evaluating right vs. wrong.
- A wrong: laws codify *some* ethical principles only imperfectly; ethics is broader (legal-but-unethical Ford Pinto example).
- C wrong: ethics is not personal taste — it is evaluative guidelines (societal/cultural/professional).
- D wrong: that defines social norms (what is *common*); ethics prescribes what is *right* — conflating the two is itself a key cause of unethical behavior.

### Q2. **A** — Faith traditions, moral foundations, professional guidelines (ethical-frameworks-sources).
- B wrong: "the law" is not one of the three sources, and "personal taste" was never on the slide.
- C wrong: outcome focus and social norms are *enabling mechanisms* of misconduct (Lecture 27), not framework sources.
- D wrong: drops moral foundations and adds the law (not a source).

### Q3. **C** — $50 billion annually (employee-misconduct-types).
- A, B, D wrong: distractor magnitudes; the slide-anchored figure is $50B.

### Q4. **B** — Ethical fading: the cost-benefit reframing made the ethical dimension non-salient (canonical Pinto example, ethical-fading-visceral concept page).
- A wrong: no hot bodily drive (hunger/fear/anger) is described — the engineer is doing calm spreadsheet work.
- C wrong: justification = acknowledged-then-explained-away; here ethics never enters awareness in the first place. Inequity is also absent.
- D wrong: revisionism is at the *recollection* stage; this is decision-time.

### Q5. **C** — Forecasting error at the prediction stage; "should self" dominated and ignored the visceral "want" pressures she would actually face (mirrors the 62%/68% → 0% sexual-harassment study).
- A wrong: revisionism is backward-looking distortion of past behavior; the gap here is prediction-to-action, not action-to-memory.
- B wrong: moral inaction is failing to confront *someone else's* unethical act; Maya is the actor, not a bystander.
- D wrong: pluralistic ignorance is misperceiving peers' norms; not relevant — the question is about her own forecast vs. her own behavior.

### Q6. **B** — Inequity ("they ain't paying me") = moral justification facilitator; "everyone does it" = social norms.
- A wrong: the second case has no inequity cue and an explicit peer-behavior cue, which is the textbook social-norms signal.
- C wrong: flips the two; also moral inaction requires a bystander, not an active thief.
- D wrong: outcome-focused culture is about leadership-driven goal pressure, not peer behavior; moral inaction is bystander failure, not active theft.

### Q7. **B** — Outcome-focused culture: ends-justify-means structure built by leadership through goals + incentives (canonical Wells Fargo case).
- A wrong: pluralistic ignorance is about *misperceiving* peer norms — not a quota-driven incentive structure.
- C wrong: inaction is *part* of why it persisted, but the question asks about the cultural mechanism *creating* the misconduct.
- D wrong: no hot bodily drive described; this is a structural, not visceral, story.

### Q8. **B** — Fading = System 1 perceptual failure (ethics never enters awareness); justification = System 2 rationalization that acknowledges the issue and explains it away, often post-hoc.
- A wrong: reverses the System 1 / System 2 mapping.
- C wrong: both happen around the decision; fading is decision-stage and justification is decision/post-hoc — neither is prediction-stage.
- D wrong: both can be individual cognitive events; the System 1 vs. System 2 distinction is what the concept page emphasizes.

### Q9. **C** — Fairness / Reciprocity: discriminatory hiring violates the rule that the rules should be fair for everyone (slide-listed canonical violation).
- A wrong: Care/Harm is "kick a dog in the head" — direct harm to the weak/vulnerable.
- B wrong: Authority/Respect is the "curse your parents" example.
- D wrong: Loyalty is the "publicly bet against your hometown" example. Common trap: discrimination *feels* like a loyalty/ingroup issue but the slide labels it Fairness.

### Q10. **A** — Moral inaction (recognized wrong + powerlessness driver → failure to report); the closest wrong answer is moral justification, which would require the auditor herself to commit and rationalize the act.
- B wrong: justification is for the *actor's own* misconduct, not the bystander's silence.
- C wrong: ethics is clearly perceived ("recognizes the behavior as wrong"), so fading is excluded; no visceral hot state.
- D wrong: this is decision-time bystander silence, not a recollection-stage memory distortion.
