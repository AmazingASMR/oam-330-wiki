### Q1. [Module: M2 Empirical] A New York Times piece headlines: *"Children read aloud to and given limited screen time read for fun more often."* The journalist treats this as evidence that limiting screens *causes* recreational reading. The single most important methodological objection is:
A. The sample is not representative of US children, so generalizability is limited.
B. The reported correlation could equally reflect reverse causation, a third variable (e.g., engaged parents), or chance — only random assignment supports a causal claim.
C. The study lacks adequate control variables for socioeconomic status, biasing the correlation upward.
D. The effect size, while statistically significant, is too small to act on.

### Q2. [Module: M3 Decision Making] An Asian-disease-style task: a country expects 600 deaths. Plan A: 200 saved for sure. Plan B: 1/3 chance all 600 saved, 2/3 chance no one saved. The same decision is later re-described as "Plan A: 400 will die for sure" vs. "Plan B: 1/3 chance no one dies, 2/3 chance 600 die." Subjects flip from preferring A (lives-saved frame) to preferring B (deaths frame). What is the rule and the underlying mechanism?
A. Risk-averse for losses, risk-seeking for gains; the mechanism is anchoring on the "200" number across both frames.
B. Risk-averse for gains, risk-seeking for losses; the mechanism is that System 1 reacts to the verbal cast (gain vs. loss), not to the equivalent expected values.
C. Risk-averse in both frames; the apparent flip is an artifact of small samples and reverses with N > 1000.
D. Risk-seeking in both frames; subjects always prefer the gamble when probabilities are explicit.

### Q6. [Module: M6 Influence] A volunteer coordinator wants more reliable Saturday turnout. ### Q7. [Module: M6 Influence] A hotel posting one of these signs on bath towels:

- Sign C: "Join the guests who stayed in **this room** in helping save the environment — 75% of past guests in *this room* reused towels."

D. Scarcity — towel reuse seems rare, and C frames it as exclusive to that room.

### Q11. [Module: M9 Negotiation] In a used-car negotiation, the buyer opens at $4,000 and the seller at $7,000. ### Q16. [Module: M12 Happiness] A senior accepts a $130K analyst job over a $95K nonprofit role she finds intrinsically interesting, expecting to be much happier with the higher salary. D. Outcome focus — the firm's culture suppressed her positive affect.

### Q17. [Module: Cross-cut — System 1/System 2 + Ethics + Decision biases (M3 + M11)] A pharmaceutical engineer running a cost-benefit analysis on a known fuel-tank-style safety defect concludes the lawsuit settlements would cost less than a $11/unit fix. He recommends *against* the fix. He is not a sociopath; he genuinely never registered the recommendation as a *moral* choice — only as a financial one. Distinguish what is happening here from two near-neighbors and identify the underlying Kahneman system involved.
A. This is **moral justification** (System 2 post-hoc rationalization); it differs from ethical fading because the engineer constructed an excuse, and from memory revisionism because it is forward-looking.
B. This is **ethical fading** (a System 1 decision-stage failure: the ethical frame drops out of awareness as the cost-benefit lens takes over); it differs from **moral justification** (which requires acknowledging the ethical issue and explaining it away) and from **memory revisionism** (a recollection-stage error after the fact). Outcome-focused culture is the structural condition that makes fading at scale more likely.
C. This is **ethical forecasting error** — the should-self over-predicted ethical action; the gap between forecast and actual decision is what produced the harm.
D. This is **want-self capture** powered specifically by visceral hot-state drives like fear or hunger; the cost-benefit reframe is irrelevant.

The mismatch is **individualism vs. collectivism** mapped onto **contract-vs-relationship as the goal** (collectivist contexts treat the relationship as primary and expect renegotiation when conditions change); per the Negotiation slides, **integrative > distributive globally** because integrative travels better and preserves relationships; on fairness, the *desire for fair process* is universal cross-culturally even though the *allocation principle* (merit/equality/need) varies — and a partner who refuses to revisit when conditions shift can read as procedurally unfair in collectivist framings.
C. The mismatch is **uncertainty avoidance** — Japan tolerates more ambiguity; the lesson is to keep contracts vague; the fairness angle is irrelevant.
D. There is no cultural mismatch — fixed contracts mean the same thing everywhere; the Japanese side is simply trying to extract concessions.

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — The slide-canonical lesson on correlation: three alternative explanations (chance, reverse direction, third variable) compete with causation, and only random assignment in a true experiment licenses the causal claim. The reading-aloud / screen-time / reading-for-fun example is straight from slide 25.
- A wrong: generalizability is a *sampling* concern; the journalist's error is causal inference, not reach. Sister-error swap (random sampling vs. random assignment).
- C wrong: control variables can adjust for *known* confounds, but unknown third variables remain — and even good controls don't license causation from a correlational design.
- D wrong: effect size is a separate concern; the headline error is treating association as causation regardless of size.

### Q2. **B** — Kahneman & Tversky: gain frame → risk-averse (sure thing); loss frame → risk-seeking (gamble). Same mathematical outcome, different verbal cast. Mechanism is System 1 reacting to framing rather than expected value.
- A wrong: reverses the direction (the most common framing-bias trap distractor).
- C wrong: the effect is replicated and robust; not a small-sample artifact.
- D wrong: subjects are *not* uniformly risk-seeking — risk preference flips with frame, that's the whole point.

### Q3. **B** — Availability heuristic: vivid plane-crash imagery is easily recalled, so flying *feels* high-risk; passive recall, not active search. The 2,170 / 2,606 numbers are slide-canonical (Module 3).
- A wrong: confirmation bias requires active info-seeking to support a prior belief; this is passive recall of vivid imagery (sister-bias swap).
- C wrong: sunk cost is escalation based on past investment; not a risk-frequency error.
- D wrong: halo generalizes across attributes for the *same target*; this is frequency-misjudgment from ease of recall.

### Q4. **C** — Conscientiousness is the one direct, across-the-board predictor of job performance; E and A predict only contingently (interpersonal-contact jobs). Slide-canonical OCEAN-performance frame.
- A wrong: Extraversion is contingent — it pays off in sales/management but not in trucking.
- B wrong: Openness predicts creativity/adaptation but has minimal direct job-performance correlation overall.
- D wrong: Agreeableness predicts liking and lower deviance, but performance only in teamwork roles.

### Q5. **B** — Cross-cultural fairness slides: collectivist allocation leans equality/need; ingroup-solidarity work counts as input; pure individual-merit public ranking violates both at once.
- A wrong: there is no slide claim that valence-of-money differs by culture — the issue is allocation principle and what counts as input.
- C wrong: legal-prohibition framing is fabricated; Williams's argument is normative/cultural, not regulatory.
- D wrong: reverses the slide's headline — the *desire for fair process* is **universal**; only the *allocation principle* varies.

### Q6. **C** — Active + public + voluntary > active + public > active > passive default. Cioffi & Garner students; patient-filled appointment cards (18% drop in misses). Slide-canonical ladder.
- A wrong: reverses the ladder; passive defaults are the *weakest* commitments (sister-error swap).
- B wrong: public vs. private commitment matters substantially — it is the entire point of the principle.
- D wrong: coerced commitments produce *backlash*, not stronger compliance — the voluntary qualifier is essential.

### Q7. **B** — Social proof works through *similarity*; tightening the reference group ("this room") increases perceived peer-likeness, which is the active ingredient. Slide-canonical hotel-towel scenario.
- A wrong: there's no expert/credential cue in the message — it's about peer behavior, not authority.
- C wrong: reciprocity requires a prior gift creating obligation; the sign delivers no gift.
- D wrong: scarcity emphasizes few-can-have-it; the sign emphasizes *many* are doing it (consensus, opposite signal).

### Q8. **B** — The power paradox: same trait set (mirroring, attentiveness) is both the on-ramp and what gets shut off; Obhi's TMS work and Keltner's research show it happens to good people, not just bad. Eight months is short of the sustained-power-plus-no-constraints threshold for the clinical hubris label.
- A wrong: hubris syndrome is the *clinical* disorder requiring sustained power and minimal constraint; power paradox is the general psychological pattern that shows up even after brief priming.
- C wrong: stereotype threat is performance loss under stereotype salience; the stem is about behavioral change *because* of acquired power, not stereotype awareness.
- D wrong: ethical fading is decision-stage; the stem is about chronic perceptual/empathic change, not a single decision's moral framing.

### Q9. **B** — The slide isolates *concealment* cost as distinct from stereotype threat. Stereotype threat = identity *salience*; concealment cost = identity *suppression*. Both hurt cognitive performance but via opposite manipulations.
- A wrong: stereotype threat would arise from being *reminded* of his stereotype, not from actively hiding identity.
- C wrong: stereotype tax is the *career* cost paid when one acts *against* an in-group stereotype (e.g., assertive woman penalized) — different actor, different mechanism.
- D wrong: the structure was inclusive in form; the cost arose from his own concealment behavior, not overt devaluation.

### Q10. **C** — Integration-and-learning / "better ideas" paradigm: heterogeneity expands hypothesis space; documented trade-off is more conflict and longer time to a solution short-term, better solutions long-term (the J-curve logic).
- A wrong: D&F is the *moral/legal* case (avoid harm/comply with disparate-impact); the stem is about idea quality, not fairness.
- B wrong: A&L is about market-mirroring credibility (J.C. Penney bilingual staff for Latino neighborhoods); the stem is internal problem-solving, not market access.
- D wrong: the slide's whole point is that the trade-off *exists* — heterogeneous teams take longer in the short run; pretending no trade-off exists misreads the J-curve.

### Q11. **C** — Slide-canonical used-car example: positions ($4K vs. $7K) hide interests (reliability vs. fair price + willingness to warranty). Adding a second issue (warranty) the parties value unequally is the integrative engine — and the position-vs-interest move is what surfaces it.
- A wrong: this is *not* compromise/splitting — they signed at $6K with a *new* issue (warranty) added, expanding the pie beyond price.
- B wrong: $6K is below her $6K cap, not above; and there's no slide framing of fairness override here — Malhotra/Shapiro is the fairness-override case, and it goes the *other* direction (above-BATNA accept that "feels unfair").
- D wrong: anchoring is real in price negotiations but doesn't capture what made the deal close — *adding the warranty issue* did.

### Q12. **C** — High cohesion + low performance norms = the worst-case cell; cohesion amplifies whatever the norm is, including a low-effort norm. Hawthorne bank-wiring room is the canonical example.
- A wrong: low cohesion + low norms produces moderate-low output, not the worst case; cohesion is high here.
- B wrong: high cohesion + high norms is the *best* cell; output is mediocre, so norms are not high.
- D wrong: cohesion is *not* irrelevant — the 2×2 explicitly shows it amplifies the norm in either direction.

### Q13. **B** — Project Aristotle / Duhigg: equal turn-taking and high average social sensitivity (Reading the Mind in the Eyes) are the two operational behaviors that produce psychological safety. Edmondson's 1999 construct.
- A wrong: the headline finding was that *who* was on the team mattered far less than the team's norms — IQ and shared background did *not* predict performance.
- C wrong: cohesion plus social time looks productive but isn't the Aristotle finding; cohesion alone can amplify low norms (see Q12). Distinguish from psychological safety.
- D wrong: clear hierarchy was not what predicted high performance; the dominant lever was norm-based safety to speak up.

### Q14. **B** — Moral justification facilitated by **anonymity of the victim** (slide-canonical). No identifiable target → less moral concern; the joking is rationalization layered on the anonymity-driven justification.
- A wrong: moral inaction is failing to confront *someone else's* misconduct — these traders are the active actors, not bystanders.
- C wrong: the slide places the Enron CA case under justification/anonymity, not norms/pluralistic-ignorance; "we thought everyone did it" is the norms framing.
- D wrong: outcome focus is the *cultural condition* (#3) — present at Enron broadly, but the *primary* slide-mapped mechanism for the Texas-vs-California case is justification/anonymity (slide explicitly uses the Enron-CA-blackout example here).

### Q15. **C** — Outcome-focused culture (ends-justify-means built by aggressive goals/incentives); antidote is the slide-canonical *culture of ethics* — transparency, checks & balances, process AND outcomes, dissent encouraged. Wells Fargo and Ford Pinto are the two paradigmatic outcome-focus cases.
- A wrong: moral inaction is bystander-failure; the bankers were active actors. Hotlines alone are not the slide's antidote (which is structural/cultural, not procedural).
- B wrong: norms/pluralistic-ignorance is mechanism #2; Wells Fargo's headline cause is the outcome-focused goal/incentive structure leadership built. Training videos are not the slide's antidote.
- D wrong: ethical fading is the individual-cognitive event; outcome focus is the *cultural* condition that *makes fading happen at scale*. Slide-named antidote is culture of ethics, not criminal penalties.

### Q16. **B** — Three forces operate together: hedonic adaptation (set point reasserts), social comparison (her referent group earns more too — the $50K-in-$25K-neighborhood vs. $100K-in-$200K-neighborhood logic), and the predicted-vs-actual gap (we forecast money will move happiness more than it does). Caring about money also predicts *less* happiness.
- A wrong: Easterlin is the *macro* finding (GDP up, happiness flat); the stem is about an individual-level prediction failure, which is the broader money-happiness mechanism. Easterlin alone is incomplete.
- C wrong: circumstances do shift happiness *temporarily*; the genetic set point limits *durable* shifts. "Never" overstates.
- D wrong: outcome focus is an Ethics-module construct about misconduct, not happiness suppression.

### Q17. **B** — Ethical fading is a System 1 decision-stage failure; the ethical frame drops out of awareness because a non-moral framing (cost-benefit) takes over. Differs from moral justification (which acknowledges the ethical issue and rationalizes it) and from memory revisionism (post-decision recollection-stage). Outcome-focused culture is the structural aggravator.
- A wrong: justification is *post-hoc rationalizing of an acknowledged ethical choice*; the stem says the engineer "never registered" the moral dimension at all — that's fading, not justification. Justification is more System 2 than System 1.
- C wrong: forecasting errors are *prediction-stage* over-promises ("I would confront the harasser" — 62%/0%); the stem is decision-stage, not prediction-stage.
- D wrong: visceral hot-state drives are bodily (hunger, fear, sexual arousal); a cool cost-benefit reframe is the opposite mechanism — the cognitive (fading) sibling, not the visceral one.

### Q18. **B** — Triple synthesis: (1) cultural mismatch is **I-C mapped onto contract-vs-relationship as the goal of negotiation** — collectivist contexts treat the relationship as primary and expect renegotiation when conditions change (slide-canonical Australia–Japan sugar example); (2) negotiation lesson — slides explicitly state **integrative > distributive globally** because integrative travels better and preserves relationships; (3) fairness angle — across cultures the *desire for fair process* is universal, while the allocation principle varies, and a refusal to revisit when conditions shift can read as procedurally unfair in collectivist framings. Procedural justice is also Williams's strongest performance predictor (deck 08, slide 16).
- A wrong: power distance is about hierarchy/deference (KAL 801 territory), not contract-vs-relationship; "defer to senior counterparty" misreads the integrative lesson.
- C wrong: the issue is not ambiguity tolerance; uncertainty avoidance doesn't map cleanly to renegotiation expectations. "Keep contracts vague" is not slide-supported advice.
- D wrong: the slide is explicitly that contract-vs-relationship treatment varies cross-culturally — denying the mismatch is the wrong-answer trap.
