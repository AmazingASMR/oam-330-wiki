# Practice — Module 10 Groups & Teams (Session 1)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 10 without referencing the concept pages. Score yourself and use the "why each distractor is wrong" notes to surface fuzzy thinking.

## Questions

### Q1. Per Robbins & Judge, which of the following best meets the OB definition of a "group"?

A. Forty employees who happen to ride the same elevator to work each morning.
B. A six-person airline flight crew assigned by the carrier to operate a scheduled flight.
C. A LinkedIn group of 12,000 alumni who share an industry but never interact.
D. A crowd of shoppers waiting in line at a Wal-Mart checkout.

### Q2. Which definition correctly distinguishes role *perception* from role *expectations*?

A. Role perception is what others believe you ought to do; role expectations is your own view of how you should act.
B. Role perception is your own view of how you should act; role expectations is how others believe you should behave.
C. Role perception is the legal contract you signed; role expectations are the unwritten norms.
D. Role perception applies only to formal groups; role expectations apply only to informal ones.

### Q3. According to the punctuated equilibrium model, when does the "transition" that triggers major changes in a temporary task group occur?

A. After the first meeting, once direction has been set.
B. At roughly the midpoint between the first meeting and the official deadline, regardless of total project length.
C. Only in projects longer than six months.
D. Immediately before the final deliverable is due, when time pressure peaks.

### Q4. A Samsung product team developing the Galaxy Note 7 is described internally as a "cannot fail" group with a strong shared identity. Engineers who privately doubt the battery design stay silent in meetings because they sense any criticism will be treated as disloyalty, and the group ships an unsafe product. Which concept BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Groupshift
B. Social loafing
C. Groupthink
D. Common-knowledge effect

### Q5. A six-person committee individually rates a slate of candidates as "moderately risky" before discussion. After an hour of group debate, the committee unanimously endorses the riskiest candidate on the list — a position more aggressive than any single member's pre-meeting rating. Which concept BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Groupthink
B. Groupshift (group polarization)
C. Social loafing
D. Production blocking

### Q6. The Grogan Air case shows that when each of five panelists holds unique private information about candidates, the group spends most of its discussion repeating facts everyone already knew and ends up choosing the wrong VP roughly 75% of the time. Which concept BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Groupthink
B. Common-knowledge effect
C. Groupshift
D. Social loafing

### Q7. A small biotech startup competes in a young, fast-moving industry where product specs change weekly. Decisions need to be made by whoever has the relevant expertise, and job descriptions stay deliberately fluid. Per the slides, which structural model best fits this firm?

A. Mechanistic — strict departmentalization, top-down decisions, formalized roles.
B. Organic — few clear departments, decisions made at many levels, fluid job requirements.
C. Tall hierarchy with a narrow span of control to maximize supervision.
D. A bureaucracy modeled on Wal-Mart, since cost minimization dominates.

### Q8. Williams's lecture compares two failure modes of group work. *Concept X* is when individual members exert less effort because their contribution is hidden in the collective output. *Concept Y* is when, in a group decision, members defer to whatever the group is leaning toward and stop offering their own private analysis. Which pairing of X and Y is correct?

A. X = groupthink; Y = social loafing
B. X = social loafing; Y = free-riding-in-decisions / conformity in group decision making
C. X = groupshift; Y = production blocking
D. X = common-knowledge effect; Y = groupthink

### Q9. A product manager wants the largest possible *number* of distinct ideas for a new feature. Per Robbins & Judge's evaluation grid, which decision-making format should she use?

A. A traditional interacting group meeting.
B. A classic verbal brainstorming session, because withholding criticism unleashes creativity.
C. The nominal group technique — silent independent generation, then structured sharing and ranking.
D. A single expert working alone, because groups are always less creative than individuals.

### Q10. A workgroup at a manufacturing plant has very HIGH cohesion, but its agreed-upon performance norm is to restrict output to keep management from raising quotas (the Hawthorne bank wiring room pattern). Per Exhibit 9-4, which productivity outcome should you predict?

A. High productivity, because high cohesion always raises productivity.
B. Moderate productivity, because cohesion and norms cancel each other out.
C. Low productivity, because high cohesion combined with LOW performance norms is the worst case for the organization.
D. Unpredictable productivity — the model says cohesion has no effect once norms are accounted for.

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — A six-person airline flight crew is R&J's canonical example of a formal group: interaction, interdependence, and a shared objective.
- A wrong: Elevator riders share proximity but lack interdependence and a shared objective — that's an aggregate, not a group.
- C wrong: A LinkedIn alumni list with no interaction fails the "interacting" criterion; mere category membership isn't a group.
- D wrong: A checkout queue is a collection in proximity; no shared objective binds the shoppers and they aren't interdependent.

### Q2. **B** — Role perception is your own view of how you should act in a role; role expectations is how others believe you should behave (the psychological contract is the unwritten employer–employee version of expectations).
- A wrong: It reverses the two terms — perception is internal (yours), expectations are external (others').
- C wrong: The legal contract isn't role perception; the unwritten employer–employee version is the psychological contract, a *type* of role expectation.
- D wrong: The perception/expectations distinction applies to all roles, not just formal or informal ones.

### Q3. **B** — R&J: the transition hits at roughly the midpoint between first meeting and deadline, whether the project lasts an hour or six months.
- A wrong: The first-meeting *direction-setting* is step 1; the transition is step 3, at the midpoint.
- C wrong: The model is duration-agnostic — the midpoint trigger occurs regardless of total project length.
- D wrong: Final-meeting acceleration (step 6) is the burst of activity at completion, not the midpoint transition.

### Q4. **C** — Groupthink: consensus pressure suppresses dissent, especially when there is a strong group identity and a positive image to protect ("cannot fail"). The mode is artificial agreement, not extremity.
- A wrong: Groupshift is amplified original position toward extremes; the engineers here aren't being pushed to a more extreme stance, they're being silenced.
- B wrong: Social loafing is effort reduction in collective work, not appraisal failure or suppression of dissent.
- D wrong: Common-knowledge effect is about over-discussing shared info and under-discussing private info; here engineers HOLD private info but are pressured into silence by conformity, which is groupthink's hallmark.

### Q5. **B** — Groupshift / polarization: discussion pushes the group toward a more extreme version of the position members already held; the final stance is more extreme than any single member's pre-meeting rating.
- A wrong: Groupthink suppresses dissent toward an artificial consensus; here the issue is amplification of an existing leaning, not silencing of dissent.
- C wrong: Social loafing is effort reduction in collective tasks, unrelated to risk-taking shifts.
- D wrong: Production blocking is a brainstorming-specific cognitive interference (people talking over each other), not a polarization phenomenon.

### Q6. **B** — Common-knowledge effect: groups over-discuss shared info and under-discuss privately held info, so they fail to leverage their pooled knowledge — exactly the Grogan Air pattern.
- A wrong: Groupthink is suppression of dissent; in Grogan Air no one is actively silencing minority views, the unique info simply never gets aired.
- C wrong: Groupshift is polarization toward extremes, not failure to share private information.
- D wrong: Social loafing is effort reduction; here members are participating, just selectively repeating already-shared facts.

### Q7. **B** — Organic model: fluid roles, distributed decisions, few clear departments — fits innovation strategies, startups, and uncertain young-industry environments.
- A wrong: Mechanistic structures fit cost-minimization and stable / routine technologies (Wal-Mart, railroads), not fast-changing innovation contexts.
- C wrong: A narrow span / tall hierarchy is associated with mechanistic models and slows the rapid, expertise-driven decisions a young biotech needs.
- D wrong: Wal-Mart's cost-minimization bureaucracy is the textbook mechanistic case — the opposite of what this startup needs.

### Q8. **B** — Social loafing is effort reduction in a collective context; the group-decision-making analogue (deferring to the group's leaning instead of contributing private analysis) is conformity / free-riding in group decisions, distinct from loafing on physical effort.
- A wrong: Reverses the constructs — groupthink is conformity-driven *suppression of dissent*, not effort reduction in collective work.
- C wrong: Groupshift is polarization toward extremes (not effort reduction); production blocking is brainstorming interference.
- D wrong: Common-knowledge effect is failure to share private information; it's not about effort reduction either.

### Q9. **C** — Per Exhibit 9-5, nominal group technique scores HIGH on number/quality of ideas; brainstorming is only moderate and interacting groups are low.
- A wrong: Interacting groups score LOW on idea quantity/quality due to conformity and dominance.
- B wrong: Classic brainstorming underperforms NGT because of production blocking, evaluation apprehension, and free-riding — the popular wisdom is wrong.
- D wrong: R&J's grid compares group formats; the claim that individuals are always more creative than groups overstates the finding (groups beat individuals on creativity overall; the question is which *group format* generates the most ideas).

### Q10. **C** — High cohesion + LOW performance norms is the worst-case cell of the 2×2: the bonded group enforces low output (Hawthorne bank wiring room).
- A wrong: Cohesion alone does NOT predict productivity; its effect is moderated by performance norms.
- B wrong: The 2×2 doesn't show cancellation — high-cohesion/low-norm is specifically the worst quadrant, not a neutral one.
- D wrong: Cohesion still has effects — it AMPLIFIES whatever the performance norm is (high or low). The model isn't a no-effect claim.
