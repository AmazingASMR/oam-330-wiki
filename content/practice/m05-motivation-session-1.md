# Practice — Module 5 Motivation (Session 1)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 10 without referencing the concept pages. Score yourself and use the "why each distractor is wrong" notes to surface fuzzy thinking.

## Questions

### Q1. Per Williams's lecture, which of the following BEST defines *procedural justice*?

A. The perceived fairness of the actual outcomes an employee receives (pay, promotion, bonus size).
B. The perceived fairness of the process by which decisions are made — voice, transparency, dignity, and consistent treatment across identities.
C. The comparison of one's own input/output ratio against a referent colleague's ratio.
D. The multiplicative chain linking effort to performance to reward to personal goals.

### Q2. According to Csikszentmihalyi, *flow* occurs at the intersection of which two conditions?

A. Low skill and low challenge.
B. High skill and low challenge.
C. Low skill and high challenge.
D. High skill and high challenge.

### Q3. Which statement best captures the lecture's claim about the *job satisfaction–job performance* relationship?

A. The two are essentially uncorrelated; happy workers are no more productive than unhappy ones.
B. The correlation is strong (~.80) — satisfaction nearly determines performance.
C. The correlation is medium (~.30) — real and replicable, but one input among many.
D. Satisfaction causes performance in a strict one-directional way; reverse causality has been ruled out.

### Q4. A regional manager at Mud Bay raises entry-level pay to comply with a new $15 minimum. Veteran employees, whose pay is now barely above the new entry rate, complain that the differential they earned over years has vanished — even though no one's pay was cut. Which framework BEST explains the veterans' reaction?

A. Expectancy theory — the effort-to-performance link has weakened.
B. Equity theory — employees evaluate rewards relative to a referent's input/output ratio, not in absolute terms.
C. Flow theory — the new pay structure has reduced challenge below the skill level.
D. The progress principle — the veterans are no longer making visible daily progress.

### Q5. A consulting firm announces large performance bonuses for a team tackling an open-ended creative-strategy problem. Two months later, the team is producing more conventional, lower-quality ideas than the (unincentivized) team next door. Per Glucksberg's candle-problem result and Pink's analysis, which concept BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Procedural-justice failure — the bonus criteria were opaque.
B. Wage compression — the bonuses violated equity comparisons.
C. Contingent extrinsic rewards corrupting performance on a creative/heuristic task (the Sawyer Effect / "if-then" backfire).
D. Apathy — low skill paired with low challenge.

### Q6. A university fundraising office runs an experiment. Group A's callers receive a 10-minute pep talk from their manager about the importance of donations. Group B's callers spend 10 minutes meeting a student scholarship recipient who describes how the aid changed her life. Over the next several weeks, Group B raises substantially more money than Group A. Which concept BEST labels this result?

A. Procedural justice — Group B perceived a fairer process.
B. Meaningful work / end-user contact — visible impact on real people drives motivation more than abstract exhortation.
C. Expectancy — Group B perceived a stronger effort-to-performance link.
D. Distributive justice — Group B received a more favorable outcome.

### Q7. An engineer says: "I'm willing to work hard, and if I work hard I'll perform well. The bonuses my company pays for great performance are real and large. But honestly — I have plenty of money already; another bonus doesn't move me." Per expectancy theory, which link is weak?

A. Expectancy (effort → performance).
B. Instrumentality (performance → reward).
C. Valence (reward → personal goals).
D. Distributive justice (outcome fairness).

### Q8. Which statement BEST contrasts equity theory with expectancy theory?

A. Equity theory is forward-looking and probabilistic; expectancy theory is backward-looking and comparative.
B. Equity theory is about a sideways comparison of one's input/output ratio to a referent's ratio; expectancy theory is about a forward, multiplicative chain from effort to performance to reward to personal goals.
C. Equity theory applies to algorithmic tasks; expectancy theory applies to heuristic/creative tasks.
D. Equity theory is about process fairness; expectancy theory is about outcome fairness.

### Q9. Williams flags one finding about organizational fairness as counterintuitive and high-yield: which is it?

A. Distributive justice is the strongest predictor of job performance, because employees ultimately care most about what they get.
B. Procedural justice is the strongest predictor of job performance — *how* decisions are made matters more than *what* is decided.
C. Procedural and distributive justice are essentially redundant — measuring one is enough.
D. In high-performing firms, fairness perceptions become irrelevant once compensation is competitive.

### Q10. A multinational allocates a year-end team bonus. The U.S. office leans toward giving the largest share to the highest-performing analyst (merit). The Tokyo office leans toward splitting the bonus more evenly so as not to disrupt team harmony, while a manager in a third office argues a larger share should go to a colleague whose family is dealing with a serious illness. Per the lecture's cross-cultural framework, which trio of allocation principles is being applied?

A. Distributive / procedural / interactional justice.
B. Expectancy / instrumentality / valence.
C. Equity (merit) / equality / need.
D. Autonomy / mastery / purpose.

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Procedural justice = process fairness: voice, transparency, dignity, consistent cross-identity treatment.
- A wrong: That's *distributive* justice (outcome fairness), the easy swap distractor.
- C wrong: That's the *equity-theory* mechanism (input/output ratio comparison) — related to distributive judgments but not the procedural definition.
- D wrong: That's the structure of *expectancy theory* (E × I × V), an entirely different framework.

### Q2. **D** — Flow is high skill + high challenge; the other three quadrants are apathy (low/low), boredom (high/low), and anxiety (low/high).
- A wrong: Low skill + low challenge = apathy.
- B wrong: High skill + low challenge = boredom.
- C wrong: Low skill + high challenge = anxiety.

### Q3. **C** — The slide-canonical effect size is "medium" (~.30): real but moderate, with reverse causality and third-variable explanations both viable. Satisfaction also predicts OCB and lower turnover/absenteeism/deviance.
- A wrong: There IS a relationship — the lecture explicitly rejects the "no link" framing.
- B wrong: ~.80 overstates a moderate effect; many other things drive performance (ability, resources, role clarity, luck).
- D wrong: Reverse causality (performance → satisfaction) is explicitly listed as a candidate explanation, not ruled out.

### Q4. **B** — Equity theory (Adams): employees evaluate rewards *relative* to referents, not in absolute terms. Wage compression collapses the veteran-vs-newcomer ratio comparison even though no veteran's pay fell — the WSJ "Push for $15" canonical case.
- A wrong: Expectancy is forward-looking effort → reward probability; the veterans aren't complaining that effort no longer leads to reward, they're complaining about peer comparison.
- C wrong: Flow is task-immersion (skill × challenge), unrelated to compensation comparison.
- D wrong: Progress principle (Amabile & Kramer) is about daily wins on meaningful tasks, not pay differentials.

### Q5. **C** — Glucksberg's candle problem and Pink's "if-then" rewards: contingent extrinsic incentives narrow focus and undermine creative/heuristic performance, sometimes turning intrinsically interesting work into a chore (Sawyer Effect).
- A wrong: The stem doesn't describe an opaque process; it describes a creative-task incentive backfire.
- B wrong: Wage compression is the equity issue from the $15 case (veterans vs. newcomers), not at issue here.
- D wrong: Apathy requires *low* skill and *low* challenge; consultants on a creative-strategy problem are at high challenge.

### Q6. **B** — Meaningful work / end-user contact: brief direct exposure to a beneficiary outperforms abstract managerial exhortation. This is the slide-6 (deck 09) canonical fundraising-callers result and maps to Pink's *purpose* leg.
- A wrong: Both groups had identical processes; nothing in the stem manipulates voice, transparency, or dignity.
- C wrong: Expectancy concerns the effort-to-performance link; the manipulation here is about *why the work matters*, not whether effort produces results.
- D wrong: Distributive justice is about outcomes received; no differential outcome is described.

### Q7. **C** — Valence is the personal value of the reward to *this* employee. If another bonus has low utility ("I have plenty of money already"), valence is low. Motivation = E × I × V, so a near-zero valence kills motivation even when E and I are strong.
- A wrong: The engineer explicitly says "if I work hard I'll perform well" — expectancy is high.
- B wrong: He acknowledges the bonuses are real and large — the performance → reward link is intact.
- D wrong: Distributive justice belongs to the equity/justice family, not to expectancy theory's three links.

### Q8. **B** — Equity theory: sideways ratio comparison to a referent (Adams). Expectancy theory: forward, multiplicative E × I × V chain (Vroom). The "comparison vs. probability chain" contrast is the core distinction the lecture stresses.
- A wrong: It REVERSES the directions — equity is the comparison/backward-or-sideways one; expectancy is the forward probabilistic one.
- C wrong: The algorithmic vs. heuristic split belongs to the financial-incentives-tradeoffs concept (Pink), not to the equity/expectancy contrast.
- D wrong: The process/outcome split is distributive vs. procedural justice, not equity vs. expectancy.

### Q9. **B** — Slide 16 (deck 08): procedural justice predicts job performance more strongly than distributive justice. Counterintuitive because managers tend to focus on outcomes, but the data say process dominates for behavior toward the org.
- A wrong: Reverses the finding — distributive is the *weaker* performance predictor (it predicts feelings about outcomes, not behavior toward the org).
- C wrong: They're empirically distinct; the lecture treats them as separate constructs with different correlates.
- D wrong: Pay-above-market reduces salience of pay but doesn't eliminate fairness as a driver — fairness perceptions still predict performance.

### Q10. **C** — The lecture's three cross-cultural allocation principles are **merit (equity)**, **equality**, and **need**. Equity dominates in individualist contexts (US); collectivist contexts may emphasize equality or need, and may credit ingroup-solidarity work as legitimate input.
- A wrong: That trio is a justice-types taxonomy (interactional adds to distributive/procedural), not the cross-cultural allocation triad.
- B wrong: That's the three components of *expectancy theory*, not allocation principles.
- D wrong: That's Pink's intrinsic-motivation triad (Motivation 3.0), not a fairness allocation framework.
