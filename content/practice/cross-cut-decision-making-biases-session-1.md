# Practice — Cross-cut: Decision-making biases (Session 1)

## Instructions
These questions test the cross-module synthesis the cumulative final is most likely to ask. Try without reference; score; re-read the cross-cut page on misses.

## Questions

### Q1. [Modules: M3, M11] Which pair correctly states the *distinguishing feature* of two sister biases?
A. Availability vs. confirmation — availability is active info-search; confirmation is passive recall.
B. Anchoring vs. framing — anchoring shifts a numeric starting point; framing flips risk preference depending on gain/loss verbal cast.
C. Overconfidence vs. hindsight — overconfidence is backward ("I knew it"); hindsight is forward ("I'll predict right").
D. Halo vs. self-fulfilling prophecy — halo changes the target's behavior; SFP is a perceiver-side judgment error.

### Q2. [Modules: M6, M3] A salesperson opens with "people in your industry are *all* switching to our platform — you don't want to be the last one in." A second salesperson says "we have only **3** units left at this price." Identify the Cialdini lever for each and the underlying System-1 bias each exploits.
A. Both are scarcity exploiting loss aversion.
B. First = social proof (exploiting availability/similarity heuristics); second = scarcity (exploiting loss aversion, the framing-bias mechanism).
C. First = authority (halo); second = commitment (sunk cost).
D. First = scarcity; second = social proof — Cialdini levers are interchangeable.

### Q3. [Modules: M11, M3] Bazerman & Tenbrunsel: 62% of women predict they'd confront a sexual harasser, but in the actual situation 0% refused to answer questions. Which Module-3 bias family is this prediction failure most directly an instance of?
A. Hindsight bias — they revise their prior beliefs after the fact.
B. Availability heuristic — confronting harassers is easy to imagine.
C. Overconfidence (specifically planning-fallacy logic) applied to the moral self — the "should self" dominates forward forecasts and ignores in-the-moment "want" pressures.
D. Anchoring bias — the question anchors them to a high confront-rate.

### Q4. [Modules: M3, M11] An executive who mishandled a product recall now insists "the data were clear from day one — anyone could have predicted this." Six months later, asked how *she* handled it, she remembers raising concerns she never raised. The two biases at play (in order) are best identified as:
A. Overconfidence then sunk cost.
B. Hindsight bias (the outcome restructures recalled predictability) then ethical memory revisionism (recollection-stage hindsight applied to one's own ethical history).
C. Confirmation bias then framing.
D. Halo effect then availability heuristic.

### Q5. [Modules: M6, M3] Which Cialdini-to-bias mapping is correct for *all four* pairs?
A. Authority↔halo; scarcity↔loss aversion (framing mechanism); commitment-consistency↔sunk-cost-style commitment; social proof↔availability/similarity heuristics.
B. Authority↔availability; scarcity↔overconfidence; commitment-consistency↔halo; social proof↔hindsight.
C. All four Cialdini principles exploit confirmation bias.
D. Authority↔central route; scarcity↔central route; commitment↔peripheral; social proof↔peripheral.

### Q6. [Modules: M3, M11] A manager has just learned about hindsight bias in a training. Three months later she still rates last quarter's failed product launch as "obviously doomed from the start" despite her awareness of the bias. Which debiasing claim is most consistent with Gilbert and Williams?
A. Awareness alone is sufficient — she must not have paid attention in training.
B. She should try harder to engage System 2 in the moment of judgment.
C. Awareness is necessary but insufficient (the brain cannot see itself fooling itself); the reliable fix is structural — broaden samples of evidence, present information in multiple ways, minimize ego stakes, or avoid bias-producing situations entirely.
D. Hindsight bias cannot be reduced under any circumstances.

---

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Anchoring vs. framing is correctly stated; both are "numeric biases" but mechanisms differ.
- A wrong: reverses passive/active (availability is passive recall; confirmation is active seeking).
- C wrong: reverses direction (overconfidence is forward; hindsight is backward).
- D wrong: reverses perceiver-side vs. target-behavior (halo = perceiver; SFP = target).

### Q2. **B** — Cross-cut explicit mapping: social proof exploits availability/similarity heuristics; scarcity exploits loss aversion (the framing-bias mechanism).
- A wrong: only second is scarcity.
- C wrong: misidentifies both levers.
- D wrong: levers are not interchangeable.

### Q3. **C** — Cross-cut explicit: ethical forecasting errors are overconfidence/planning-fallacy logic applied to moral self-prediction.
- A wrong: hindsight is backward; this is forward miscalibration.
- B wrong: availability is evidence-recall; not the mechanism here.
- D wrong: anchoring requires a numeric reference; not present.

### Q4. **B** — First sentence = textbook hindsight bias; second sentence = memory revisionism, the recollection-stage application of hindsight to one's own ethical history.
- A wrong: overconfidence is forward; sunk cost is past spend.
- C wrong: confirmation is active info-search; framing is gain/loss wording.
- D wrong: halo and availability don't fit either utterance.

### Q5. **A** — Cross-cut explicitly maps each Cialdini lever to its System-1 bias.
- B wrong: scrambled mapping.
- C wrong: confirmation bias is not the universal mechanism.
- D wrong: false — all six Cialdini principles are peripheral, not central.

### Q6. **C** — Gilbert quote + the five slide-listed structural fixes; awareness alone is insufficient.
- A wrong: contradicts Gilbert.
- B wrong: willpower in-the-moment is the wrong mode.
- D wrong: too strong; structural fixes do reduce hindsight.
