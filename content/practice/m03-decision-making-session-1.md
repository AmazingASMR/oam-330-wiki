# Practice — Module 3 Decision Making (Session 1)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 12 without referencing the concept pages. This module is bias-heavy; the distractors are deliberately the *sister* biases. Score yourself and use the per-distractor "why wrong" notes to surface fuzzy thinking.

## Questions

### Q1. Which of the following best matches Kahneman's definition of **System 1**?

A. The slow, effortful, prefrontal-cortex process that executes deliberate calculations and learns new skills.
B. The fast, automatic, limbic process that produces snap judgments and is not under conscious control.
C. A sequential cognitive stage that activates only after System 2 has finished its analysis.
D. A computer-like algorithmic module that runs structured rule-based reasoning.

### Q2. Per the slides, which statement about reducing decision biases is correct?

A. Awareness of a bias is sufficient to eliminate it, because conscious attention overrides automatic processing.
B. Per Gilbert, the brain cannot see itself fooling itself, so the most reliable fix is to **avoid the situations that produce the bias**.
C. Biases are random rather than systematic, so structural debiasing is unreliable.
D. The recommended fix is to make decisions faster, since slow deliberation amplifies framing effects.

### Q3. Per the Freakonomics episode, the **planning fallacy** is best classified as:

A. A standalone bias unrelated to overconfidence.
B. A sub-effect of overconfidence, in which forecasts are based on best-case scenarios for *this specific* project rather than the average of past similar projects.
C. A version of anchoring bias, where the project deadline anchors the estimate.
D. A version of hindsight bias, where past project overruns reshape memory.

### Q4. After the September 11 attacks, many Americans switched from flying to driving. The slides cite **2,170 additional fatal car accidents** in the months that followed — nearly matching the **2,606 deaths** at the Twin Towers. People judged flying as more dangerous than driving because vivid plane-crash coverage came easily to mind. Which bias BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Confirmation bias
B. Availability heuristic
C. Anchoring bias
D. Hindsight bias

### Q5. A hiring manager is told an applicant is "introverted" before the interview and instructed to "get to know" her. He asks questions like *"In what situations do you wish you could be more outgoing?"* He never asks questions that would reveal extraverted behavior. After the interview he reports that the applicant is, indeed, introverted. Which bias BEST labels the manager's behavior?

A. Availability heuristic
B. Confirmation bias
C. Halo effect
D. Self-fulfilling prophecy

### Q6. A manager who has decided a junior analyst is "not leadership material" gives him only routine, low-visibility work, rarely speaks to him in meetings, and signals visible disinterest in his ideas. Over six months the analyst becomes withdrawn and disengaged. The manager points to this behavior as proof of his original assessment. Which bias BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Halo effect
B. Confirmation bias
C. Self-fulfilling prophecy
D. Hindsight bias

### Q7. In a slide demo, students were asked whether Gandhi died at more or less than **114** years old, or more or less than **35** years old, then to estimate his actual age at death (78). The 114 group's mean estimate was **79.4 years**; the 35 group's mean was **70.7 years** — a roughly 9-year shift driven by the irrelevant starting number. Which bias does this illustrate?

A. Framing effect
B. Anchoring bias
C. Availability heuristic
D. Sunk cost fallacy

### Q8. A program director continues funding a struggling pilot program because, in her words, "we've already poured two years and $400,000 into it — we can't walk away now." Her own analysis shows the next dollar of investment is very unlikely to produce a positive return. Which bias BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Overconfidence (planning fallacy)
B. Anchoring bias
C. Sunk cost fallacy
D. Confirmation bias

### Q9. In Kahneman & Tversky's **Asian disease problem** (confirmed in slides), the "lives saved" frame led **81%** of subjects to pick the sure-thing plan, while the mathematically identical "deaths" frame led **76%** to pick the gamble. What general rule does this illustrate?

A. Gain frame → risk-seeking; loss frame → risk-averse.
B. Gain frame → risk-averse; loss frame → risk-seeking.
C. Frame has no effect when expected values are equal; only anchors do.
D. Subjects always prefer the sure thing, regardless of frame.

### Q10. Two related biases both depend on outcome knowledge but operate differently. **Bias X** is forward-looking — people are wildly miscalibrated when predicting their own future performance, and Dunning-Kruger shows novices are *more* confident than experts. **Bias Y** is backward-looking — once people know an outcome, they reconstruct their memory of prior beliefs to match it (the slides' Playboy/Time circulation demo: 32% chose Playboy when the answer was hidden, but **66%** "knew" Playboy was right when shown the answer in boldface). Which pairing of X and Y is correct?

A. X = hindsight bias; Y = overconfidence
B. X = overconfidence; Y = hindsight bias
C. X = halo effect; Y = self-fulfilling prophecy
D. X = anchoring bias; Y = framing effect

### Q11. A board reviews two CEOs whose actual day-to-day behavior is nearly identical. The board describes the CEO whose firm's stock soared as *"flexible, methodical, and decisive,"* and the CEO whose firm's stock crashed as *"confused, rigid, and authoritarian."* Kahneman calls this *"exaggerated emotional coherence."* Which bias BEST labels this dynamic?

A. Self-fulfilling prophecy
B. Hindsight bias
C. Halo effect
D. Confirmation bias

### Q12. Both **anchoring** and **framing** are slide-bucketed under "biases in numeric decision making," and both involve how information is presented. Which statement BEST captures their distinction?

A. Anchoring shifts the *magnitude* of a numeric estimate via an initial reference number; framing flips *risk preference* depending on whether the same outcome is cast as a gain or a loss.
B. Anchoring is backward-looking (past spend); framing is forward-looking (future projections).
C. Anchoring is interpersonal (it changes others' behavior); framing is purely internal.
D. Anchoring affects only experts; framing affects only novices.

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — System 1 is the fast, automatic, limbic (amygdala) process producing snap judgments and is *not* under conscious control. Slides explicitly contrast it with the conscious, slow, effortful prefrontal System 2.
- A wrong: That description is **System 2**, not System 1 — the question stem asked for System 1.
- C wrong: The two systems run **simultaneously**, not sequentially. The slides explicitly flag "they run sequentially" as a classic distractor.
- D wrong: The slides explicitly **reject** the "humans = computers running algorithms" framing. We are partly animal/reactive, partly deliberate — System 1 is *not* an algorithm module.

### Q2. **B** — The Gilbert (2006) quote is the slide's headline: *"Because the brain cannot see itself fooling itself, the only reliable method for avoiding bias is to avoid the situations that produce it."* Structural change beats willpower.
- A wrong: Awareness is **necessary but not sufficient**. The slides explicitly say studies show people aware of biases (e.g., hindsight) still fall for them — the "more aware" answer is the classic distractor.
- C wrong: Dobelli's whole frame is that thinking errors are **systematic, not random** — that's exactly why structural fixes work.
- D wrong: The slide-listed fix is "**allow enough time** to evaluate information thoroughly" — engaging System 2, not rushing into System 1.

### Q3. **B** — Per Buehler (and the Freakonomics episode), planning fallacy = predictions made on best-case scenarios, based on *this specific* task rather than the average of past tasks of this type. Slides put it under overconfidence.
- A wrong: It is *explicitly* a sub-effect of overconfidence in slide 5 of deck 05 — not standalone.
- C wrong: Anchoring is over-weighting an initial *number*; planning fallacy is best-case scenario thinking, a different mechanism.
- D wrong: Hindsight is backward-looking memory reconstruction. Planning fallacy is forward-looking forecasting — opposite temporal direction.

### Q4. **B** — Availability heuristic: judging frequency/risk by what comes easily to mind. Vivid 9/11 plane-crash imagery → flying *feels* more dangerous than its base rate; the resulting 2,170 driving deaths is the slides' canonical illustration.
- A wrong: Confirmation bias is *active* selective seeking of evidence to confirm a prior belief. Here people aren't searching out evidence — vivid memories are passively *popping* to mind. (Availability vs. confirmation = passive recall vs. active selection.)
- C wrong: Anchoring requires an initial *number* contaminating an estimate. There is no anchor here.
- D wrong: Hindsight bias is "I knew it all along" after an outcome is revealed. Drivers in 2001 were forecasting forward, not reconstructing past predictions.

### Q5. **B** — Confirmation bias: the manager *actively chooses* questions whose answers can only confirm the "introverted" label. This is the slide-cited interview study — the applicant predictably "confirms" the label because no disconfirming question is asked.
- A wrong: Availability is passive ease-of-recall — the manager is not retrieving vivid examples; he is *strategically choosing* questions, an active selection process.
- C wrong: Halo effect is generalizing one observed trait to *other* traits of the same person. Here the manager is reinforcing the *same* trait, not generalizing across traits.
- D wrong: Self-fulfilling prophecy requires the *target's actual behavior to change* in response to the manager's expectations. In this stem the applicant's behavior isn't being altered — only the manager's question selection. (SFP would apply if the manager's treatment caused her to act more introverted; this is filtered information-gathering instead.)

### Q6. **C** — Self-fulfilling prophecy: the manager's expectation changes how he *treats* the analyst (low-visibility work, visible disinterest) → the analyst's *actual behavior* deteriorates → the manager points to that real behavior change as confirmation. The behavior change is real, not illusory.
- A wrong: Halo is generalizing across *traits* of one target — "good at X → good at Y." It is purely perceiver-side; no behavior change in the target. Here the analyst's behavior actually changes.
- B wrong: Confirmation bias is one-sided info-*gathering* about a belief. Here the manager isn't merely searching selectively — he is *causing* the very evidence he then cites. SFP creates the evidence; confirmation bias merely filters it.
- D wrong: Hindsight bias is "I knew it all along" memory reconstruction *after* an outcome. Here the manager's prior expectation is producing the outcome going forward — different mechanism, opposite temporal direction.

### Q7. **B** — Anchoring bias: the irrelevant starting number (114 vs. 35) contaminates the subsequent estimate, shifting answers by ~9 years for the same target person. F(1,63) = 6.90, p = .01 in the slide demo.
- A wrong: Framing flips *risk preference* via gain/loss verbal cast (Asian disease, 81%/76%). There is no risk choice here — only a magnitude estimate.
- C wrong: Availability concerns evidence ease-of-recall (vivid examples). The anchoring demo gives subjects an explicit number, not a vivid memory.
- D wrong: Sunk cost is over-weighting *past investment*. Subjects haven't invested anything in this estimation task.

### Q8. **C** — Sunk cost fallacy, escalation-of-commitment flavor: the past $400K and two years are non-recoverable; the rational decision turns only on *future* costs and benefits. Dobelli's Concorde effect / Vietnam War extension are the canonical reading-side examples.
- A wrong: Overconfidence is forward-looking miscalibration — predicting future success too rosily. The director here is *not* claiming the next dollar will succeed; she explicitly says it likely won't. Her error is over-weighting past spend, not over-predicting future success.
- B wrong: Anchoring requires an initial *reference number* contaminating a numeric estimate. The $400K isn't anchoring her estimate of project value — she's using it as a justification not to abandon the project, which is sunk-cost reasoning.
- D wrong: Confirmation bias is selective evidence-seeking to confirm a prior belief. The director isn't filtering evidence — she explicitly acknowledges the analysis is unfavorable.

### Q9. **B** — Gain frame → risk-AVERSE (take the sure thing: 81% pick Plan A in lives-saved framing). Loss frame → risk-SEEKING (gamble to avoid the loss: 76% pick Plan B in deaths framing). Plans A and B are mathematically identical across the two frames; only the verbal cast changes.
- A wrong: This *reverses* the rule. Students often invert the direction — memorize: gains → averse, losses → seeking.
- C wrong: Frame demonstrably has a large effect (81% vs. 76% pick opposite plans for the *same* outcome) — that's the point of the demonstration.
- D wrong: Subjects don't always prefer the sure thing; in the loss frame 76% pick the gamble. Preference flips with frame.

### Q10. **B** — Overconfidence is forward-looking calibration failure (Dunning-Kruger: less skill → MORE overconfidence; planning fallacy; better-than-average effect). Hindsight bias is backward-looking memory reconstruction (Playboy 32% → 66% once the answer is shown). They reinforce each other but face opposite temporal directions.
- A wrong: Reverses the constructs. X is the *forward* bias, Y is the *backward* bias — the slides' "predict the future correctly" vs. "I knew it all along" framing.
- C wrong: Halo and SFP are perception/judgment biases about *other people*, not self-prediction or post-hoc memory. Different module bucket entirely.
- D wrong: Anchoring (numeric magnitude) and framing (gain/loss risk preference) are decision-stage "we're not computers" biases, not the ego-protective forward/backward pair.

### Q11. **C** — Halo effect: one observed cue (the stock outcome) colors all *other* judgments of unrelated traits. Kahneman's "exaggerated emotional coherence" wording is slide-anchored. The success/failure trait-flip on essentially identical CEO behavior is the diagnostic.
- A wrong: Self-fulfilling prophecy requires the *target's behavior to actually change* in response to expectations. Here the CEOs' behavior is held constant by stipulation; only the *board's labels* change.
- B wrong: Hindsight bias recolors the *predictability of the outcome* ("we knew the stock would crash"), not the *traits of the actor*. Halo and hindsight both depend on outcome knowledge but recolor different things — slides flag this confusion explicitly.
- D wrong: Confirmation bias is selective evidence-*seeking* to confirm a prior belief. Here the board isn't filtering its information search; it is generalizing across traits of one target — the halo signature.

### Q12. **A** — Anchoring shifts numeric *magnitude* via an initial reference number (Gandhi 114/35 → 79.4/70.7). Framing flips *risk preference* via gain/loss verbal cast (Asian disease 81% sure-thing in gains / 76% gamble in losses). Same slide bucket, different mechanism.
- B wrong: Past-spend is sunk cost; future projections describes overconfidence. Neither anchoring nor framing is defined by temporal direction.
- C wrong: Self-fulfilling prophecy is the interpersonal bias that changes a target's behavior. Anchoring and framing are both perceiver-side numeric biases.
- D wrong: Both biases affect novices and experts. Anchoring is "very difficult to avoid even when deliberately trying" per the slides — expertise does not protect from it.
