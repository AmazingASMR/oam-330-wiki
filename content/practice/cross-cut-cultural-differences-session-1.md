# Cross-cut: Cultural differences — practice questions
## Instructions
These questions test the cross-module synthesis the course most often asks across modules. Try without reference; score; re-read the cross-cut page on misses.

## Questions

### Q1. [Modules: M4, M7] Which statement most precisely describes the relationship between individualism–collectivism and power distance in Hofstede's framework?
A. They are two labels for the same underlying cultural dimension; high collectivism implies high power distance.
B. They are separate Hofstede dimensions that often correlate but measure different things — group orientation vs. acceptance of hierarchy.
C. Power distance is an individual-level personality trait; individualism–collectivism is the corresponding cultural dimension.
D. Individualism–collectivism describes Western cultures; power distance describes Eastern cultures.

### Q2. [Modules: M7, M9] A U.S. account director closes a fixed-price three-year supply contract with a Japanese counterpart. Eighteen months in, currency markets shift and the Japanese side requests a renegotiation; the U.S. side replies "we have a signed contract" and refuses. The Japanese counterpart goes silent and the relationship deteriorates. The cultural-dimension mismatch most directly responsible is:
A. High power distance on the Japanese side: subordinates won't dispute the captain.
B. A contract-vs.-relationship mismatch tied to individualism–collectivism: the U.S. side treats the paper agreement as the deal, the Japanese side treats the relationship as the deal and expects renegotiation when the climate changes.
C. Uncertainty avoidance: the Japanese side cannot tolerate the ambiguity of a fixed contract.
D. A KAL-801-style mitigated-speech failure on the U.S. side.

### Q3. [Modules: M7, M5] A multinational airline imports its U.S. cockpit-resource-management protocol — which encourages first officers to challenge captains directly — into its Seoul-based subsidiary. Crashes per departure remain elevated. A separate engagement survey shows Seoul employees, like U.S. employees, strongly want fair, transparent procedures and interesting autonomous work. The best read of these two findings together is:
A. The Seoul subsidiary's culture rejects all Western management practices wholesale, including fairness and autonomy.
B. Power distance is universal; fairness preferences are not.
C. Power distance varies across cultures (and produces the cockpit failure via mitigated speech), while desire for fair process and autonomy is a near-universal cross-cultural finding.
D. The Seoul subsidiary's high collectivism, not power distance, is causing the cockpit issue.

### Q4. [Modules: M4, M5] A Crunchers Inc. manager rolls out an individual-leaderboard sales contest in Beijing that worked well in NYC; sales decline. Separately, when bonuses are allocated, Beijing employees expect mentoring and team-harmony work to count as legitimate input. Both observations are best explained by:
A. Power distance: the Beijing employees won't challenge the leaderboard publicly.
B. Collectivism: group orientation suppresses standing-out behavior under the leaderboard and broadens what counts as a contribution worthy of reward.
C. Uncertainty avoidance: Beijing employees prefer fixed pay to variable bonuses.
D. The rice-wheat finding: Beijing is a wheat region, so it should look like NYC.

### Q5. [Modules: M4, M8] Which pair best contrasts the rice-vs-wheat finding (M4) with the culture-shock finding (M8)?
A. Rice-wheat says cultural values originate in crop labor demands within a country; culture shock says interpersonal conflict comes from small unrecognized rule differences, not large obvious ones.
B. Rice-wheat says East Asia is uniformly collectivist; culture shock says Westerners can never adapt.
C. Both are about climate-driven differences in personality across countries.
D. Rice-wheat is about between-country differences only; culture shock is about within-country differences only.

### Q6. [Modules: M6, M4] An NYC-trained sales rep gives an early personalized gift to a prospective client in a collectivist Beijing context, expecting Cialdini reciprocity to lift compliance. The client cools rather than warming. Which integrated explanation is best?
A. Reciprocity is a central-route argument and the client wanted peripheral cues instead.
B. Reciprocity is universally automatic and nonconscious, so the client must have missed the gift.
C. Reciprocity is a peripheral, System-1 lever in individualist contexts but in collectivist contexts can read as brown-nosing or bribery — the gift surfaces as instrumental rather than sincere.
D. The gift triggered a high-power-distance violation by skipping the boss.

---

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — PD and I-C are separate Hofstede dimensions; they correlate but measure different things.
- A wrong: the cross-cut explicitly warns against conflating them.
- C wrong: PD describes cultures, not individuals (slide 13, M7).
- D wrong: geographic mapping is not how Hofstede defines them.

### Q2. **B** — Contract-vs.-relationship is the textbook M9 mismatch (Australia → Japan sugar example), tied to I-C.
- A wrong: PD is about cockpit hierarchy, not contract renegotiation.
- C wrong: uncertainty avoidance is listed but not the active dimension here.
- D wrong: KAL 801 is a cockpit/PD scenario, not a contract dispute.

### Q3. **C** — High PD produces the mitigated-speech cockpit failure (KAL 801 family); fair process and autonomy are universal across cultures (M5 cross-cultural fairness slide).
- A wrong: overgeneralizes; only the PD-specific protocol fails.
- B wrong: reverses the universality direction.
- D wrong: collectivism is a different dimension; the cockpit case is a PD case.

### Q4. **B** — Collectivism explains both the leaderboard backfire (M4) and the broadened input definition under equity-theory fairness (M5).
- A wrong: PD is about hierarchy, not standing-out vs. group solidarity.
- C wrong: uncertainty avoidance is not the active mechanism.
- D wrong: Beijing is a wheat-region city, but the explanation is still I-C, and the leaderboard backfire holds; this distractor misuses Talhelm.

### Q5. **A** — Rice-wheat = origin story for values via labor demands; culture shock = small unrecognized norm mismatches drive conflict.
- B wrong: rice-wheat *defeats* the "Asia = collectivist" stereotype.
- C wrong: rice-wheat is specifically *not* climate-driven (slide isolates crop labor).
- D wrong: rice-wheat is within-country; culture shock can be either.

### Q6. **C** — Cross-cut explicitly flags reciprocity as backfire-prone in collectivist contexts (read as brown-nosing/bribery); reciprocity is peripheral, not central.
- A wrong: reciprocity is peripheral, not central; client didn't reject the route.
- B wrong: slide says reciprocity is "not always nonconscious — especially in collectivistic cultural contexts."
- D wrong: PD violation requires a hierarchy story; this is an I-C story.
