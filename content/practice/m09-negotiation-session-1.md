# Practice — Module 9 Negotiation (Session 1)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 10 without referencing the concept pages. Score yourself and use the "why each distractor is wrong" notes to surface fuzzy thinking.

## Questions

### Q1. Which of the following BEST matches the course definition of negotiation?

A. Changing someone's attitudes or behavior through influence techniques.
B. Deciding on the terms of an exchange — whether for goods/services, dividing a shared resource, or resolving a conflict.
C. Any disagreement between two or more parties about a substantive issue.
D. A formal written agreement between two organizations regarding pricing.

### Q2. Which option correctly defines BATNA?

A. The minimum in-deal price you would still accept before walking away.
B. The fair-market or "objective criteria" benchmark used to anchor offers.
C. Your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — the outside option you would actually pursue if this deal failed.
D. The most aggressive opening offer you can defensibly justify.

### Q3. Which option correctly defines a *reservation price*?

A. The aspirational target / best plausible outcome going into the negotiation.
B. The quantification of your BATNA into a single in-deal number — the worst terms in this deal you would still accept.
C. A deposit you put down to hold a deal while you continue to negotiate.
D. The midpoint of the zone of possible agreement (ZOPA).

### Q4. Shapiro is offered $8.5M for his 49% stake. He rejects the offer because it feels unfair next to a "fair" $10M number. His litigation BATNA, however, has an expected value of (0.7 × $10M) + (0.3 × $3M) − $0.5M = **$7.4M**. Per Malhotra, what mistake did Shapiro make?

A. He correctly rejected the offer because $8.5M is below his $10M fairness anchor.
B. Failure Mode A — he rejected a deal that beat his BATNA because it "felt unfair"; he should have accepted.
C. Failure Mode B — he accepted a deal worse than his BATNA because the concession sequence felt like winning.
D. He miscalculated his BATNA; with uncertain litigation outcomes, the correct rule is to use the best-case payoff, not the expected value.

### Q5. Olivia walks into a dealership with a reservation price of $31K (her BATNA is a comparable Car Y elsewhere). The salesperson lists at $34K, then concedes to $33K → $32,250 → $32,000. Olivia accepts at $32K, feeling she "won" $2K of concessions. Per Malhotra, what is going on?

A. Olivia made the rational choice — she captured value through concessions.
B. Failure Mode B (concession illusion): she accepted a deal $1K above her own reservation price; she should have walked to her BATNA.
C. Failure Mode A: she rejected a deal that beat her BATNA because it felt unfair.
D. This is a successful application of integrative bargaining via packaging.

### Q6. A Texoil rep and a station owner are stuck: the rep's reservation price is $500K (above this he'd build new), the owner's is $553K (below this he'd take BP's offer). Initial ZOPA = empty. They eventually close a deal by adding **post-trip employment for the sailing couple, fuel trade, health insurance, and ad placement on the sailboat**. Which concept does this BEST illustrate?

A. Distributive bargaining succeeds once the buyer makes a strong anchoring first offer.
B. Compromise — both parties met in the middle on price.
C. Integrative bargaining: parties trade across multiple issues they value unequally, expanding the pie and creating a ZOPA where none existed.
D. The concession illusion overrode the no-ZOPA constraint.

### Q7. Two siblings argue over a single orange. Each insists, "I want the orange." A parent suggests cutting it in half. Later it turns out one wanted only the **peel** (for baking) and the other wanted only the **juice**. Per Fisher & Ury, which lesson does this BEST illustrate?

A. Compromise (cutting it in half) is the optimal integrative outcome because it feels fair to both.
B. Positions ("I want the orange") often hide interests (peel vs. juice); focusing on interests rather than positions can serve both parties in full.
C. Distributive bargaining always beats integrative bargaining when the resource is indivisible.
D. The orange should go to whichever sibling has the stronger BATNA.

### Q8. An OAM 330 student claims: *"Integrative bargaining just means compromising fairly — you each give up something on the same issue and meet in the middle."* Per the slides, this claim is:

A. True — "win-win" is essentially a fair compromise on the central issue.
B. True for distributive negotiations and false for integrative ones.
C. False — compromise on a single issue can leave both parties worse off; integrative bargaining instead trades **across multiple issues** that the parties value unequally.
D. False — integrative bargaining means one party wins outright and the other concedes gracefully.

### Q9. A buyer is preparing for a **one-shot, single-issue** purchase from a vendor she will never see again. Which combination of in-the-room tactics is MOST consistent with the slides' "distributive success" toolkit?

A. Soft on people / hard on the problem; insist on objective criteria; invent options for mutual gain.
B. Make the first offer to anchor; reveal a deadline; keep most information to yourself; display anger strategically.
C. Open with all of her information; emphasize the long-term relationship; package multiple issues.
D. Suppress any anchor; let the seller speak first; defer to the seller's framing.

### Q10. An Australian sugar exporter signs a fixed-price multi-year contract with a Japanese buyer. Two years in, market conditions shift sharply, and the Japanese counterparts request to renegotiate; the Australian side insists the signed contract is binding and final. Per the slides on cultural differences, what is the BEST reading?

A. The Japanese side is acting in bad faith — a signed contract is universally the closing of the deal.
B. Cultural mismatch on the goal of negotiation: in more relationship-oriented (often collectivistic) contexts the **relationship** is primary and renegotiation as conditions change is expected, while the Australian side treats the **paper agreement** as the deal.
C. This is an example of the concession illusion — the Japanese side is using fake concessions to extract value.
D. The slides argue distributive bargaining travels better globally; the Australian rigid-contract stance is the integrative best practice.

---

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Slide definition: negotiation is deciding on the terms of an exchange, across three flavors (price, dividing a resource, resolving conflict).
- A wrong: That defines persuasion/influence (Cialdini), which overlaps with but is distinct from negotiation; negotiation always involves agreeing on terms of an exchange.
- C wrong: That defines conflict, not negotiation. Negotiation is one mechanism for resolving conflict; not all negotiations involve conflict.
- D wrong: A signed contract is an *output* of some negotiations, not the definition; many negotiations (resource division, conflict resolution) have no formal contract.

### Q2. **C** — BATNA = Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement: the real outside option you would pursue if this deal fell apart (e.g., backup job offer, secondhand store quote, litigation).
- A wrong: That defines the **reservation price** — the in-deal walk-away number, which is the *quantification* of BATNA, not BATNA itself. Classic distractor.
- B wrong: Objective criteria (Blue Book, salary surveys) are integrative-bargaining anchors, not your alternative course of action.
- D wrong: That describes an aggressive opening / target point, not the alternative-to-this-deal floor.

### Q3. **B** — Reservation price quantifies BATNA into a single in-deal number — the worst terms inside this deal you would still accept before walking. Synonyms: reservation point, resistance point, walk-away threshold.
- A wrong: That defines the **target** (aspiration / best plausible outcome). Confusing target with reservation is a slide-flagged classic prep mistake.
- C wrong: A deposit/earnest money is a contractual mechanism, not a reservation price.
- D wrong: ZOPA is the *overlap* of the two parties' reservation prices; the midpoint of ZOPA is not itself anyone's reservation price.

### Q4. **B** — Failure Mode A: rejecting a deal that beats your BATNA because it "feels unfair." $8.5M > $7.4M expected-value BATNA; per Malhotra, evaluate destination (offer vs. BATNA), not path (offer vs. fairness anchor). Shapiro himself later admitted the rejection was a mistake.
- A wrong: $10M is a fairness anchor, not a BATNA. The accept/reject rule compares offer to BATNA, not to a "fair" number — the article's central thesis.
- C wrong: Mode B is *accepting* below-BATNA deals because of concession choreography; Shapiro *rejected*, so this is Mode A.
- D wrong: Malhotra's prescription for uncertain BATNAs is exactly **expected-value** calculation, not best-case (which would be self-deceiving).

### Q5. **B** — Mode B (concession illusion): a sequence of price drops feels like progress regardless of where the final number lands. Olivia accepted $32K vs. her $31K reservation — $1K above the walk-away — because the concession choreography overrode the math. The defense: pre-commit a reservation number in writing.
- A wrong: She paid $1K more than her own walk-away number; that is BATNA-violating, not rational value capture.
- C wrong: Mode A is rejection of above-BATNA deals due to fairness; Olivia *accepted*, and the issue was concession aesthetics, not fairness.
- D wrong: No multi-issue trades occurred — this is a single-issue distributive purchase. Integrative bargaining requires trading across multiple issues valued unequally.

### Q6. **C** — Integrative bargaining: structural foundation is that people **rarely value things to the same degree**, so trades across multiple issues (job, fuel, health insurance, ads) can shift each party's reservation enough to create a ZOPA where price-only negotiation had none. (The owner's reservation moved from $553K to $467K once non-price issues were on the table.)
- A wrong: Anchoring is a distributive tactic for splitting a fixed pie; the Texoil block was structural (no ZOPA), and an aggressive anchor wouldn't have unlocked it.
- B wrong: Compromise on price was exactly what *failed* — there was no overlap to compromise within. Compromise on a single issue is a distributive default.
- D wrong: The concession illusion is a pathology of accept/reject; here the parties expanded the pie, they did not deceive themselves about the destination.

### Q7. **B** — Fisher & Ury / "Getting to Yes": positions are visible and look opposed ("I want the orange"); interests (peel vs. juice) are hidden and often complementary. Focus on interests, not positions, is the canonical integrative move.
- A wrong: Cutting in half gives each sibling 50% of what they want. Peel-for-juice trade gives each 100%. Compromise can leave both worse off than an interest-based trade — slide-flagged anti-pattern.
- C wrong: Indivisibility doesn't favor distributive; it makes interest discovery *more* valuable, since the resource cannot be split without loss.
- D wrong: BATNAs are the default when negotiation fails; here negotiation succeeds richly via interest discovery, and no BATNA comparison is involved.

### Q8. **C** — Slide-stated principle: "Integration is not just compromising." Compromise on the same issue is a symmetric concession that can leave both worse off than a creative cross-issue trade. Integration exploits unequal valuations across multiple issues so both parties land above their compromise point.
- A wrong: This is the exact distractor the slides warn against; "win-win" is not a synonym for fair compromise.
- B wrong: The framing is backwards — compromise is more naturally a distributive move (split the pie); integrative bargaining transcends compromise.
- D wrong: That describes a graceful distributive loss, not integrative bargaining at all.

### Q9. **B** — Distributive success toolkit (claim value): make the first offer to anchor, reveal a deadline, keep most information to yourself, display anger strategically. Fits one-shot, single-issue, no-relationship contexts.
- A wrong: Soft on people / objective criteria / invent options for mutual gain are the **integrative** toolkit (create value); they fit long-term relationships and multi-issue settings.
- C wrong: Full information disclosure and relationship emphasis are integrative moves; deploying them in a one-shot distributive setting leaves value on the table.
- D wrong: Anchoring is a distributive *strength*, not a weakness — the first offer pulls the negotiation toward the anchor.

### Q10. **B** — Slide example of cultural difference: in relationship-oriented (often collectivistic) contexts the **relationship is the deal** and renegotiation as conditions change is expected; in more individualistic contexts the **paper agreement is the deal** and signing = closing. The Australia → Japan sugar case is a mismatch on what the deal *was*.
- A wrong: The slides explicitly reject a single universal contract norm — this is the cultural difference being illustrated, not bad faith.
- C wrong: The concession illusion is an accept/reject pathology in distributive bargaining; nothing here involves a sequence of fake price drops.
- D wrong: The slides' bottom-line claim is the opposite — **integrative** bargaining travels better globally because it preserves relationships and accommodates indirect communication. Rigid-contract distributive stances are precisely what blow up cross-cultural deals.
