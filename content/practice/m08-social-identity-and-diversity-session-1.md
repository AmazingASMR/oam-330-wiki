# Practice — Module 8 Social Identity & Diversity (Session 1)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 10 without referencing the concept pages. Score yourself and use the "why each distractor is wrong" notes to surface fuzzy thinking.

## Questions

### Q1. Per Williams's slides, which of the following best states the textbook definition of "social identity"?

A. The set of demographic categories (race, gender, age) an HR system records about an employee.
B. How you define who you are in the world — the categories you complete the sentence "Being ______ is a big part of who I am" with.
C. The proportion of minority employees on a given team relative to the broader labor market.
D. A legally protected class status that triggers anti-discrimination obligations under U.S. federal law.

### Q2. The slides characterize the *employee-side* reason for caring about diversity at work as wanting to be:

A. Treated equally regardless of background, in line with anti-discrimination law.
B. Authentic but not conspicuous — able to be oneself without being seen as only one's identity.
C. Granted access to growing minority customer markets through one's cultural knowledge.
D. Placed on heterogeneous teams so that one's perspective contributes to better ideas.

### Q3. Williams contrasts stereotype *threat* and stereotype *tax*. Which pair correctly maps each term to its mechanism?

A. Threat = career cost imposed by evaluators' System 1 inference; Tax = the target's own performance drop under identity salience.
B. Threat = the target's own performance drop under identity salience; Tax = career cost imposed by others' System 1 inference on identical work.
C. Threat = intentional discrimination; Tax = unintentional disparate impact.
D. Threat and Tax are interchangeable terms for the same internal mechanism described by Steele & Aronson.

### Q4. J.C. Penney finds that staffing stores in heavily Latino neighborhoods with bilingual, predominantly Latino sales associates raises both customer satisfaction and store earnings. The CMO defends the practice in a board memo as "a way to credibly serve and grow share in fast-expanding minority markets." Per Thomas & Ely, this justification illustrates which paradigm?

A. Discrimination & fairness (paradigm 1) — the moral/legal case for diversity.
B. Access & legitimacy (paradigm 2) — diversity as strategic market asset.
C. Better ideas / innovation (paradigm 3) — heterogeneous problem-solving.
D. Authentic-but-not-conspicuous — the employee-side rationale.

### Q5. A national firm hires almost exclusively through referrals from current employees. After three years, internal audits show the workforce has become demographically more uniform than the local labor pool, and women and Black applicants are dramatically under-represented in callbacks. The hiring manager protests: "I have nothing against any group — we just hire who our people recommend." Per the slide treatment of Thomas & Ely paradigm 1, which conclusion is most accurate?

A. There is no discrimination problem because the manager has no biased intent.
B. Discrimination need not be intentional; a referral-only practice that "operates to exclude" can produce disparate impact and is what paradigm 1 is designed to catch.
C. This is a stereotype-threat scenario — the under-represented applicants are underperforming due to identity salience.
D. This is an access-and-legitimacy failure — the firm is missing a chance to serve diverse customers.

### Q6. A Black MBA student takes a high-stakes quantitative exam. In one condition she is first asked to "describe what it is like to be African-American at this school"; in the control she is not. Per the Steele/Aronson lineage cited on the slides, what should we predict?

A. Identical performance — written prompts cannot affect a well-prepared test-taker.
B. Higher performance in the identity-prompt condition because identity salience increases motivation.
C. Lower performance in the identity-prompt condition due to stereotype threat — cognitive load from worrying about confirming a group stereotype.
D. Lower performance, but only if the proctor is of a different race — i.e., the effect requires an out-group evaluator present.

### Q7. In *Invisibilia: The New Norm*, the McDonald's-Russia (Pushkin Square, 1990) team's hardest installation problem was *not* yeast paperwork or telephone infrastructure but getting Russian counter staff to smile at strangers, since smiling-at-strangers had been read in Soviet culture as insincere or dishonest. Russian managers and trainees experienced confusion, frustration, and blame on both sides. Per the Five Tricks framework, this is best described as:

A. Stereotype tax — Russian employees paid a career cost for not smiling.
B. Culture shock from a *small unrecognized* rule difference — neither side had recognized that smiling-at-strangers was a *rule* at all, which is the lecture's prototype for cross-cultural conflict.
C. Workplace stigmatization — McDonald's was devaluing the Russian employees' identity.
D. Stereotype threat — the Russian employees' performance dropped because their national identity was made salient.

### Q8. From the Five Tricks debrief, the slides name two factors that *reduce* the impact of a culture shock when groups with different rules first work together. Which pair correctly identifies them?

A. Having more obvious cultural differences, and having a high-power-distance leader to enforce a single rule set.
B. Having a partner who shares your rules, and having lived through a prior culture shock.
C. Maximizing demographic similarity on the team, and avoiding any non-verbal communication.
D. Speaking the same first language, and having an external HR mediator present at every interaction.

### Q9. A Fortune-500 CEO three months into an aggressive diversity-hiring push tells the board, "Cross-team conflict is up and engagement-survey satisfaction is *down* compared to last year. The whole initiative has failed." Per Williams's Five Tricks takeaways and the J-curve framing, the most defensible response is:

A. He is correct; if diversity were working, both conflict and dissatisfaction should have *fallen* in the short term.
B. He is correct on dissatisfaction but wrong on conflict; conflict should drop while satisfaction climbs.
C. The pattern he describes is the *predicted* short-term cost of diversifying, not a sign of failure; the slide forecasts conflict and dissatisfaction up front, with better ideas and performance arriving longer-term.
D. The data show stereotype threat, which means the new hires were under-qualified.

### Q10. A homogeneous five-person product team and a demographically and experientially heterogeneous five-person team are each given the murder-mystery exercise (sift 12 facts to identify which are irrelevant to motive). Per the diversity-innovation literature on the slides, which prediction best fits?

A. The homogeneous team will produce more creative answers because lower conflict frees cognitive resources.
B. The heterogeneous team will likely take longer and surface more disagreement, but generate a broader, more creative set of solutions because different perspectives expand the hypothesis space.
C. The two teams will perform identically; demographic composition has no measured effect on idea generation.
D. The heterogeneous team will perform worse on every dimension because conflict swamps any informational benefit.

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Slide definition: social identity is "how you define who you are in the world," operationalized by the "Being ______ is a big part of who I am" sentence test.
- A wrong: That conflates social identity with *demographic categories*. The slide flags the distinction: demographics are observable; identity is the subjective importance the person attaches.
- C wrong: Diversity is the *heterogeneity of identities*, not a minority-proportion statistic, and identity itself is at the individual level.
- D wrong: Legally protected status is a paradigm-1 (discrimination & fairness) construct, not the definition of social identity.

### Q2. **B** — The lecture frames the employee-side rationale as the *authentic but not conspicuous* tension: "I can be myself" + "people see me as a whole person, not just my identity."
- A wrong: That is paradigm 1 (discrimination & fairness) — an *organization*-side, legal/moral rationale.
- C wrong: That is paradigm 2 (access & legitimacy) — also an organization-side, strategic rationale.
- D wrong: That is paradigm 3 (better ideas) — also organizational, performance-driven. All three are *org* rationales; the question asks for the *employee* rationale.

### Q3. **B** — Threat is the target's *own* performance decrement under identity salience (internal mechanism, Steele/Aronson). Tax is a career cost imposed by *others'* System 1 inference on identical inputs (external mechanism, e.g., 5,000-résumé Boston/Chicago callback gap).
- A wrong: Reverses the two — internal vs. external mapping is flipped.
- C wrong: The threat/tax distinction is not intentional vs. unintentional; both can occur without intent. Tax is specifically the *evaluator-imposed* cost.
- D wrong: They are explicitly distinguished on the slides; the high-confusability pair is the trap, not a synonym pair.

### Q4. **B** — Access & legitimacy (paradigm 2): diversity treated as a strategic asset for credibility/access in growing minority markets — the J.C. Penney example.
- A wrong: Paradigm 1 is morally/legally driven (avoid harm). The CMO's justification is profitability and market access, not fairness.
- C wrong: Paradigm 3 (better ideas) is about *internal* problem-solving from heterogeneous perspectives, not external market access.
- D wrong: Authentic-but-not-conspicuous is the *employee-side* rationale; the memo is articulating an organizational rationale.

### Q5. **B** — The slide is explicit: discrimination "does not have to be intentional"; courts look for whether a practice "operates to exclude" (disparate impact). Referral-only hiring tends to reproduce the demographics of current employees and is a paradigm-1 case.
- A wrong: Lack of conscious intent does not absolve a practice that produces disparate impact — System 1 stereotyping and structural exclusion are paradigm-1 territory.
- C wrong: Stereotype threat would describe applicants' *internal* performance drop under identity salience; the issue here is exclusion at the *callback* stage, not test performance.
- D wrong: Access & legitimacy concerns customer markets; this scenario is about hiring fairness, paradigm 1 not paradigm 2.

### Q6. **C** — Canonical Steele/Aronson result on the slides: identity-salience prompt produces *worse* performance on the stereotyped task; mechanism is cognitive load from worrying about confirming the stereotype.
- A wrong: The whole point of the lineage is that priming has measurable performance effects independent of preparation.
- B wrong: The slide says salience *hurts* performance; the prediction is the opposite of "motivation boost."
- D wrong: Stereotype threat is triggered by identity salience itself; an out-group evaluator's presence is not required for the effect.

### Q7. **B** — Five Tricks lesson: the conflicts that bite are *small, unrecognized* rule differences; both sides treat their own rules as the standard. Russians-and-strangers smiling fits exactly.
- A wrong: Tax is a career cost imposed by an evaluator's System 1 inference; the McDonald's case is about norm collision, not differential evaluation of identical work.
- C wrong: Stigmatization is *devaluing* of identity through interpersonal incivility, not a symmetric norm clash where both sides are confused.
- D wrong: Threat is an *intra*personal performance decrement under identity salience; the Pushkin Square case is *inter*personal rule mismatch.

### Q8. **B** — Slide-listed culture-shock reducers: a *partner* who shares your rules, and *prior shock* experience.
- A wrong: The slide says small unrecognized differences cause more conflict than big obvious ones; high-PD enforcement is not a Module-8 prescription.
- C wrong: Maximizing similarity is the opposite of diversifying; "no non-verbal communication" misreads Five Tricks (the silence was a *language-barrier analogue*, not a fix).
- D wrong: Shared first language and HR mediation are not the slide-listed reducers — partner and prior shock are.

### Q9. **C** — Slide takeaway: in the short term, diversifying organizations should *expect* conflict and employee dissatisfaction; the long-term payoff is better ideas and a more successful org. The pattern is *predicted, not pathological*.
- A wrong: That inverts the slide forecast — short-term conflict and dissatisfaction are *expected*, not evidence of failure.
- B wrong: Same inversion; the slide does not promise satisfaction climbs while conflict drops in the short term.
- D wrong: Stereotype threat is a within-target performance phenomenon; an aggregate dip in engagement and a rise in inter-group conflict is the J-curve, not threat.

### Q10. **B** — Slide claim: heterogeneous teams may experience more conflict and take more time, *but* generate more creative ideas because different perspectives expand the information set. Murder-mystery exercise demonstrates: heterogeneous teams flag and reject different irrelevancies, aggregating to a better solution.
- A wrong: Inverts the finding; lower conflict in homogeneous teams is real but it doesn't produce *more* creativity — it produces *less*.
- C wrong: Williams cites measurable creativity gains from heterogeneity (and even from living abroad); the slide's punchline — "when two people always agree, one of them is unnecessary" — directly contradicts a no-effect claim.
- D wrong: The slide does not claim heterogeneity dominates on every dimension. It explicitly trades short-term conflict/time for long-term idea quality.
