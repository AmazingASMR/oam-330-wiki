## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Random assignment ensures irrelevant variables average across conditions so the IV is the only systematic difference; this is what licenses causal inference (Harrah's slide example).
- A wrong: random *sampling* concerns generalizability, not causation; the two are routinely swapped in distractors.
- C wrong: control variables address known confounds statistically; randomization handles them all without measurement.
- D wrong: it was an experiment with random allocation, not self-selection.

### Q2. **C** — Hindsight bias: outcome reshapes the recalled prior belief ("I knew it all along").
- A wrong: overconfidence is forward-looking ("I'll predict right"); hindsight is backward ("I knew it").
- B wrong: confirmation bias is active info-search to support a current belief, not memory distortion.
- D wrong: sunk cost is escalation/refusal-to-fix based on past investment, not memory of prediction.

### Q3. **B** — Big 5 is empirically derived, continuous, and modestly predictive; MBTI is theory-driven (Jung), dichotomous, and weak (Grant: ~50% retest within weeks).
- A wrong: reverses the mapping (MBTI is dichotomous, Big 5 continuous).
- C wrong: popularity is not validity — Williams, Grant, and Gosling all flag this confusion.
- D wrong: Big 5 has no Honesty dimension (HEXACO does); the Big 5 is OCEAN.

### Q4. **C** — Performance→reward link (instrumentality) is opaque: she cannot see how performance maps to bonus.
- A wrong: nothing in the stem says she doesn't value the bonus.
- B wrong: expectancy is effort→performance; the stem's break is performance→reward.
- D wrong: equity is comparison to a referent; the stem describes a missing link, not a comparison.

### Q5. **C** — Scarcity. The "before it goes out broadly" framing is exclusive access and triggers loss aversion (the prospect of losing access > equivalent gain).
- A wrong: social proof would emphasize that many others are choosing it (consensus, not exclusivity).
- B wrong: authority is about the speaker's expertise/credentials.
- D wrong: commitment/consistency requires a prior public stance the client is being held to.

### Q6. **C** — Power distance: high-PD cultures use mitigated, receiver-decoded speech; the captain (also high-PD) missed hints. KAL 801 is the canonical PD scenario (Korean Air loss rate ~17× United's pre-fix; PD-crash r = .47).
- A wrong: I-C and PD are *separate* Hofstede dimensions; this scenario is hierarchy, not group orientation.
- B wrong: uncertainty avoidance is about tolerance for ambiguity, not deference to authority.
- D wrong: long-term orientation is about future-vs-present focus, not communication style.

### Q7. **B** — Access & legitimacy: diversity as a strategic asset for credibility with a target market (Penney slide example).
- A wrong: paradigm 1 is the moral/legal case (avoid harm, disparate impact); the stem is profit/strategy.
- C wrong: stereotype threat is an employee-side performance phenomenon, not the org-side rationale here.
- D wrong: integration-and-learning / "better ideas" is about innovation from heterogeneous perspectives on internal problems.

### Q8. **C** — Always accept above BATNA; fairness perceptions overriding rational accept is Malhotra's central thesis. Litigation EV $7.4M < $8.5M offer.
- A wrong: signaling logic is not the framework Malhotra uses; the rule is BATNA.
- B wrong: anchoring on the original demand is exactly the cognitive error to resist.
- D wrong: counter-at-midpoint is fair-feeling but not BATNA-driven; it ignores the EV math.

### Q9. **C** — Punctuated equilibrium: Phase 1 inertia, midpoint transition (here, week 4), Phase 2 burst. The midpoint hits regardless of total length.
- A wrong: that's Tuckman; R&J argue Tuckman's stages may all occur within Phase 1.
- B wrong: social loafing is effort reduction in groups, not a model of progress over time.
- D wrong: cohesion is not the punctuated equilibrium prediction; the model predicts inertia, not peak cohesion early.

### Q10. **B** — The common-knowledge effect is best fought by getting information on the table *before* people commit to a preference (slide-canonical VC firm example: private ratings, then discuss only disagreements).
- A wrong: devil's advocate addresses groupthink (dissent suppression), not info-sampling failures.
- C wrong: bigger groups worsen the effect (more shared anchoring; more loafing).
- D wrong: framing as personal judgment *reduces* info sharing — slides specifically recommend problem-solving framing.

### Q11. **C** — Fairness/Reciprocity violation: rules should apply to everyone equally. Slide-canonical wording: "secretly commit to hire only people of your own race/religion."
- A wrong: Care/Harm is the most common trap distractor; the violation here is structural fairness, not harm to feelings.
- B wrong: Authority/Respect violation would be cursing/disobeying superiors.
- D wrong: Loyalty violation would be acting *against* the in-group.

### Q12. **B** — Ethical fading: the moral dimension never enters awareness at the decision stage; the issue is reframed as cost-benefit.
- A wrong: justification is *active* rationalization — but here ethics never registered to need justifying.
- C wrong: visceral responses are hot bodily drives (hunger, fear); cost-benefit analysis is a cognitive reframe.
- D wrong: memory revisionism is the *recollection-stage* sibling — the question is decision-stage.

### Q13. **B** — Social connections (strong + weak) plus experiential and prosocial spending are the two slide-emphasized intentional levers; ~40% of variance is intentional action.
- A wrong: Easterlin/Kahneman-Deaton: above the absence-of-misery threshold, more income does not reliably yield more happiness.
- C wrong: relative income matters (social comparison), but the stem asks for the *strongest reliable lever* — that's social connections + how money is used.
- D wrong: Illinois lottery winners ($50K–$1M) were no happier a year later (adaptation).

### Q14. **B** — Peripheral route maps to System 1; all six Cialdini principles are peripheral cues. Celebrity = liking/authority; "only six slots" = scarcity; accreditor logo = authority.
- A wrong: central route is argument-quality engagement; the deck is cue-stacked, not argument-driven.
- C wrong: central route engages System 2, not System 1.
- D wrong: peripheral cues activate System 1 (fast/automatic), not System 2 (slow/deliberate).

### Q15. **A** — Distributive justice (the outcome) was equal; procedural justice (process: explanation, voice, dignity) differed. Procedural justice is the stronger predictor of job performance (slide 16, deck 08). The Negotiation parallel: Shapiro rejecting an above-BATNA $8.5M because it "felt unfair" — fairness perceptions can override rational accept rules.
- B wrong: distributive was *equal* (both denied); reverses the slide's headline finding about which type predicts performance.
- C wrong: equity theory is the input/output-ratio mechanism behind distributive judgments — the stem isolates *process*, not ratio comparison.
- D wrong: expectancy theory is forward-looking effort→performance→reward; the stem is about how the decision was delivered, not effort-reward links.
