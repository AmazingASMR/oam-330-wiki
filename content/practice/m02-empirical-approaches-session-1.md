# Practice — Module 2 Empirical Approaches to OB (Session 1)

## Instructions
Try to answer all 10 without referencing the concept pages. Score yourself and use the "why each distractor is wrong" notes to surface fuzzy thinking.

## Questions

### Q1. Which option lists the **three course themes** in the form Williams stated them?

A. (1) Common sense is necessary but not sufficient for predicting human behavior in organizations; (2) Behavior is predictable; (3) Empirical evidence outperforms other types.
B. (1) Common sense is sufficient for most managerial decisions; (2) Behavior is largely random; (3) Empirical evidence is one option among many.
C. (1) Leaders matter most; (2) Common sense is necessary but not sufficient; (3) Behavior is predictable.
D. (1) Behavior is predictable; (2) Empirical evidence outperforms other types; (3) HR is the foundation of OB.

### Q2. Per Williams' slide, which of the following is **NOT** one of the three alternative explanations for an observed correlation between X and Y?

A. The relationship is spurious (merely coincidental).
B. The causal direction is reversed — Y causes X.
C. A third variable causes both X and Y.
D. The result is driven by measurement error or confounding.

### Q3. Williams says a true experiment isn't always possible. Which trio correctly names the **three obstacles to experimentation**?

A. Impractical, impossible, unethical.
B. Impractical, impossible, unethical, expensive.
C. Costly, time-consuming, ethically risky.
D. Confounded, underpowered, non-generalizable.

### Q4. Casino-Co wants to know whether a $60 chips offer drives more revenue than a $125 room-and-meals package. Researchers walk up to each arriving guest, flip a coin, and send heads to the $60 condition and tails to the $125 condition. Outcomes are then compared. Which study design is this, and why?

A. Correlational study — researchers measured guest spending and offer type without manipulating either.
B. Quasi-experiment — the casino used existing customer segments (regulars vs. first-timers) as the groups.
C. Randomized controlled trial — the experimenter controls the input variable AND randomly assigns participants to conditions, including a comparison group.
D. RCT, but only because the sample was randomly drawn from the population of all U.S. casino-goers.

### Q5. A researcher wants to test whether agreeableness causes higher sales performance. She concludes she **cannot run an RCT**. Which obstacle from Williams' list applies, and what design should she fall back on?

A. Unethical → quasi-experiment, because paying salespeople differently violates fairness.
B. Impossible → correlational study, because you cannot randomly assign people a personality trait.
C. Impractical → RCT with matched pairs, because logistics block randomization.
D. Expensive → quasi-experiment, because the budget is the binding obstacle.

### Q6. A consultant tells a board: "Southwest is the most successful airline, and Herb Kelleher drinks Wild Turkey bourbon — so your CEO should drink more bourbon." Which empirical problem most directly undermines this advice?

A. Correlation ≠ causation (Reason #2): even with many cases, association doesn't prove causation.
B. The N-of-1 problem (Reason #1): you cannot generalize from a single company/CEO; you can't know all the reasons for one company's experience and the likelihood of replicating it is low.
C. Reverse causal direction: success may cause bourbon drinking, not the other way around.
D. Failure to include control variables in a multiple regression.

### Q7. A company rolls out a new wellness program at its Atlanta branch but not its Boston branch, then compares year-over-year absenteeism between the two branches. There is no random assignment of employees to branches. Which design BEST describes this study?

A. Randomized controlled trial, because there is a clear treatment group and a comparison group.
B. Correlational study, because no groups are involved — only measured variables.
C. Quasi-experiment, because the design uses a natural / existing division (branch) rather than random assignment.
D. N-of-1 case study, because only one company is being studied.

### Q8. Williams emphasizes that **random assignment** and **random sampling** are different. Which statement is correct?

A. Random sampling allocates participants to experimental vs. control conditions; random assignment selects them from the population.
B. Random assignment allocates participants to conditions (internal validity / causal inference); random sampling is how participants are drawn from the population (external validity / generalizability).
C. They are synonymous — both refer to using a random number generator at any point in the study.
D. Random assignment is required for a correlational study; random sampling is required for an RCT.

### Q9. A researcher finds that being read aloud to as a child correlates with reading for fun. With no random assignment available, she adds # of books in the home, parental income, and free time as **control variables** in a multiple regression. What role do these control variables play?

A. They serve as the independent variables of interest; the regression now tests whether they cause reading for fun.
B. They function as a control *group* — a no-treatment condition the experimental group is compared against.
C. They are added to the model in order to remove their influence, ruling them out as third-variable alternative explanations. Random assignment would do this work automatically in an RCT.
D. They guarantee that all possible confounds — including ones the researcher didn't measure — are eliminated.

### Q10. Which row correctly distinguishes RCT vs. quasi-experiment vs. correlational study?

A. RCT: manipulates IV + random assignment → causation possible. Quasi-experiment: uses a natural/existing division, no random assignment → no clean causal claim. Correlational: measures variables only, no manipulation → no causal claim.
B. RCT: random sampling from the population. Quasi-experiment: random assignment without a control group. Correlational: random assignment of measured variables.
C. RCT and quasi-experiment both establish causation; correlational studies establish correlation only.
D. Quasi-experiments sit at the top of the evidence pyramid because they use real-world groups; RCTs are below them because lab settings reduce realism.

## Answer Key

### Q1. **A** — Verbatim from the slides: (1) common sense is necessary but not sufficient, (2) behavior is predictable, (3) empirical evidence outperforms other types.
- B wrong: Inverts every theme. Common sense is *not* sufficient; behavior is complex but *not random*; empirical evidence is *the* standard, not one option.
- C wrong: "Leaders matter most" is a fabricated 4th theme — there are exactly three. Trap for students who pad the list.
- D wrong: "HR is the foundation of OB" reverses the relationship — OB is the underlying behavioral science; HR is one applied domain.

### Q2. **D** — Williams' slide 25 lists exactly three: spurious, reverse causal direction, third variable. "Measurement error / confounding" is real methodological vocabulary but it is *not* on the verbatim list of three.
- A wrong: Spurious IS one of the three.
- B wrong: Reverse causal direction IS one of the three.
- C wrong: Third variable IS one of the three.

### Q3. **A** — Slide 14 verbatim: "It may be **impractical, impossible, or unethical** to assign people to different conditions."
- B wrong: Adds "expensive" as a 4th obstacle. Williams gives exactly three. (Cost is captured under "impractical.")
- C wrong: Paraphrases the obstacles in non-Williams terms; the slide's exact words matter.
- D wrong: These are validity threats / study weaknesses, not Williams' obstacles to running the experiment in the first place.

### Q4. **C** — Both RCT criteria are met: experimenter controls the IV (which offer each guest gets) AND randomly assigns participants to conditions, including a comparison group. This is the Harrah's setup.
- A wrong: The researcher *did* manipulate the offer — coin flips assigned condition. That's not a correlational design.
- B wrong: A quasi-experiment uses a *pre-existing* division (e.g., regulars vs. first-timers). Here the groups are created by random coin flip, not by a natural division.
- C right.
- D wrong: Classic random-assignment vs. random-sampling trap. RCT status comes from random *assignment to conditions*, not from random *sampling from the population*. Most lab RCTs lack random sampling and are still RCTs.

### Q5. **B** — You cannot manipulate personality, so the obstacle is **impossible**, and the standard fallback is a correlational study (measure agreeableness and sales as they exist).
- A wrong: Wrong obstacle (unethical applies to things like underpaying employees, not measuring personality), and a quasi-experiment would still need pre-existing personality "groups" — correlational is the natural fit.
- C wrong: Logistics aren't the obstacle — personality literally cannot be manipulated. "Matched pairs" doesn't make a study an RCT without random assignment.
- D wrong: "Expensive" isn't on Williams' list of obstacles (slide gives three: impractical, impossible, unethical).

### Q6. **B** — Pfeffer & Sutton's exact example. The flaw is the **N-of-1 problem**: one company / one CEO cannot ground a generalizable claim. You can't know all the reasons for Southwest's success, and the likelihood that bourbon explains it is low.
- A wrong: Correlation ≠ causation (Reason #2) requires *many* cases that are merely correlated. Here we have a single case — that's the N-of-1 problem (Reason #1), not the correlation problem. Trap: swapping the two reasons.
- C wrong: Reverse causal direction is one alternative within Reason #2. With only one observation you don't have a correlation in the first place.
- D wrong: Multiple regression / control variables address third-variable confounds in correlational designs with many cases — irrelevant when N=1.

### Q7. **C** — Quasi-experiment: the "groups" exist as a natural division (Atlanta vs. Boston branches); the experimenter didn't randomly assign employees, so causation can't be cleanly inferred.
- A wrong: Without random assignment, it isn't an RCT — even if there's a treatment group and a comparison group.
- B wrong: A correlational study has no defined treatment / no group structure, just measured variables. This study has discrete branch-level conditions, so it's quasi-experimental.
- D wrong: An N-of-1 study generalizes from a single case; here we have many employees across two branches with a defined natural intervention — that's a quasi-experiment.

### Q8. **B** — Random *assignment* allocates participants to conditions (powers causal inference / internal validity); random *sampling* draws participants from the population (powers generalizability / external validity). You can have one without the other.
- A wrong: Reverses the two definitions — the classic confusion the slide warns against.
- C wrong: They are not synonyms. They occur at different stages and serve different validity goals.
- D wrong: Random assignment is the hallmark of an RCT, not a correlational study. Correlational studies have no assignment.

### Q9. **C** — Slide 16 / 19: control variables are measured covariates **added to a multiple regression to remove their influence**, ruling them out as third-variable alternative explanations. Random assignment in an RCT does this automatically; without it, you must measure each suspected confound.
- A wrong: Control variables are *not* the IV of interest — the IV is reading-aloud. Controls are measured only to be ruled out.
- B wrong: A control *variable* (covariate) is completely different from a control *group* (no-treatment condition in an experiment). Classic terminology trap.
- D wrong: Controls only neutralize the confounds you *thought to measure* — not unmeasured ones. That's why RCTs (which handle all confounds via random assignment) sit higher on the evidence pyramid.

### Q10. **A** — Three-way distinction from the slides. RCT: experimenter controls IV + random assignment → causation possible. Quasi: natural/existing division, no random assignment → no clean causal inference. Correlational: just measure variables → only association.
- B wrong: Confuses random assignment with random sampling (RCTs are defined by assignment, not sampling), and "random assignment of measured variables" is incoherent — correlational studies have no assignment at all.
- C wrong: Quasi-experiments do **not** establish causation cleanly because they lack random assignment.
- D wrong: Inverts the evidence pyramid. RCTs sit *at the top*; quasi-experiments and correlational studies sit below because they cannot resolve correlation ≠ causation.
