# Practice — Cross-cut: System 1 vs. System 2 (Session 1)

## Instructions
These questions test the cross-module synthesis the cumulative final is most likely to ask. Try without reference; score; re-read the cross-cut page on misses.

## Questions

### Q1. [Modules: M3, M6, M11] Which statement most accurately captures Kahneman's two-system model as Williams uses it across modules?
A. System 1 runs first; once it finishes, System 2 takes over to verify the output.
B. System 1 (limbic, automatic, fast) and System 2 (prefrontal, deliberate, slow) run simultaneously; System 2 is effortful and conserved, so System 1 shortcuts often slip through uncorrected.
C. System 2 is morally good; System 1 is morally bad — the goal of debiasing is to silence System 1.
D. The two systems run sequentially, with System 2 having priority when stakes are high.

### Q2. [Modules: M6, M3] An infomercial pairs a celebrity endorser with a "limited-time only" countdown clock. Mapping this onto Kahneman's two-system frame and Petty/Cacioppo's routes:
A. Central route, System 2 — the audience is being asked to evaluate argument quality (celebrity expertise + scarcity logic).
B. Peripheral route, System 1 — authority and scarcity are System-1 cues that produce faster but more fragile attitude shifts; central-route argument quality is largely absent.
C. Central route, System 1 — celebrity is a central cue but scarcity is peripheral.
D. Peripheral route, System 2 — peripheral cues require deliberate evaluation.

### Q3. [Modules: M11, M3] A finance executive analyzes a vehicle fuel-tank redesign as a pure cost-benefit calculation, never registering the lives-at-stake angle as a moral question. This is best described as:
A. Moral justification — the executive constructs a story to defend an unethical act.
B. Visceral response — a hot bodily drive overrides deliberation.
C. Ethical fading — a System-1 decision-stage failure in which the ethical frame drops out of awareness; closest Module-3 cousin is motivated perception (input bent before deliberation).
D. Memory revisionism — the executive reconstructs the past to preserve self-image.

### Q4. [Modules: M3, M11] Which pairing of "stage in the prediction-decision-recollection ethics arc" with "Module-3 bias family" is correct?
A. Forecasting errors ↔ hindsight bias; ethical fading ↔ overconfidence; memory revisionism ↔ availability.
B. Forecasting errors ↔ overconfidence/planning fallacy (forward); ethical fading ↔ motivated perception (input-stage System 1); memory revisionism ↔ hindsight bias (backward "I always acted ethically").
C. All three stages map onto availability heuristic — they are vivid-recall failures.
D. Forecasting and recollection are System-2 errors; only ethical fading is a System-1 error.

### Q5. [Modules: M3, M6, M11] Which debiasing claim is most consistent with Williams's slides and Gilbert's quote?
A. Awareness of a bias is sufficient to neutralize it because System 2 can then override System 1.
B. The most reliable strategy is willpower — try harder to engage System 2 in the moment.
C. Because the brain cannot see itself fooling itself, structural fixes — broaden samples, present info multiple ways, minimize ego stakes, avoid the bias-producing situation — beat awareness alone.
D. Debiasing is impossible; cognitive biases are evolutionary adaptations and should not be resisted.

### Q6. [Modules: M6, M11] A junior analyst pitches a recommendation by leaning heavily on a credentialed third-party introducer (authority) and on her own slogan-style commitment statements ("I always deliver on time"). Which two-route + two-system reading is most accurate?
A. Both authority (peripheral, System 1) and commitment-consistency (peripheral, System 1) are Cialdini levers exploiting System-1 cues; central-route argument quality is not engaged.
B. Authority is peripheral but commitment-consistency is central — they target different routes.
C. Both are central-route levers; Cialdini principles primarily activate System 2.
D. Authority engages System 1 and commitment engages System 2 because consistency requires conscious deliberation.

---

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Slide-canonical: simultaneous, not sequential; System 2 conserved; System 1 is the default, not the enemy.
- A wrong: reverses sequence claim.
- C wrong: cross-cut warns explicitly against System 1 = "bad."
- D wrong: sequential framing is the high-frequency distractor.

### Q2. **B** — Peripheral route = System 1; authority + scarcity are pure peripheral cues per cross-cut and M6.
- A wrong: central engages System 2, not the case here.
- C wrong: split assignment is wrong; both are peripheral.
- D wrong: peripheral cues are System 1, not System 2.

### Q3. **C** — Cross-cut and M11 explicitly: ethical fading = System-1 decision-stage failure; closest M3 cousin is motivated perception.
- A wrong: justification is post-hoc rationalization (M11 separate concept), not invisible-ethics fading.
- B wrong: visceral requires a hot bodily drive; cost-benefit framing is cognitive.
- D wrong: memory revisionism is recollection-stage, not decision-stage.

### Q4. **B** — Cross-cut maps each stage to its Module-3 ancestor: forecasting → overconfidence/planning fallacy; fading → motivated perception; revisionism → hindsight.
- A wrong: scrambles the mapping.
- C wrong: availability isn't the bias family for any of these.
- D wrong: all three are System-1 failures (System 2 fails to catch).

### Q5. **C** — Gilbert quote ("the brain cannot see itself fooling itself") + the five slide-listed structural fixes.
- A wrong: cross-cut explicitly says awareness alone is insufficient.
- B wrong: willpower is the wrong mode; restructure the situation.
- D wrong: too strong; debiasing is possible, just structural.

### Q6. **A** — All Cialdini principles are peripheral, System-1 levers per cross-cut and M6.
- B wrong: both are peripheral; consistency is no exception.
- C wrong: reverses the route assignment.
- D wrong: consistency feels deliberative but operates as a peripheral commitment cue.
