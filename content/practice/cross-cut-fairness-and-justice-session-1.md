# Practice — Cross-cut: Fairness and justice (Session 1)

## Instructions
These questions test the cross-module synthesis the cumulative final is most likely to ask. Try without reference; score; re-read the cross-cut page on misses.

## Questions

### Q1. [Modules: M5, M11] Which definition pair is correct?
A. Distributive justice = was the process fair? Procedural justice = was the outcome fair?
B. Distributive justice = was the outcome fair (did I get the share I deserved)? Procedural justice = was the process fair (transparent, voice, dignity, consistent across identities)? Procedural is the *stronger* predictor of job performance.
C. Distributive and procedural justice are interchangeable terms in Williams's slides.
D. Distributive justice predicts job performance more strongly than procedural justice.

### Q2. [Modules: M9, M5] In Malhotra's case, Eric Shapiro rejects an $8.5M settlement when his litigation BATNA's expected value is $7.4M, citing that the offer "feels unfair" relative to the $10M he sought. From a Module-9 BATNA perspective combined with the M5 fairness lens, this is best described as:
A. A correct distributive-justice judgment that overrides a flawed BATNA estimate.
B. A fairness perception irrationally overriding BATNA — the deal beats his outside option, so he should accept even though it feels unfair; this is the central Malhotra/Shapiro punchline.
C. A procedural-justice violation that justifies rejection regardless of BATNA.
D. An optimal use of reservation price as the binding floor.

### Q3. [Modules: M8, M11] A retailer hires Spanish-speaking sales associates specifically to enter and serve Latino market segments. A separate hiring manager at the same firm secretly commits to hiring only people of his own race for a different role. Which pair correctly classifies these two acts?
A. Both are paradigm-1 (discrimination & fairness) cases.
B. Both are paradigm-2 (access & legitimacy) cases.
C. The Spanish-speaking hires are paradigm 2 (access & legitimacy — strategic/market case); the secret same-race commitment is a Fairness/Reciprocity moral-foundation violation (and a paradigm-1 / disparate-impact concern).
D. The Spanish-speaking hires violate Care/Harm; the same-race commitment is paradigm 2.

### Q4. [Modules: M5, M4] Two employees in the same firm receive identical denied-promotion outcomes. Employee A's manager explained the criteria, listened to her, and treated her with dignity; Employee B's manager did not. Employee A reports much higher fairness even though her outcome was identical. Separately, in a collectivist site of the same firm, employees expect mentoring and team-harmony work to count as legitimate input under equity theory. The integrated lesson Williams draws is:
A. Procedural justice strongly predicts performance — and what counts as "fair allocation" varies cross-culturally (merit/equality/need), but the *desire for fair process* is near-universal.
B. Distributive justice is universal; procedural justice varies.
C. Equity theory is a Western theory and does not apply in collectivist contexts at all.
D. Procedural justice is identical across cultures, but distributive justice varies in mechanism, not content.

### Q5. [Modules: M9, M5] A negotiator rejects every "split-the-difference" offer she encounters because the splits "don't feel fair," and never explores why each side is asking for what they're asking for. Which two-concept synthesis best diagnoses what she's missing?
A. Equity theory — she should compute input/output ratios.
B. Positions vs. interests + integrative bargaining — splitting positions can suppress integrative gains because aligned (or orthogonal) interests beneath the positions are never surfaced; "fairness on positions" is not the same as a fair, value-creating deal.
C. Procedural justice — she should add more voice mechanisms.
D. BATNA — she should walk away whenever an offer feels unfair.

### Q6. [Modules: M11, M5] Which statement most precisely contrasts *Fairness/Reciprocity* (one of the five Moral Foundations) with *distributive justice* in the workplace fairness literature?
A. Fairness/Reciprocity and distributive justice are the same construct under different labels.
B. Fairness/Reciprocity is a cross-cultural intuitive moral category ("rules should be fair for everyone"; canonical violation: secretly hiring only one's own race/religion); distributive justice is the workplace-fairness construct concerning whether *outcomes* (e.g., pay, promotion) are deserved relative to inputs. They overlap but operate at different levels (moral foundation vs. organizational construct).
C. Distributive justice is a Moral Foundation; Fairness/Reciprocity is an organizational fairness construct.
D. Fairness/Reciprocity in M11 specifically means procedural justice, not distributive.

---

## Answer Key

### Q1. **B** — Slide-canonical definitions; "procedural is the strongest predictor of job performance" is the headline counterintuitive finding (deck 08, slide 16).
- A wrong: reverses the definitions.
- C wrong: not interchangeable per slides.
- D wrong: reverses the predictor strength.

### Q2. **B** — Cross-cut explicit: above-BATNA deals that "feel unfair" are still rationally accept-worthy; this is Malhotra/Shapiro's punchline.
- A wrong: BATNA EV is $7.4M; $8.5M is above BATNA — fairness override is irrational.
- C wrong: no procedural-justice claim is made.
- D wrong: reservation price is being violated, not respected.

### Q3. **C** — Spanish-speaking hires = paradigm 2 (A&L, market case); secret same-race commitment = canonical Fairness/Reciprocity violation per M11 slide and a paradigm-1 / disparate-impact concern.
- A wrong: A&L is paradigm 2, not 1.
- B wrong: secret same-race hiring is fairness violation, not A&L.
- D wrong: discriminatory hiring is Fairness, not Care (high-frequency cross-cut trap).

### Q4. **A** — Synthesizes both findings: procedural justice predicts performance, and allocation principles vary cross-culturally (merit/equality/need) while desire for fair process is near-universal.
- B wrong: reverses the universality.
- C wrong: too strong; equity theory still applies — input definitions just expand.
- D wrong: oversimplified; both vary in different ways.

### Q5. **B** — Cross-cut explicit synthesis: positions vs. interests; orange/cake-and-juice example; fairness-on-positions can suppress integrative gains.
- A wrong: equity theory is workplace fairness, not negotiation positions/interests.
- C wrong: not a procedural-justice case.
- D wrong: BATNA isn't the diagnosis; she's not at her walk-away.

### Q6. **B** — Cross-cut and M11 distinguish the foundation (intuitive cross-cultural category) from the M5 organizational fairness construct.
- A wrong: not the same construct.
- C wrong: reverses the labeling.
- D wrong: Moral Foundation Fairness/Reciprocity is not specifically procedural justice.
