# Module 11 — Ethics

## What this module is fundamentally about

The gap between how ethical we *think* we are and how we actually behave — and how organizations widen that gap. Lecture 26 (Apr 16) zooms in on the *individual* arc: we predict we'll act on our principles ("should self"), but at decision time the "want self" wins via ethical fading and visceral responses, and afterward memory revisionism preserves our self-image. Lecture 27 (Apr 21) zooms out to the *organization*: misconduct is enabled by four mechanisms — moral justification, social norms (often misperceived), outcome-focused culture, and moral inaction. The antidote is a culture of ethics built on transparency, checks and balances, process focus, and dissent. Bazerman & Tenbrunsel's *Blind Spots* (pp. 61–72, 152–159) provides the cognitive scaffolding for both halves.

## Headline empirical points

The numbers, named cases, and exact slide phrasings most likely to anchor a stem.

- **$50B/yr employee misconduct cost** — slide-explicit headline number framing the organizational half of the module. See [[concepts/employee-misconduct-types]].
- **Seven misconduct categories (deck 27, slide 7)** — embezzlement / supply theft / time theft / harassment / safety corner-cutting / conflicts of interest / information leaks. Mostly mundane, mostly committed by ordinary employees, not "bad apples."
- **Sexual-harassment forecasting study — 62% predict / 0% refuse** — when asked, 62% of women predict they would confront a harassing interviewer; in the live experiment, **0% actually refused** to answer the inappropriate questions. The single sharpest demonstration of the *want-self vs. should-self* gap. See [[concepts/want-self-vs-should-self]] and [[concepts/ethical-forecasting-errors]].
- **Wells Fargo "Eight is great" / John Stumpf** — outcome-focused sales-quota culture (cross-sell eight products per customer) drove 1.5M+ fake accounts; CEO Stumpf is the canonical name. Anchor for [[concepts/outcome-focused-culture]].
- **Pythias Brown — TSA officer, $800K stolen** — long-running luggage theft justified by *sense of inequity* ("I'm underpaid for what I do"). Anchor for the inequity-flavored facilitator of [[concepts/moral-justification]].
- **Ford Pinto — $11/car fix dropped → 27 deaths** — cost-benefit framing made the ethical content of the decision *fade*; engineers compared $11/car to projected litigation cost rather than treating it as a moral question. Anchor for [[concepts/ethical-fading-visceral]] (cognitive fading) *and* [[concepts/outcome-focused-culture]] (leadership-built incentive structure that produced it).
- **Enron California traders / "synergistic corruption"** — anonymous interactions plus deliberate market manipulation produced rolling blackouts; the Enron tapes ("Grandma Millie") are the canonical anonymity-facilitator example for [[concepts/moral-justification]]. The phrase *synergistic corruption* (formal codes coexisting with corrupt culture) is slide-explicit.
- **Milgram — ~2/3 obeyed to "XXX"** — classic obedience study; ~65% of participants administered the maximum (450V) shock under authoritative pressure. On slide 15 the slides treat Milgram as the canonical *social-norms / obedience-to-authority* exemplar in the misconduct half. See [[cross-cuts/distractor-pairs]] entry for Asch vs. Milgram.
- **~2/3 of witnesses don't report misconduct** — symmetric-looking number to Milgram but a different finding: pivotal anchor for [[concepts/moral-inaction]] — the fourth enabling mechanism.
- **Johnson & Johnson 1982 Tylenol crisis — "First, how do we protect the people?"** — verbatim slide phrasing of the leadership question used at J&J. The canonical *process-focus* example, anchor for the [[concepts/ethics-antidote]] pillar.

## Concepts

### What ethics is

- [[concepts/ethics-definition]] — guidelines and principles for evaluating right vs. wrong; broader than laws; we all hold principles but not necessarily the same ones.
- [[concepts/ethical-frameworks-sources]] — three slide-named sources of ethical principles: faith traditions (top-down doctrinal), Moral Foundations (bottom-up intuitions), professional guidelines (formal codes).
- [[concepts/moral-foundations-five]] — five cross-cultural intuitive categories: Care/Harm, Fairness/Reciprocity, Ingroup/Loyalty, Authority/Respect, Purity/Sanctity; people disagree on weightings, not categories.

### The individual gap (decision-time)

- [[concepts/bounded-ethicality]] — Bazerman & Tenbrunsel's umbrella term for systematic, mostly-unconscious limits on ethical self-perception; bounded rationality applied to ethics.
- [[concepts/want-self-vs-should-self]] — "should self" (deliberate, principled) dominates predictions; "want self" (impulsive, self-interested) dominates the moment of action — the structural gap behind every ethical failure.
- [[concepts/ethical-forecasting-errors]] — at the prediction stage we systematically over-predict ethical action (62% predict confronting harasser; 0% actually refuse); should self crowds out want self in the forecast.
- [[concepts/ethical-fading-visceral]] — at decision time, ethical fading (Ford Pinto cost-benefit framing) and visceral responses (hunger, fear, time pressure) drag behavior away from principles.
- [[concepts/ethical-memory-revisionism]] — at recollection we distort the past so we still see ourselves as ethical; the cycle then repeats because self-view is never updated.
- [[concepts/behavior-principles-alignment]] — the two-step fix: voice the *want* self when anticipating (so the prediction is realistic), voice the *should* self when deciding (mom test, abstract framing, precommitment).

### Organizational mechanisms enabling misconduct

- [[concepts/employee-misconduct-types]] — $50B/yr cost; seven slide-listed categories (embezzlement, supply theft, time theft, harassment, safety corner-cutting, conflicts of interest, info leaks); mostly mundane, mostly ordinary employees.
- [[concepts/moral-justification]] — first enabling mechanism: cognitive rationalizations let people stay self-perceived as ethical; two facilitators are anonymity (Enron CA traders) and sense of inequity (TSA officer Pythias Brown).
- [[concepts/social-norms-unethical-behavior]] — second enabling mechanism: people cheat more when they think others do (best predictor of tax cheating); plus pluralistic ignorance — coworkers are often more lenient than we predict.
- [[concepts/outcome-focused-culture]] — third enabling mechanism: leadership-built culture where ends justify means (Wells Fargo "Eight is great", Ford Pinto $11/car fix dropped); goals + incentives are the levers.
- [[concepts/moral-inaction]] — fourth enabling mechanism: ~2/3 of witnesses don't report; drivers are powerlessness, learned helplessness, moral disengagement.

### Antidote

- [[concepts/ethics-antidote]] — culture of ethics: transparency, checks and balances, process focus (J&J 1982 "First, how do we protect the people?"), dissent encouraged — the organizational counter to all four enabling mechanisms.

## Reading-specific testable points

- **Bazerman & Tenbrunsel — bounded ethicality** is the umbrella construct: systematic, mostly-unconscious limits on ethical self-perception. Bounded *rationality* applied to moral choices. See [[concepts/bounded-ethicality]].
- **Want-self vs. should-self** — the structural fault line. *Should self* dominates predictions and post-hoc evaluations (deliberate, principled, abstract, System 2). *Want self* dominates the live moment of decision (impulsive, self-interested, concrete, System 1). The gap is not hypocrisy — it is two different selves at two different decision times. See [[concepts/want-self-vs-should-self]].
- **Forecasting → decision → recollection arc** — the three-stage cycle Bazerman & Tenbrunsel describe:
  1. **Forecasting (before)** — over-predict ethical action; should-self crowds out want-self in the prediction (62% predict confronting harasser).
  2. **Decision (live)** — ethical fading + visceral responses pull behavior away from principles (Pinto cost-benefit, hunger/fear/time pressure).
  3. **Recollection (after)** — memory revisionism distorts the past so we still see ourselves as ethical; the cycle then repeats unchecked.
- **Four categorized interventions** Bazerman & Tenbrunsel propose, mapped to where they hit the arc:
  1. **Precommitment** — bind your future self to the should-self position (Ulysses contracts, automatic enrollment, advance directives).
  2. **Mom test** — at decision time, ask "would I do this if my mother were watching?" — re-introduces the ethical frame the want-self is screening out.
  3. **Abstract framing** — frame the decision categorically/morally rather than as an isolated transaction (raises the should-self's salience).
  4. **Structural separation** — separate forecasters from decision-makers, or auditors from auditees (anti-conflict-of-interest design).
- **Ethical fading via cost-benefit framing** — when a moral question is reframed in dollars/utility (Pinto $11/car, "shrinkage" instead of "theft"), the *ethical content drops out of awareness* — not rationalized away (that's moral justification), but never present in the decision frame at all. Slide-explicit distinction.

## High-yield MCQ traps

- **Ethical fading vs. visceral responses** — both are decision-time mechanisms but differ in channel. *Fading* is **cognitive** — ethics drops out of the conceptual frame (Pinto cost-benefit, "shrinkage"). *Visceral* is **bodily/affective** — hot drives override (hunger, fear, anger, time pressure, sexual arousal). The slides keep them as distinct items; do not collapse.
- **Ethical fading vs. moral justification** — both look like "I had a reason" but differ in *when* and *visibility*:
  - **Fading**: at decision time, ethics *never enters awareness* — invisible. Cognitive frame is non-moral.
  - **Justification**: at decision time, ethics *is acknowledged but explained away* (post-hoc rationale). "I'm underpaid, so taking these is fair" (Pythias Brown).
- **Forecasting errors vs. memory revisionism** — both involve self-deception about ethics, but on opposite sides of the action:
  - **Forecasting errors**: *before* the decision (predict ethical behavior — 62% predict confronting).
  - **Memory revisionism**: *after* the decision (re-encode the past as more ethical than it was). One is over-prediction, the other is retrospective whitewashing.
- **Four enabling mechanisms recognition** — given a vignette, name which of the four it illustrates:
  - **Moral justification** — *internal* rationalization in one employee's head (anonymity, sense of inequity; Pythias Brown TSA, Enron CA traders).
  - **Social norms (unethical behavior)** — *external* "everyone does it" cue (pluralistic ignorance, Milgram obedience to authority).
  - **Outcome-focused culture** — *cultural / leadership-built / incentive-structured* (Wells Fargo "Eight is great," Ford Pinto $11/car).
  - **Moral inaction** — *passive* failure to confront someone else's act (~2/3 non-reporting; powerlessness, learned helplessness, moral disengagement).
- **Five Moral Foundations recognition** — Care/Harm, Fairness/Reciprocity, Ingroup/Loyalty, Authority/Respect, Purity/Sanctity. Trap: people *disagree on weightings*, not on the categories themselves. Distractors often add a sixth ("Liberty" — not on the slide-five list) or drop one.
- **Antidote four pillars recognition** — transparency / checks and balances / process focus / dissent. The four are direct mirrors of the four enabling mechanisms (transparency counters justification; checks-and-balances counters norms; process focus counters outcome focus; dissent counters inaction). J&J 1982 Tylenol = process-focus exemplar.
- **Bounded ethicality vs. hypocrisy** — hypocrisy implies *awareness*; bounded ethicality is the *absence* of awareness. A hypocrite knows they violated their values; the bounded-ethicality actor does not.
- **Obedience (Milgram) vs. moral inaction** — both look passive but: obedience is *active compliance* with a directive (administer the shock); inaction is *passive failure* to intervene when no directive is given.

## Likely exam patterns

- **Scenario stem matching to one of the four enabling mechanisms (highest-frequency expected pattern).** "An anonymous online trader manipulates the market" → moral justification (anonymity facilitator). "Everyone in the office leaves at 4:30 so I do too" → social norms. "Sales reps open fake accounts to hit quota" → outcome-focused culture. "Coworker watches harassment, says nothing" → moral inaction. Expect 2–3 of these on the Final.
- **Moral-foundations recognition.** Stem describes a moral concern and asks which foundation it instantiates. Trap distractors invent foundations not on the slide-five list (e.g., "Liberty," "Honesty," "Justice").
- **Want/should-self scenario application.** Stem describes someone who "swore they'd report any misconduct" but then didn't when the moment came → want self overriding should self at decision time, *not* hypocrisy. The 62% / 0% sexual-harassment study is the reference scenario.
- **Antidote pillars recognition.** Stem describes a company implementing one of the four (e.g., J&J's "First, protect the people" → process focus; SOX-style separation of auditor and auditee → checks and balances). Trap: "compliance training" alone — that is *not* the antidote per slide; culture is.
- **Forecasting vs. fading vs. recollection placement.** Given a behavior, identify which stage of the arc it instantiates. "I'm sure I'd never do that" = forecasting error; "I didn't even see it as an ethical issue" = fading (decision time); "Looking back, I had no real choice" = memory revisionism.
