# Module 8 — Social Identity & Diversity

## What this module is fundamentally about

Two questions:
1. **Why do *employees* care about diversity?** Because they want to be **authentic but not conspicuous** at work. Stigmatization, stereotype threat, and the cost of identity concealment all degrade satisfaction and performance.
2. **Why do *organizations* care about diversity?** Four reasons: (i) keeping employees happy and productive (mirrors #1), (ii) **discrimination and fairness** (Thomas & Ely paradigm 1 — legal/moral), (iii) **access and legitimacy** (paradigm 2 — strategic/market), (iv) **better ideas / innovation** from heterogeneous perspectives.

The Five Tricks card-game exercise grounds the **culture-shock** mechanism and the **J-curve** expectation: diversifying produces short-term conflict and dissatisfaction, then long-term innovation gains.

## Headline empirical points

- **Blind symphony auditions** — putting a screen between the candidate and the judges raised the likelihood of a **female hire by ~25%**; canonical "shape the environment" intervention against System-1 bias.
- **AirBnB first-name acceptance gap** — ~6,400 booking requests, identical except for first name; hosts accepted minority-coded names at lower rates. Stereotype-tax demonstration in a peer-to-peer market.
- **Bertrand & Mullainathan résumé study** — **5,000 résumés** mailed to Boston/Chicago employers; African-American-sounding names received **fewer callbacks** for the same content. THE canonical résumé-callback study.
- **Steele/Aronson stereotype-threat study** — African-American students taking GRE problems performed **worse** when first asked to "tell us what it's like to be African-American on this campus" than when not primed. Replicated with women on math, white men on jump shots — i.e., it travels to whichever group is the stereotyped one on a given task.
- **J.C. Penney store-workforce diversity → store earnings** — diverse workforces in minority-community stores produced higher customer satisfaction and higher store earnings; the slide's anchor for paradigm 2 (access & legitimacy).
- **Project Aristotle** (cross-references to M10) — Google's two-year team-effectiveness study found **psychological safety** as the strongest predictor; ties to M8 because identity-safe environments are the precondition for authentic-but-not-conspicuous experience.
- **Workplace incivility prevalence** — **~3/4 of employees** report workplace incivility in the last year; ~50% of women report sexual harassment over a career; 10–20% of racial minorities report discrimination in their *current* job. Direct respect → performance link: racial-minority salespeople sell more under managers who report valuing diversity.
- **Facebook 44% / 25%** — Asian Americans = **44% of staff but 25% of leadership** at Facebook (Chen WSJ); ~6% of US population. The executive drop-off is the structural evidence that the model-minority myth is *not* a substitute for inclusion.

## Concepts

### Social identity & employee experience
- [[concepts/social-identity-definition]] — how you define who you are ("being ___ is a big part of who I am"); diversity = heterogeneity of identities, so every new person is simultaneously in-group and out-group.
- [[concepts/why-employees-care-diversity]] — employees want to be **authentic but not conspicuous**: "I can be myself here" *and* "people see me as a whole person."
- [[concepts/workplace-stigmatization]] — devaluing of identity at work, from quid-pro-quo harassment down to everyday incivility; ~3/4 of employees report incivility in the last year and racial-minority salespeople sell more under managers who value diversity.
- [[concepts/stereotype-threat]] — *awareness* of a stereotype about your group hurts performance under identity salience (Steele/Aronson GRE study with African-American students; replicated with women on math, white men on jump shots).

### Why orgs care — Thomas & Ely paradigms
- [[concepts/discrimination-and-fairness]] — paradigm 1: the moral/legal case; discrimination need not be intentional (disparate impact, System 1); blind symphony auditions raised female hires ~25%.
- [[concepts/stereotype-tax]] — career cost imposed by *evaluators'* System 1 (5,000-résumé Boston/Chicago callback gap; AirBnB first-name acceptance gap) — the external mirror of stereotype threat.
- [[concepts/access-and-legitimacy]] — paradigm 2: diversity as a *business asset* for serving and being credible in growing minority markets (J.C. Penney store-workforce → store earnings); caveat: pigeonholing if no promotion path.

### Diversifying organizations
- [[concepts/culture-shock]] — disorientation from unrecognized *small* rule differences (Five Tricks card game); reduced by having a partner and by prior shock experience.
- [[concepts/diversifying-expectations]] — J-curve: short-term **conflict + dissatisfaction**, long-term **better ideas**; the early dip is *predicted, not pathological*.
- [[concepts/diversity-innovation-relationship]] — heterogeneous teams take longer and conflict more, but expand the hypothesis space and produce better solutions (murder-mystery exercise; living abroad raises creativity).

## Reading-specific testable points

**Chen — *Asian-American Professionals Push for Visibility* (WSJ, Apr 2021):**
- **Model-minority myth** = the perception that Asian Americans are uniformly prosperous and need no DEI attention. It systematically *excludes* Asian employees from corporate DEI plans even as the **Facebook 44% staff / 25% leadership** ratio shows a clear executive drop-off.
- **Post-Atlanta-spa shootings shift** — after the March 2021 attacks, Asian-American professionals stopped the "blend in" pattern: testimonials at company town halls, lobbying for donations to Asian causes, demanding inclusion in DEI plans.
- **"Worker bees" stereotype** (Henry Fong, LinkedIn VP Legal): "good workers, but not assertive enough to be leaders" — the specific stereotype-tax pattern blocking promotion.
- **1,000 Asian-American business leaders** (incl. Steve Chen, Eric Yuan) — $10M pledge and full-page WSJ ad: "angry and afraid," "tired of being treated as less than American."
- **Kaycee Lai's bubble tea** — hid bubble tea at work to avoid being marked as Asian; canonical authentic-but-not-conspicuous bind.
- **Eric Toda** — walks into rooms and gets assumed to be "the IT guy" or DoorDash; some white colleagues "don't see him as Asian," intending it as a compliment — everyday-incivility end of the stigmatization range.

**Invisibilia — *The New Norm* (NPR, Jun 2016):**
- **McDonald's-Russia smile export (1990, Pushkin Square)** — installing the "American smile" in a culture where smiling at strangers signals dishonesty/insincerity. The deeper challenge of opening McDonald's there wasn't yeast paperwork — it was changing employees' affective defaults.
- **Igor Korganov line** — Russians "were not allowed to be happy in the process" of building the happy future; explains the cultural baseline against which the smile export ran.
- **Alicia Grandey (Penn State org psych)** — coined **emotional labor** (the emotions companies *demand* from employees) and showed sustained **emotional dissonance** (internal state diverging from required external display) drives **burnout, performance errors, exhaustion, health problems**; smiling spreads to customers but produces no clear bottom-line revenue gain.
- **Surface acting vs. deep acting** — surface acting (faking the display) produces dissonance and burnout; deep acting (actually summoning the emotion) is less harmful but harder to sustain. The slide-relevant takeaway: enforced emotional norms have *real* internal costs even when they "work" on customers.
- **"Service with a smile" power asymmetry** — once installed, customers come to expect it and feel free to behave however they want toward employees who must "grin and take it"; structural form of identity-as-employee being devalued.

## High-yield MCQ traps

- **Discrimination & fairness vs. access & legitimacy** — THE Thomas-and-Ely distractor pair. Paradigm 1 = avoid harm / be fair (legal/moral); paradigm 2 = access diverse markets (strategic). Different *justification*, different *policy*. Spanish-speaking reps for the Latino market = A&L; symphony blind auditions = D&F. Same fact pattern can match either paradigm depending on the *stated motive*.
- **Stereotype threat vs. stereotype tax** — threat is the *target's own* performance dropping under identity salience (internal mechanism, Steele/Aronson); tax is the *career cost imposed by others'* System 1 stereotyping (external mechanism, Bertrand & Mullainathan, AirBnB). Pick "threat" when the *target's* performance drops; pick "tax" when an *evaluator* penalizes identity.
- **Stereotype threat vs. workplace stigmatization** — stigmatization is interpersonal devaluing by others; threat is the internal performance hit from identity salience even with no overt mistreatment.
- **Intentional vs. disparate-impact discrimination** — discrimination need not be intentional. The legal standard is whether a practice "operates to exclude"; **disparate impact** + System 1 inference are enough. Hiring primarily via referrals reproduces the demographics of current employees → disparate impact even with no intent.
- **J-curve as predicted, not pathological** — short-term **conflict + employee dissatisfaction** during diversification is the *explicit slide forecast*, not evidence that "diversity isn't working." Long term: better ideas, more successful organization. Distractor: "more harmony than a homogeneous team in the short term" — the slide says the opposite.
- **Authentic but not conspicuous (employee reason)** — distractor: "to comply with anti-discrimination law" (that's the org's reason, paradigm 1); "to access minority markets" (paradigm 2, also org-side). The employee reason is dual: *be myself* AND *be seen as a whole person*.
- **Diversity → innovation vs. access & legitimacy** — better-ideas = *internal* problem-solving from heterogeneity; A&L = *external* market access. Both strategic, opposite directions.
- **Social identity vs. demographic diversity** — demographics = observable categories; social identity = subjective importance attached to them ("being ___ is a big part of who I am").
- **Model-minority myth** (Chen) — keeps Asian Americans *out of* DEI plans, masking the executive drop-off. Trap: assuming the myth is benign because the surface stereotype is "positive."
- **Emotional labor vs. emotional dissonance** (Invisibilia) — labor = the demand the role places on you; dissonance = the gap between internal state and required display. Dissonance, not labor itself, is what drives burnout.

## Likely exam patterns

- **Thomas & Ely paradigm-recognition stems** — given a workplace policy or initiative, identify whether it operationalizes paradigm 1 (D&F) or paradigm 2 (A&L). Cue: motive language. "Avoid harm" / "fair treatment" / "legal compliance" → paradigm 1. "Reach diverse markets" / "credibility with customers" / "business asset" → paradigm 2. J.C. Penney store-workforce example = paradigm 2.
- **Threat-vs-tax scenario** — stem describes either a target whose performance drops under identity salience (threat: GRE-prompt study) or an evaluator producing different outcomes for identical inputs (tax: 5,000 résumés, AirBnB). Pick based on *who is acting* and *which side the cost lands on*.
- **Disparate-impact recognition** — stem describes a facially neutral practice (referral-based hiring, particular qualifications) that *operates to exclude* a protected group with no stated intent to discriminate. Identify as paradigm-1-relevant **disparate impact**; identify Gilbert's antidote ("avoid the situations that produce bias") as the slide-canonical fix.
- **J-curve / "diversifying expectations" recognition** — manager sees short-term conflict and dissatisfaction after diversifying a team and concludes "this isn't working." Correct answer: **predicted, not pathological**; the long-term innovation gain still arrives if the short-term dip isn't reversed. Pair with **culture shock** as the micro-mechanism.
- **Asian-American visibility / model-minority stems** — Chen-style scenario where an Asian-American employee is excluded from the DEI plan because they're seen as "already successful." Identify as model-minority-myth-driven exclusion, paradigm-1 unintentional disparate impact, plus stereotype-tax effects on promotion ("worker bees").
- **Emotional labor / smile-export stems** — McDonald's-Russia or call-center-script-style scenario where employees are required to display emotions inconsistent with their internal state. Identify **emotional dissonance** as the burnout mechanism (Grandey); pair with culture-shock as the J-curve micro-mechanism in cross-cultural import.
