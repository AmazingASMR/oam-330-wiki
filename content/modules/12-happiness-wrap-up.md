# Module 12 — Happiness & Wrap-up

## What this module is fundamentally about

Why pursue happiness, why is it stable despite massive gains in objective quality of life, and what (if anything) can actually move it. The slides build a set-point model anchored by genetics, hedonic adaptation, and social comparison, then identify two intentional levers — social connections (strong and weak) and how money is spent (experiences over things; others over self). The module closes the course with three mega-takeaways: human behavior is predictable, what we think is not always reality, and other people matter.

## Headline empirical points

The numbers, named studies, and exact slide phrasings most likely to anchor a stem.

- **Variance breakdown — 50% / 40% / 10%** (Lyubomirsky-derived, slide-explicit): ~50% genetics (set point) / ~40% intentional activity / ~10% circumstances. The single most testable numeric on this module.
- **Minnesota Twin Studies — 3,200 twins** raised apart; identical twins had similar happiness levels, fraternal twins showed little relationship. The empirical foundation for the genetic ~50% set-point claim. See [[concepts/happiness-stability]].
- **Illinois lottery study — $50K to $1M winners** showed minimal happiness change after ~1 year — no happier than non-winners. The canonical hedonic-adaptation anchor.
- **Marriage adaptation — ~2 years to baseline** (German panel data, n=1,761): the boost from getting married fades back to set point within roughly two years. Adaptation works on positive shocks too, not just negative ones.
- **Olympics silver-vs-bronze** — silver medalists are typically *less* happy than bronze medalists despite the better outcome — bronze compares downward ("I almost didn't medal"), silver compares upward ("I almost won gold"). The canonical social-comparison demo.
- **Dunn $20 prosocial-spending experiment** — students given $20 and told to spend it on themselves vs. on others; the *prosocial* condition produced more happiness, the *opposite* of what observers predicted. Anchor for [[concepts/happiness-improvement]].
- **Brooks family typology — 59% / 22% / 19%** (Rochester–Notre Dame, 2010): cohesive (59%, warm/harmonious — the happy model) / enmeshed (22%, warm but hostile and meddling) / disengaged (19%, cold/controlling/withdrawn). See [[readings/brooks-path-to-happiness]].
- **Williams's three mega-takeaways (verbatim)**:
  1. **Human behavior is predictable** — "humans aren't irrational, just insufficiently modeled."
  2. **What we think is not always reality** — "common sense is necessary, but not sufficient — ground your decisions in solid evidence."
  3. **Other people matter** — social context, relationships, and influence shape outcomes.
- **Kahneman-Deaton — money/happiness decoupling at the top** — slide-explicit quote: "wealth is like health — its utter absence breeds misery, yet having it is no guarantee of happiness." Money matters *at the bottom* (out of poverty) and *decouples at the top* (diminishing returns). Note: the slides do *not* name the popular $75K threshold figure, so do not expect that specific number on the exam — answer with "diminishing returns / decoupling above basic needs," not "$75K." See [[concepts/happiness-vs-objective-quality]] and [[concepts/money-happiness-relationship]].

## Concepts

### Why pursue happiness

- [[concepts/happiness-consequences]] — happiness is a cause, not just a result: happy people have more friends, higher productivity, better health; longevity gain from happiness exceeds the gain from not smoking.
- [[concepts/happiness-vs-objective-quality]] — objective conditions (plumbing, life expectancy, education) have soared but subjective happiness has stayed flat; Kahneman–Deaton: "wealth is like health — its utter absence breeds misery, yet having it is no guarantee of happiness."
- [[concepts/easterlin-paradox]] — the macro version: as a nation's GDP rises over time, average self-reported happiness does not keep pace once basic needs are met; relative position dominates above the threshold.

### What stabilizes vs. moves happiness

- [[concepts/happiness-stability]] — set-point model with three forces: genetics (~50% of variance, Minnesota Twin Studies), hedonic adaptation (lottery winners, marriage, even limb loss return to baseline within ~1 year), social comparison (relative standing, Olympics silver-vs-bronze).
- [[concepts/happiness-improvement]] — ~40% intentional: strong social ties (5+ confidants), weak ties (small talk with strangers), experiences over things (no comparison, hard to adapt, often social), spending on others over self ($20 prosocial-spending experiment).

### Course wrap-up

- [[concepts/mega-takeaways]] — Williams's three closing-slide synthesis points: (1) human behavior is predictable ("not irrational, just insufficiently modeled"), (2) what we think is not always reality (intuition systematically misleads), (3) other people matter.

## Assigned readings

- [[readings/brooks-path-to-happiness]] — Tolstoy's Oblonsky hypothesis: many ways to be unhappy, few to be happy; cohesive (59%) vs. enmeshed (22%) vs. disengaged (19%) families; the narrow path is relationships.
- [[readings/frank-finding-job-you-love]] — Easterlin paradox plus mission alignment, compensating differentials, deliberate practice (Ericsson), and flow (Csikszentmihalyi); pursue work you love because it sustains the practice required for true expertise.

## Reading-specific testable points

- **Brooks — Tolstoy / Oblonsky hypothesis** — "happy families are all alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way." Asymmetric distribution: many ways to be unhappy, few to be happy.
- **Brooks — few ways to be happy** — the narrow path is relational: warm, cohesive social/family ties. The empirical signature is heavy time investment in close ties.
- **Brooks — family typology** (memorize the percentages):
  - **Cohesive — 59%** — warm, harmonious; the happy model.
  - **Enmeshed — 22%** — warm but meddling, hostile, conflict-laden.
  - **Disengaged — 19%** — cold, controlling, withdrawn.
- **Brooks — strong-tie investment** — very happy people socialize 11 more times/year with relatives, 7 more with neighbors, 5 more with friends than the unhappy. They also rate close friendships 54% / family 73% / romance 161% higher.
- **Frank — Easterlin paradox** — once basic obligations are met, more income does *not* straightforwardly buy more happiness; *relative* position dominates. Macro-level (national GDP up, average happiness flat).
- **Frank — mission alignment** — Cornell seniors demanded an >80% pay premium to take a pro-tobacco vs. anti-smoking job. People want their work to make the world better; they will pay (in salary terms) for that alignment. See [[readings/frank-finding-job-you-love]].
- **Frank — compensating differentials** — jobs with better working conditions (autonomy, learning, safety, meaning) systematically *pay less*. The pay gap is the price of the better conditions, not a market failure.
- **Frank — deliberate practice (Ericsson)** — true expertise requires many thousands of hours of effortful, focused practice; only sustainable if you love the task. The mechanism behind the article's headline argument.
- **Frank — flow (Csikszentmihalyi)** — deeply satisfying state of immersion in which time disappears; landing a job with substantial flow is a top-tier outcome. Connects back to [[concepts/flow-state]] from Module 5.
- **Frank — central thesis** — pursue work you love *because it sustains the practice required for true expertise*; expertise + technology eventually pays. Resist the soul-crushing higher-paying job for the more satisfying lower-paying one.

## High-yield MCQ traps

- **Subjective happiness vs. objective quality of life (Easterlin)** — objective indicators (GDP, plumbing, life expectancy, education) have soared 1940 → today; subjective happiness is essentially flat. Macro-level Easterlin paradox: GDP up, happiness flat. Trap distractors: "money never matters" (false — utter absence breeds misery) or "GDP linearly predicts happiness" (false). Anchor quote: Kahneman-Deaton "wealth is like health…"
- **Genetics vs. adaptation vs. social comparison — three set-point drivers** — given a vignette, identify which mechanism it illustrates:
  - **Genetics** — stable baseline regardless of life events; Minnesota Twin Studies; "born that way."
  - **Adaptation** — *absolute level* resets after a shock (lottery winners ~1 year, marriage ~2 years, even limb loss within a year).
  - **Social comparison** — *relative standing* determines feeling (Olympics silver < bronze; "$50k in a $25k neighborhood vs. more in a richer one"; Vladimir's Choice).
  Trap: confusing adaptation (you adjust to the new level) with social comparison (you compare to a referent).
- **Strong vs. weak ties — different mechanisms** — strong ties (5+ close confidants, family, romance) provide deep emotional support and are the dominant predictor of "very happy." Weak ties (small talk with strangers, acquaintances, the barista) raise well-being via micro-positive interactions even though most people *avoid* them. Both help; the *mechanisms* differ. Trap: claiming weak ties don't matter, or that only strong ties do.
- **Experiences vs. things vs. prosocial spending — three distinct improvement levers**:
  - **Experiences > material goods** — three reasons: (1) no easy comparison, (2) hard to adapt to, (3) often social.
  - **Spending on others > self** — Dunn $20 study; prosocial spending predicts happiness, self-spending does not.
  - These are *separate* findings. A trap distractor might fuse them ("experiences are good because they're prosocial").
- **Three mega-takeaways verbatim recognition** — distractors paraphrase incorrectly:
  - "Humans are irrational" — **wrong**; the slide says *insufficiently modeled*.
  - "Common sense is sufficient" — **wrong**; necessary but not sufficient.
  - "Other people matter" — correct as stated; sometimes presented as "social context shapes outcomes."
- **Easterlin paradox vs. Kahneman-Deaton** — Easterlin = *macro* / *over time / cross-national* (GDP up, happiness flat). Kahneman-Deaton = *individual-level* / *cross-sectional* (money matters at the bottom, decouples at the top — "wealth is like health"). Both point at the same diminishing-returns truth, at different scales. Do *not* answer "$75K" — slides do not name that figure.

## Likely exam patterns

- **50/40/10 split numeric recall.** Direct stem: "What are the approximate variance contributions to happiness?" → 50% genetics / 40% intentional / 10% circumstances. Trap distractors swap numbers (e.g., 40% circumstances, 10% intentional — the inversion).
- **Mega-takeaways verbatim recognition.** "Which of the following is NOT a mega-takeaway of OAM 330?" or "Which best summarizes the course?" — memorize the three exactly. Strongest distractor: "humans are irrational" (slide says the opposite).
- **Spending-category scenarios — which type maximizes happiness?** Stem offers experiences / material goods / prosocial / self-spending and asks which is most likely to improve happiness. Correct: experiences (with three reasons) and prosocial (Dunn $20).
- **Set-point-stability mechanism recognition.** Vignette-to-mechanism: "Lottery winner reports same happiness as before within a year" → adaptation. "Bronze medalist happier than silver" → social comparison. "Identical twins separated at birth show similar happiness" → genetics.
- **Synthesis stems across modules.** The wrap-up module is prime ground for synthesis questions: "Other people matter" + Project Aristotle (M10) + social influence (M6) + diversity (M8). Or: Frank's flow (M12) ↔ flow state (M5) ↔ deliberate practice. Watch for stems that pull two or three modules together.
