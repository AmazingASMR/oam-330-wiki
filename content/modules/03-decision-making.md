# Module 3 — Decision Making

## What this module is fundamentally about

**Master frame: [[concepts/system-1-system-2|System 1 vs. System 2]]** (Kahneman). Two cognitive systems run *simultaneously* — System 1 fast/automatic/limbic, System 2 slow/effortful/prefrontal — and System 2 frequently doesn't know when System 1 is operating. Every bias in this module is a **System 1 malfunction** the conscious System 2 fails to catch. Slides explicitly: *"We frequently think we are making a more rational, data-driven decision than we are."*

The module is organized around the **stages of decision making**, with biases at each stage:

1. **Perception / seeing the evidence** — [[concepts/perception-vs-reality]], [[concepts/motivated-perception]], [[concepts/self-fulfilling-prophecies]].
2. **Obtaining & interpreting the evidence** — [[concepts/availability-heuristic]], [[concepts/confirmation-bias]], [[concepts/halo-effects]].
3. **Using evidence to make decisions** — split into two sub-buckets per slide 5 of deck 05:
   - *"We want to think well of ourselves"* → [[concepts/overconfidence]], [[concepts/hindsight-bias]].
   - *"We're not computers"* → [[concepts/anchoring-bias]], [[concepts/framing-and-risk]], [[concepts/sunk-cost-fallacy]].
4. **Debiasing** — [[concepts/reducing-decision-biases]] (Gilbert: "the only reliable method for avoiding bias is to avoid the situations that produce it").

Recurring across the rest of the course: System 1/System 2 returns in **Influence & Persuasion** (peripheral route = System 1 cues) and **Ethics** (ethical fading is System 1).

## Headline empirical points

- **Availability — 9/11 driving fatalities:** **2,170** additional fatal car accidents (people drove instead of flew) — nearly matching the **2,606** Twin Towers deaths. Vivid plane-crash coverage made flying feel more dangerous than it was.
- **Framing — Asian disease problem (Kahneman & Tversky):** lives-saved frame → **81%** pick the sure thing (Plan A); deaths frame → **76%** pick the gamble (Plan B). Plans A and B are **mathematically identical** across frames. χ²(N=57) = 14.92, p < .001.
- **Framing — gain/loss gamble:** "$900 sure" vs. "90% chance to get $1,000" → **79%** sure thing (risk-averse for gains); "lose $900 sure" vs. "90% chance to lose $1,000" → **81%** gamble (risk-seeking for losses). χ²(N=64) = 23.40, p < .001.
- **Anchoring — Gandhi study:** "more/less than **114**" anchor → mean estimate **79.4 yrs**; "more/less than **35**" anchor → mean estimate **70.7 yrs**. Same person; anchor shifted answers by ~9 years. F(1,63) = 6.90, p = .01. Correct answer was 78.
- **Anchoring — iPad pricing:** Apple announced "just **$499**" after pundits anchored on under-$1,000 → $499 felt cheap by contrast.
- **Hindsight — Playboy magazine circulation study:** without answer shown, **32%** picked Playboy (68% Time); with the answer ("Playboy") shown in boldface, **66%** then "knew" Playboy was the answer. χ²(N=57) = 6.35, p = .017.
- **Motivated perception — Bruner & Goodman 1947 / horse-or-seal study:** **50/50** split when perception not tied to outcomes; **70%** "saw" the animal that led to orange juice (vs. nasty smoothie).
- **Halo — outcome-based CEO trait flip:** successful company → CEO described as *"flexible, methodical, and decisive"*; failing company → same kind of CEO described as *"confused, rigid, and authoritarian."* Trait labels follow the outcome, not the actual behavior.
- **Overconfidence — better-than-average effect (slide demo, N=63):** OAM 330 students rated own niceness 3.9, driving 3.6, academic ability 3.8 (all above the 3.0 midpoint). Statistically impossible at scale.
- **Planning fallacy — Buehler:** even at **99% confidence**, fewer than half of student projects finish on time. Japanese-students study: predicted finishing essays 10 days before deadline; actual average was 1 day before.
- **Reference-class forecasting — UK DfT 2004:** average overruns dropped to **~7%** after mandated outside-view forecasting (Flyvbjerg).
- **Gilbert (2006), verbatim:** "Because the brain cannot see itself fooling itself, the only reliable method for avoiding bias is to **avoid the situations that produce it**." Awareness alone fails.
- **Slide framing:** "We frequently think we are making a more rational, data-driven decision than we are."

## Concepts

### Perception-stage biases

- [[concepts/perception-vs-reality]] — perception ≠ reality; the input is biased before any thinking starts (optical illusions, "what do you see?" priming).
- [[concepts/motivated-perception]] — we see what we expect or want to see (Bruner & Goodman 1947; horse-or-seal study went from 50/50 to 70% toward the outcome that earned orange juice).
- [[concepts/self-fulfilling-prophecies]] — expectations change *the target's* actual behavior, which then "confirms" the expectation ("HOT! NOT." voice study; Pygmalion effect).

### Evidence-handling biases

- [[concepts/availability-heuristic]] — judging by what comes easily to mind (9/11 driving fatalities: 2,170 vs. 2,606 Twin Towers deaths; vivid plane-crash coverage made flying feel more dangerous than it was).
- [[concepts/confirmation-bias]] — actively seeking confirming, not disconfirming, evidence (introvert/extravert interview-question study).
- [[concepts/halo-effects]] — one good trait → assumed good elsewhere (Kahneman's "exaggerated emotional coherence"; CEO trait-flip on success/failure; attractiveness/height earnings premium).

### Decision-stage biases (ego-protective)

- [[concepts/overconfidence]] — wildly overconfident in our own abilities and predictions; includes Dunning-Kruger (less skill → MORE overconfidence), planning fallacy (best-case forecasts), and better-than-average effect.
- [[concepts/hindsight-bias]] — "knew it all along"; outcome reshapes recalled prior belief (magazine-circulation study: 32% → 66% Playboy guesses with answer revealed).

### Decision-stage biases (we're not computers)

- [[concepts/anchoring-bias]] — initial number contaminates numeric estimate even when irrelevant (Gandhi 114 vs. 35 anchor → 79.4 vs. 70.7 yrs; iPad "just $499").
- [[concepts/framing-and-risk]] — gain frame → risk-averse, loss frame → risk-seeking, even with identical net outcomes (Asian disease problem, Kahneman & Tversky).
- [[concepts/sunk-cost-fallacy]] — over-weighting past investment (lost-ticket vs. lost-cash mirror; Concorde, Vietnam, William Chan / Gourmet Food consultant).

### Debiasing

- [[concepts/reducing-decision-biases]] — five fixes: be aware (insufficient), allow time, broaden samples, present info multiple ways, minimize ego stakes; awareness alone is not enough (Gilbert: "the brain cannot see itself fooling itself").
- [[concepts/reference-class-forecasting]] — Flyvbjerg's operationalized outside view: identify reference class of similar past projects → build distribution of overruns → locate yours → apply uplift. UK DfT 2004 adoption dropped average overruns to ~7%.

## Reading-specific testable points

Items not enshrined as concept pages but MCQ-eligible because every study-guide section has a "Content of readings" bullet:

**From [[readings/dobelli-art-of-thinking-clearly]] (Ch 5 sunk cost & Ch 14 hindsight):**
- **Concorde effect** — alias for the sunk-cost fallacy; Britain & France kept funding the supersonic-jet failure to save face. Named alias is MCQ-bait.
- **Need for consistency** — Dobelli's psychological engine of sunk-cost behavior; we hate admitting our prior selves were wrong.
- **Great-uncle's diary in occupied Paris (1940)** — predicted Germans would leave by year-end; occupation lasted 4 years. Diaries reveal the actual uncertainty hindsight erases.
- **2007 economists / 2008 crash** — same experts then list the "obvious" causes (Greenspan, lax mortgages, rating agencies); causes look inevitable only after the fact.
- **Awareness-alone-fails refrain** — "studies have shown that people who are aware of [hindsight] fall for it just as much as everyone else" — directly supports the Gilbert quote on the slide.

**From [[readings/freakonomics-planning-fallacy]] (*Freakonomics Radio*):**
- **Reference-class forecasting** (Flyvbjerg) — operationalized outside view: identify reference class → build distribution of overruns → locate your project → apply uplift. UK DfT 2004 → ~7% overruns.
- **Algorithm aversion** (Milkman) — refusing to defer to a model even when it's demonstrably more accurate than expert judgment. Pairs with Kahneman's "algorithms beat intuition" thesis.
- **Buehler's 99%-confidence studies** — fewer than half of student projects finish on time even at the 99% confidence level. Headline planning-fallacy stat.
- **Strategic misrepresentation** — deliberate (not innocent) lowballing to win project approval; distinct from honest forecasting error.
- **Implementation intentions** — concrete *when/where/how* plans that reduce slippage at the individual level.
- **Inside view vs. outside view** (Kahneman & Tversky) — inside = bottom-up, this-project specifics; outside = look at the historical reference class.
- **Second Avenue Subway** — 1968 plan = 8.5 miles, 15 stations; 2016 reality = 2 miles, 3 stations, ~48 years late, $4.5B. Iconic case at scale.
- **Christmas-shopping study** — more *detailed* planning produced more *optimistic* (and less accurate) forecasts. Counterintuitive trap: detailed planning makes things *worse*.
- **Optimism bias** (Sharot) — systematic expectation of rosier futures than evidence warrants; absent in depression. Has motivational benefits.

**From [[readings/kahneman-dont-blink]] (*NYT Magazine*):**
- **Illusion of validity** — the subjective sense that one's judgment is accurate, driven by the *coherence* of the impression rather than its predictive power. Coined from Kahneman's Israeli Army officer-selection experience.
- **Illusion of skill** — collective overconfidence in domains (e.g., stock picking) where outcomes are largely random; persists because the social environment validates the practice. From the 8-year stock-picker firm review (year-to-year correlations near zero, bonuses paid as if rankings reflected skill).
- **Substitution** — System 1 substitutes an easier question (does this officer impress me right now?) for the hard one asked (will this officer succeed?). Textbook System 1 move.
- **Algorithms beat intuition** — in low-validity environments, simple statistical rules outperform expert judgment; experts resist this finding. Tetlock cited approvingly: pundits no better than chance, the *most famous* often the least accurate.
- **Israeli Army leaderless group task** — the structured exercise that produced confident judgments barely better than chance against later officer-school outcomes.

## High-yield MCQ traps

- **[[concepts/availability-heuristic]] vs. [[concepts/confirmation-bias]]** — availability = passive *recall* ease (what pops up); confirmation = active *seeking* (what we go look for). The single most common M3 distractor pair.
- **[[concepts/anchoring-bias]] vs. [[concepts/framing-and-risk]]** — anchoring shifts a *starting number* (magnitude); framing shifts *risk preference* by gain/loss wording. Both are "biases in numeric decision making" per slide bucket — different mechanism.
- **[[concepts/anchoring-bias]] vs. [[concepts/sunk-cost-fallacy]]** — anchoring = *initial reference number*; sunk cost = *past investment* of money/time/effort. Both involve over-weighting prior info, different mechanism.
- **[[concepts/overconfidence]] vs. [[concepts/hindsight-bias]]** — overconfidence is *forward* ("I'll predict right"); hindsight is *backward* ("I knew it all along"). They reinforce each other but are distinct directionally.
- **[[concepts/halo-effects]] vs. [[concepts/self-fulfilling-prophecies]]** — halo = perceiver-side trait generalization (you misjudge); SFP = target's *actual behavior* changes in response to expectations. Halo is illusory; SFP is real behavioral change.
- **[[concepts/halo-effects]] vs. [[concepts/hindsight-bias]]** — both depend on outcome knowledge, but halo recolors *traits of the actor* (decisive vs. rigid CEO); hindsight recolors *predictability of the outcome*.
- **[[concepts/halo-effects]] vs. [[concepts/confirmation-bias]]** — halo generalizes across *traits of one target*; confirmation generalizes evidence search toward *a belief about anything*.
- **[[concepts/perception-vs-reality]] vs. [[concepts/motivated-perception]]** — perception/reality is the general claim (we don't see reality directly); motivated perception is the specific case where *desires* shape perception.
- **[[concepts/motivated-perception]] vs. [[concepts/confirmation-bias]]** — motivated perception bends what we *see in the first place* (input stage); confirmation bias bends what we *seek and weigh* (evidence-gathering stage).
- **[[concepts/sunk-cost-fallacy]] vs. [[concepts/framing-and-risk]]** — sunk cost = pull of *already-spent* resources; framing = present option presentation. Lost-ticket vs. lost-cash is the canonical mirror pair.
- **Dunning-Kruger direction** — **less skill → MORE overconfidence**. Students often invert this; novices feel more certain than experts.
- **System 1/System 2 run sequentially?** — NO, **simultaneously**. Distractor: "first System 1 fires, then System 2 corrects."
- **"Just be more aware" debiasing answer** — **wrong**. Gilbert: "the brain cannot see itself fooling itself; the only reliable method … is to **avoid the situations that produce it**." Awareness alone is insufficient.
- **"Three alternatives explain a correlation"** in a M3 stem — that's an M2 trap leaking into M3 territory; verify the stem is actually about correlation, not bias.

## Likely exam patterns

- **Scenario-application stems** — by far the most common M3 question type. Williams describes a workplace situation ("a manager remembers the salespeople who made big sales recently and concludes those are the top performers") and asks which bias is at work. Memorize one trigger phrase per bias: *vivid recall* → availability; *seeking confirming evidence* → confirmation; *one trait colors all judgments* → halo; *expectations changed behavior* → SFP; *initial number contaminates estimate* → anchoring; *gain/loss wording flips risk* → framing; *throwing good money after bad* → sunk cost; *I knew it all along* → hindsight; *better-than-average self-rating* → overconfidence.
- **Comparison stems for sister biases** — pairs two related biases and asks the distinguishing feature. The distractor pairs above are MCQ-bait one item at a time. Practice the distinguishing feature aloud for each.
- **Numeric-recall stems** — quote one of the headline anchors (2,170 / 2,606; 81%/76%; 79.4 vs. 70.7 yrs from 114/35 anchors; 32% → 66%; ~7% UK DfT) and ask which bias it illustrates. Memorize the number-to-bias map.
- **"How would you reduce this bias?" stems** — "be more aware" is **wrong** (Gilbert quote). Right answers are *structural*: allow time, broaden samples, present info multiple ways, minimize ego stakes, use algorithms / reference-class forecasting.
- **Verbatim-quote / cloze stems** — Kahneman's "exaggerated emotional coherence" (halo); Gilbert's "brain cannot see itself fooling itself"; "we frequently think we are making a more rational, data-driven decision than we are."
- **Reading-specific stems** — Concorde effect (Dobelli) → sunk cost; reference-class forecasting / algorithm aversion / Buehler 99%-confidence (Freakonomics) → planning fallacy / overconfidence; illusion of validity / illusion of skill / substitution (Kahneman) → expert overconfidence. Map term to source author and to slide concept.
