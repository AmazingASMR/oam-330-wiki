# Module 2 — Empirical Approaches to OB

## What this module is fundamentally about

Module 2 is the **methods spine** of the course: how do we know what we know about behavior in organizations? Williams introduces the three course themes — common sense is necessary but not sufficient, behavior is predictable, and **empirical evidence outperforms other types** — and then operationalizes Theme 3 by walking up the evidence pyramid from anecdote to RCT.

Two reasons empirical evidence wins: (1) the **N of 1 problem** (you can't generalize from one company or one CEO), and (2) **correlation ≠ causation** (with three alternative explanations: spurious, reverse direction, and third variable). Only a **randomized controlled trial** with **random assignment** can establish causation, because random assignment averages out all other variables — so the IV is the only thing that could have caused the effect.

When experiments aren't possible (impractical, impossible, or unethical), OB falls back on **quasi-experiments** (natural or existing divisions) or **correlational studies**, which require explicit **control variables** to rule out third-variable confounds.

The biggest MCQ landmines: random assignment vs. random sampling; the three (not four) alternative explanations for a correlation; and distinguishing RCT vs. quasi-experiment vs. correlational study.

## Headline empirical points

- **The three course themes (verbatim):** (1) "Common sense is necessary — but not sufficient — for predicting human behavior in organizations." (2) "Behavior is predictable." (3) "Empirical evidence outperforms other types." Memorize all three word-for-word.
- **The three alternative explanations to "X causes Y" (slide 25):** **spurious** / **reverse causal direction** / **third variable**. Exactly three. Trap distractors: "measurement error," "selection bias," "chance" (chance ≈ spurious, but Williams' label is *spurious*).
- **The three obstacles to experimentation (slide 14):** **impractical** / **impossible** / **unethical**. Exactly three. Trap distractor: "expense" (subsumed under impractical, NOT a 4th category).
- **Evidence pyramid order (bottom → top):** anecdote / N-of-1 → correlation → causal/RCT. "The higher up you go, the more accurately you can predict the **NEXT** event."
- **Two reasons empirical evidence wins:** Reason #1 = [[concepts/n-of-1-problem]] (can't generalize from one case); Reason #2 = [[concepts/correlation-vs-causation]] (even with many cases, can't infer causation without manipulation).
- **Two RCT criteria (slide 8):** (1) experimenter has **control over the IV**, (2) **random assignment** to conditions including a control/comparison group.
- **Harrah's casino RCT (slide 11):** $60 chips (experimental) vs. $125 promo package = room + 2 steak dinners + $30 chips (control). Random assignment of casino-goers → $60 offer generated **more revenue at lower cost**. Canonical RCT example.
- **N-of-1 examples (slide):** Steve Jobs' turtleneck, GE's rank-and-yank, "Herb Kelleher drinks Wild Turkey." Pfeffer & Sutton's mockery of benchmarking from a single company.
- **Reading-to-kids correlation (slide 18-19):** kids read aloud to + restricted online time correlated with reading for fun. Possible third variables to control: parents' reading enjoyment, books in home, free time, library distance, parental income.
- **Slide 20:** "More memorable ≠ more useful." Anecdotes feel sticky; that's not validity.

## Concepts

### Defining OB

- [[concepts/ob-definition-and-goals]] — OB as the systematic, empirical study of behavior in organizations; goals are predict, influence, and rely on empirical evidence.
- [[concepts/input-outcome-variables]] — IV (input/predictor) vs. DV (outcome being predicted); a sufficiently complex model sums many inputs (agreeableness, job type, mentoring, bonus, …) to predict the outcome.
- [[concepts/three-course-themes]] — common sense is necessary but not sufficient (T1); behavior is predictable (T2); empirical evidence outperforms other types (T3).
- [[concepts/empirical-evidence-utility]] — empirical evidence = systematic observation/testing; outperforms hearsay, intuition, authority, anecdote because more memorable ≠ more useful.

### Why empirical evidence wins

- [[concepts/n-of-1-problem]] — Reason #1: you can't generalize from a single case (Steve Jobs' turtleneck, GE's rank-and-yank, Herb Kelleher's Wild Turkey).
- [[concepts/correlation-vs-causation]] — Reason #2: even reliable correlations don't prove causation; only an RCT with random assignment does.
- [[concepts/alternative-explanations-correlation]] — exactly three alternatives to "X causes Y": spurious / reverse direction / third variable (NOT measurement error or selection bias).
- [[concepts/evidence-hierarchy]] — pyramid from anecdote → correlation → causal/RCT; "the higher up you go, the more accurately you can predict the NEXT event."

### Methods

- [[concepts/rcts-experiments]] — only design that supports causation; two criteria are control over IV + random assignment to conditions including a control/comparison group (Harrah's $60 chips vs. $125 package).
- [[concepts/random-assignment]] — equal-chance allocation to conditions; averages out irrelevant variables across groups so the IV is the only thing that could have caused any difference. **Not** the same as random sampling.
- [[concepts/experiment-obstacles]] — three reasons RCTs aren't always possible: impractical, impossible, unethical (personality traits, regional offices, salary).
- [[concepts/quasi-experiments-correlational]] — fallback designs when RCTs aren't possible: quasi uses natural/existing divisions (no random assignment); correlational just measures variables as they exist.
- [[concepts/control-variables]] — measured covariates added to a regression to *rule out* alternative (third-variable) explanations; what random assignment does for free in an RCT.

## High-yield MCQ traps

- **[[concepts/random-assignment]] vs. random sampling** — the single biggest M2 landmine. *Assignment* = how subjects go into conditions (internal validity / causation). *Sampling* = how subjects are selected from a population (external validity / generalizability). Most labs have one but not the other. Distractors will swap them on a study-description stem.
- **[[concepts/rcts-experiments]] vs. [[concepts/quasi-experiments-correlational|quasi-experiment]] vs. correlational study** — RCT manipulates IV + randomly assigns; quasi uses *natural/existing* division (no random assignment); correlational only *measures* variables as they exist. Quasi cannot establish causation cleanly. Trap: "different branches with no manipulation" → quasi-experiment, NOT RCT.
- **The three alternative explanations** ([[concepts/alternative-explanations-correlation]]) — **exactly three**: spurious / reverse direction / third variable. Trap distractors: "measurement error," "selection bias," "chance." If the stem says "four alternatives," the answer is wrong.
- **Three obstacles to experimentation** ([[concepts/experiment-obstacles]]) — **exactly three**: impractical / impossible / unethical. Trap distractor: "expense" or "cost" as a 4th category.
- **[[concepts/n-of-1-problem]] vs. correlation problem** — N-of-1 = not *enough* cases (Reason #1); correlation = even with many cases, can't infer causation without manipulation (Reason #2). Two distinct reasons; trap swaps which is which.
- **[[concepts/control-variables]] vs. control group** — completely different. Control *variable* = a measured covariate added to a regression. Control *group* = the no-treatment condition in an experiment. Distractor will conflate them.
- **[[concepts/control-variables]] vs. random assignment** — both rule out third variables, but random assignment handles all confounds (including unmeasured ones); controls only handle the specific variables you measured. Random assignment > controls.
- **Spurious vs. third variable** — spurious = literal coincidence (no causal mechanism). Third variable = there *is* a cause, but it's a hidden Z, not X. Trap: defining spurious as "Z causes both."
- **More memorable ≠ more useful** — anecdotes are sticky but low on the [[concepts/evidence-hierarchy|pyramid]]. Distractor: claiming a vivid case study counts as strong evidence.
- **OB vs. common sense** — common sense is *necessary but not sufficient*. Distractor: "common sense is sufficient" (denied by Theme 1).

## Likely exam patterns

- **Definition-match stems** — "Which of the following best describes a quasi-experiment?" / "Which design supports causation?" Memorize the one-line definition for each method type (RCT, quasi, correlational, control variable, control group, random assignment, random sampling).
- **Exact-list recognition stems** — "Which of the following is NOT one of the three alternative explanations?" / "Which is NOT one of the three obstacles to experimentation?" / "Which is NOT one of the three course themes?" The trap is always a real concept that's just not on Williams' list.
- **Random-assignment-vs-sampling distractor** — almost guaranteed. Practice the "internal validity / external validity" distinction cold.
- **Scenario-classification stems** — Williams describes a study (e.g., "researchers compared employee outcomes at the Atlanta branch before and after a new wellness policy") and asks which design type it is. Map keywords: "natural division" → quasi; "manipulated + randomly assigned" → RCT; "measured as they exist" → correlational.
- **Verbatim-quote / cloze stems** for the three themes and the irrationality framing ("insufficiently modeled," "necessary but not sufficient," "outperforms other types," "the higher up you go on the pyramid, the more accurately you can predict the NEXT event").
- **N-of-1 vignette stems** — Steve Jobs / GE / Herb Kelleher type story; the right answer is "this is N-of-1 reasoning" or "you cannot generalize."
