# Module 1 — About Organizational Behavior

## What this module is fundamentally about

Module 1 is the course's framing lecture: it defines **organizational behavior** as the use of scientific methodologies to understand and predict human behavior at work, motivates *why* OB is taught in every BBA/MBA program (career success = task skills + management skills), and introduces the first of the **three course themes** ("Common sense is necessary — but not sufficient — for predicting human behavior in organizations"). It also previews the **input/outcome variable** machinery that Module 2 develops in detail. Slide 01 closes by setting up Theme 2 ("Behavior is predictable") for the next class. Almost everything in M1 is reinforced and operationalized in M2, which is why the study guide starts at "Empirical Approaches" — but the framing here recurs on the cumulative final.

## Headline empirical points

- **Career success = task skills + management skills** — the verbatim slide framing for *why* OB is required in every BBA/MBA program. "As long as organizations are made up of people …"
- **~80% of companies use OB-style measures** (engagement surveys, climate surveys, leadership 360s) and spend roughly **$2-3 billion/year** on them — the field is institutionally enormous, not a side discipline.
- **Cambridge alumni survey** — alums rated *Management & Strategy* courses **4.0/5** for usefulness vs. **3.5/5** for other core courses; the empirical case from former students that OB content pays off.
- **Economist Foresight 20/20 study, N = 1,656 senior executives** — surveyed what executives say drives organizational success; named-N is MCQ-bait.
- **Every top BBA/MBA program** (Stanford GSB, Haas, Kellogg, Wharton, HBS, Booth) requires an OB-style course — universal, not optional.
- **Theme 1 verbatim:** "Common sense is necessary — but not sufficient — for predicting human behavior in organizations." Cloze-stem ready.
- **Williams' irrationality framing:** "Humans aren't irrational … just **insufficiently modeled**." "Human behavior is complex … but **not random**."
- **OB definition (slide):** "Organizational behavior … uses **scientific methodologies** to understand and predict human behavior at work … aims to **maximize individual and organizational success**."
- **Goals of OB (3):** **predict** behavior, **influence** behavior, rely on **empirical evidence** over hearsay/intuition.
- **Slide 20-21 preview:** input (independent) variable / outcome (dependent) variable framing; "a sufficiently complex model" sums many inputs to predict the outcome.

## High-yield MCQ traps

- **"Common sense is ___ but not ___"** cloze — fill with **necessary** / **sufficient**. Distractor: "useful but not relevant," "necessary but not important." Verbatim slide phrase.
- **"Humans aren't irrational, just ___"** cloze — fill with **insufficiently modeled**. Distractor: "irrationally biased," "emotionally driven," "predictably irrational" (Ariely's phrase, NOT Williams'). Verbatim slide phrase.
- **"Behavior is complex … but ___"** cloze — fill with **not random**. Distractor: "but learnable," "but trainable."
- **Why OB is taught in every B-school** — answer = success requires task skills **AND** management skills. Distractor: "to comply with accreditation," "because HR departments need OB grads," "to teach finance majors interpersonal skills."
- **Number of course themes** — exactly **three**, not four. Distractor: a 4th fake theme like "leaders matter most," "culture eats strategy," "people are the most important asset."
- **OB vs. HR confusion** — OB is the **underlying behavioral science**; HR is one applied domain that uses it. MCQ trap: framing OB as a sub-field of HR.
- **OB goals (3):** predict / influence / use empirical evidence. Distractor: "control," "maximize profit," "manage diversity" — not on Williams' list.
- **Theme 1 vs. Theme 3 confusion** — Theme 1 = common sense is *insufficient*; Theme 3 = the *better alternative* is empirical evidence (not authority, tradition, or intuition).
- **IV vs. DV mapping** — DV *depends on* the IV; mnemonic. Trap: swapping which is which on a study-description stem.

## Likely exam patterns

- **Cloze / verbatim-quote stems** are the dominant M1 question type. The four anchor quotes at the bottom of this page are MCQ-bait one fill-in-the-blank at a time. Memorize them word-for-word.
- **Definition-match stems** for OB itself: "Which of the following best defines organizational behavior?" — the right answer always names *systematic/empirical* study + *organizations*.
- **Theme-recognition stems** — Williams gives a quote or example and asks which of the three themes it expresses. Practice mapping examples to T1 / T2 / T3.
- **"Why is OB required?" stems** — the right answer always pairs **task skills** with **management skills** (or "as long as organizations are made up of people"). All other answers are distractors.
- **N-recall stems** for the supporting evidence: Cambridge **4.0 vs. 3.5**, Economist **N = 1,656**, top-school list. Numbers may appear in stem or in answer choices.
