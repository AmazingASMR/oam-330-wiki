# Module 10 — Groups & Teams

## What this module is fundamentally about

How collections of interdependent individuals turn into functioning (or dysfunctional) groups, and how the structure managers impose around them shapes performance. The unit defines what a group is, traces how groups develop (punctuated equilibrium), enumerates the six properties (roles, norms, status, size, cohesiveness, diversity), and then drills into group decision making — its strengths, its failure modes (groupthink, groupshift, social loafing, common-knowledge effect), and the techniques (interacting / brainstorming / nominal group) that can mitigate them. The second class extends to organizational structure: span of control, mechanistic vs. organic models, and the tradeoffs hierarchy creates between efficiency, creativity, and satisfaction. Builds on Module 8 (Social Identity) and threads back to Decision Making (Module 3).

## Headline empirical points

The numbers and named studies most likely to anchor a stem.

- **Asch conformity — 75% conformed at least once** across the line-judgment trials; only ~25% never yielded. The number is the canonical anchor for normative social influence and norm-enforcement on groups. See [[concepts/asch-conformity]] and [[concepts/roles-and-norms]].
- **Hawthorne bank-wiring room** — informal worker norms capped output ("rate-busters" and "chiselers" both sanctioned) even though formal incentives rewarded more output. The empirical proof that *norms can override formal incentives*, and the canonical example of the cohesion-times-norms interaction.
- **Project Aristotle — 180+ Google teams** studied; *who* was on the team mattered far less than the team's *norms*, and **psychological safety** (Edmondson) was the dominant productive norm. Produced by **equal turn-taking** in conversation and **high social sensitivity** (reading emotional cues). See [[concepts/psychological-safety]] and [[readings/duhigg-google-perfect-team]].
- **Edmondson's psychological-safety construct** — shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking (asking dumb questions, admitting mistakes, dissenting) without humiliation. *Equal turn-taking* and *social sensitivity* are the two empirical signatures.
- **Grogan Air / hidden-profile task** — when information is divided across members, ~75% of groups pick the *wrong* VP candidate because they over-discuss what everyone already knows and under-share unique info. Anchor for the [[concepts/common-knowledge-effect]].
- **Punctuated equilibrium — midpoint trigger** — temporary deadline-driven groups hit a transition at the **midpoint of allotted time, regardless of total duration** (1-hour or 6-month projects both flip at the halfway mark). Phase 1 inertia → midpoint awakening → Phase 2 inertia → final-meeting burst. See [[concepts/punctuated-equilibrium-model]].
- **Exhibit 9-4 cohesion × norms 2×2** — productivity is *cohesion times performance norms*, not cohesion alone:
  - High cohesion + high norms → **highest productivity**.
  - High cohesion + low norms → **lowest productivity** (worst case; Hawthorne bank wiring).
  - Low cohesion + high norms → moderate-to-high.
  - Low cohesion + low norms → moderate-to-low.
- **Brainstorming production blocking** — individuals working alone generate *more and better* ideas than equivalent face-to-face brainstorming groups; production blocking (one-at-a-time talk interferes with thinking), evaluation apprehension, and free-riding are the mechanisms. See [[concepts/group-brainstorming]].
- **NGT > brainstorming** — Nominal Group Technique (silent individual idea generation → round-robin sharing → group discussion → independent ranking) outperforms open brainstorming on idea *count and quality* by removing production blocking.

## Concepts

### What is a group

- [[concepts/group-definition]] — two or more individuals, interacting and interdependent, who have come together to achieve certain objectives; formal vs. informal vs. mere aggregate.
- [[concepts/social-identity-in-groups]] — self-esteem becomes tied to group fate; ingroup favoritism (not outgroup hostility) drives most discrimination; "we/us" CEO language.
- [[concepts/punctuated-equilibrium-model]] — temporary deadline-driven groups: first-meeting direction → Phase 1 inertia → midpoint transition → Phase 2 inertia → final-meeting burst.

### Group properties

- [[concepts/roles-and-norms]] — roles tell members what to do, norms tell them how; Asch (75% conformed at least once) and Hawthorne bank-wiring room are the canonical demos.
- [[concepts/social-loafing]] — individuals expend less effort working collectively than alone; high in Western/individualist cultures, lower in collectivist; five prevention levers (group goals, peer evaluations, individual identifiability, etc.).
- [[concepts/group-cohesion]] — shared bond keeping members together; productivity depends on cohesion × performance norms (Exhibit 9-4 2×2) — high cohesion + low norms is the worst case.
- [[concepts/psychological-safety]] — Edmondson's shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking; Project Aristotle's headline finding; produced by equal turn-taking + high social sensitivity.

### Group decision-making

- [[concepts/group-vs-individual-decisions]] — groups are more accurate than the average individual but less accurate than the best; individuals are faster, groups beat individuals on creativity and acceptance.
- [[concepts/groupthink-and-groupshift]] — groupthink = consensus pressure suppresses dissent (Samsung Note 7); groupshift = group polarization toward more extreme version of initial leaning.
- [[concepts/group-brainstorming]] — individuals working alone generate more ideas than equivalent brainstorming groups; production blocking is the primary mechanism; nominal group technique outperforms.
- [[concepts/common-knowledge-effect]] — groups over-discuss shared info and under-discuss private info (Grogan Air: ~75% pick the wrong VP candidate when info is divided); fix by collecting info before commitment.

### Organizational structure

- [[concepts/group-structure-outcomes]] — hierarchy increases efficiency/consistency at the cost of creativity/satisfaction; unstructured teams are more variable; hierarchies form spontaneously.
- [[concepts/span-of-control]] — number of direct reports per manager; wider span = flat (cheap, easy comms), narrower span = tall (more control, slower communication).
- [[concepts/mechanistic-vs-organic]] — mechanistic = bureaucratic, top-down, formalized (Wal-Mart, railroads); organic = fluid, distributed, flexible (Apple, startups, young industries).

## Reading-specific testable points

- **Robbins & Judge — six group properties** (the canonical chapter taxonomy, memorize): **roles, norms, status, size, cohesiveness, diversity**. Roles tell members *what* to do, norms tell them *how*; status confers ranking and shapes deference; size affects loafing and idea generation (smaller groups loaf less, larger groups generate more total but per-capita less); cohesiveness is the "stickiness" of the bond; diversity drives both faultlines and innovation.
- **R&J — five social-loafing prevention levers** (often listed verbatim on exams): set group goals / introduce inter-group competition / use peer evaluations / select highly motivated members / reward identifiable individual contributions. See [[concepts/social-loafing]].
- **R&J — seven cohesion levers**: smaller group / agreement on goals / time spent together / status difficulty of joining / external competition / group rewards / physical isolation.
- **R&J — punctuated equilibrium phases**: first-meeting direction set → Phase 1 inertia → midpoint transition → Phase 2 acceleration → final-meeting burst. The midpoint is *time-based*, not progress-based.
- **R&J — groupthink antecedents**: high cohesion, group insulation, directive leadership, lack of methodical decision procedures, homogeneous member backgrounds, time pressure.
- **Duhigg / Project Aristotle — headline finding**: norms beat composition. Across 180+ Google teams, *who* was on the team mattered far less than the team's *norms*. Two strongest predictors of effective norms: **equal distribution of conversational turn-taking** and **high average social sensitivity** (reading nonverbal cues / emotion recognition).
- **Duhigg — psychological safety as the dominant productive norm**. The slogan-level summary: "the *what* of the team mattered less than the *how*." The norm cluster Edmondson labels psychological safety subsumes the equal-turn-taking and social-sensitivity findings.
- **Duhigg — disconfirming intuition** — managers' instinct ("hire stars / mix introverts and extroverts / put smart people together") was largely *not* what predicted performance; norms swamped composition.

## Cross-cut connections

- [[cross-cuts/distractor-pairs]] — groupthink vs. groupshift, mechanistic vs. organic, brainstorming vs. nominal, and cohesion vs. psychological safety are exam-cram pairs.

## High-yield MCQ traps

- **Groupthink vs. groupshift** — both are group decision pathologies but mechanistically distinct. Groupthink = *suppressed dissent → artificial consensus* around an early position (Samsung Note 7, Bay of Pigs). Groupshift = *amplified initial leaning → polarization to a more extreme version* of the majority's pre-discussion preference (risky-shift or cautious-shift). If the stem says "they all agreed even though some had doubts," that's groupthink; if it says "the group ended up more extreme than any individual started," that's groupshift.
- **Mechanistic vs. organic** — mechanistic = bureaucratic, top-down, formalized, narrow span, strict chain of command (Wal-Mart, railroads, fast-food chains, mature regulated industries). Organic = fluid, distributed authority, low formalization, wider span, cross-functional (Apple, startups, young high-velocity industries). Trap: "mechanistic = bad / organic = good." Both are rational responses to environment — mechanistic dominates in stable, predictable contexts.
- **Brainstorming vs. nominal group technique (NGT)** — counterintuitively, NGT *outperforms* open brainstorming on both idea count and idea quality. Brainstorming suffers from production blocking (only one person can talk at a time), evaluation apprehension, and free-riding. NGT eliminates these by starting with silent individual idea generation. Trap: "brainstorming is the best group idea-generation technique" — false.
- **Cohesion vs. psychological safety** — cohesion is the *bond* (we like each other / we want to stay together); psychological safety is the *willingness to dissent and speak up* (we can challenge each other without humiliation). High cohesion *without* psychological safety produces groupthink; high psychological safety produces productive disagreement. They are not the same construct.
- **Common-knowledge effect vs. groupthink** — both produce bad group decisions but via different routes. Common-knowledge effect = *information-sampling failure* (groups over-discuss shared info, under-discuss unshared/unique info; Grogan Air ~75% wrong VP). Groupthink = *dissent-suppression* (people withhold known objections to maintain consensus). One is about *what info gets surfaced*; the other is about *what objections get voiced*.
- **Social loafing prevention levers** — five canonical levers (group goals / inter-group competition / peer evaluations / select for motivation / reward identifiable individual contributions). Trap distractors: "increase group size" (worsens loafing), "reduce individual identifiability" (worsens loafing — opposite of the right answer).
- **Punctuated equilibrium midpoint** — the transition is **time-based** (halfway through the allotted clock), not progress-based or tied to milestones. A team given 6 months hits transition at month 3 even if they're far behind schedule.

## Likely exam patterns

- **Scenario stem: identify groupthink vs. groupshift.** Read for cues: "everyone agreed even though some had concerns" (groupthink) vs. "the group ended up more extreme than any individual" (groupshift). The Samsung Note 7 vignette → groupthink.
- **Mechanistic-vs-organic structure recognition.** Stem describes a company (Wal-Mart, railroad, fast food, 100-year-old utility) → mechanistic. Stem describes Apple, an early-stage startup, a young/turbulent industry → organic. Span-of-control language ("narrow span / many layers") → mechanistic; ("wide span / flat") → organic.
- **Cohesion-norms 2×2.** "A tight-knit team that has agreed informally to coast" → high cohesion + low norms → *worst* productivity. The trap answer is "high cohesion always helps" — false; cohesion only multiplies whatever norms already exist.
- **Psychological-safety scenario.** Stem describes a team where members admit mistakes, ask "dumb" questions, and challenge the senior person without retaliation → psychological safety / Project Aristotle. Distractors: "cohesion," "groupthink avoidance," "trust" (close but not the named construct).
- **Common-knowledge / hidden-profile stem.** Group with divided info picks the wrong candidate → common-knowledge effect; fix is "collect individual judgments before discussion" or "explicitly solicit unique info" (NGT-style).
- **Punctuated equilibrium midpoint.** "The team did little for the first three weeks of a six-week project, then dramatically shifted approach" → midpoint transition. Trap: "Tuckman forming-storming-norming-performing" — that's a different (linear-stages) model not used as the canonical answer here.
