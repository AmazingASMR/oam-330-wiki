# Module 6 — Influence & Persuasion

## What this module is fundamentally about

Persuasion runs through **two simultaneous channels**: the **central route** (System 2 — argument quality, durable attitude change) and the **peripheral route** (System 1 — cues like authority, liking, scarcity that shift attitudes faster but more fragilely). Cialdini's six principles are the canonical peripheral-route toolkit; central-route strategy adds two-sided framing, beware-of-dilution, and inoculation effects. The module then applies the framework to **minority-position influence** (Asch conformity, *12 Angry Men*, push vs. pull) and to **special peripheral effects** from the Kidney Case (negative advertising, order/recency effects, individual identifiability, advocacy effect). The ethical spine throughout: peripheral cues are defensible when they amplify a true case System 2 would also accept — manipulative when they distract from a weak one.

Cialdini's six principles are MCQ gold — each requires the definition, trigger, concrete example, and at least one sibling-distinction (e.g., scarcity vs. social proof both spike urgency but for opposite reasons: rarity vs. consensus). Central vs. peripheral is the meta-frame: distractors will swap System 1/System 2 mappings or claim peripheral produces longer-lasting change.

## Headline empirical points

- **74% Asch conformity** — proportion of subjects who conformed to a unanimously wrong group on at least one trial; conformity *decreases* with one ally, written answers, or smaller groups.
- **DAV reciprocity 18% → 35%** — Disabled American Veterans direct-mail response rate jumped from 18% to 35% with a small personalized address-label gift; the canonical reciprocity number.
- **Save the Children "Rokia" study** — donations to a named 7-year-old from Mali **more than doubled** vs. donations framed by statistics about millions in need; one identifiable face beats aggregate suffering.
- **Missed-appointment 18% drop with active commitment** — having patients write down their own appointment time (vs. receptionist writing it) cut no-shows by ~18 percentage points; commitment-and-consistency in action (Cioffi & Garner cousin study).
- **Tupperware** — purchase predicted by social bond with the host, not product features; the canonical *liking* example (build relationships before you need them).
- **Diploma display / real-estate receptionist credentialing** — both classic *authority* studies; mentioning credentials via a third party lifts compliance significantly without coming across as boastful.
- **Cialdini's six principles** — Liking / Reciprocity / Social proof / Consistency / Authority / Scarcity; built on five decades of social-psych research, **compound when stacked** (HBR 2001).
- **"The last shall be first"** — recency wins in *serial-judgment competitions* (figure skating, *American Idol*, the Kidney Case order-effect demo); primacy wins after delay.
- **Asch one-ally reduction** — adding a single dissenter drops conformity dramatically (from ~37% mean error rate down to single digits); private/written responses similarly collapse it.

## Concepts

### The two-route model (master frame)
- [[concepts/central-vs-peripheral-route]] — central (System 2, argument quality, durable) vs. peripheral (System 1, cues, faster but fragile); they co-occur, not mutually exclusive.
- [[concepts/central-route-strategies]] — stronger arguments + two-sided framing + beware **argument dilution** (weak arguments hurt) and **inoculation** (don't disclose persuasion intent early).
- [[concepts/ethical-uses-peripheral-route]] — defensible when amplifying a case System 2 would accept; the test: "Would your audience be upset if they knew you were using peripheral techniques?"

### Cialdini's six peripheral-route principles
- [[concepts/cialdini-authority]] — defer to credible experts; surface credentials via a third party (diploma display, real-estate-receptionist credential lift).
- [[concepts/cialdini-reciprocity]] — repay first givers; DAV mailing 18% → **35%** with a small personalized address-label gift; can backfire as "brown-nosing" in collectivist contexts.
- [[concepts/cialdini-commitment-consistency]] — once you take a public stance, internal/external pressure aligns behavior; **active + public + voluntary** stickiest, coerced commitments backfire.
- [[concepts/cialdini-scarcity]] — rare/exclusive feels more valuable; works via **loss aversion** — frame what will be lost, not just gained; manufactured scarcity destroys trust.
- [[concepts/cialdini-liking]] — we say yes to people we like; driven by similarity, praise, and rapport; build relationships *before* you need them (Tupperware).
- [[concepts/cialdini-social-proof]] — peers' behavior as a heuristic; **horizontal** (similar peers) beats vertical; "use peer power, not positional power" — hotel-towel reuse and the Columbia, SC neighbor-donor list.

### Minority-position influence
- [[concepts/asch-conformity]] — **74%** conformed to a unanimously wrong group at least once; conformity *decreases* with one ally, written answers, or smaller groups.
- [[concepts/minority-persuasion-push-pull]] — when outnumbered use **PULL** (attract, bridge, listen, disclose); switch to PUSH only after achieving a majority; private vote to encourage dissent, public vote to lock in support.

### Kidney Case effects
- [[concepts/negative-advertising]] — "risky, but effective"; peripheral attack ad makes the audience *more negative* about the other side (vs. advocacy effect, which makes the speaker more positive about their own).
- [[concepts/order-effects]] — in serial competition (figure skating, *American Idol*, Kidney Case), **the last shall be first** — recency wins live; primacy wins after delay.
- [[concepts/individual-identifiability]] — donations to "Rokia, a 7-year-old from Mali" **more than doubled** vs. statistics about millions; one named face beats aggregate suffering.
- [[concepts/advocacy-effect]] — arguing for a position pulls the *speaker's* attitude toward it (selective positive-argument search + consistency motive); cousin to commitment/consistency but on the advocate, not the audience.

## Assigned readings

- [[readings/cialdini-harnessing-persuasion]] — Cialdini (2001), HBR; persuasion is a learnable evidence-based skill built on six compounding peripheral-route principles, and manipulation is self-defeating because it destroys trust.

## Reading-specific testable points

- **The six principles compound when stacked** (Cialdini HBR 2001) — using two or more in concert (e.g., authority + reciprocity, or liking + social proof) produces multiplicatively larger compliance than any single principle alone. The MCQ-ready phrase is "the principles **compound**."
- **Manipulation is self-defeating ethically AND strategically** (Cialdini) — fabricated cues (manufactured scarcity, fake credentials, manufactured liking) destroy trust and end *future* persuasion. The argument is not just moral; it's instrumentally worse for the persuader's long-term influence.
- **The 4-phenomena framework** — slide deck groups the six principles into two clusters: (1) **Authority / Reciprocity / Liking** (cues about *the source*) and (2) **Scarcity / Social proof / Consistency** (cues about *the situation or the self*). Some stems test recognition of these clusters rather than individual principles.
- **Transparency as ethical test** — Cialdini's litmus question: "Would your audience be upset if they knew you were using these techniques?" If yes, you're manipulating; if no, you're amplifying a true case System 2 would also accept. This is the slide-anchored ethical test.
- **F. B. Evans (1963) similarity-buying study** (Cialdini) — insurance prospects buy from agents who match them on age, religion, politics, and smoking habits; reading-only label for *liking via similarity* mechanism.
- **Cioffi & Garner (1996) active-commitment study** (Cialdini) — students who *wrote down* their volunteer commitment showed up at far higher rates than those who passively opted in; the canonical evidence for active + public + voluntary as the stickiest commitment ladder rung.
- **Columbia, SC neighbor-donor list** (Cialdini) — charity drive showed donations rose with *longer* lists of *neighbor* donors (horizontal social proof beats vertical). Reading-anchored example.

## Cross-cut connections

- [[cross-cuts/distractor-pairs]] — the six Cialdini principles plus central/peripheral are exam-cram pairs (scarcity vs. social proof = rarity vs. consensus; authority vs. social proof = vertical vs. horizontal; reciprocity vs. consistency = repay another's gift vs. honor your own commitment).
- [[cross-cuts/cultural-differences]] — Cialdini's principles vary in salience across cultures; reciprocity especially can read as bribery rather than sincerity in collectivist contexts (slide-flagged).
- [[cross-cuts/system-1-system-2]] — central route = System 2 (argument quality, durable); peripheral route = System 1 (cues, fragile); all six Cialdini principles are System 1 levers.
- [[cross-cuts/decision-making-biases]] — peripheral-route levers exploit the M3 bias catalog (scarcity rides loss aversion, social proof rides availability/consensus, authority rides halo, consistency rides sunk-cost-style commitment).

## High-yield MCQ traps

| Pair | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|
| [[concepts/central-vs-peripheral-route]] | Central = System 2, argument quality, **durable** attitude change; peripheral = System 1, cues, faster but **fragile**. They co-occur, not mutually exclusive. Distractor: claiming peripheral produces longer-lasting change. |
| [[concepts/cialdini-scarcity]] vs. [[concepts/cialdini-social-proof]] | Both spike urgency, but: scarcity = "*few* can have it" (exclusivity / loss-frame); social proof = "*many* are choosing it" (consensus / horizontal). **Opposite signals.** |
| [[concepts/cialdini-authority]] vs. [[concepts/cialdini-social-proof]] | Authority = *vertical* (defer to expert above); social proof = *horizontal* (follow similar peers). Same compliance behavior, opposite source direction. |
| [[concepts/cialdini-liking]] vs. [[concepts/cialdini-social-proof]] | Liking = *one-to-one* affective bond (Tupperware host); social proof = *many-to-one* peer behavior (Columbia donor list). |
| [[concepts/cialdini-reciprocity]] vs. [[concepts/cialdini-commitment-consistency]] | Reciprocity binds you to repay *another's* gift (DAV mailing); consistency binds you to *your own* prior public statement (Cioffi & Garner). |
| [[concepts/cialdini-commitment-consistency]] ladder | Active + public + voluntary > active + public > active > passive default. **Coerced commitments backfire.** Distractor: "stronger coercion produces stronger commitment." |
| [[concepts/cialdini-authority]] vs. [[concepts/cialdini-liking]] | Authority = competence-based deference (credentials, expertise); liking = warmth-based affinity (similarity, praise). Two different routes to the same yes. |
| [[concepts/asch-conformity]] vs. Milgram (obedience) | Asch = conformity to *peers* (74% conformed at least once); Milgram = compliance with *authority* (~2/3 to "XXX"). Peer vs. hierarchy. |
| Push vs. pull (minority influence) | When **outnumbered** use **PULL** (attract / bridge / listen / disclose); switch to PUSH only after achieving a majority. Trap: "push harder when in the minority" is the wrong move. |
| Argument dilution | Adding a *weaker* argument to a strong one makes the case **weaker**, not stronger; the average argument quality drops. Distractor: "more arguments always persuade more." |
| Primacy vs. recency ([[concepts/order-effects]]) | Recency wins in *serial-judgment competitions* live ("the last shall be first" — figure skating, *American Idol*); primacy wins after delay. Memorize both halves. |
| Negative advertising vs. advocacy effect | Negative ad = makes audience *more negative about the other side*; advocacy effect = makes the *speaker* more positive about their own position. Different targets of attitude shift. |
| [[concepts/individual-identifiability]] vs. statistical framing | One named "Rokia" beats aggregate suffering; donations *more than doubled*. Distractor: "scale of need increases donations" — opposite of the finding. |

## Likely exam patterns

- **Scenario stems where you identify which Cialdini principle is at work** — vignette describes a tactic (free sample, neighbor-donor list, expert credential, exclusive deadline, similarity rapport, written commitment); pick the principle. The sister-confusion traps (scarcity vs. social proof, authority vs. social proof, reciprocity vs. consistency) are the discriminators.
- **Central/peripheral System-1/System-2 swap** — stem maps central to System 1 or claims peripheral produces durable change. Both wrong; central = System 2 = durable, peripheral = System 1 = fragile.
- **Ethical-test stems** — given a persuasion technique, apply Cialdini's transparency test: would the audience object if they knew? If yes, manipulation; if no, ethical amplification of a true case.
- **Compounding stem** — vignette stacks two or three principles (e.g., a credentialed expert who reciprocates with a small gift and mentions limited availability); answer recognizes that the principles compound rather than picking just one.
- **Order-effects scenario** — serial-judgment situation (interviews, competition rounds); identify whether recency or primacy applies based on whether judgment is live or delayed.
- **Asch reducer recognition** — given a setup with one ally, written responses, or a small group, identify which conformity-reducer is operative.
