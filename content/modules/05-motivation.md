# Module 5 — Motivation

## What this module is fundamentally about

Why people work hard (or don't), and what managers can do about it. The spine is the **three-pillar model of motivating environments** (slide 11, deck 08; slide 4, deck 09):

1. **Clear paths from inputs to outputs** — *expectancy theory* (Vroom).
2. **Fair procedures and outcomes** — *equity theory* (Adams) + *distributive vs. procedural justice*.
3. **Meaningful work that affects others** — purpose, end-user impact (Pink).

Layered on top: the **money-happiness** story (positive but non-linear; we overestimate how much money will help), and the **intrinsic/extrinsic** story (contingent financial incentives can corrupt intrinsic motivation, especially on creative/conceptual tasks).

## Headline empirical points

- Job satisfaction–performance correlation is **medium (~.30)** — not zero, not perfect; counterintuitive on both ends (distractors: "no relationship" or "very strong").
- **Procedural justice** is the strongest predictor of job performance — *stronger* than distributive justice (slide-flagged counterintuitive).
- **Flow** is more often experienced at work than during leisure (Csikszentmihalyi) — work tends to provide the high-skill × high-challenge structure leisure usually lacks.
- **~90% of US workers** report being "very" or "somewhat" satisfied — empirically debunks the "myth of the unhappy worker."
- **Glucksberg candle problem** — contingent rewards make people *slower* on the creative-insight version (the box-as-platform reframe); on the easy version (tacks pre-removed), incentives speed people up. Same task, opposite effects depending on whether insight is required.
- **Lepper-Greene-Nisbett preschoolers** — children given an *expected* "Good Player Award" for drawing later drew less in free play than those given an *unexpected* award or no award; only contingent/expected rewards corrupted intrinsic motivation.
- **Babcock raise-negotiation gap** — women negotiate starting pay less often than men; small initial gaps **compound** over a career via percentage-based raises (Korkki).
- **Amabile & Kramer diary research** — small daily progress on meaningful work is the **single strongest day-level driver** of inner work life (perceptions, emotions, motivation).
- **Cornell pro-tobacco vs. anti-smoking copywriting** (Frank) — students demanded a **>80% pay premium** to take the pro-tobacco job; mission alignment carries quantifiable monetary value.
- **Pink's Motivation 2.0 vs. 3.0** — carrots-and-sticks (2.0) work for *algorithmic/routine* tasks but backfire on *heuristic/creative* tasks; replace with autonomy / mastery / purpose (3.0).

## Concepts

### Job satisfaction
- [[concepts/job-satisfaction-factors]] — predictors ranked by effect size (large/medium/small); the work itself, supervision, and growth beat pay.
- [[concepts/flow-state]] — Csikszentmihalyi's high-skill × high-challenge immersion; reported more often at work than during leisure.
- [[concepts/job-satisfaction-performance]] — correlation is **~.30 ("medium")** — real and replicable; also predicts citizenship behaviors and lower turnover.

### Motivating environments
- [[concepts/motivating-workplace-factors]] — the three-pillar spine: clear paths, fair procedures/outcomes, meaningful work.
- [[concepts/expectancy-theory]] — Vroom; motivation = expectancy × instrumentality × valence; any link near zero kills motivation (multiplicative).
- [[concepts/equity-theory]] — Adams; employees evaluate rewards by **relative**, not absolute, comparison to a referent's input/output ratio.
- [[concepts/distributive-vs-procedural-justice]] — outcome fairness vs. process fairness; **procedural is the strongest predictor of job performance**.
- [[concepts/cultural-differences-fairness]] — three allocation principles (merit/equality/need); the *desire for fair process* is universal, the *content* of fair allocation isn't.

### Meaningful work & inner work life
- [[concepts/meaningful-work]] — third pillar; brief end-user contact (10-min beneficiary meeting) outperforms manager pep talks for sustained motivation.
- [[concepts/progress-principle]] — Amabile & Kramer; small visible wins on meaningful work are the single strongest day-level driver of inner work life.

### Money & motivation
- [[concepts/money-happiness-relationship]] — positive but non-linear with diminishing returns; we overestimate how much money will help, and *valuing* money predicts less happiness.
- [[concepts/intrinsic-vs-extrinsic-motivation]] — "I like doing this" vs. "I like what it brings me"; Pink's autonomy/mastery/purpose; Sawyer Effect when contingent rewards corrupt play.
- [[concepts/financial-incentives-tradeoffs]] — six downsides (less creativity, less teamwork, unethical behavior, unfairness, short-termism, contingent effort); Glucksberg candle problem lives here.
- [[concepts/non-financial-incentives]] — pay above market, good benefits, unexpected rewards, status/recognition/thanks; sidesteps the contingent-pay trap.

## Reading-specific testable points

- **Progress principle / inner work life** (Amabile & Kramer) — the *single strongest* day-level driver of motivation, creativity, and engagement is **small visible progress on meaningful work**. Inner work life = perceptions + emotions + motivation as a unified construct measured via daily diary studies. (Caveat: this reading PDF is image-only / OCR-limited; the slide deck and the wiki concept page are the canonical sources.)
- **Babcock raise-compounding math** (Korkki) — a $1–2K starting-salary gap, compounded over a career via percentage raises, becomes a six-figure lifetime gap. The reading-only label is **pay-conversation taboo**: 60%+ of US workers say discussing pay with coworkers is forbidden or strongly discouraged.
- **Minimum-wage wage compression** (Feintzeig & Weber) — when a minimum-wage hike pushes new hires up to or past tenured workers, **veterans feel under-rewarded though no pay was cut**. The textbook real-world equity-theory case: input/output ratio comparison drives the felt unfairness.
- **Sawyer Effect** (Pink) — turning *play* into *work* via contingent rewards (whitewashing the fence as labor vs. game); the named effect for "incentives convert intrinsic motivation into extrinsic dependence."
- **Glucksberg candle problem** (Pink) — incentives slow people on the *insight* version (must reframe the box as a platform) but speed them on the *no-insight* version (tacks pre-removed). The canonical takeaway: **contingent rewards narrow attention**, helping on routine tasks and hurting on creative ones.
- **Motivation 2.0 vs. 3.0** (Pink) — 2.0 = carrots-and-sticks for *algorithmic* tasks; 3.0 = autonomy + mastery + purpose for *heuristic* tasks. Pink's "third drive" is intrinsic. Distractor: Motivation 1.0 (biological survival drive) is the *first* drive, not the upgrade target.
- **Deliberate practice** (Frank, citing Ericsson) — many thousands of hours of focused effortful practice; only sustainable on work you genuinely love. Reading-only label.
- **Mission alignment / >80% Cornell premium** (Frank) — quantifies the monetary value of working for a cause you support: students demanded >80% extra pay to switch from anti-smoking to pro-tobacco copywriting. Reading-only number.
- **Compensating differentials** (Frank) — better-conditions jobs tend to pay *less* (the wage offsets the perks); also a reading-only labeled term.

## Common points of confusion

- **Expectancy vs. equity theory** — *forward* effort→reward chain (multiplicative; one zero kills motivation) vs. *sideways* comparison to a referent's input/output ratio. Distractor: swapping "multiplicative" or "referent comparison" between the two.
- **Distributive vs. procedural justice** — what I get vs. how I got it. **Procedural is the stronger predictor of job performance** (slide-flagged counterintuitive). Distractor: stems will reverse the predictor direction.
- **Equity theory vs. distributive justice** — equity = the *mechanism* (input/output ratio comparison); distributive justice = the *feeling* of outcome fairness equity produces. Sister-confusion trap.
- **Intrinsic/extrinsic vs. financial-incentive tradeoffs** — taxonomy of motivation types vs. *when-and-why* incentives backfire (Glucksberg, six downsides). Different constructs.
- **Expected/contingent vs. unexpected rewards** — only **contingent/expected** rewards reliably corrupt intrinsic motivation (Lepper-Greene preschoolers); unexpected/non-contingent ones don't. Distractor: "all extrinsic rewards corrupt intrinsic motivation."
- **Job satisfaction factors vs. motivating environment factors** — different dependent variables: *liking the job* vs. *driving effort*. Don't conflate "what makes people satisfied" with "what makes people work hard."
- **Flow vs. job satisfaction** — flow = moment-to-moment state during a task; satisfaction = global evaluation of the job over time. Both can be present, absent, or split.
- **Flow 2×2** — high skill × high challenge = flow; low+low = apathy; high skill + low challenge = boredom; low skill + high challenge = anxiety. Mismatching the quadrants is the trap.
- **Money-happiness curve** — positive but **non-linear** with diminishing returns; *valuing* money predicts *less* happiness. Distractor: "no relationship" or "linear positive" both wrong.
- **Three allocation principles (cultural fairness)** — **merit / equality / need**. Equity = individualist default; the *desire for fair process* is universal, the *content* of fair allocation isn't.
- **Pink's Motivation 2.0 vs. 3.0** — 2.0 (carrots/sticks) for algorithmic tasks; 3.0 (autonomy/mastery/purpose) for heuristic/creative tasks. Distractor: applying 2.0 to a creative-insight task.

## Notes on application
- **Scenario stems applying expectancy or equity to a workplace situation** — given a vignette (effort→reward link broken; coworker hired at higher pay), identify which theory diagnoses it. The multiplicative-vs-comparative cue is the discriminator.
- **"Which justice type predicts performance"** — answer is **procedural** (slide-flagged counterintuitive). Almost guaranteed appearance.
- **Creative-task incentive-backfire stem** — Glucksberg-style scenario where contingent rewards are introduced on a creative/insight task; identify it as a financial-incentive tradeoff (not a motivation-2.0 success story).
- **Lepper-Greene-style intrinsic-corruption stem** — children/employees rewarded for an activity they previously did for fun then do less of it; identify the **expected/contingent** mechanism, not "all rewards demotivate."
- **Allocation-principle scenario** — a manager applies equity (merit-based) to a culture that defaults to equality or need; identify the cultural mismatch (links to M4 cross-cut).
- **Three-pillar diagnostic** — given a low-motivation workplace, identify which pillar is broken (clear paths / fair procedures / meaningful work) and which theory diagnoses it.
