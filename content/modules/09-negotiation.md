# Module 9 — Negotiation

## What this module is fundamentally about

Negotiation is **deciding on the terms of an exchange** — for goods/services, dividing a shared resource, or resolving conflict. The module's master comparison is **distributive vs. integrative bargaining**: fixed-pie / win-lose vs. expand-the-pie / win-win. Most other concepts (BATNA, reservation price, positions vs. interests, success strategies, compromise) hang off this dichotomy.

Two recurring traps:
1. **Defaulting to distributive** when integrative would create more value (especially in long-term relationships).
2. **Letting fairness perceptions override BATNA** — rejecting deals above your alternative because they "feel unfair," or accepting deals below it because the concession sequence felt like winning (Malhotra).

Classroom anchors: the **Texoil exercise** (no-ZOPA negotiation rescued by integrative moves: post-trip job offer, fuel trade, ads on sailboat) and the **Paper Clip exercise** (trade up from a paper clip via a chain of trades — works because people rarely value things to the exact same degree, the structural foundation of integrative bargaining).

## Headline empirical points

- **Texoil exercise** — Texoil rep cap **$500K** vs. station owner floor **$553K** → **no ZOPA on price alone**. Integrative moves (post-trip job offer for the sailing couple, fuel trade, line of credit, health care, ads on sailboat) shifted the owner's reservation to **$467K** and produced a deal.
- **Shapiro (Malhotra Mode A failure)** — offered **$8.5M** for 49% stake; rejected because it felt unfair vs. his $10M anchor. **Litigation BATNA** (expected value) = (0.7 × $10M) + (0.3 × $3M) − $0.5M = **$7.4M**. He should have accepted; later admitted as much. Canonical "reject what you should accept."
- **Olivia (Malhotra Mode B failure)** — car listed at $34K, **reservation = $31K** (BATNA: cheaper Car Y). Salesperson concedes $34K → $33K → $32,250 → $32,000. Olivia accepts at **$32K — $1K above her own reservation** — because the concession choreography felt like winning. Canonical "accept what you should reject."
- **Australia → Japan sugar exports** — Japanese counterparts expected to **renegotiate** as conditions changed; the Australian side treated the contract as fixed. Cultural mismatch on what the deal *was* — contract-as-finality vs. relationship-as-ongoing.
- **Orange / cake-and-juice** — two parties fight over an orange (positions). Compromise = cut in half (each gets 50%). Integrative move: ask **why** — one wants juice, one wants the peel for baking → each gets **100%** of what they want. Slide-canonical illustration that **integration ≠ compromise**.
- **Paper Clip exercise** — trade up a paper clip across a chain of trades; structural insight that **people rarely value things to the exact same degree**, so each trade can leave both parties better off. The structural foundation of integrative bargaining.

## Concepts

### Definition & framework
- [[concepts/negotiation-definition]] — deciding on the terms of an exchange; covers price (incl. employment), dividing a shared resource, and conflict resolution — distinct from raw persuasion or conflict itself.
- [[concepts/distributive-vs-integrative-bargaining]] — **master comparison.** Distributive = fixed pie / one issue / guarded info / one-shot; integrative = expand-the-pie / multiple issues / open / long-term — works because parties rarely value issues equally.

### Decision floors
- [[concepts/batna]] — Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement: the real outside option you'd pursue if this deal fails; slide rule **always accept an offer above your BATNA** (use expected value when the alternative is uncertain, e.g., Shapiro's $7.4M litigation BATNA).
- [[concepts/reservation-price]] — quantification of BATNA into a single in-deal walk-away number; overlap of two reservation prices = the **ZOPA** (Texoil reps had a $500K cap vs. owner's $553K floor → no ZOPA until integrative moves shifted it to $467K).

### Strategy & tactics
- [[concepts/negotiation-preparation]] — three questions: **what do I want, what do they want, what's the larger situation?** Distinguish target (aspiration) from reservation (walk-away); counterpart-side prep (their BATNA, their interests) is as important as your own.
- [[concepts/negotiation-success-strategies]] — in-the-room tactics split sharply by mode: distributive = make the first offer, reveal a deadline, hide info, strategic anger; integrative = soft on people / hard on problem, take their perspective, objective criteria, **package multiple options**.
- [[concepts/compromise-in-integrative-negotiation]] — slide-stated principle: **"integration is not just compromising"**; meeting in the middle on one issue can leave both worse off than trading across issues each party values unequally.
- [[concepts/positions-vs-interests]] — Fisher & Ury distinction; **positions** = stated offers ("I want the orange"), **interests** = underlying needs (juice vs. peel) — debating positions hides the trade that solves both.

### Cultural variation
- [[concepts/cultural-differences-negotiation]] — two dimensions vary cross-culturally: **goal** (binding contract vs. ongoing relationship — Australia/Japan sugar exports) and **communication** (direct vs. read-between-the-lines); slide takeaway: integrative travels better globally.

## Assigned readings

- [[readings/korkki-talk-about-pay]] — *Talk About Pay Today, or Suffer Tomorrow* (NYT): Babcock — % raises mean an under-negotiated starting salary compounds for years; gather salary intel, ask ~10% above offer, don't bluff your walk-away, and use integrative workarounds (bonus / vacation / equity) when base is capped.
- [[readings/malhotra-accept-or-reject]] — *Accept or Reject?* (Negotiation, HBR): the discipline of comparing offers to BATNA; symmetric failures are **Mode A** (Shapiro rejecting $8.5M above his $7.4M expected-value litigation BATNA) and **Mode B** (Olivia accepting $32K, $1K above her $31K reservation, because the concession sequence felt like winning).

## Reading-specific testable points

**Korkki — *Talk About Pay Today, or Suffer Tomorrow* (NYT):**
- **Babcock (Carnegie Mellon)** — because **raises and retirement contributions are typically percentage-based**, an under-negotiated starting salary compounds against you for years. The single under-negotiated number drags every future dollar down.
- **Salary intelligence** — research pay ranges via Glassdoor, Salary.com, PayScale, and personal contacts *before* the conversation. Maps directly onto slide-19 prep: "what do I want, what do they want, larger situation."
- **Babcock's "ask 10% above"** rule of thumb — anchors above target without seeming unreasonable.
- **Don't bluff your walk-away** — your reservation price has to be *real*. Stating a walk-away you wouldn't actually exercise is the failure mode of unreal BATNAs.
- **Defer salary discussion early; don't accept on the spot; ask for a few days** (Safani, Career Solvers).
- **Integrative workarounds when base is capped** (Rueff, Glassdoor) — bonus, signing bonus, equity, vacation, parking, relocation, hours, title. Textbook integrative packaging applied to a salary stem.
- **Gendered backlash** — women under-negotiate partly due to socialization and fear of perceived assertiveness penalty; the prescription is *preparation and information*, not adopting an aggressive style.

**Malhotra — *Accept or Reject?* (HBR):**
- **The decision rule** — accept iff offer beats your **reservation value** (the quantification of your BATNA). One comparison, no fairness side-quests.
- **Mode A (reject what you should accept)** — fairness perceptions override BATNA. Shapiro: "fair $10M" anchor sank a deal at $8.5M when his expected-value BATNA was only $7.4M.
- **Mode B (accept what you should reject)** — concession choreography feels like winning. Olivia: $34K → $33K → $32,250 → $32K accepted, $1K above her own $31K reservation.
- **Symmetric failure** — both modes are violations of the same rule, in opposite directions. The discipline is the same: **destination, not the path**.
- **Expected-value math for uncertain BATNAs** — when the alternative is uncertain (litigation, backup offer probability), compute probability-weighted payoffs minus costs *before* entering the room, and **write the number down**.
- **Application breadth** — job offers, litigation settlements, M&A, vendor procurement; the principle generalizes.

## Common points of confusion

- **Distributive vs. integrative bargaining (with matched tactics)** — **fixed-pie / one issue / guarded info / one-shot** vs. **expand-the-pie / multiple issues / open / long-term**. Tactic mismatch is the trap: anchoring first, revealing a deadline, hiding info, and strategic anger are **distributive**; soft-on-people / hard-on-problem, perspective-taking, objective criteria, and packaging multiple options are **integrative**. **Anger** is mode-dependent — strategic in distributive, counterproductive in integrative.
- **BATNA (outside option) vs. reservation price (in-deal walk-away)** — BATNA = the *alternative* (e.g., another job offer, secondhand-store quote, litigation); reservation price = the *number* the alternative produces inside this deal. Olivia ($31K reservation, accepted $32K = Mode B) and Shapiro ($7.4M EV BATNA, rejected $8.5M = Mode A) are the canonical violations.
- **BATNA vs. target point** — target = highest *aspiration*; BATNA = walk-away alternative. Classic prep mistake. The Ivy-League-equipment example: target ≈ $75K, BATNA = secondhand-store quote → reservation $35K.
- **BATNA vs. fairness anchor** — a "fair" number is **not** a BATNA. Shapiro rejected $8.5M because it felt unfair vs. $10M, but his EV BATNA was $7.4M — should have accepted.
- **Positions vs. interests** — position = *what* I ask for; interest = *why*. Two parties can have opposed positions and aligned (or orthogonal) interests. Debating positions hides the trade that solves both. Orange/peel-and-juice is the slide-canonical illustration.
- **Compromise is NOT integration** — slide-explicit. Compromise splits a single issue (each side gives up something on the same dimension); integration trades **across** issues each party values unequally. "Meet in the middle" is a distributive default. TRUE/FALSE distractor: "Integrative bargaining means compromising fairly" → **False**.
- **Fairness perceptions overriding BATNA (Malhotra)** — both Mode A and Mode B violate the same single rule: compare to BATNA. Both are framed by Malhotra as *symmetric* failures of the same discipline.
- **Integrative requires multiple issues** — if there is genuinely one issue and no way to add more, the negotiation is structurally distributive. The Paper Clip / Texoil insight is that you can almost always find more issues to add.
- **Cultural mismatch on what the deal *is*** — contract-oriented (often individualist) cultures treat signing as closing; relationship-oriented (often collectivist) cultures expect to renegotiate as conditions change. Don't conflate "indirect communication" with "weak commitment."

## Notes on application
- **Mode-recognition stems** — given a scenario, identify whether it's **distributive** (one issue, fixed pie, one-shot, guarded info) or **integrative** (multiple issues, expand-the-pie, long-term, open about goals). Cue: number of issues on the table and length of the relationship. Texoil-style multi-issue scenarios = integrative; one-time market purchase = distributive.
- **BATNA-vs-reservation-price scenarios** — stem describes either an outside option (alternative job, backup buyer, litigation) or an in-deal walk-away number; pick the right label. Pair with the **always-accept-above-BATNA** rule and the Mode A / Mode B failure modes. Stems may include the **expected-value math** for uncertain alternatives — Shapiro's $7.4M is the worked example.
- **Positions-vs-interests recognition (orange example)** — stem describes two parties stuck on incompatible positions; the right answer asks **why** each wants what they ask for, uncovers complementary interests, and serves both in full. Distractor: splitting the position (compromise) — slide says this can leave both worse off.
- **Cross-cultural negotiation style stems** — Australia/Japan-style scenario where one party treats the contract as fixed and the other expects renegotiation; or where direct confrontation reads as disrespectful. Identify the contract-vs-relationship and direct-vs-indirect dimensions; recall slide bottom-line: **integrative travels better globally**.
- **Tactic-to-mode matching stems** — a tactic is described (anchoring first; revealing a deadline; "soft on people, hard on problem"; objective criteria; packaging multiple issues; strategic anger) and you pick the mode it belongs to. Anchoring / deadlines / info-hiding / strategic anger = **distributive**; perspective-taking / objective criteria / interest focus / packaging = **integrative**.
- **Salary-negotiation stems (Korkki)** — applicant refuses to discuss base salary, asks for a few days, packages bonus + vacation + equity when base is capped. Identify as **integrative packaging** + slide-19 prep + a real (not bluffed) reservation price. Babcock's % compounding is the "why bother negotiating starting salary" stem.
