## Main argument
**Subjective confidence is a feeling, not a judgment of accuracy.** Drawing on his Israeli Army officer-selection experience, Kahneman argues that experts — including himself — produce confident predictions that are barely better than chance, and they keep producing them even after being shown the predictions are worthless. The phenomenon Kahneman calls the **illusion of validity**: the coherence of the *story* the expert tells (a vivid, internally consistent narrative built from a few salient cues) feels like evidence of accuracy. It isn't. The expert's confidence reflects how good the story sounds, not how predictive the cues actually are. The article generalizes the lesson from officer selection to stock pickers, political pundits, and clinicians, and argues that **algorithms beat intuition** in environments where the underlying signal is weak.

## Key terms introduced
- **Illusion of validity** — the subjective sense that one's judgment is accurate, driven by the *coherence* of the impression rather than its predictive power.
- **Illusion of skill** — collective overconfidence in domains (e.g., stock picking) where outcomes are largely random; persists because the social environment validates the practice.
- **Substitution** — when asked a hard question (will this officer succeed?), [[concepts/system-1-system-2|System 1]] substitutes an easier one (does this officer impress me right now?).
- **Expert intuition vs. algorithms** — in low-validity environments, simple statistical rules outperform expert judgment; experts resist this finding.

## Examples used
- **Israeli Army officer-selection (leaderless group task):** Kahneman and colleagues evaluated candidates via a structured exercise; their confident judgments turned out to barely beat chance against later officer-school outcomes. They knew the validity was near-zero — and still felt confident on the next round. Coined the **illusion of validity**.
- **Stock pickers:** reviewed an investment firm's individual advisors over 8 years; year-to-year correlations of performance were near zero — i.e., no skill detectable — yet bonuses were paid as if the rankings reflected skill. Coined the **illusion of skill**.
- **Pundits and political forecasters** — Tetlock's work, cited approvingly: experts no better than chance, and the *most famous* experts often the least accurate.
