## Main argument
Money matters for jobs, but in non-obvious ways: relative position, working conditions, mission alignment, and the chance to experience flow matter more than absolute pay once basic obligations are met. Pursue work you love because (a) it lets you sustain the deliberate practice required for true expertise, and (b) expertise + technology eventually pays. Resist the "soul-crushing" higher-paying job for the more satisfying lower-paying one.

## Key terms introduced
- **Easterlin paradox / relative income** — Position in the income distribution shapes satisfaction more than absolute income.
- **Compensating differentials** — Jobs with better conditions (autonomy, learning, safety) tend to pay less.
- **Mission alignment** — Cornell seniors demanded an >80% pay premium to take a pro-tobacco vs. anti-smoking job; people want their work to make the world better.
- **Deliberate practice (Ericsson)** — Many thousands of hours required for true expertise; only sustainable if you love the task.
- **Flow (Csikszentmihalyi)** — Deeply satisfying state of immersion in which time disappears; landing a job with substantial flow is a top-tier outcome.

## Examples used
- Two-parallel-worlds thought experiment on relative income.
- American Cancer Society vs. tobacco-industry copywriting choice.
- Frank & Cook's "winner-take-all" argument — top performers capture larger shares of income across domains.
