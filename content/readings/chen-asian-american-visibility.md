## Main argument
After the Atlanta-spa shootings and a surge in anti-Asian attacks (and following the post-George-Floyd workplace conversations on race), Asian-American professionals are dropping a long pattern of *blending in* at work and pushing to be seen — sharing testimonials at company town halls, lobbying employers for donations to Asian causes, and demanding inclusion in corporate diversity plans that have historically overlooked them. The piece argues that the **model-minority myth** — the perception that Asian Americans are prosperous and don't need help — has caused both employers and Asian employees themselves to under-invest in Asian inclusion, despite a measurable drop-off of Asian representation at executive levels.

## Key terms introduced
- **Model-minority myth** — the perception that Asians are uniformly prosperous and need no DEI attention; obscures the widest income disparity of any U.S. ethnicity (Pew) and an executive-level drop-off.
- **Visibility / invisibility** — Soyoung Kang: "This is a community that's long suffered from invisibility." The article's framing concept.
- **"Worker bees" stereotype** — Henry Fong (LinkedIn VP Legal): "good workers, but not assertive enough to be leaders" — the specific stereotype blocking promotion.
- **Affinity groups / ERGs** — e.g., Medtronic's Asian employee resource group, where interest spiked.

## Examples used
- **Kaycee Lai** (Promethium founder, ex-Microsoft/VMware): hid bubble tea, laughed off "Asians should be in coding not sales" comments.
- **1,000 Asian-American business leaders** (incl. Steve Chen, Eric Yuan): $10M pledge, full-page WSJ ad — "angry and afraid," "tired of being treated as less than American."
- **Facebook**: Asians = 44% of employees, 25% of leadership; 6% of U.S. population.
- **Eric Toda**: walks into rooms and gets assumed to be "the IT guy" or DoorDash; some white colleagues "don't see him as Asian," intending it as a compliment.
- **Delta Air Lines**: Goswami's weekly all-hands time for diversity testimonials; ~300 flight attendants wear pins indicating diversity training.
