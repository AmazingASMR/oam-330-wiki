## Main argument
Failing to negotiate your starting salary is a mistake that compounds for years — because raises and retirement contributions are typically percentage-based, an under-negotiated starting number drags every future dollar down with it. Even in a tough job market, polite, prepared negotiation rarely costs you the offer; it usually strengthens the relationship. Women in particular under-negotiate, partly due to socialization and fear of backlash, but the solution is not adopting an aggressive style — it's preparation, information, and knowing your walk-away.

## Key terms introduced
- **Salary intelligence** — researching pay ranges via Glassdoor, Salary.com, PayScale, and personal contacts.
- **Walk-away threshold** — the salary below which you'd genuinely decline (don't bluff about it).
- **"Ask for more than you want"** — Babcock's rule of thumb: ~10% above the offer.
- **"How close can you come to that figure?"** — keep negotiating after a "no."
- **"Meet in the middle"** — proposing a compromise; effective because it feels fair.
- **Backlash** (gendered) — perceived social cost of women negotiating assertively.

## Examples used
- Linda Babcock (Carnegie Mellon): % raises mean every future raise is anchored on the starting number.
- Barbara Safani (Career Solvers): defer salary discussion early; don't accept on the spot; ask for a few days.
- Rusty Rueff (Glassdoor): if base salary is capped, negotiate around it — bonus, equity, signing bonus, vacation, parking, relocation, hours, title.
- Gender gap: men negotiate pay more often than women.
