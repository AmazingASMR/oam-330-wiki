## Main argument
Power produces lasting changes in the brain that look like a mild traumatic brain injury: more impulsive, less risk-aware, worse at perspective-taking. Dacher Keltner's two decades of behavioral studies and Sukhvinder Obhi's TMS neuroscience converge on the same conclusion: the powerful stop **mirroring** others — the subtle, automatic neural simulation that underlies empathy. This produces an "empathy deficit" and gives a neurological basis to Keltner's **power paradox**: once we have power, we lose some of the capacities we needed to gain it. Effort to consciously compensate doesn't help (Obhi & Naish). The change is largely functional — induceable by ten-minute priming — but can become long-lasting under sustained power.

## Key terms introduced
- **Power paradox** (Keltner) — gaining power erodes the capacities (empathy, attentiveness) that produced it.
- **Mirroring** — automatic neural simulation of others' actions; basis of empathy. Power dampens it (Obhi's TMS finding on rubber-ball videos).
- **Empathy deficit** (Keltner) — powerful people stop simulating others' experience.
- **Hubris syndrome** (Lord David Owen, 2008/2009 *Brain*) — proposed clinical disorder of sustained power with minimal constraint. 14 features: contempt for others, loss of contact with reality, restless/reckless action, displays of incompetence.
- **Toe holder** (Louis Howe re: FDR) — a person who refuses to defer and keeps the leader grounded.

## Examples used
- **John Stumpf** (former Wells Fargo CEO) at congressional hearing on the 5,000 fake-account scandal — couldn't read the room; "Cross-selling is shorthand for deepening relationships."
- **Galinsky's "E on the forehead" study (2006)** — high-power participants 3× more likely to draw the E self-readable (backwards to others). George W. Bush holding the flag backwards at 2008 Olympics as analog.
- **Obhi's TMS / rubber-ball study** — high-power subjects' mirror-neuron pathways fire less.
- **Indra Nooyi's mother**: "Leave that damn crown in the garage" — toe-holder example.
- **Clementine Churchill's letter** to Winston, day Hitler entered Paris: "you are not as kind as you used to be."
- **Rau et al., *J. Finance* 2017** — CEOs who lived through fatal natural disasters as children are *less* risk-seeking; those who lived through non-fatal disasters are *more* risk-seeking.
