## Main argument
Persuasion is not a charismatic gift but a **learnable, evidence-based skill** built on five decades of social-psychology research. People say yes for predictable reasons rooted in mental shortcuts, and those shortcuts cluster into **six fundamental principles**: liking, reciprocity, social proof, consistency, authority, scarcity. The principles **compound** when stacked, and they collapse into manipulation — bad ethically *and* bad strategically — when fabricated rather than used to surface what is already true.

## Key terms introduced
- **Liking** — say yes to people you like; driven by similarity and praise.
- **Reciprocity** — repay in kind; give first what you hope to receive.
- **Social proof** — follow the lead of *similar others*; horizontal beats vertical.
- **Consistency** — align with active, public, voluntary commitments.
- **Authority** — defer to credible experts; surface, don't assume, your expertise.
- **Scarcity** — value what is rare; loss-frame, don't gain-frame.
- **Compounding** — principles reinforce when stacked; violating one undermines others.
- **Manipulation as self-defeating** — fabricated cues destroy trust, ending future persuasion.

## Examples used
- **Tupperware**: purchase predicted by social bond with host (liking).
- **F. B. Evans (1963)**: insurance prospects buy from agents matching them on age, religion, politics, smoking (liking via similarity).
- **Disabled American Veterans mailing**: 18% response → **35%** with personalized address-label gift (reciprocity).
- **Columbia, SC charity drive (1982)**: longer list of *neighbor* donors → higher giving (social proof).
- **Cioffi & Garner (1996)**: active written volunteer commitment → far higher show-up rate vs. passive opt-out (consistency).
- **Physiotherapist diplomas in view**: higher patient compliance (authority).
- **Real-estate receptionists** mentioning agent experience: more appointments and contracts (authority).
- **Loss-framed information sharing**: "before this goes out broadly" (scarcity).
