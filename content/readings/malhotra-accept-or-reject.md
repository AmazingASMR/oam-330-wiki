## Main argument
The accept/reject decision on a final offer should be governed by exactly one comparison: the offer vs. your **BATNA**. Negotiators routinely violate this rule because **self-serving fairness perceptions** and **concession-pattern aesthetics** hijack judgment. Two symmetric failure modes: (A) rejecting deals that beat your BATNA because they feel unfair, (B) accepting deals worse than your BATNA because the concession sequence felt like winning. Discipline = compute reservation value as a number (use expected value if BATNA is uncertain), then evaluate the **destination, not the path**.

## Key terms introduced
- **BATNA** — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement; what you'll actually do if no deal.
- **Reservation value** — the minimum economic value you'd accept; derived from BATNA.
- **Decision rule** — accept if offer beats reservation value, reject otherwise.
- **Fairness bias / self-serving fairness** — both sides believe they were the more reasonable, more conceding party.
- **Concession illusion** — sequence of price drops feels like progress regardless of where it lands.
- **Failure Mode A** (reject what you should accept) and **Failure Mode B** (accept what you should reject).
- **Expected-value BATNA** — for uncertain alternatives (e.g., litigation): probability-weighted payoffs minus costs.

## Examples used
- **Shapiro (Mode A):** offered $8.5M for 49% stake; rejected it as below his "fair" $10M. Litigation BATNA = (0.7 × $10M) + (0.3 × $3M) − $0.5M = **$7.4M**. He should have accepted; later admitted as much.
- **Olivia (Mode B):** car listed at $34K, reservation value $31K (BATNA = cheaper Car Y). Salesperson concedes to $33K → $32,250 → $32,000. Olivia accepts at $32K — $1K above her own reservation. The concession choreography overrode the math.
- Application breadth: job offers, litigation settlements, M&A, vendor procurement.
