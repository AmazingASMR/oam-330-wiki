## Main argument
Personality can be **read from physical and digital traces** — bedrooms, offices, music collections, websites — because behaviors aren't randomly distributed. The **Big 5 / OCEAN** is the empirically validated framework for those readings: traits cluster, dimensions are continuous, and everyone scores on all five. Gosling contrasts this with Myers-Briggs's forced-type approach, which throws away information at the midpoint where most people actually live.

## Key terms introduced
- **Identity claims** — deliberate self-presentation (posters, diplomas, displayed objects).
- **Feeling regulators** — items kept to manage one's own mood (favorite mug, photos).
- **Behavioral residue** — inadvertent traces left by behavior (a stack of unfinished projects).
- **Big 5 / OCEAN** — Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism — five continuous dimensions.
- **Facets** — narrower sub-dimensions inside each Big 5 trait (e.g., Extraversion contains friendliness, gregariousness, assertiveness, activity level, excitement-seeking, cheerfulness).
- **Profile thinking** — comparing a person across all 5 traits, not assigning a single type.
- **MBTI critique** — 16 forced types ignore that most people sit near the midpoint of every dimension; types blend rather than cluster.

## Examples used
- **Theophrastus's Characters** (ancient Athens) — earliest systematic personality typology; established that traits co-occur.
- **Galen's four humors** — biological mechanism wrong, but the "small number of basic temperaments" insight survived.
- **Rudy vs. Mimi profiles** — same person can be high on E *relative to her own profile* but low on E *relative to other people*; relative vs. absolute comparison.
- **Da Vinci** as openness icon; **Robocop** as conscientiousness icon.
- **Bedroom snooping demo** — organized desk + box of unfinished projects = high orderliness, low self-discipline (different facets of C).
- **Neuroticism caveat** — Big-5 N includes anger and low mood, not just anxiety; broader than everyday "neurotic" usage.
