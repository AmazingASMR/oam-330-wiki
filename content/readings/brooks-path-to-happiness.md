## Main argument
There are many ways to be unhappy but only a few to be happy ("Tolstoy's Oblonsky hypothesis" / asymmetric distribution). The narrow path is straightforward, however: prioritize relationships. Happy people and happy families are alike in being warmly social and cohesive; unhappy ones diverge in many directions.

## Key terms introduced
- **Oblonsky hypothesis** — Tolstoy's "happy families are all alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way."
- **Asymmetric distribution** — Negative outcomes have greater range/variance than positive ones; few paths up, many paths down.
- **Negativity bias** — Cognitive tendency to weight bad news more than good; evolutionary origin (threats matter more than treats).
- **Cohesive / Enmeshed / Disengaged families** — 2010 Rochester–Notre Dame typology: cohesive (59%, warm, harmonious) is the happy model; enmeshed (22%, warm but hostile/meddling) and disengaged (19%, cold/controlling/withdrawn) are unhappy.

## Examples used
- General Social Survey: 30% of Americans "very happy" in 2018, 14% "not too happy" — many do reach the narrow gate.
- 2008 "What Do Happy People Do?" — very happy people socialize 11 more times/year with relatives, 7 more with neighbors, 5 more with friends, attend 7 more religious services.
- 2002 study: very happy people rate close friendships 54% higher, family 73% higher, romance 161% higher than very unhappy people.
