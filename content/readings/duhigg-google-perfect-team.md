## Main argument
Google's Project Aristotle studied 180+ teams and found team COMPOSITION (who is on the team — skills, personality, friendship, hierarchy preference) didn't predict effectiveness. What predicted effectiveness were group NORMS — and one norm above all: **psychological safety**, the shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking. The two operational behaviors that produce psychological safety are equality in conversational turn-taking and high average social sensitivity.

## Key terms introduced
- **Group norms**: traditions, behavioral standards, and unwritten rules governing how people behave when they gather; these often override individual proclivities.
- **Psychological safety** (Amy Edmondson, 1999): a shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — confidence that the team will not embarrass, reject, or punish someone for speaking up.
- **Equality in distribution of conversational turn-taking**: by the end of a session, everyone has spoken roughly the same amount.
- **Average social sensitivity**: ability to intuit others' feelings from tone, expressions, nonverbal cues; measured by Reading the Mind in the Eyes test.
- **Project Aristotle**: Google's 2012 internal initiative to identify what makes teams effective.

## Examples used
- Julia Rozovsky's two Yale groups — toxic study group vs. high-functioning case-competition team — same caliber of people, opposite dynamics.
- Woolley et al. (2010, *Science*): 699 participants, brick-uses + grocery shopping tasks; teams good at one task were good at all; collective IQ predicted by turn-taking equality + social sensitivity.
- Matt Sakaguchi (Google manager): disclosed Stage 4 cancer at off-site, triggering openness across team and adoption of new norms.
- Team A (efficient, expert-driven, no chitchat) vs. Team B (free-flowing, interrupting, socializing) — Team B is more collectively intelligent.
