## Main argument
Interviewers feel highly confident in their ability to "read" candidates — but **unstructured interviews are weak predictors of long-term job performance**. General mental ability, work samples, job-knowledge tests, and peer ratings of past performance all beat the gut. The phenomenon — interviewers overestimating their own discernment — is the **interview illusion** (Nisbett's term). The same illusion infects mate selection: love-struck interviewees rate divorce probability at zero while statistical predictors paint a soberer picture.

## Key terms introduced
- **Interview illusion** — interviewers' systematic overconfidence in their ability to predict job performance from a brief, unstructured conversation.
- **Thin-slice judgments** — accurate intuitions about *expressive* traits (extraversion, warmth) form within seconds (e.g., 15-second clip of a candidate entering the room predicts 9 of 11 trained interviewers' ratings).
- **What thin slices CAN'T do** — predict long-term job performance; intentions and present behavior in a special situation don't generalize to habitual workplace behavior.
- **Schmidt & Hunter (85-year review)** — for all but the least-skilled jobs, **general mental ability** is the best single predictor of on-the-job success; informal interviews trail aptitude tests, work samples, job-knowledge tests, and peer ratings.
- **Fundamental attribution error** — root cause of the illusion: we attribute interview behavior to disposition and underweight situation; but interviews are a special situation.
- **Statistical (actuarial) prediction beats clinical judgment** — Dawes: a half-hour interview cannot match a GPA composite from 28+ courses graded by 20+ professors.
- **Self-fulfilling prophecy in interviews** — Word, Zanna & Cooper: white interviewers behave differently with Black applicants (more distance, shorter, more speech errors); when those behaviors are reproduced toward white interviewees, performance deteriorates.

## Examples used
- Bernieri & Prickett — strangers shown 15-second silent door-to-chair clips predicted trained interviewers' ratings on 9 of 11 traits.
- DePaulo's expressiveness studies — inhibited people *can't* feign expressiveness convincingly, and vice versa.
- Texas Medical School (1970s) — top 150 interviewer-rated admits and lower-rated 50 admits performed identically on M.D. completion and honors.
- Myers's niece — rejected by U-Washington Med after a poor interview, sailed through Johns Hopkins.
- Marilyn Monroe / Elvis / Fred Astaire — famous reject-the-future-star interview misfires.
- Mate selection as the "biggest interview" — actuarial predictors (age at marriage, two-parent home, similar values, no premarital cohabitation) beat the gut feeling of being in love.
