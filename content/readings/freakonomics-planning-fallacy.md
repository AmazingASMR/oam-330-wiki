## Main argument
Projects of every size — kitchen renovations to multibillion-dollar infrastructure — finish later and cost more than predicted. The cause is not random noise but a **structured set of forecasting biases**: the **planning fallacy**, **optimism bias**, and **overconfidence**. The fix is counterintuitive: the antidote to overconfident forecasting is **not better planning of this project** but disciplined attention to *past* projects — the **outside view**, **reference-class forecasting**, and ultimately **algorithms over intuition**. The episode also concedes a productive tension: optimism bias is what gets ambitious projects launched in the first place; rigor at the planning stage and optimism at the commitment stage need to be institutionally separated.

## Key terms introduced
- **Planning fallacy** (Buehler, with Griffin & Ross) — underestimating completion times even while *knowing* past projects took longer than expected. Realistic memory of the past coexists with unrealistic forecasts of the future.
- **Optimism bias** (Sharot) — systematic expectation of rosier futures than evidence warrants. Has motivational benefits; absent in depression.
- **Inside view vs. outside view** (Kahneman & Tversky) — inside = bottom-up, this-project specifics; outside = look at the historical reference class.
- **Reference-class forecasting** (Flyvbjerg) — operationalized outside view: identify reference class → build distribution of overruns → locate your project → apply uplift.
- **Strategic misrepresentation** — deliberate (not innocent) lowballing to win project approval.
- **Algorithm aversion** (Milkman) — refusing to defer to a model even when it's demonstrably more accurate than expert judgment.
- **Implementation intentions** — concrete *when/where/how* plans that reduce slippage at the individual level.

## Examples used
- **Second Avenue Subway, NYC:** 1968 plan = 8.5 miles, 15 stations. 2016 Phase 1 reality = 2 miles, 3 stations, ~48 years late, $4.5B (~$700M over budget). Iconic case of the planning fallacy at scale.
- **Buehler confidence-interval studies:** even at the **99% confidence** level, fewer than half of student projects finish on time.
- **Christmas-shopping study:** more *detailed* planning produced more *optimistic* (and less accurate) forecasts.
- **Japanese students:** predicted finishing essays 10 days before the deadline; actual average was 1 day before.
- **UK Department for Transport** adopted reference-class forecasting in 2004 → average overrun fell to ~7%.
- **Buehler's own briefcase** — admits he still over-stuffs it with work he won't get to.
