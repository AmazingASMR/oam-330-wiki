## Main argument
The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator is a popular, profitable, organizationally entrenched personality test that **fails the basic scientific tests of reliability and predictive validity**. The Big 5 outperforms it on every measure that actually matters for hiring, team-building, and self-understanding. MBTI persists despite the evidence because it's intuitive, fun, and Barnum-friendly — not because it works.

## Key terms introduced
- **Test–retest reliability** — does the same person get the same result twice? MBTI fails badly: by some estimates roughly **half** of people are classified into a different type when retested even within a few weeks.
- **MBTI's four dichotomies** — E/I, S/N, T/F, J/P, each forcing a binary cut on what is actually a continuous distribution.
- **Big 5 / OCEAN** — the empirically grounded alternative; continuous traits with three decades of validation evidence.
- **Conscientiousness as the workplace winner** — the single trait that consistently predicts job performance across roles.
- **Neuroticism (emotional stability)** — the trait that predicts who stays calm and collected under stress; MBTI has no equivalent.
- **Ambiverts** — people in the middle of the E/I distribution; MBTI forces them into a type, but Adam Grant (citing Pink's *To Sell Is Human*) argues ambiverts often *outperform* extreme extraverts in sales.
- **HEXACO** — a research-grade extension of the Big 5 that adds Honesty/Humility.

## Examples used
- The classic stat that ~50% of MBTI takers get a different type on retake.
- Three decades of meta-analytic evidence linking Big 5 traits — not MBTI types — to job performance and team effectiveness.
- *To Sell Is Human* (Pink): ambiverts beat extreme extraverts in sales — the kind of finding MBTI's binary E/I cut obscures.
- Grant's "MBTI, If You Want Me Back, You Need to Change Too" framing — MBTI could be salvaged only by becoming continuous and empirically validated, i.e., by becoming the Big 5.
