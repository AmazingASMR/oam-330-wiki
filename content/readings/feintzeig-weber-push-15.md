## Main argument
Minimum-wage hikes to $15 produce **wage compression**: less-experienced new hires earn the same as veterans who took months or years to reach that pay level. Veterans feel unfair­ly under-rewarded, even though no one's pay was cut. The article is a real-world case of **equity theory** in action: employees evaluate pay relative to peers, not in absolute terms, and managers must scramble (raise veteran pay, add benefits, communicate transparently) to preserve perceived fairness.

## Key terms introduced
- **Wage compression** — narrowing pay differential between newer and tenured workers.
- **Anchor / benchmark effect of minimum wage** (Alan Krueger) — the floor sets a reference point for what counts as fair pay everywhere above it.
- Implicit: **distributive vs. procedural justice** — companies repair fairness through both outcome adjustments (raising veteran pay) and process moves (showing pay scales, explaining cost constraints).

## Examples used
- **Mud Bay** (Pacific Northwest pet supply): district manager Catherine Knowles shows store employees the company pay scale and explains the cost constraint when veterans complain.
- **Gap & Walmart**: raised entry pay; managers had to field fairness complaints; Gap raised some veterans above the new $10 floor preemptively.
- **Tropical Smoothie Café (Long Island)**: owner Jankowski raised shift-leader pay to $10.75 to keep differential after entry pay rose to $9.75.
- **Solnick & Hemenway (1998)**: half of 257 surveyed preferred earning $50K when peers earned $25K to earning $100K when peers earned $200K — relative > absolute.
- A Chicago Gap associate who left after seeing two-year-veterans paid the same as new hires.
