## Main argument
"Carrots and sticks" — the contingent **if-then reward** model that Pink labels **Motivation 2.0** — often *don't* work, and frequently *backfire*, on tasks that require creativity, judgment, or moral commitment. Above a threshold of adequate **baseline rewards** (fair pay/benefits), extrinsic incentives can extinguish intrinsic motivation. The chapter catalogs **seven downsides** of contingent rewards: less intrinsic motivation, lower performance on creative tasks, less creativity, crowding out of good behavior, encouragement of unethical behavior, addiction (to escalating rewards), and fostered short-term thinking. The replacement model — **Motivation 3.0** — is built on **autonomy, mastery, and purpose**.

## Key terms introduced
- **Motivation 2.0 / 3.0** — the carrot-and-stick model vs. the autonomy/mastery/purpose model.
- **Baseline rewards** — adequate, equitable pay/benefits; below this threshold, intrinsic motivation can't be unlocked.
- **Sawyer Effect** — turning play into work (or vice versa) via contingent rewards (named for Tom Sawyer's fence-whitewashing trick).
- **"If-then" rewards** — contingent rewards offered up front; the corrosive form.
- **Crowding out** — paying for an act (e.g., blood donation) replaces the moral/altruistic motive with a market motive, often reducing the behavior.
- **Algorithmic vs. heuristic tasks** — extrinsic rewards work for the former, backfire on the latter.

## Examples used
- **Glucksberg candle problem** — incentivized solvers took ~3.5 minutes *longer* than non-incentivized; rewards narrow focus, blocking the "wide view" creative reframing demands.
- **Lepper & Greene preschoolers** — kids told they'd get a "Good Player" certificate for drawing later drew *less* than kids who got an unexpected certificate or none. Contingent rewards specifically corrupt intrinsic motivation.
- **Madurai (Ariely et al.)** — large incentives ($5 ≈ two weeks' pay) led to *worse* performance on tasks needing concentration/creativity.
- **Amabile artist study** — commissioned (extrinsically motivated) art was rated lower than non-commissioned by independent judges. Intrinsically motivated artists succeeded long-term.
- **Swedish blood donation** — paying women halved donations (53% → 30%); offering charity-donation alternative restored them.
- **Israeli day-care fine** — fining late parents *increased* lateness; the price replaced the moral norm.
- **Sears auto-repair quotas, Enron revenue goals, Ford Pinto deadlines** — extrinsic goal pressure breeds unethical shortcuts.
- **Tom Sawyer fence** — inverse Sawyer Effect (turning work into play by withholding rewards).
