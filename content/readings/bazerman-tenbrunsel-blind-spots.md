## Main argument
Smart, honest people behave less ethically than they believe — not because they're hypocrites but because predictable cognitive biases distort the **prediction → decision → recollection** arc. Before deciding, our "should self" dominates and we over-predict ethical behavior. At decision time, our "want self" wins via *ethical fading* (the ethics drops out of awareness) and *visceral responses* (hot-state bodily drives toward self-preservation). Afterward, we revise our memory so we still see ourselves as ethical, and the cycle repeats. Ch. 8 ("Narrowing the Gap") prescribes interventions: anticipate the want self, voice the should self via abstract thinking, the "mom test," precommitment devices, and joint (not separate) evaluation of options.

## Key terms introduced
- **Bounded ethicality** — systematic, mostly-unconscious limits on ethical decision making.
- **Want self vs. should self** — emotional/impulsive vs. rational/principled selves; their dominance flips across the decision arc (Figure 5).
- **Behavioral forecasting errors** — overpredicting our own future ethical behavior.
- **Ethical fading** — at decision time, the ethical dimension disappears from view (Tenbrunsel & Messick).
- **System 1 / System 2** — fast/automatic vs. slow/reflective; System 1 responses are more likely to be immoral.
- **Precommitment devices** — structural commitments that bind the future "want" self (lockbox, public commitment, personal trainer).

## Examples used
- Sexual-harassment interview prediction study — 62%/68% predicted confronting; 0% actually refused.
- Daffodil Days — 80% predict, ~50% buy.
- Clinical-trial-for-your-child vignette — flips from "yes, society benefits" to "no" when concrete.
- Ford Pinto — cost-benefit lens caused ethical fading; $11/car fix dropped.
- Marshmallow experiment — abstract framing aids self-control.
- Mom litmus test, eulogy exercise, joint vs. separate evaluation.
