## Main argument
Yes — happier employees work harder, and the relationship runs primarily from positive **inner work life** (emotions, motivation, perceptions of the workday) to better creative and productive performance. The piece reports on Amabile & Kramer's diary studies of thousands of workdays from professionals: on days when people felt happier, more intrinsically motivated, and more positive about their work environment, they did demonstrably better creative work. The strongest single driver of a good inner work day is **making progress on meaningful work** — what they call the **progress principle**.

## Key terms introduced
- **Inner work life** — the running stream of emotions, motivations, and perceptions employees experience during the workday.
- **Progress principle** — small, visible wins on meaningful work are the most powerful day-level motivator.
- **Catalysts and nourishers** — small managerial supports (clear goals, autonomy, resources, recognition, emotional support) that keep inner work life positive.

## Examples used
- Diary entries from professionals across industries showing that good days coincided with progress on meaningful tasks; bad days coincided with setbacks, blocked progress, or unclear direction.
- Manager behaviors that derail inner work life: pulling resources, shifting goals, ignoring contributions, public criticism.
