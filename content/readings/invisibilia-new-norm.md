## Main argument
Hosts Alix Spiegel and Lulu Miller (with Hanna Rosin) ask: **can you take an emotional norm — something that feels intractable, like the temperament of a whole nation or workplace — and deliberately change it?** The episode pairs two stories of organizations trying to install a new emotional norm, and ends on a critique: enforced emotional norms ("emotional labor") can produce worker exhaustion, burnout, and dissonance, even when they "work" on customers.

## Key terms introduced
- **Emotional norm** — the default emotional display expected in a culture or workplace.
- **Emotional labor (Alicia Grandey, Penn State org psych)** — the emotions companies *demand* from employees.
- **Emotional dissonance** — when your internal state diverges from the external display the role requires; sustained, this drives burnout and health problems.
- **"Service with a smile" default** — once installed, customers come to expect it and feel free to behave however they want toward employees who must "grin and take it."

## Examples used
- **McDonald's Russia (1990, Pushkin Square)** — Michael Gerling's team had to install the "American smile" in a culture where smiling at strangers was seen as dishonest/insincere ("evil capitalists" propaganda posters). Igor Korganov: Russians "were not allowed to be happy in the process" of building the happy future. The deeper challenge of opening McDonald's there wasn't yeast paperwork or phone infrastructure — it was changing employees' affective defaults.
- **Claire Nuer / "Learning as Leadership"** — Holocaust survivor turned consultant whose program used multi-day encounter sessions and exercises (including the now-infamous **foot massage** between Louisiana oil-field workers) to shift workplace emotional norms toward vulnerability and trust.
- **Alicia Grandey's research** — forced cheer is associated with health problems, performance errors, exhaustion/burnout; smiling is contagious and spreads to customers, but no clear bottom-line revenue gain. Customers' awareness of the norm tilts power toward customers and traps employees in dissonance.
