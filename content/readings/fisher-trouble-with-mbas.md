## Main argument
Employers find that freshly minted MBAs are quantitative geniuses but lack **interpersonal skills** — communication, teamwork, listening, leading peers. In response, top B-schools (MIT Sloan, Wharton, Tuck, Chicago Booth, UVA Darden, Berkeley Haas) are rebalancing curricula away from pure quant and toward "soft" skills, ethics, and team-based work. The article frames OB as exactly the kind of content B-schools were under-teaching — i.e., the content of OAM 330.

## Key terms introduced
- **Soft skills** — communication, teamwork, listening, persuasion, mentoring.
- **Interpersonal coursework** — team-building, communications, leadership coaching.
- **Double bottom line** — financial return *and* ethical/social impact (Tuck dean Paul Danos on a microfinance course).
- **B-schools as businesses** — responding to two customers: **corporate recruiters** (employers) and **students**.
- **Online degrees** — competitive pressure that pushed traditional B-schools to differentiate via in-person interpersonal training.

## Examples used
- **Jack Welch at MIT Sloan (2005)**: told students to "just concentrate on networking — everything else you can learn on the job." Dean Schmalensee was stunned; MIT launched a curriculum rethink.
- **GMAC's annual recruiter survey (since 2002)**: every year, recruiters say MBAs lack the ability to write and speak clearly and persuasively.
- **Chicago Booth**: only required course is communications + team-building (taught by trained 2nd-years); added "Spring Launch" people-skills seminar.
- **Wharton**: leadership program with one-on-one professional coaching; enrollment nearly doubled (50 → 90).
- **Tuck (Dartmouth)**: required microfinance course on global poverty (the "double bottom line").
- **Yale SOM**: required first-year course on planning a career, including coping with job loss and aging.
